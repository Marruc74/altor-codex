# Djupets Fasor — Detailed Summary

*"The Terrors of the Deep"* — an adventure module for the Swedish roleplaying
game **Drakar och Demoner Expert**.

> This is an original summary written for reference. It paraphrases the module's
> content and does not reproduce its text. *Djupets Fasor* is © RiotMinds AB
> (originally © 1989 Target Games AB).

---

## Publication details

- **Type:** Adventure module ("äventyr") for *Drakar & Demoner Expert*. It is
  not a standalone game — you need the Expert rules to use it. (Art. nr. 02-304.)
- **Published:** 1989, Target Games AB, Stockholm. Printed in Västerås.
- **Design:** Mikael Svanberg, Anders Blixt, Olle Sahlin.
- **Editing:** Anders Blixt, Olle Sahlin.
- **Interior art:** Tony Darwiche. **Cover:** Ken Kelly. **Maps:** Anne-Sophie
  Quarnström. **Graphic design/layout:** Stefan Thulin.
- **Length:** 48 interior pages (50 page-images including covers).

A note on game-stat abbreviations used below (Drakar och Demoner): **STY** =
Strength, **STO** = Size, **FYS** = Constitution, **SMI** = Agility, **INT** =
Intelligence, **PSY** = Psyche, **KAR** = Charisma, **SB** = damage bonus,
**KP** = body/hit points, **FV/B-värde** = skill value.

## The Plot

### The deep backstory

**Dracht and its dwarves.** Dracht was a small, almost completely isolated
dwarven mining village of about 25 houses and roughly a hundred dwarves, built
some 100 m into an extremely rich iron mine high in the Ula mountains
(Ula-bergen). About two months before the adventure begins it was sacked by a
warband of more than four dozen mountain trolls and orcs. After centuries of
feuding, the svartfolk ("black-folk") tribes had finally united under a single
leader specifically to seize the legendary dwarven riches and kill every
inhabitant. The raid was brutal but a bitter disappointment for the attackers —
they found almost nothing of value, and in their fury burned everything to the
ground. The dwarves never had a chance to defend themselves; old and young alike
were slaughtered in cold blood.

**The two missing dwarves.** When the attack came, the dwarves happened to be
assembled at the village square for a council. Two of them were absent: they had
been working in the deepest mine vein, almost 4,000 m down, where the thin rock
wall between the vein and an ancient natural cavern had cracked. The pair tumbled
through into a small underground lake. Fascinated by the enormous cavern, they
explored a beautiful passage in the southwest wall; it forked, they took the
right branch, and the porous opening collapsed behind them, forcing them onward.
They eventually reached a cavern containing a seemingly abandoned castle of
dwarven make. Exploring its cellar, they found a vault whose door was locked but
with the key still in it. They turned the key — and the door was flung open from
inside and something dragged them in. This was about 100 years before the events
of the adventure.

**The spirit (Anden / Bergets Ande).** Some 700 years earlier, a shaman had
asked the mountain's spirit where the dwarves should dig for the greatest ore
deposits. So offended was the spirit that it challenged the shaman to a spiritual
duel; against all odds the shaman won, and was granted a boon. The villagers used
the victory to have their mighty mage **bind the spirit into a grotesque stone
face** carved on the door of their treasure chamber, where it would serve as
guardian. The terms of the binding:

- While the door is shut, the spirit stays with the mountain. The door opens only
  if ten dwarves simultaneously shout "open!" in Dwarvish.
- The spirit is freed and returns into the mountain only when the *entire* living
  population has failed to present itself before the face for exactly two months.
  In that case nothing dramatic happens — the treasure simply becomes lost,
  sealed forever in solid stone.
- **The trap:** the spirit can *also* be freed if, within two months of the
  villagers last appearing before it, someone lines up *all* the inhabitants'
  corpses in front of the face. In that case the spirit is released — but the
  face explodes and triggers a local earthquake so violent that every hall,
  shaft and tunnel collapses within a couple of minutes.

**The castle (Slottet) and the Gruvgrävare family.** Nearly 200 years ago, a
surface baron named **Thor Gruvgrävare** had a daughter, **Esmeralda**, who
suffered an illness that made her unable to bear sunlight. So that she would not
have to live indoors forever, the baron renounced his title, sold everything, and
— with the help of dwarven friends — moved his family and a handful of volunteer
servants underground near the trading town of Nordsal. The dwarves built him a
small castle in a deep, secluded cavern beside a warm spring. The family lived
happily for about three years until Esmeralda, aged 15, vanished while exploring
a narrow passage; she was swept into the mountain by a stream and killed against
a boulder, staining the brook that fed the castle blood-red. The grief broke her
parents; they returned to the surface and died a few years later. No one visited
the castle cavern for generations.

**The necromancers and the death knight.** About a hundred years after the family
left, a necromancy academy of roughly a dozen mages used the abandoned castle.
Their **Leader** had recently returned from studying under a great sorcerer in the
south, bringing a new ritual to animate a uniquely powerful skeleton (the spell
*Frammana Dödsriddare*, "Summon Death Knight"). The academy's patron was a
ruthless, ambitious baron named **Skäktesköld**, who dreamed of ruling the whole
land. Learning that the ritual doubled-to-tripled a creature's physical and
mental power, he had himself transformed into a death knight. To the mages'
horror the resulting death knight kept his own will and proved far stronger than
expected — gaining, among other things, the ability to turn the creatures he kills
into a type of fast-made undead called **banemän** ("bane-men"). Skäktesköld and
the Leader secretly schemed to subjugate the entire cave system. But at a meeting
in the cellar vault the plan collapsed into a quarrel; Skäktesköld slaughtered
all the mages but one (who fled and locked the door behind him, going mad). Inside
the sealed vault the death knight turned the dead mages into banemän — and there
they remained, trapped, until the two curious dwarves from Dracht unlocked the
door a century later. Those dwarves were killed at once and animated as powerful
zombies.

**Skäktesköld's plan and the Nordsal crisis.** Skäktesköld intends to conquer the
underworld. His method: ambush the trade caravans in the pitch-dark Margoroth
cavern (through which all caravans to Nordsal must pass), build an army of strong,
skilled zombies, then attack and enslave a starving, isolated Nordsal. To make his
zombies, he and the banemän capture caravan guards, drug or terrify them, drain
them of blood, and animate the corpses — using a victim's own psyche as the power
source until he dies, then animating him too. The "careful" handling makes the
zombies hard to tell from living people (corpse-pale skin is normal underground
anyway). Within roughly a month of work he has built up about thirty skilled
zombie warriors and begun preying on the larger caravans. By the time the player
characters arrive, no caravan has reached Nordsal in over a month; food and fuel
are running out, and the blame has fallen unfairly on the vättar.

### The adventure proper

**Borreby.** It is early January. The player characters have spent over three
months as poorly paid laborers digging a moat and palisade for **Baron
Jalt-Tidwindson** in the remote northern village of Borreby. One bitterly cold
evening at the inn *Aftonlampan*, the baron's herald **Snikon Brylle** announces
that fifteen to twenty volunteer sled-drivers are wanted: under **Captain Osvald
Snirring** and his soldiers, they are to travel to the herders' base camp
**Tarros-Leba** and bring back the herders' annual taxes (14,000 silver pieces'
worth of dried reindeer meat) for the king. The pay — 11 sm/day — is over triple
their current wage, and the PCs sign up.

**The sled journey.** At dawn, 17 sleds set out, escorted by a decuria of heavy
cavalry, traveling some 35 km north up the frozen Borreå river to Tarros-Leba at
the foot of the Ula mountains.

**Tarros-Leba and the rumor of Dracht.** Among the nomadic herders (who call
themselves *aoale*), the PCs hear that the dwarven mining village of Dracht — said
to hold vast riches — was plundered by svartfolk two months earlier, and that the
raiders may not have found all the hidden treasure. The very old herder
**Marjolo** can give them a vague description of the route and a crude, not-to-
scale map sketch. Tempted, the PCs slip away and head for Dracht (which also means
deserting the baron's expedition — a real crime if they ever return to Borreby).

**Down into the underworld.** At Dracht the PCs find only burned ruins and
~96 frozen, mutilated dwarf corpses. In one mine hall they find the stone face and
make contact with the spirit, which tells them the face is the door to the
treasure chamber and that they can open it by lining up *all* the villagers'
corpses before it — but two corpses are missing (the dwarves who fell into the
deep hole). To open the vault, the PCs must descend the old mine hoist into the
depths, retrieve those two bodies, and complete the "key" of corpses. The spirit
does **not** tell them that doing so triggers the catastrophic earthquake. The
deep hole leads, via natural caverns, into the great primordial cave system — and
to the death knight.

**The cave system, Nordsal, and the truth.** The PCs explore the caves, reach the
trading town of Nordsal, and learn of the caravan crisis. Investigation (the town
library, the cave elves, the vättar, the death knight's hideout, and especially
the necromancer's coded diary in the castle) gradually reveals that the "robbers"
are undead led by a death knight, not ordinary bandits.

**The final battle.** The module stresses there is no single solution; meeting
the undead head-on is dangerous. Suggested approaches include: getting the cave
elves' envoy to use **Curse Undead** (which halves the death knight's
abilities); throwing **iron filings** over the spellcasting banemän to stop their
magic; stealing the keys at the death knight's side to lock undead inside the
vault; and assassinating them one by one. Even if the death knight and his
banemän are destroyed, the zombies still pursue their last order — kill all living
in sight — and all undead see perfectly in the dark.

**The treasures.** The module deliberately makes loot scarce. The mithril in
Dracht's treasure chamber is essentially unreachable, because retrieving the two
bodies and opening the vault collapses the whole mine. Nordsal, impoverished, can
offer its liberators little but honor. The realistic prizes are the death knight's
gear and his warhorse's barding, and the castle itself if the PCs choose to occupy
it.

### The sequel: "Insektoiderna kommer" ("The Insectoids Come")

A second, more military scenario that can follow on or stand alone. An insectoid
hive — driven from its old home by volcanic activity — invades the Nordsal region.
A capable PC is appointed neutral commander of the combined human, cave-elf and
vätte forces and must repel or delay the insectoids using a set of mass-combat
rules (rounds of 30 minutes, "combat points" per fighter, terrain bonuses for
fortifying in the castle or behind walls, an odds-based result table, scouting,
messengers, etc.). The insectoids are not suicidal: they withdraw or sue for peace
once they lose half their warriors, two-thirds of their workers, or two leaders.

## Places

### Surface / region

- **Grevskapet Isfågel** — the county containing Borreby and Tarros-Leba, on the
  edge of the wilderness near the Ula mountains.
- **Borreby** — grim northern village where the adventure starts; site of Baron
  Jalt-Tidwindson's donjon and the inn *Aftonlampan*.
- **Tarros-Leba** — the aoale herders' main base camp at the foot of the Ula
  mountains: roughly half a dozen log cabins plus animal pens and the large
  guesthouse *Yvilia-Leba*. Where the tax-meat is gathered and where the PCs hear
  of Dracht.
- **Dracht** — the sacked dwarven mining village in a deep valley high in the Ula
  mountains, ~100 m down; burned ruins and ~96 corpses.

### Dracht's mine (Gruvan)

1. **Nedgångssalen (the Descent Hall)** — contains the great winch-and-platform
   hoist over the deep mine shaft (descends in stages to the ~4,000 m vein), and a
   large clay smelting cauldron amid drifts of coal.
2. **Festsalen (the Feast Hall)** — three long tables (overturned by the
   raiders); ~100 high-relief carvings of dwarves working ore. A hidden mechanism
   (press a carved dwarf's eye in the SE corner) opens a passage to the Spirit
   Hall.
3. **Andesalen (the Spirit Hall)** — water-drop-shaped; holds the grotesque,
   horned **stone face** with a tilting stone stick in its mouth that grants
   contact with the bound mountain spirit.
4. **Skattkammaren (the Treasure Chamber)** — mirror of the Spirit Hall but
   entered through the "drop tip," with no face on the inner side; floor, walls,
   ceiling and even the door lined with ~30 cm of lead. Two chests: one holds
   5–10 mithril weapons, the other 25 mithril ingots (~10,000 sm each). Reaching
   it triggers the earthquake; PCs may grab only a couple of items per round
   before everything collapses.

### The primordial cave system (Urgrottorna)

1. **Källgrottan (the Spring Cave)** — a ~10 m-wide, 23 m-deep lake under the
   hole the PCs descend through; a passage leads SE.
2. **Pelarsalen (the Pillar Hall)** — a vast 35 m-high cavern with two huge
   fused-stalactite pillars and a drinkable brook.
3. **3a — Vättehemsgrottan / 3b — Stjärngrottan** — the vättar's mining caves,
   home to ~30 of them (dwellings hewn skillfully into the rock; see the vätte
   section).
4. **Handelsleden (the Trade Route)** — the broad main passage running the length
   of the cave system, from the northern coast to the southern mountains, with
   hewn rest-stops.
5. **Nordsalsgrottan** — the immense cavern holding the city of Nordsal, lit by
   countless star-light fungi, split by the river Elbion.
6. **Margorothgrottan (Margoroth)** — a vast, near-lightless cavern where all
   caravans pass and where the undead ambush them; its eastern end opens onto
   **Gorgorahavet**.
7. **Gorgorahavet (the Gorgora Sea)** — actually a 500 m-deep lake, not endless;
   rumored to host "unholy nymphs" whose song is really the cave elves singing
   from a hidden hollow on its eastern shore.
8. **Alvgrottan (the Elf Cave, no. 7 on the cave map)** — the cave elves'
   beautiful fungus-lit home, reached via the hollow at Gorgorahavet.
9. **8a / 8b (Spökgrottan) / 8c** — minor caves (Stensalen, Mellansalen, etc.);
   8b and 8c are occasionally used by the undead.
10. **Vapengrottan (the Weapon Cave, no. 9)** — the undead's hideout, holding a
    small stash of arms and armor looted from caravans, plus month-old human
    corpse-rot. The terrible wounds here (from the death knight's broadsword) are
    a key clue that the raiders are not the small vättar.
11. **Vårgrottan (no. 10)** — a wedge-shaped cavern with a warm-spring-fed lake
    (Varmsjön, ~20 °C) and a fast river (Slussån) one can float down (nearly
    naked) to reach Slottsgrottan. Along the way, Esmeralda's wedged skeleton is
    found.
12. **Slottsgrottan (the Castle Cave, no. 11)** — the deep, oppressive cavern
    holding Skäktesköld's base, the Gruvgrävare castle, guarded by a baneman and
    five zombies.

### Nordsal (the city)

A trading town of ~900 inhabitants (~70% human, ~30% dwarf, a few others) in a
huge cavern, lit round-the-clock by cultivated star-light fungi. Stone houses with
flat roofs and ornate facades, kept apart for fire safety. Governed by an elected
**burgomaster** (currently the dwarf Gervar Rytare) and a seven-member council.
Strict fire- and fishing-permits and harsh penalties for damaging the fungi. A
24-strong city watch; a Saturday market. By the adventure's timeline it is
starving and increasingly desperate; if not relieved within four weeks the
inhabitants abandon the city and flee north.

Notable buildings (numbered as on the city map):

- **Rådhuset (Town Hall, 1)** — burgomaster's home, council chamber, courthouse,
  treasury, and archive (records of the Gruvgrävare family).
- **Bibliotek (Library, 27)** — run by **Aini Dimfaer**; abbreviated tale of
  Baron Gruvgrävare and a castle map can be researched here.
- **Kloster (Monastery, 20)** — Eilana-monks who venerate the dark and the
  star-light fungi; provide healing (skills 13–20).
- **Värdshus/Tavernor (17, 30, 32)** — *Den glade alven* (the finest inn, run by
  Jonvar Stark and Mala); *Den lustiga mössan* (run by Elsifir) and *Den
  stegrande bärbaggen* (run by Melina and Gordo) — grubbier taverns.
- **Animistmagiker (19)** — **Nilkand Svampvän**, the town's weather-mage; can
  advise on necromancy and on iron filings vs. spellcasting.
- **Alkemist (48)** — **Ehalen Likvis**.
- **Spåman (18)** — the fortune-teller **Fereske Avalo**, who foretells "meeting
  the wandering death" and grave danger.
- **Trubadur (35)** — **Simono Shî**, who knows the romantic ballad of Baron
  Gruvgrävare and Esmeralda.
- Plus the usual trades: bakery (21, threatened with closure by the flour
  shortage), engravers (Akarin Gebra, Sohia Kvard), mining-gear seller (Svarthök),
  cart-makers (Budd & Pulma), rental stable of carry-beetles (9), herald (Yleva
  Enhörning, 14), midwife/apothecary (Gudri Katta, 46), customs office (33),
  executioner (the dwarf Sekvin Flåse, 42), armorer (Hagbard Olstin, 34),
  weaponsmiths (Enrid Olstin and the dwarf Kurin Skarpegg, 8 & 41), oil merchants
  (24, 43), pawnbrokers (a reliable dwarf at 13, a shifty human at 45), herbalist
  (Vilmar Breti, 16), teachers (Reso & Akarina Tustafi, 47), scribe (Evrik Surde,
  2) and translator (his brother Veserin Surde, 28), tailors/seamstress, smiths
  (who will sell iron filings), and the warm-spring aqueduct (54).

### The Castle (Slottet)

A robust dwarf-build of mortared granite, showing classic dwarven hexagon/octagon
forms. Three levels:

- **Ground floor:** veranda and silted-up fountains outside; the **Salon (1)**
  (Gruvgrävare's reception hall, with his arms on shields and banners); **Great
  Dining Hall (2)**; **Kitchen (3)**; **Pantry (4)** — hiding a joke "monster," a
  huge animated gorgonzola cheese; **Laundry (5)** and bathing rooms heated by the
  warm spring; a small hall, toilet, and servants' quarters (8, 10, 11), plus the
  stablemaster's room (12).
- **Upper floor:** Gruvgrävare's workroom and bedroom (rooms 1–2); **the Library
  (5)** — the building's "harmonic center," a diamond-shaped room hiding a secret
  10 km escape tunnel and ~100 books (20 of them necromantic manuals up to FV 20),
  and where the **coded Diary** is found; bathrooms and toilets; **Esmeralda's room
  (9)**, now the dead mages' quarters; **Junior's room (10)**, later the Leader's;
  the living room (11).
- **Cellar:** wine cellar (1, hiding priceless aged cognac); a corridor of
  iron-doored storerooms (2–6); and **the Laboratory/vault (7)** — the room with
  the gold pentagram where Skäktesköld and the others were animated, and where the
  unbearable stench imposes penalties on the living.

### The vättar's dwellings

Six rooms hewn into the rock by the river Elbion: cooking hearth, a bedroom, a
dining hall, storerooms of salted meat/fish and mead/beer, and a children's room.
Squalid and reeking, but very skillfully made.

## Characters & Creatures

### Central story figures

- **Baron Skäktesköld (the Death Knight / Dödsriddare)** — the villain: an
  ambitious, evil baron transformed into an exceptionally powerful undead knight
  who retains his own will and intelligence. Plans to conquer the entire
  underworld. Carries the keys to all the castle's locks. Statistically a juggernaut
  (STY 45, INT 30, PSY 51, large damage bonus, KP 29) clad in matte-black
  mithril plate with a large shield; skilled in sword, brawling, tactics, magic-
  lore and leadership. Can unleash a **paralyzing aura** (10 m radius) and can
  animate the dead into banemän.
- **The Leader (Banemansledare)** — head of the necromancer academy, who brought
  back the death-knight ritual and conspired with Skäktesköld; now one of the
  undead. The most accomplished spellcaster of the group (Necromancy 24; spells
  including *Summon Death Knight*, *Animate Dead*, *Death-hand*, *Paralysis*,
  *Antimagic*).
- **The mage diarist** — the one academy mage who survived, fled, and went mad.
  His secret coded diary, left in the castle library, is the adventure's single
  most important clue.
- **The two dwarves of Dracht** — the pair who fell into the deep hole and opened
  the vault; killed and animated as powerful **dwarf zombies** (STY 54, KP 31).
- **Fillo** — Skäktesköld's black warhorse, now an undead **war-horse** (STY 48,
  KP 39) in matte-black mithril barding; attacks anything living that enters the
  stable.
- **The ten banemän** — the animated corpses of the dead necromancers; skeletal
  undead spellcasters (Necromancy 19; spells incl. *Animate Dead*, *Paralysis*,
  *Blindness*, *Terror*) who can fire a beam of darkness from their eyes that
  drains a target's PSY.
- **Bergets Ande (the Mountain Spirit)** — the immaterial spirit bound to the
  stone face at Dracht; manifests as a translucent, very muscular bearded man
  (SMI 23, INT 18, PSY 34). Guardian of the treasure chamber and the source of the
  earthquake trap.
- **Thor Gruvgrävare** — the surface baron who built the castle for his
  sun-sensitive daughter. **Valhild** — his wife. **Esmeralda** — their daughter,
  who died in the underground stream; her skeleton is found in Vårgrottan and her
  fate underlies the whole castle backstory.

### Borreby & the journey

- **Baron Jalt-Tidwindson** — lord of Borreby, whose moat-and-palisade project
  employs the PCs.
- **Snikon Brylle** — the baron's self-important young herald.
- **Captain Osvald Snirring** — leads the soldiers escorting the tax expedition.
- **Marjolo** — the very old aoale elder at Tarros-Leba who knows the way to
  Dracht and provides the rough map.
- **The fur-trapper couple** — a possible roadside encounter: a man and his
  pregnant wife desperately searching for their infant, stolen by svartfolk; they
  offer the PCs their few coins for help.

### Nordsal NPCs (statted)

- **Gervar Rytare** — the dwarf burgomaster (167 years old), an experienced,
  shrewd politician; in the sequel he advises the PC commander. (STY 16, INT 17,
  skilled in administration/law, history, appraisal, bargaining and persuasion.)
- **Klamorkan Grå** — captain of the city watch; a tough brawler-type, loyal to
  the burgomaster, but no tactician. (STY 15, SMI 15, broadsword/shield 18.)
- **Nilkand Svampvän** — the 43-year-old animist weather-mage (spells incl.
  *Heal*, *Plant Growth*, *Control Plants*, *Wind/Weather Control*).
- **Ehalen Likvis** — the alchemist (Botany 18, Drug-lore 19).
- Generic **city-watch** and **militia** profiles are given for the sequel battle.

### The cave elves (Grottalverna)

A reclusive colony of 25–30 elves plus two "elf-cats" (alfiner) in the
fungus-lit Alvgrottan, living by mushroom-farming and cave-wine. They help only if
convinced the raiders are undead, and only if the PCs keep their home secret.

- **Garafin** — the chieftain. **Tindo** — his wife. Garafin recounts the full
  tragic history of the Gruvgrävare castle.
- **Senadi, Moder Jords budbärare (Mother Earth's Envoy)** — the colony's
  divinely chosen agent (INT 20, PSY 35). Her god-gifts include boosting her own
  Strength, merging into surroundings, speaking with beasts and plants, and most
  importantly **Förbanna Odöd (Curse Undead)**, which halves a chosen undead's
  abilities, skills and movement for several days — the PCs' best weapon against
  the death knight.

### The vättar

A clan of ~30 reclusive, mistrusted goblin-kin miners, unfairly blamed for the
caravan attacks. They know the cave system well (except the elves' cave) and can
be won over by curing their sick, impressing them with magic, or proving the
raiders are undead.

- **Kassits** — the cunning chieftain (INT 17), happy to use the PCs to remove a
  rival.
- **Mustuf** — a cocky, warlike vätte champion (SMI 18, FYS 17) and Kassits's
  rival; may challenge a PC to a duel, leading (depending on outcome) to internal
  power struggles the PCs can exploit.

### Monster / creature profiles (statted)

- **Death Knight, banemän, baneman-leader, and zombies** (human / dwarf / vätte
  variants) — the undead detailed above.
- **Svartfolk:** **Orch** (orcs, STY 14, broadsword/shield 17) and **Rese**
  (huge mountain-troll/giants, STY 35, SB 1T10, great clubs) — the raiders who
  sacked Dracht.
- **Vätte** — generic profile (skilled miners; cave navigation 20).
- **Grottalv (cave elf)** — generic profile; like all their kind, equipped with
  natural sonar to "see" in darkness.
- **Aoale-krigare (herder warrior)** — the nomads of Tarros-Leba (spear/shield/
  bow, tracking and skiing).
- **Animals:** **Grottsvin** (cave pig) and **Bärbagge** (a large rideable/pack
  "carry-beetle").
- **Insectoids** (for the sequel) — ant-like underground hive creatures with keen
  smell and bat-like sonar, organized into a non-mobile **Queen**, **Leaders**
  (nobility/commanders), flying **Messengers** (perfect memory), **Warriors**
  (throwing spears, bite and claws), **Diggers** (armor-shredding bite), and
  **Workers**.

## Notable Items, Gear & Magic (beyond ordinary equipment)

- **The mithril hoard (Dracht treasure chamber)** — 5–10 mithril weapons and
  25 mithril ingots worth ~10,000 sm each; practically unobtainable because
  claiming it collapses the mine.
- **Skäktesköld's gear** — matte-black mithril full plate and large shield (smith
  quality B5); a broadsword/mace with a damage bonus that leaves the tell-tale
  brutal wounds; and the **ring of keys** to every castle lock (used to seal undead
  in the vault).
- **Fillo's barding** — matte-black mithril ring-mail for head and body
  (recoverable loot).
- **Magic-potion caches in the castle** —
  - Gruvgrävare's old bedroom (Skäktesköld's bundle): a long dagger (1T4+2),
    coins, one *Heal* (E1) and three *Darkvision* (E3) potions.
  - Esmeralda's room (the dead mages' belongings): coins, two *Heal* (E1) and one
    *Darkvision* (E1).
  - Junior's room (the Leader's): coins only.
  - Wine cellar: a few bottles of priceless aged cognac.
- **The Diary (Dagboken), "Volume Nine"** — the necromancer's journal, written in
  a simple **substitution cipher** (each letter shifted forward by its own
  alphabetical position, wrapping around; "ö" unchanged). Decoded, it gives a dated
  first-person account of the Leader's return, Skäktesköld's transformation into a
  death knight, the mages' growing dread, and the final betrayal — the key to
  understanding the whole mystery.
- **Iron filings (järnfilspån)** — buyable from Nordsal's smiths; thrown over the
  banemän, they prevent them from casting spells.
- **Förbanna Odöd (Curse Undead)** — Senadi's divine ability that halves the death
  knight's power; see above.
- **The stone face / spirit-binding** — the magical guardian of Dracht's treasure
  and the mechanism of the climactic earthquake.

## Key hazards & mechanics to remember

- **The earthquake trap** — lining up the corpses to open Dracht's vault frees the
  spirit and destroys the entire mine within minutes (a damage table escalates each
  round); the PCs get only a few rounds to grab loot before everything caves in.
- **The lead-lined treasure chamber** — its 30 cm lead lining is total and even
  coats the door.
- **Undead persistence** — even after the death knight and banemän are destroyed,
  the zombies carry out their final standing order (kill all living in sight), and
  every undead sees perfectly in the dark.
- **No single solution** — the module explicitly rewards planning, clue-gathering
  and creativity (curse, iron filings, locking them in, picking them off) over a
  stand-up fight.
