# The Cold That Stays

Three people who each walked away from the life they were handed, a runaway dockhand, a street thief, and a heretic scholar, are drawn together on the road, only to find that one of them carried something out of an ancient tomb that has been patient for a thousand years and is no longer content to wait. An original story set in Ereb Altor, the world of Drakar och Demoner. The series is the story of an unremarkable found family standing between that thing and a world that does not believe it exists.

## The Road Out of Brinewatch

Three chapters. Bram leaves the dock and his father; falls in with a struggling caravan at Vesket by correctly diagnosing a cracked axle; is taken on as muscle and quietly trained by Orvith on the Morral run. On the southern loop he learns merchant Corvel is enslaving the village of Mern through rigged debt, and, against his own contract and interest, gives the village elder the truth, losing his post but keeping himself. Ends with Bram heading north with a new caravan, at peace with his father’s nature, the coin still in his pocket. Function: establishes Bram, his code, the road, and the texture of the working world.

## Kaelene, Part One

Kaelene, sixteen and starving in Poane, mistimes a market theft by four seconds, is caught and beaten by an indifferent crowd, then is rescued by Tarek, who has been watching not the theft but the skill behind it. Over soup on Cutter’s Lane he reframes her survival instinct as a trainable craft and offers her apprenticeship in escort/retrieval/investigation work, on the promise that “what I teach you will be yours regardless.” She accepts. Function: establishes Kaelene, her perception-skill, Tarek, and the work that will later take them to Jorpagna.

## The Watchful Mind

Three chapters. At the Academy in Atrema, Aelthira finds two foundational citations that cannot both be true, traces the discrepancy into a restricted archive, and discovers that the school’s magic has been quietly “touching” something, a fact documented in pre-Academy texts and deliberately buried. A watchful presence notices her. Faculty (Carenthal and two seniors) pressure her to stop; none of them are lying, which is worse. Unable to teach a model she knows is incomplete, she leaves, heading south to Jorvaine to find a vanished practitioner. Function: establishes Aelthira, the magic system, the cover-up, and the intellectual route into the mytharc.

## Prologue: A Complete Draft

Eight numbered beats, structured as a hook-and-pivot:

| Beat | Content |
| --- | --- |
| 1 to 4 The tomb | Kaelene and Tarek, three years on, delve the ruins of Jorpagna. They wake the Crowned One; it strikes Tarek down; he tells Kaelene to run; she escapes alone. “She was out. Tarek was not.” |
| 5 Time skip | One year later, in a tavern, Bram performs his (ever-growing) bear story to a charmed room. Kaelene watches the fire, hollowed out; Aelthira reads her silence. The trio is established as a travelling company. |
| 6 The nightmare | Kaelene’s recurring dream of the endless corridor, this time it grows a wrong right turn, and the cold that finds every crack. She wakes drenched, runs Tarek’s breathing drill. |
| 7 The comfort | Bram, already awake, comforts her without prying. Their relationship and his particular gentleness are drawn. “For now, that was enough.” |
| 8 The promise | Far below, the Crowned One feels the “thread” leading up to her. “She had not escaped the tomb. She had been let out.” The series engine starts. |

Function: converts three character studies into one story by (a) seating all three at the same table, (b) revealing the loss that haunts Kaelene, and (c) planting the antagonist’s intent. It is, structurally, the true Chapter Zero of the series.

## The Secret of the Skeleton Village

A complete standalone adventure of roughly 30,000 words, set after the prologue with the trio already a seasoned company. Where the first four drafts are character studies and convergence, this one is plot-forward and deliberately lighter, a self-described “beach read” built on a twelve-step hero’s-journey structure rather than the prologue’s eight beats. The whole thing unfolds in a single day at a single ruined location.

On a quiet road the trio are stopped by the ghost of a priest, who begs them to cleanse a nearby village of the skeletons a necromancer left behind a century ago. Kaelene hesitates, undead and tombs are precisely what cost her Tarek, but it is also Kaelene who says yes. They fight through the ruined village and into the church, where an ambush from the shadowed alcoves is the hardest beat of the day. Behind the altar Kaelene finds a carved sunburst-and-sword emblem of The Shining Way, turns it, and opens a hidden stair to the crypt; she knows the mark because Tarek taught her to read it. Below, they clear the last skeletons and reach the intact grave chamber of the nameless knight. They take nothing. The ghost thanks each of them by name, is finally at peace, and the village is freed.

Function: the first proof of the episodic “job on the road” shape §10 anticipates, a complete, satisfying outing that exercises the trio’s combat dynamic and their bond without leaning on the mytharc. It deepens Tarek (the emblem he taught Kaelene becomes the key to the crypt) and introduces The Shining Way and its nameless champion. Tonally it is almost pure warm clock: the cold plot does not advance here, though a grave-robbing necromancer, an army of raised dead, and the choice to honour a forgotten knight all rhyme with the larger story’s concerns. A useful template for how a self-contained volume can stand alone and still feed the spine.

## What the Mist Keeps

A complete adventure of roughly 40,000 words, the second self-contained road job, and the first written at full novel length. It is set after “The Secret of the Skeleton Village,” with the trio a seasoned company resting, for now, in the forest village of Outskirt. The plot is adapted from the Drakar och Demoner module Dimön (here renamed The Misty Island). Where the skeleton village unfolds in a single day at one ruin on an eight-beat shape, this one runs a longer arc on a twelve-step structure: an ordinary world at the inn, a call, a chosen crossing, a delve on two levels, and a long, quiet aftermath.

Three terrified dwarves bring word of an island that appears in the mist on the Grey, and the shabby villager Sirter sells the trio a burned map of it. Against the exact shape of her nightmare, Kaelene chooses to go. They provision from the hermit alchemist Mifalodor, take a quiet memory of Tarek from Rabindranath’s chapel of The Shining Way, talk the farm-children Vagnhild and Biorn out of following, and row across the misted lake. On the island they go down through cold water into a dead sorcerer’s drowned hall: the ghost Cadal gives Bram a warding medallion; they bluff past the cave troll Okvir; and Bram, knocked into the deep and stripped of shield and then sword, kills the bound death-demon Akrae with a knife. Below, they find the cold cells of Tuviol and her brother Emrys and read a century-old tragedy off the walls. They answer the demon Khaa’s three riddles, leap a rigged plank, and take the Deathbringer, Cadal’s own black-bladed sword, plus a modest share of plain coin and loose stones, Kaelene leaving the worked and bound pieces of the hoard where they lie. They carry the answer back to Cadal, who is laid to rest, and go home.

Function (warm): the second proof of the episodic road-job shape §10 anticipates, and the first to run at novel length. It is Kaelene’s book, she walks back into the furniture of the thing that broke her (an island, mist, a cold drowned tomb) and climbs back out on her own feet, a quiet resurrection she never has to name, and it is Bram’s clean lost-and-found: the man who goes into the water with a shield and comes out with a knife, then comes up out of the vault carrying a dead champion’s medallion and sword, and modest plain spoils besides, a good part of which they press on the Greatstead family on the way home, for a dowry and a hard winter, and which Vagnhild turns to arming Biorn’s training. The Shining Way returns through Rabindranath’s chapel, a second living touchpoint for the order and for Tarek’s teaching. Taking the Deathbringer is the deliberate inverse of the skeleton village’s leave-it-untouched: there the dead wanted his blade kept, here the dead wants his carried.

Function (cold): unlike the skeleton village, this job brushes the cold plot for real, though it stays background colour. The island amplifies magic, Aelthira feels her own power run cheap and abundant, the thing the Academy buried proven in her body, and the sorcerer’s hand-cut Hollow Mark is, she privately sees, the same figure her school locked away. Only she carries that cold off the island; the others walk away whole. See §3.5 and §4 for the mythology this advances, and §8 for the continuity it raises (the unnamed sorcerer; the Deathbringer as a possible new way to be noticed).

## The Unicorn Horn

A complete adventure and the trio’s third road job, longer and more mobile than the first two: where the skeleton village and the Misty Island each turned on a single ruined site in a single day, this one is a journey. An old priestess of the Delitha order, called the Sanctified, hires the three at a healing temple near the river village of Kérem-el-Krôm to do two linked things: recover a relic the order has lost track of, and find the child the enemy has taken for it. The relic is a unicorn-horn fragment and the spirit-stone bound to it, hidden a generation ago by the dead smith-adventurer Larm Legast in catacombs beneath his own house in the free city of Kandra, behind three locked doors that need three scattered keys. The child is Liella, twelve years old, last of the bloodline of the order’s founder, taken by the Zeeri, the priests of the left-hand path, because she is bound to the relic and dreams the cold corridor in her sleep, the same dream Kaelene has called grief for three years. The trio travel down the great river by way of the harbour town Hébanon to Kandra, find Larm’s daughter Liva and the first key, recover the second from a well on the southern square and the third from the city’s own owl coat-of-arms above Larm’s door, and go down into the catacombs, where the Zeeri priest Sarn is already working a rite at a figure cut into the rock at the foot of the buried pillar, the deepest knock-point in the country. The rescue becomes a race, then a trap, then a thing much larger than either.

Function (warm): the third proof of the road-job shape, and the one that most tests the family it depends on. It is Kaelene’s turning. She goes down on purpose into stone and bone and the cold corridor of her own nightmare, with the child’s hand in hers, and at the heart of it she stops running, steps toward the thing instead of away, and says the words it took her three years to find: that someone came, that the child is not a girl no one looks for. Liella saves herself, which is the point, refusing the open door from the inside with the relic warm in her hands. The order takes her in at the close, a true home where she can be taught to live alongside the dreams, which is the right thing and also a loss, and the trio ride out closer than they came in.

Function (cold): this is the job where the cold clock stops simmering and spikes. The figure cut into the cavern floor is the Hollow Mark, finished where the magicians of Kandra spent four hundred years refusing to finish their own, and it is the same figure Aelthira knows from the island and from the Academy: one thing, seen now four times. The rite worked at the deepest knock-point in the country, and Bram’s loosing of the Deathbringer’s fire to break it, ring like a bell in the deep, and the thing on the far side hears. The book closes on the Crowned One no longer merely following Kaelene’s thread but climbing it, and on Aelthira at last telling Kaelene part of the truth: that the figure is real, that the thing that took Tarek is real and patient and has just heard them, and that she is done being able to call the dream grief. The dramatic-irony gap the series runs on closes another notch, now inside the trio as well as between the trio and the reader. See §3.2 for Kandra and the Magicians’ House, §6 for Liella and the orders, and §9 for the relic and the Hollow Mark.

## The Borrowed War

A complete adventure of roughly twenty-two thousand words and the trio’s fourth road job, the first taken as a deliberate side story. Set about two weeks after “The Unicorn Horn,” it adapts the Drakar och Demoner module Enhörningen och Drakormen and moves the trio off their own map: they answer a recruiter’s poster on the Berendien coast and sail east across the Copper Sea to Hynsolge, a small sun-baked land of lemon groves and river cities in the tenth year of a civil war between a freed-serf New Army, marching under the slogan of the twisted horn, and the old baronage. Hired as foreign muscle, the three find the war shadowed by something older: a star-cult, the Star Doctrine, serving Onaabys of the Red Moon, which has carried a dying fallen star down out of the forest into the city of Greyburg and means to use it. Across one packed week they read the city, break a well-poisoning, spring a thief, watch a grave-rite that speaks a recipe of living parts (a burnt magician’s heart, a beast-man’s blood, the skin of a burning elf), and go down into a temple beneath the city where the cult’s half-demon master, Parikila Omurtaag, waits with a blade that drinks the light. The rescue and the war resolve in the same dawn.

Function (warm): the proof that the series can take a holiday. It is the lightest and most external of the road jobs, the trio at their best as a working unit far from anything that hurts them, and it lands three quiet character beats. Bram resolves the worry “The Unicorn Horn” left him: cornered against a half-demon he cannot out-fight, he wakes the Deathbringer’s salamanders again and feels none of the wrongness he feared, because the fire is plainly and only needed, which tells him the thing he was afraid of was never the sword but the temptation to reach for it when he didn’t have to. Kaelene, who reads the strange city as none of the others can, laughs more than she has since the tomb. And the book closes on the warm clock alone: a medal stamped with a unicorn, a freed poet, a ship turning west, an apple shared at the rail.

Function (cold): a held breath, on purpose, and the series’ first demonstration of its own firewall. Everything in Hynsolge that rhymes with the mytharc is deliberately not the mytharc. The figure cut in the temple floor is a star-cult’s pentacle and explicitly not the Hollow Mark; the fallen star is a spent relic of the Red Moon Raukhra, nearly dead, and not the cold that took Tarek; Onaabys and the Star Doctrine are a separate cosmic axis, the Red Moon’s business, not the door under Kandra. Aelthira sees every one of these rhymes, judges them, sets them aside, and says nothing, so the only thing that crosses into the spine is a private lesson in telling the real thing from its echo. The one live thread it leaves is parked off the map on that separate axis: the half-demon Parikila escapes and carries the dying star south to Soluna, to a fire his son keeps, to be fed back to strength over years against a day his kind reckon in centuries. A threat, but a different one, and a long way away. See §3.2 for Hynsolge and Greyburg, §6 for the eastern cast, and §9 for the Star Doctrine and the Red Moon.

## The Shadow of a Rose

A complete adventure of roughly thirty-two thousand words across twenty-one chapters, and the trio’s fifth road job, the second taken as a deliberate side story. Set about three weeks after “The Unicorn Horn” and adapting the Drakar och Demoner module Skuggan av en ros from the Krilloan campaign, it carries the trio further from their own map than they have ever gone: down the one-way strait the locals call the Way Out, on a careless whim, to Krilloan, a canal-laced jungle port on the Sanritra peninsula south of the Copper Sea, easy to sail to and all but impossible to leave, because the strait only opens north on the rare RhabdoRhinn wind that blows perhaps once in five years. They mean to spend one idle evening and instead catch a dying courier’s silver cup, a coven’s grudge, and a vampire’s patience.

A clerk dies in a lane pressing a nailed box into Kaelene’s arms, and a cold that goes under the skin and chooses passes through all three. The cup inside is holy to an imprisoned death-goddess; the magicians of Ordo Magica covet it; a witch-coven, the Black Rose Brotherhood, hunts it. The trio are read from the inside by a passenger riding Bram, herded through a clue-trail of poisoned suppers, a ransacked room, a brothel murder, a parrot with a pawn-ticket number in its head, and a stolen Black Book, until a frame-up for sacrilege in a temple of the Shining Way lands them under a death sentence. Broken out by a masked benefactor, they surface into a city the vampire Lerajie has set alight with walking dead, and find that the patient coven has lost control of its own monster. The Brotherhood sues for peace; the trio ally with one evil against a worse one, cross by a mushroom-ring rite into the demon-prince Hemaquiel’s owned dimensions, bargain the vampire’s life out of his own god, hunt him through a labyrinthine Black Library, and end him in fire. They wake a week gone in the waking world, the cup and book returned to the hands with the better claim, their name cleaned, a house deeded to them, and no wind to carry them home.

Function (warm): the second proof that the series can take a holiday, and the first to leave the trio somewhere new for good. It is the book where the couple is most at ease: Bram and Kaelene are written plainly as the pair they are, with light touches throughout, a complicated beat the night he learns he is the one being ridden, a quiet hour in the condemned cell, a wink at the crossing, and a fuller, tasteful scene once he is freed of the passenger and they have a roof. Kaelene reads the strange city as none of the others can and is, for the first time, simply happy in a place; Aelthira carries the firewall load; Bram anchors the climax and, cornered against a vampire and a two-headed nightwolf, looses the Deathbringer’s fire again and feels none of the old dread, because it is plainly needed and is only a tool. The ending is a true resolution that still keeps them in Krilloan: the threat over, the debts paid, the three settled into a gifted house to wait out a wind that may not come for years, ready for whatever the city brings next.

Function (cold): a held breath and the series’ hardest firewall, because this module rhymes with the spine more loudly than any other. Everything that looks like the mytharc is deliberately not it: the five-pointed star on the Black Book is a common pentagram (Kaelene names it; Aelthira rules it not the Hollow Mark), Hemaquiel’s pocket-dimensions are keyed, owned real estate and not the other side, Hemaquiel is a transaction and not the cold, and the goddess Imaria is a different imprisoned prisoner on a different axis, not the Crowned One and not freed here. Aelthira sees each rhyme, judges it, and tells no one. The one thing that crosses toward the spine is a deferred lever: she rules the Black Library a demon’s vanity but files it as the one place that could answer what the Hollow Mark is and whether Tarek is dead, a resource she could return to on her own account. Vlad Kamantur, the deathless memory-wiped founder of Ordo Magica and Tannatopol, is seeded as a portrait for the same purpose. The cup also teaches her, quietly, that the dark holds more than one imprisoned death-power, that she has been learning the shape not of one enemy but of a condition. See §3.2 for Krilloan, §6 for the Krilloan cast, and §9 for the new terms.

## The Made Dragon

A complete novel of about twenty-two thousand words across twelve chapters, the trio’s sixth road job and their first taken as residents rather than travellers, set about a month after “The Shadow of a Rose” and adapting the Drakar och Demoner module Fiender av begynnelsen from the Krilloan campaign. Settled into the house Mildred deeded them, with Krilloan turned at last from a puzzle into a home, the three are sought out by Yrea Destanior, the Commendant’s daughter, whose youngest brother has vanished from a locked room with nothing taken but a chicken bone. The missing brother proves the smallest visible end of a conspiracy to turn the city against the necromancers of Tannatopol and burn the island, and the rescue becomes a race, against a built mob and a made dragon, to take the whole machine apart before a dawn march.

Groffotor was taken through his family’s hundred-year-old escape tunnel with the family’s own key, and turned green by an animism forced into him too young; a hidden letter gives the real demand, that the Commendant keep his swords sheathed while an organisation marches on Tannatopol, or never see his son again. Kaelene reads the conspiracy’s hand in the streets, a gentle necromancer driven to murder by a harmonist’s sour music and torn apart by the crowd, a tavern burned while the watch stands idle, a tainted blessing in a temple of the Shining Way that opens the faithful for an illusionist to seed with hatred. The hand is the animist Jesefael Bamilit, a hundred and twenty-six and looking forty, who has regrown the long-dead dragon Morriart from a scrap of her hide, had the witch Torquivel age the hatchling to full size in a season, and had the dead dragon’s vengeful soul poured into it through a drugged spiritist prodigy, Narnie Santhan, and her hidden passenger, the nature-spirit Perzotlios. His weapon is a lirisin, an elf-made sun-crystal that annihilates the undead; his target is the necromancer-isle that is also the city’s hospice for the dying. The trio follow the symbolist Lucretia Meleagros to Jesefael’s floating palace in Meh-Sylfhin, find a Caliban’s Plate that teleports down to the Dragon Cave, and descend before the march to free the boy, free the girl, and break the dragon.

Function (warm): the first book of the Krilloan arc taken as residents, leaning hardest on the city the trio earned in the last. They are known now in parts of Krilloan, and the plot grows straight out of the riots of “The Shadow of a Rose”, so the trouble they survived is the soil this one grows in. Recurring faces return, Sereth Vohr and the square-debt easing the way into the tower, Guazzo turned from wary appraiser to outright ally with his gilded apple, Aidun and Xaviera nodding them through their own quarters, and Lauritz Huggard recognised from the trial. The couple is at ease and openly teasing, with banter over a settling-in morning, a quiet kiss before the descent, and an observed dock-side moment at the close. And it is the book where the lesson of “The Borrowed War” finally lands by being unneeded: Bram carries the Deathbringer into a dragon’s cave as plain steel, is tempted at the worst moment to wake its fire, and does not, crossing the dragon’s magic on Cadal’s antimagic medallion to plant Guazzo’s one-use apple. No salamanders; the apple reverts the monster to the bewildered hatchling it truly is, the borrowed soul scatters, the witch Torquivel turns, and the dying island is saved by cleverness and mercy rather than fire.

Function (cold): the firewall held a third time, the cold clock kept dead still and the Crowned One never once looking south. The module rhymes with the spine, a soul called out of death and poured into a made body, a nature-spirit riding a girl, a sun-crystal against the undead, and Aelthira rules each a near rhyme and not the thing: a contained spiritist working with a maker, a method and a price; Perzotlios in Narnie a known forest-power and an echo of Hilja riding Bram, not possession by the cold. The book deepens her at last into open conflict, because she is an elf, and to the elder elven memory the raising of the dead is the one abomination never forgiven, so defending a necromancers’ island costs her more than it costs the humans, and she says so plainly and does it anyway, for the dying and the truth. Tannatopol is developed from a name into a real place, both grave-scholars’ isle and the city’s hospice for the incurably sick, and given a wild magic node in its rock, the failsafe that bursts Jesefael’s own lirisin and ends him by his own plan. The deferred levers stay cold: Vlad Kamantur is a portrait on a stair and no more, the Black Library is not mentioned, and her sense that she is learning the shape of a condition and not a single enemy is quietly reinforced. The book closes settled, the island grateful and a standing welcome offered, the three more at home in Krilloan than ever, and a wind that still will not blow for years. See §3.2 for Tannatopol, §6 for the Krilloan cast, and §9 for the new terms.

## The Day of Wrath

A complete novel of about thirty thousand words across twelve chapters, the trio’s seventh road job and their third as residents of Krilloan, adapting the Drakar och Demoner module Vredens dag. About three weeks after “The Made Dragon,” impossible murders climb through the canal-city, forty-nine sinners killed in seven groups of seven, each by a different wrath: a fall, a burning, salt, drowning, starving, plague, terror. They fulfil the prophecy of the Order of Saint Kleol, a four-century-old book of the Shining Way that promised the archangel Angarion would come to scour a sinful city. The Commendant’s daughter Yrea Destanior hires the trio, because the wind has stranded them and a secret that cannot leave the city is the only kind she trusts, and the first victim, Scargil, was a Brother the city will not miss. An honest new Kardian priest of Saint Minatius, replacement for the corrupt one, gives Kaelene a sun-token and points them to Migael Kalvuonos, the one scholar who has studied the order, who sends them four days north to the burned monastery on the Forithos plateau and to Ulrik Oddbratt, the mad solitary who is Angarion’s living host. The Black Rose Brotherhood, allies of a sort since “The Shadow of a Rose,” propose the only counter their Black Book names: summon the abyss-being Teitan, Angarion’s opposite, into a living vessel so the two powers spend their wrath on each other in the sky instead of on the city. The full prayer is walled in the abbot’s grave on holy ground no Brother can walk, so the trio fetch it, fighting the burned monks’ ghosts in the catacombs with Aelthira’s light, the Brotherhood’s warding powder, and the Deathbringer used as the plain enchanted blade it also is, its fire never spoken. At the rite the crystal chooses its vessel, and it is not the warrior but Kaelene. She goes up as Teitan to meet Angarion in Ulrik’s worn shape five hundred metres over Krilloan, wins by holding her own mind inside the god with Tarek’s counting drill, ends the angel and the old monk together, and falls. The city wakes never knowing how near a thing it was.

Function (warm): quieter than the earlier holidays, because the cold thread runs hot through the whole book, but it lands two beats. Bram braces without complaint to be the vessel and is, for once, the one who is afraid and steady about it, the fear he has carried since the sword paying off at last as ordinary courage. And the trio’s bond is the literal weapon of the climax: the two fixed points on the ground, Bram and Aelthira, are the true things Kaelene counts back to from inside a god. The book closes on the warm clock alone, the wind turning at last and a homecoming, the three of them pointed north for home and free.

Function (cold): the capstone of the Krilloan arc and the book the whole series has walked toward. The corrupted rite is keyed to the Brotherhood’s god, and on Kaelene it finds not the rose of Hemaquiel alone but the Hollow Mark with that rose set in its dead centre, a single mark claimed twice over, the cold’s ancient figure and the demon-prince’s rival seal, both hidden her whole life and surfaced together only under the god-light. Aelthira’s firewall does not fail from bad reasoning; it fails because the true door is present at last, cut into the chest of the woman she loves, and she rules the demon-rite a separate axis correctly to the end. In the duel the cold reaches through the Hollow Mark once, the highest the clock has ever stood, a brush and not an arrival, and Kaelene does more than shut it: she takes the thread the cold ran into her in the Jorpagna tomb and cuts it, ending the hunt. Her arc with the Crowned One resolves here, in victory; the corridor nightmare is over and she is no longer hunted. The reveal fixes the origin, that the cold marked her in the tomb the night it took Tarek and not at birth, and leaves one new thread: Hemaquiel’s surviving claim catches her from the fall, so the rival now holds an uncontested vessel, a matter Aelthira takes up alone. See the Book Seven mytharc note earlier in this bible, §7.9 for the Brotherhood, and §9 for the new terms.

## Griffinburg

A complete novel of about twenty thousand four hundred words across twelve chapters, the trio’s eighth road job and their first back in the north, adapting the Drakar och Demoner module Gripeborgs hemlighet. About four weeks after “The Day of Wrath”, the RhabdoRhinn wind that turned years early carries them up out of the southern seas and west across the Copper Sea, past Erebos and holy Caddo, to make landfall at Pendon, the capital of Zorakin. An old comrade’s letter is waiting: Marquis Thorvald Griffin, whom Bram stood beside on the Vesket road a lifetime ago, has restored his family’s long-abandoned seat and bids Bram come to the inauguration feast. They walk north into the Grindanu hills to the village of Grifton, below the castle Griffinburg, and find the feast in disarray and the marquis vanished, gone down alone behind a sealed cellar door whose one key went with him. Reading a poem left in a bone tube, six couplets and each couplet a room, they descend under the castle through bats and a fire pit and an antimagic shaft, a guardroom of the dead, a blind heart-thing chained in the dark, a false floor and a petrifying pillar, and come at last to a grotto floored with three centuries of the dead, where a house-huge many-armed horror has fed on the hill’s pilgrims and lords since the seal first failed, and the marquis hangs in iron, kept alive for the eating. They free him and put the thing down, and carry a beaten lord and a good sword back up into the light.

Function (warm): the series’ furthest-flung holiday brought home again, a clean haunted-castle delve with no wound in it and the trio at their working best. It is Bram’s book above the others: the letter is his, the vanished lord an old comrade he will not leave, and his hero’s fearlessness is shown rather than told, an ordinary terror that simply no longer reaches him. He wins the monster with the Deathbringer used as the plain enchanted blade it also is, its fire never loosed, and carries out a dead hunter’s helm, the one whole thing in all that ruin, that fits him as though it had been waiting for him. Kaelene is the honest rogue throughout, never a thief, her locks and nerve and quick hands turned to opening a lord’s own door and freeing him from behind it; she lands the kill with the reward blade. Aelthira earns the fight, shielding and healing the chained marquis under fire where she cannot break his iron chains, and blinding the horror at the last so the killing blow can fall. The book closes warm: a freed house, a good blade each, and a road home.

Function (cold): none, on purpose. This is a standalone with no claim on the spine and no move on the cold clock. The evil under Griffinburg is old and undead-adjacent, a buried necromantic order and the guard-things and the demon it left behind, but it is a local horror, bounded and long-sealed, not the Crowned One’s road and not the Hollow Mark. The register is kept deliberately off the cold’s own signature: this is rot and damp and old hunger, not frost. Aelthira reads the death-work for exactly what it is and never once mistakes it for her enemy; there is nothing here to file and nothing to withhold. She casts the ordinary way, hand and voice together, the gesture and the spoken phrase the handbook calls for. The one piece of working gear she leans on is a slim wand of silverlind (silver linden), one of the woods named as good at holding magic. It is not where her power lives, break it and she still casts. What it holds is a charge: she primes it in advance with Laddning (Charge) and, now and then, feeds that stored PSY into a spell as it leaves her hands, pushing it a level or two past her own strength. The charge is small and slow to renew, so the wand makes a working stronger only sometimes, never on command, a little held in reserve, not a second engine. It stays inside the limit below: a focus carrying her own Laddning charge, not a permanently enchanted item.

Most of what the trio actually see is the small, undramatic end of this list. Her literacy is a skill here, not a spell, she reads and writes by hand, by Ljus (Light) when it is dark, seals a note with Försegla (Seal), and finds the safe path with Varseblivning (Detection). One common ritual she knows and never uses is Föryngra (Rejuvenate, SV 14), which youthens a body for a time: an elf who already outlives everyone she loves has no wish to reset the clock. A door left closed by temperament, not by rule, which is the whole shape of her craft.

What she cannot do, and why the limits are useful.

Mentalism is closed to her entirely. With no mind-school she has no Telepati (Telepathy), Tankeläsning (Mind-Reading), Kontrollera person (Control Person), Teleportera (Teleport), Osynlighet (Invisibility), Levitation, Flyga (Fly), Syn (Farsight) or Magisk syn (Magic Sight). The party’s knowledge character cannot read or seize a mind, scry at a distance, vanish, or teleport out of trouble. Most of the convenient escape hatches are simply shut.

Her Animism ceiling is now FV 16, which has opened two doors the earlier books kept shut and left one closed by choice. She has learned Neutralisera gift (Neutralise Poison, SV 15): from the next book poison is no longer a free danger to the company, so a poisoning that is meant to bite has to act faster than she can reach the victim, strike someone out of her range, or be magical or necrotic enough to resist a straight cleansing. And she has learned Regnkontroll (Rain Control, SV 13), so she can start or stop rain outright now, which changes fire, siege, drought and tracking scenes; note that it governs rain only, not the trade wind, so it still cannot raise the rare RhabdoRhinn that carries them north and the honest-stranding logic of Krilloan holds (her Vindkontroll can nudge a breeze, not summon a season). The one still out of reach by temperament rather than number is Dimma (Mist, SV 15): she does not raise concealing fog, a door left closed on purpose that a later book can open without breaking the rules.

One spell she has now learned, in the weeks after The Day of Wrath, is Hela (Heal, Animism SV 12), the animist’s wound-mending working. This was flagged as load-bearing for continuity, it is why no injury in the story up to and including Day of Wrath was ever closed by magic; that gap closes here, on the record. From the next book on she can mend a wound, so any healing on the page should be hers and deliberate rather than assumed, and a bad enough hurt should still outrun a single casting. The company no longer has to heal only the slow way. In the same window, as her schools climbed, she has also learned three more, all Animism: Väderförutsägelse (Weather Prediction, SV 8), the scholar’s read of the coming sky; Regnkontroll (Rain Control, SV 13), which can start or stop rain outright; and Neutralisera gift (Neutralise Poison, SV 15). The last two each relax a limit the earlier books leaned on, deliberately (see the reframed box below and the judgment-call note). The creature spells stay unlearned (Tala med varelse / Speak with Beasts, Tillkalla varelse / Summon Beasts), so an elf who could simply ask a bird still cannot, which is why she had to prise a word out of the parrot the hard way.

On the cold: the only cold in her repertoire is the elemental spell Frost, ordinary, physical cold, no different from any other element. Nothing she knows touches the mytharc’s cold, the thing that took Tarek. Her craft can chill a room; it cannot read, ward against, or answer the cold that stays.

And the honest limit: under the base rules her FV 15 would let her cast Eld (Fire), Blixt (Lightning Bolt), Frost, Explosion, even summon an elemental at full effect. She has not learned them. The limit is the woman, not the book, she is the decoder, not the cannon, and the series leans on what she chooses not to do as much as on what she can. (She also lacks any way to forge a lasting magic item; with the common ritual Laddning (Charge), SV 14, she can charge an object with PSY as a one-shot battery, and no further.)

<!-- sb:perbook {"bookId":"tcts-b3"} -->

**The Watchful Mind:** From gifted insider → inconvenient heretic → self-directed investigator walking south toward the people who went quiet (“I was not running from something. I was walking toward something”).

<!-- sb:perbook {"bookId":"tcts-b6"} -->

**What the Mist Keeps:** in The Misty Island, the decoder who feels the Academy’s buried truth in her own body and reads the dead sorcerer’s Hollow Mark as the same figure her school locked away, now privately certain the buried thing is real, and carrying that cold off the island alone.

<!-- sb:perbook {"bookId":"tcts-b7"} -->

**The Unicorn Horn:** in The Unicorn Horn she sees the buried figure a third time, finished beneath Kandra, and finally says part of the truth aloud to Kaelene, closing the gap she has carried alone since the island.

<!-- sb:perbook {"bookId":"tcts-b8"} -->

**The Borrowed War:** in The Borrowed War she meets a false rhyme of her own buried truth, a star-cult, a fallen star, a cut figure, and proves she can now tell her door from a thing that only looks like it: she judges it the Red Moon’s separate business, not the Hollow Mark, and keeps even that recognition to herself.

<!-- sb:perbook {"bookId":"tcts-b9"} -->

**The Shadow of a Rose:** in The Shadow of a Rose her discernment becomes settled discipline and is then tempted past it: she rules out a whole chain of false rhymes (a pentagram, a demon-prince’s keyed dimensions, a bargaining god, the imprisoned goddess Imaria) and tells no one, and in the Black Library, said to hold a copy of everything ever thought, she stands one question from the answer to what the Hollow Mark is and whether Tarek is dead, and sets the temptation down because the price is unknown and her friends are not hers to spend, filing the place against a day she returns to it alone. She also learns the dark holds more than one imprisoned prisoner, that she has been studying a condition and not a single enemy.

“The Shadow of a Rose” turns that lonely discernment into her settled discipline and then tempts her past it. The Krilloan module rhymes with her buried truth harder than anything before, and she rules out each rhyme in turn, the pentagram that is only a pentagram, the demon-prince’s keyed dimensions that are only owned real estate, the bargaining god who is only a transaction, the imprisoned goddess Imaria who is only a different prisoner, and tells no one she ever had to weigh them, a row of true pages filed in the wrong drawer. The hardest moment of the book is hers and entirely interior: in the Black Library, which is said to hold a copy of everything ever thought or done, she stands one question away from the answer to the only question of her life, what the Hollow Mark is and whether Tarek is dead, and she sets the temptation down because the price is unknown and her friends are not hers to spend, then files the place against a day she returns to it on her own account. She also takes a piece of cosmology home: Imaria teaches her that the dark holds more than one imprisoned death-power, that she has been learning the shape not of one enemy but of a condition. She carries it all alone, as she carries everything, and the gap the series runs on widens by one more secret.

<!-- sb:perbook {"bookId":"tcts-b10"} -->

**The Made Dragon:** in “The Made Dragon”, the firewall holds a third time, every near rhyme (a soul poured into a made dragon, a spirit riding a girl, a sun-crystal) ruled not the cold and filed in silence; and the elf in her is forced into open conflict for the first time, swallowing the elder elven horror of necromancy to defend a necromancers’ island for the sake of the dying and the truth. She reads the wild node that becomes the villain’s undoing, and her sense of a condition rather than a single enemy deepens.

<!-- sb:perbook {"bookId":"tcts-b11"} -->

**The Day of Wrath:** in “The Day of Wrath” the firewall finally fails, not from bad reasoning but because the true door is present at last, the Hollow Mark surfaced on the living skin of the woman she loves; she rules the demon-rite a separate axis correctly to the end, watches Kaelene cut the thread and confirms the hunt is over, and takes up alone the new and quieter thread of Hemaquiel’s surviving claim.

<!-- sb:perbook {"bookId":"tcts-b12"} -->

**Griffinburg:** the standalone gives her cold-iron constraint room to matter and her healing its first real test. She carries an elf-forged pale mithril knife because cold iron fouls a spellcaster’s craft, and that same rule governs the descent, she works the silver of a warded door and leaves its iron alone. She names the blind heart-thing an onaqui out of her reading, having never seen one. In the last fight she is no bystander: she crosses to the chained marquis under the horror’s fury, throws a whirl of air between him and its barbed tail, and pours the mending into him one breath at a time, leaving the iron chains to Kaelene’s hands, then blinds the thing’s seeing side at the climax so the killing blow can land. The cold is not here and she knows it; for once there is nothing to keep secret.

## Zorakin
<!-- sb:field {"label":"Status"} -->
**Status:** Realm (canon)
<!-- sb:body -->
A feudal kingdom of Ereb on the Copper Sea, and in canon the heartland of The Shining Way (Lysande vägen), which means the order's chapel in Outskirt and the buried Shining Way knight of the skeleton village both sit naturally inside the realm. Its capital is Pendon, a coastal city of the royal mint and the Lyceum, where the trio make landfall in “Griffinburg”. Light elves are common here and city-dwelling, kept in whole quarters of Pendon and the other great cities, so an elf in Zorakin draws little remark except up in the remote hill country. Holds several of the author's settlements, kept far apart: Outskirt in the eastern forest interior, Brinewatch on the coast, and Grifton with its castle Griffinburg in the Grindanu uplands.

## Krilloan
<!-- sb:field {"label":"Status"} -->
**Status:** City-state (canon; from the Krilloan campaign)
<!-- sb:body -->
A canal-laced jungle port of some eighty thousand on the Sanritra peninsula in north-east Samkarna, the jungle continent south of the equator, walled off from the land by the plateau of Forithos (the Fault). Truly ruled by the magicians of Ordo Magica through a council of twenty-six and a music-loving Grand Vizier. Free worship over a deep underworld of demon-cults. The setting of "The Shadow of a Rose" and of a planned Krilloan arc; the trio are stranded there at the close.

## The Way Out / the RhabdoRhinn
<!-- sb:field {"label":"Status"} -->
**Status:** Strait & wind (canon)
<!-- sb:body -->
The one-way strait linking the Copper Sea down to the seas beyond Krilloan; its current lets a ship slip south with ease but never beat back north, except on the RhabdoRhinn ("the venom frog's breath"), a hard southerly that blows perhaps once in five years and lasts about two months. The reason the trio reach Krilloan on a whim and cannot leave, which frames the whole Krilloan arc.

## The Sink
<!-- sb:field {"label":"Status"} -->
**Status:** Quarter of Krilloan (author rendering of canon Kitteln)
<!-- sb:body -->
The old thieves' quarter of Krilloan, sunk into a stone hollow in the crumbling district of Meh-Phoe and laced with tunnels into the sewers, so a careful traveller crosses half of it unseen. Holds the rumour-tavern the Cracked Wine-Jug and Aidun's pawnshop. Kaelene's element. (Anglicised from the canonical Kitteln, per the project's naming rule.)

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-loc-tannatopol","category":"locations","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":26} -->
## Tannatopol
<!-- sb:field {"label":"Status"} -->
**Status:** Necromancer-isle (canon)
<!-- sb:body -->
A fog-bound island three kilometres off Krilloan where the necromancers keep their dead within the law; founded, like Ordo Magica, by the deathless Vlad Kamantur. Krilloan alone among the lands of the light tolerates raised dead, which is its pride and the fuse the vampire Lerajie lights. A seeded future spine-site. It is also the city's hospice and leper-colony, where the incurably sick are sent to live out their lives, tended by the only people who fear neither the dead nor the dying. "The Made Dragon" adds a wild magic node deep in its rock, old and strong enough to tear delicate workings apart, the reason the dead lie so quiet there and the failsafe that bursts the lirisin and ends Jesefael; a standing welcome to the trio is opened at that book's close.

**Status:** The demon-prince Hemaquiel's three owned dimensions (canon)
<!-- sb:body -->
Hemaquiel's private pocket-worlds, reached by a keyed mushroom-ring rite: Rodomelantos, the burning island where his witches gather from across Altor; Yflasefugh, the fire-place where he sends his enemies to burn; and the Black Library, a shifting labyrinth said to hold a copy of everything ever thought or done. Firewalled as owned real estate with a door and a key, explicitly not the other side. The Black Library is seeded as a resource Aelthira may return to.

The rich quarter built atop the pillars of a long-dead river, houses perched on stone columns and reached by hand-cranked cage-lifts and the city's sky-traffic. ## Grifton and Castle Griffinburg
<!-- sb:field {"label":"Status"} -->
**Status:** Village and castle (author, in Zorakin)
<!-- sb:body -->
A hill village and the restored castle above it, in the Grindanu uplands of Zorakin, the setting of “Griffinburg”. Grifton below keeps an inn under the innkeeper Harl. The register of the place is rot, damp, and old hunger, never frost.

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-item-deathbringer","category":"items","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":0} -->
## The Deathbringer (Dödsbringaren)
<!-- sb:body -->
Cadal's own blade, a great pitch-black broadsword of first-class steel, very sharp and very hard (canon: 45 BV, damage 2T8+4). Its engraved runes are the magic words of a bound spell: spoken aloud they summon four first-grade Salamanders, lizard-shaped fire elementals, that obey the wielder for fifteen minutes, once per day, then return to their plane. Reading the runes needs a mage's SYN or a scholar to decipher them. Stripped from Cadal's body by the sorcerer, named in Emrys's message, taken up by Bram. (The novel tells this summons in its own voice as "living fire" and leaves the words unspoken on the page; the specifics above come from the module, see the continuity tracker.) In "The Unicorn Horn" the words are spoken at last: Bram, cornered at the climax, looses the salamanders to save the child.

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-item-cadals-medallion","category":"items","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":1} -->
## Cadal's medallion
<!-- sb:body -->
The dead champion's warding talisman, given to Bram. Magic slides off it, and it glows blue when death-magic is near. The reason Bram survives Akrae where Cadal did not.

<!-- sb:body -->
A freely-given fragment of a unicorn's horn and the spirit-stone bound with it, holding the founder's spirit ("the White"); it strengthens and heals the order's faithful and can reincarnate the founder's line. The object of the quest in "The Unicorn Horn."

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-item-three-keys","category":"items","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":3} -->
## The three keys
<!-- sb:body -->
The dwarf-forged keys to Larm's catacomb door, scattered so no single hand could open it: the first kept by Liva, the second sunk in a well, the third hidden in Kandra's owl coat-of-arms.

## The silver cup
<!-- sb:body -->
An ancient salt-crusted silver cup ringed with skulls and old-Jori runes ("after death, no rest"), holy to Imaria's death-cult; impossible to destroy by ordinary means. The object that pulls the trio into "The Shadow of a Rose"; returned to the Brotherhood at the close.

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-item-calibans-plate","category":"items","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":5} -->
## The Caliban's Plate
<!-- sb:body -->
A made teleport-stone of the old times, twinned to a distant mate, so that to set a foot on one is to lift it from the other. In "The Made Dragon", the only road down from Jesefael's floating palace to the Dragon Cave.

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-item-dragon-coin","category":"items","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":6} -->
## The dragon-coin
<!-- sb:body -->
Coin still minted in Krilloan from the hoard of the dragon Morriart, slain by Duke Arneval over a century ago. Always rendered in the prose by its English name, never the canon Swedish drakmynt.

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-item-lirisin","category":"items","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":7} -->
## The lirisin
<!-- sb:body -->
An elf-made sun-crystal from before the dark times whose light annihilates undead things; Jesefael's weapon against Tannatopol. Bursts rather than fires when carried into the island's wild node.

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-item-grey-fang","category":"items","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":8} -->
## The Grey Fang
<!-- sb:body -->
A single-edged falchion, dwarf-forged to bite exactly the kind of old horror that ordinary steel slides off, found in an iron coffer behind a petrifying pillar in the deep under Griffinburg. Its weight rides up near the point, a cleaving working blade; Kaelene wins the demon-kill with it and carries it out as her reward and keepsake from “Griffinburg”. One of the two enchanted edges, with the Deathbringer, that the hill cannot turn.

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-item-monster-hunter-helm","category":"items","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":9} -->
## The monster-hunter's helm
<!-- sb:body -->
An old great helm of heavy make, taken by Bram from the demon’s grotto under Griffinburg, where it lay unmarked and whole amid three centuries of rot, the one untouched thing in all that ruin. It fits him surprisingly well, as though beaten to his head, and its cold old craft turns the natural weapons of beasts and monsters that strike the head, teeth and tusks and horns, though not a wielded weapon. It saves his skull from the demon’s tusks at the climax. His keepsake from the book.

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-creature-the-presence","category":"creatures","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":0} -->
## The presence on the other side
<!-- sb:body -->
In the restricted archive, Aelthira is watched by something with no eyes, from "a direction that did not have a name", present, aware, and patient since before the archive was built around it.

Working model: everyday magic is a series of small, unknowing knocks on a door the Confluxes once came through.

The crowned tomb-entity Kaelene met (see the Crowned One) and this archive presence are strongly implied to be the same order of being. The deeper mytharc analysis, the unifying horror hypothesis and the Yndar axis, is set out in the lore entry "The Central Conflict / Mytharc".

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-creature-skeleton-village-skeletons","category":"creatures","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":1} -->
## The skeleton-village skeletons
<!-- sb:body -->
The dead the necromancer raised and left behind. "His patrolling skeletons, soldier-precise, not shambling, are the story's antagonists." Twenty were raised a century ago to strip the nameless knight's grave and loot the church treasury; they still patrol the ruined village and, inside the church, an ambush from the shadowed alcoves is the hardest beat of the day. Cleared by the trio in "The Secret of the Skeleton Village."

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-creature-made-dragon","category":"creatures","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":2} -->
## The made dragon (Helsikel / Morriart)
<!-- sb:body -->
Jesefael's great working in "The Made Dragon": the long-dead dragon Morriart regrown from a scrap of hide in a giant tortoise's egg, aged to full size by the witch Torquivel, and given Morriart's vengeful soul through a drugged spiritist. Reverted to a harmless hatchling by Guazzo's one-use dispelling apple, the borrowed soul scattered.

From the cast list: the made dragon is Morriart regrown from a scrap of hide and loaded with her vengeful soul, reverted to a harmless hatchling by the dispelling apple. Soul unmade; hatchling survives with Torquivel.

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-creature-onaqui","category":"creatures","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":3} -->
## The onaqui
<!-- sb:body -->
A southern necromancers’ undead horror, met in “Griffinburg” as a guard-thing chained in the dark by the demon below. A great winged shape with a red pulsing heart hung in its empty ribs, whose beat drums the whole room; blind, it hunts by that sound, and it throws off a wave of pure animal dread that breaks over everyone at once, mastered, in the trio’s case, by Kaelene’s counting drill and Bram’s fearlessness. Aelthira names it from her reading, a thing she had taken for a story got up to frighten students and never believed she would meet. Not the cold; a local horror on a necromancer’s old leash.

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-creature-griffinburg-demon","category":"creatures","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":4} -->
## The horror under Griffinburg
<!-- sb:body -->
The true monster of the book: a house-huge, many-armed, tusked and horned demon, black as a beetle’s back, that has lain sealed under the Grindanu hills since before Zorakin had a name and, for three hundred years since the seal first failed, has fed on the hill’s pilgrims and lords. It wears its own hide like armour that ordinary blades slide off; only enchanted edges bite it, which is why it takes both the Deathbringer and the Grey Fang, working together, to open it up. Put down by the trio in “Griffinburg”. Old death-work, bounded and local, explicitly not the Crowned One.

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-faction-the-academy","category":"factions","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":0} -->
## The Academy
<!-- sb:body -->
The author's institution at Atrema where the magic schools are taught; keeper (and concealer) of the restricted archive.

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-faction-shining-way","category":"factions","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":1} -->
## The Shining Way
<!-- sb:body -->
Canonical Drakar och Demoner knightly order (Swedish: Lysande vägen), marked by a sunburst-and-sword emblem. A nameless champion of the order lies buried beneath the skeleton-village church. Kaelene recognises its mark because Tarek taught her to. Appears in "The Secret of the Skeleton Village"; align the order's details with published canon (see the continuity tracker).

In Krilloan (from "The Shadow of a Rose"): the order's small, mostly-foreign presence in Krilloan, following the sun-god Etin by the teaching of Odo and the six commandments; its temple of Saint Minatius is desecrated to frame the trio. The one canon order deliberately kept connected across the firewall, touching the established faith without touching the spine.

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-faction-ordo-magica","category":"factions","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":2} -->
## Ordo Magica
<!-- sb:body -->
The order of magicians that truly rules Krilloan from a seventy-six-floor tower, holding fourteen of the city's twenty-six council votes and enforcing its will through the Sabertooth Guard. Founded by the deathless Vlad Kamantur.

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-faction-sabertooth-guard","category":"factions","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":3} -->
## The Sabertooth Guard
<!-- sb:body -->
Seventeen elite swords of Ordo Magica in helms wrought as a sabertooth's skull, each sworn to one master. Seen across "The Shadow of a Rose"; the recurring guard Sereth Vohr is one of them.

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-faction-black-rose-brotherhood","category":"factions","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":4} -->
## The Black Rose Brotherhood
<!-- sb:body -->
A Krilloan witch-coven serving the demon-prince Hemaquiel, allied of old to the goddess Imaria's cult; patient, indirect, hidden behind clean shops. The antagonist-then-ally of "The Shadow of a Rose." Mildred Yeovil and the mind-walker Hilja are of it.

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-faction-oktagon","category":"factions","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":5} -->
## The Oktagon

## Series at a Glance
<!-- sb:body -->
**Logline**

Three people who each walked away from the life they were handed, a runaway dockhand, a street thief, and a heretic scholar, are drawn together on the road, only to find that one of them carried something out of an ancient tomb that has been patient for a thousand years and is no longer content to wait.

**Premise**

The series braids together three origin stories that begin apart and converge into a single travelling company. Each protagonist leaves a closed, self-satisfied world, a fishing village, a working city, an academy, because they cannot stop seeing what those worlds have agreed not to look at. The personal arc of "leaving home to become yourself" is the engine of the early material; underneath it runs a slower, colder plot about a cataclysm the world has chosen to forget and an intelligence that survived it.

By the prologue's midpoint, the three have been travelling together for about a year. The warmth they have built, firelight, tall tales, a relationship, the ordinary safety of a tavern room, is real, and it is also the thing the larger threat will test. The watchful presence Aelthira found behind a locked door at the Academy and the crowned thing that took Kaelene's mentor in the ruins of Jorpagna are almost certainly the same order of being. The series is the story of an unremarkable found family standing between that thing and a world that does not believe it exists.

**Format & genre**

*Setting.* Ereb Altor, the original campaign world of Drakar och Demoner (Dragonbane). Original characters and plot inside an established shared world.

*Genre.* Character-driven fantasy with a slow cosmic-horror undercurrent, drawing on the setting's Third Conflux / fleshbiter mythology.

*Register.* Grounded and interior; magic and monsters are rare, weighty, and frightening rather than routine.

**Proposed shape**

A multi-volume series. Drafts so far read as Book One material: three origin novellas/sections plus a prologue that sets the throughline.

**POV**

Close third person, one protagonist at a time; each origin section sits fully inside one character's head.

**Core trio**

Kaelene (perception / infiltration), Bram (force / heart), Aelthira (knowledge / magic), a complementary party.

**Antagonist**

"The Crowned One" of Jorpagna (working name) and, more broadly, whatever the world's magic has been quietly "touching."

**The three pillars**

Leaving is not the same as arriving. Each hero escapes a defining place, then has to learn that freedom is a set of new questions, not an answer.

What you refuse to look at does not go away. The villages, the city, and the Academy all survive by not-looking. So does the threat. The heroes are, structurally, people who cannot stop looking.

Warmth is earned and fragile. The series treats kindness, competence, and chosen family as hard-won and worth defending, which is exactly why the cold plot can hurt.

## Tone, Theme & Voice
<!-- sb:body -->
**Thematic spine**

Every draft is built on the same load-bearing idea, approached from a different angle: there are things a community agrees, sensibly and collectively, not to look at directly, and the cost of that agreement is paid by whoever is unlucky or stubborn enough to look anyway.

Brinewatch does not look at the horizon. Bram does, and leaves.

Poane does not look at the people it uses up. Kaelene is one of them, unseen until Tarek looks at her "like she was worth watching."

The Academy has turned a deliberate refusal into something that looks like settled science. Aelthira finds the gap and will not pretend it is a conclusion.

The cosmic plot is the same theme rendered literal: a thing that has been waiting in the dark, that "notices" when the world's practitioners open a particular channel, and that an entire civilisation has decided not to acknowledge. The heroes' defining trait, the inability to stop seeing, is both their virtue and the reason the dark has now noticed them back.

**Recurring motifs to protect**

| Motif | Where it appears | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| The fish market at dawn | Opens both Bram's and Kaelene's stories (cold water, old salt, the thing underneath the smell) | Signature establishing image of a hard working world; signals "origin" mode. |
| A carried object | Bram's worn ship-coin from his brother; Aelthira's new journal; Kaelene's mapped exits | Externalises each character's anchor and unfinished business. |
| The mentor who looks first | Orvith to Bram; Tarek to Kaelene; (inverted) Carenthal to Aelthira | Recruitment-by-recognition; "someone once did the same for me." |
| The cold that stays | The tomb; the dream; the archive presence; the final "thread" | The horror signature, not heat or blood but the end of warmth, arriving from a wrong direction. |
| Counting / reading the room | Kaelene's eleven seconds; her breathing drill | Competence as survival; also the thing that breaks under trauma. |
| Made things that should not exist | "The Borrowed War" (a made half-demon); "The Shadow of a Rose" (a monster loosed); "The Made Dragon" (a cloned, soul-loaded dragon and a cave of made creatures) | The series' recurring body-horror note: life and death meddled with by craft, always kept on the human side of the firewall and never the cold itself. |

**Prose voice (style guide)**

The drafts share an unusually consistent narrative voice. Keep it:

Close third person, deeply interior. The camera sits behind one character and reports their assessments, not just events.

Plain declaratives carrying weight. Short, flat sentences are used for the heaviest beats ("He laughed." / "She ran." / "Tarek was not.").

Understatement over melodrama. Violence and grief are brief and matter-of-fact; the horror is in restraint, not gore.

Dry, warm humour, mostly via Bram. It exists to make the cold beats land harder, never to undercut them.

Wisdom delivered without sentiment. Mentors state facts ("you moved to the threat...") rather than give speeches; the character turns them over later.

Italic interior asides (used in "The Watchful Mind") for a beat of pure thought, use sparingly and keep consistent across books.

**Voice guardrail**

Avoid: ornate "high fantasy" diction, info-dumped lore, omniscient narration, magic explained in mechanics.

The world is revealed in passing, through a character who already lives in it and only notices what is unusual to them.

## The Central Conflict / Mytharc
<!-- sb:body -->
**Magic: the schools and what waits on the other side**

Drakar och Demoner organises magic into magiskolor ("magic schools"); the three core schools are Animism, Elementalism (Elementarmagi), and Mentalism, with further schools (demonology, necromancy, illusionism, symbolism, and others) in expansions. The drafts map directly onto this: "animist channeling" is the Animism school and "elemental binding" is Elementalism, with the Academy functioning as a place where these schools are taught.

*Disciplines.* Animist channeling (Animism) and elemental binding (Elementalism), shown interacting. "Binding sequences" are performed; advanced study covers "elemental binding geometry."

*Texts.* The Mireth Principles (cited with a tell-tale "generally"); treatises on animist channeling; an animist boundary-theory paper out of Jorvaine. (Author-coined titles within the canonical disciplines.)

*The flaw.* The Academy's foundational model is incomplete in a specific, deliberate way: the standard interaction of the two schools consistently "touches something", every time a student opens that channel in that manner, something on the other side notices.

*The cover-up.* Documents older than the Academy (in three languages) describe the phenomenon; they were locked away and scrubbed from every syllabus. The "gap" is a refusal dressed up over decades as a settled conclusion.

*The presence.* In the restricted archive, Aelthira is watched by something with no eyes, from "a direction that did not have a name", present, aware, and patient since before the archive was built around it.

*The unifying horror hypothesis.* The archive presence (Aelthira) and the crowned tomb-thing (Kaelene) are strongly implied to be the same category of being, ancient, patient, attentive, reachable through the "other side" the school's channel opens onto.

This fits canon neatly: Jorpagna's magical schools demonstrably made things that should not exist, and the Third Conflux is exactly the scale of catastrophe such meddling could court. Working model: everyday magic is a series of small, unknowing knocks on a door the Confluxes once came through. (Canon-aware option: the crowned entity could be tied to demonology / necromancy or to a fallen Jorpagnan ruler, see Open Questions.)

*First physical confirmation ("What the Mist Keeps").* The second road job is the first time the buried truth is touched in the body and in the rock rather than only in an archive. On the Misty Island Aelthira's craft runs cheap, fast, and abundant, the amplification Mifalodor warns of, and she feels, with the cold clarity she trusts, exactly the thing the Academy taught her not to look at, working freely a thousand miles from any school. More than that: cut into the cavern floor is the Hollow Mark, the sorcerer's deliberate figure named for the pointed emptiness at its centre, and privately, on the last page, Aelthira sets it beside the figure the Academy locked away and sees they are the same shape. The conclusion she could only infer from forbidden pages is now confirmed from the outside, the buried thing is real, older and wider than the Academy, and a line of sorcerers has been cutting it into stone and dying on it for generations. The cover-up is a cover-up of something that works.

**Two clocks run at once. Keeping both visible is the core craft challenge of the series.**

*The warm clock (character).* Can three people who each fled a closed world actually build a life on the open road, and can that chosen family survive contact with grief, fear, and the weight of what they left behind? This is where the books spend most of their pages: the tavern, the work, Bram's stories, Kaelene's nightmares, Aelthira's investigation.

*The cold clock (plot).* Something that once wore a crown felt Kaelene as a "small bright particular fact" the night after her nightmare and began, slowly, to follow the thread that leads up out of the dark toward her. She did not escape the tomb, "she had been let out," and let out is the exact and terrible word for it. The Hollow Mark is not something Kaelene was born carrying; it is a wound the crowned thing cut into her in the dark of the tomb, the night it took Tarek, before it let her walk out alive to ripen, and the thread it has climbed ever since runs into that wound. Meanwhile Aelthira is independently chasing the intellectual trail toward the same truth from the other end. The two clocks are set to collide: the people best equipped to understand the threat are already, unknowingly, travelling with the person it is hunting.

*Dramatic irony engine.* The reader knows (from the prologue's final beat) that the Crowned One is coming. The characters mostly do not. Aelthira has the theory but not the personal stakes; Kaelene has the wound but not the framework; Bram has neither but is the one holding them together. The series tension is the closing distance between what the trio knows and what the reader already fears.

**Update, what "The Misty Island" advances**

The skeleton village kept the cold clock still; this job moves it, quietly. For the first time the cold clock advances through Aelthira rather than Kaelene: she gains physical, bodily confirmation that the Academy's buried model is real (the island's amplification, and the sorcerer's Hollow Mark, the cut figure that matches the one her school locked away). She carries that knowledge off the island alone and tells no one, the dramatic-irony gap now runs inside the trio as well as between the trio and the reader.

It also plants a new object on the board. Bram carries the Deathbringer, a working blade out of the sorcerer's hall, its runes a summons rather than a flame, out into the daylight, and Khaa's parting line ("mind it does not start to feel like yours") marks it as exactly the kind of door this story is full of. Whether the sword, or the act of carrying a working of that hall into the world, is how the trio first get truly noticed is left open. What Kaelene's wound and Aelthira's research share is now one step closer to being one problem.

**Update, what "The Unicorn Horn" advances**

If the Misty Island moved the cold clock quietly, this job moves it loudly and on purpose. For the first time the enemy's status changes. The Crowned One, which had been only following the thread toward Kaelene, is woken by the noise of a rite worked at the country's deepest knock-point and the Deathbringer's fire loosed to break it, and it begins to climb the thread toward her. Aelthira closes the gap from her side too, telling Kaelene part of what she has carried since the island: that the cut figure is real and is one thing across the Academy, the island and Kandra, and that her dream is a memory of the thing that took Tarek. And Bram crosses the line he had held all series; cornered, he speaks the Deathbringer's runes at last and the salamanders come, which saves the child and, because it is a working at that place, is exactly the kind of knock the cold was always going to answer. The three end the book closer, clearer, and far more exposed than they began it.

**Update, what "The Borrowed War" advances**

Nothing, on purpose, and that is the point of it. "The Borrowed War" is the first deliberate side story: a job taken between heavier books, set only about two weeks after Kandra, that holds the cold clock completely still while the warm clock runs on. The Crowned One does not gain a step. The trio sail east across the Copper Sea, do a hard human job in someone else's war, and sail home west with the thread no nearer than it was. The series needed to prove it could breathe, that a volume can stand on warmth and craft alone without advancing the spine, and this is that proof.

It does, however, do one quiet thing for the dramatic-irony engine. The enemy in Hynsolge is a star-cult that serves a demon of the Red Moon, and its workings throw off a false rhyme with everything Aelthira has carried since the island: a fallen star, a cut figure on a temple floor, a rite at a buried place. She notices. She looks at all of it with the cold attention that is her whole craft and concludes, correctly, that it is not her door. The figure is a star-chart and not the Hollow Mark; the comet is a spent relic of the Red Moon and not the thing that took Tarek; the demon Onaabys is a different cosmic axis entirely. So the book's real cold-clock move is a non-move she keeps to herself: Aelthira learns to tell her own door from a thing that merely looks like it, and tells no one she ever had to. The gap the series runs on widens by one more secret, and the reader watches her set a true page in the wrong drawer, on purpose.

**Update, what "The Shadow of a Rose" advances**

Nothing on the spine, on purpose, and that is the second proof of the firewall. "The Shadow of a Rose" is the trio's fifth road job and the second deliberate side story, a beach-read holiday set about three weeks after Hynsolge that holds the cold clock dead still while the warm clock runs. The Crowned One does not gain a step; it never once looks south. The trio sail down the one-way strait to Krilloan, a jungle port at the bottom of the world, on a careless whim, and the whole book happens in a city where nobody knows their names and nothing is theirs.

What it does for the engine is what "The Borrowed War" did, only louder, because this module rhymes with the mytharc harder than any before it: a left-hand death cult, an imprisoned goddess who wants the world drowned, a holy relic, a cut figure, a journey to another world. Every rhyme is rendered, on purpose, as not the thing. The five-pointed star on the cult's Black Book is a common pentagram, named as such by Kaelene and ruled out by Aelthira as not the Hollow Mark, the figure named for the pointed emptiness at its centre. The demon-prince Hemaquiel's three pocket-dimensions, reached by a keyed mushroom-ring rite, are owned real estate with a door and a key, mechanically unlike the cold that travels along a thread from no direction. Hemaquiel himself is a transaction, a god who keeps oaths and punishes betrayal, the opposite of a thing that bargains for nothing. And the cup's goddess, Imaria, is a different prisoner on a different axis, explicitly not the Crowned One and explicitly not freed here. Aelthira sees every rhyme, weighs it, sets it aside, and tells no one, the same discipline she practised in Hynsolge: a row of true pages filed in the wrong drawer.

The one move it makes for the future is a deferred one. The Black Library under Hemaquiel's island claims to hold a copy of everything ever thought, said, and done, and Aelthira, who has carried the only question of her life unanswered since the Academy, rules it a demon's vanity and a false rhyme, walks past it, and does not forget it. It is the one place that could tell her what the Hollow Mark is, what took Tarek, and whether he is truly dead, and the book is careful to leave it as a loaded resource she could return to on her own account. A second seed is planted in passing: Vlad Kamantur, the deathless, memory-wiped necromancer who founded both Ordo Magica and the necromancer-isle of Tannatopol, glimpsed only as a portrait on a stair. The trio do not leave at the close; the wind that would carry them home will not blow for years, so they settle into Krilloan, which opens a Krilloan arc and is where the cold clock can be made to tick again later, through the Library and through Kamantur, without this book ever touching it.

**The spine revised, the Yndar axis (after the Konfluxsviten investigation)**

An investigation into the Konfluxsviten, the published campaign that runs from Svavelvinter to Den femte konfluxen, resolves what the cold clock has been measuring all along. The cold is not a god and not the weather. It is necromancy, and it has an address. In Ereb Altor canon the island of Yndar is the origin of necromancy, and its necromantic capital, Krau-Ki, was torn from Yndar and set down on the arctic island Marjura as divine punishment for ungodly magic. That move was not a stray miracle. It was a conflux, its path fixed by the stars of the Third Conflux, the same Third Conflux that devoured Jorpagna with the fleshbiters. Necromancy and the conflux mechanism are one thing at the root: a conflux is what moved the necromancers, and a conflux is what they have waited under the ice to seize ever since.

The Crowned One is therefore not an abstraction and not a Jorpagnan ghost. It is the deathless crowned ruler of that relocated necromantic empire, the Cruri, whose lost capital lies frozen beneath the Marjura glacier and whose intelligent undead kept their minds through the long sleep. What it wants is the Fifth Conflux, dated in canon to between 610 and 620 eO, which is to say now. The series title was describing the antagonist all along: the cold that stays is the frozen necromantic empire, and the cold that stays is what it means to make permanent across the whole of Altor if it reaches the conflux's centre.

Kaelene's entity is the residue left in Jorpagna. Canon fixes the two endpoints, Yndar in the warm east and Marjura at the northern ice, and the timing, the Third Conflux that was simultaneously gutting Jorpagna in central Ereb. It leaves the path of the lift unspecified, and that gap is the series' to fill: as the cursed city crossed toward the western sea it passed over the dying empire, and something of the necromancy rubbed off into Jorpagna's ruin. That lingering residue is what the Academy at Atrema has been brushing for centuries, the magic that is touching something, what is cut into the cavern under Kandra, and what Kaelene stumbled onto by chance. Aelthira, working from books rather than luck, found the older and truer thing, the axis itself, Yndar to Marjura, origin to vault. The straight line on the map is the author's invention, not canon, and the stronger for filling a gap the canon genuinely leaves open.

This reframes the firewall as tragedy rather than mere discipline, and it does so without unsaying a single call Aelthira made. She was right about every case. The Hynsolge star-cult, Hemaquiel, Imaria, the made dragon, the necromancers of Tannatopol, none of those was the thread, and she ruled each one correctly. What she missed is the relationship between them. Necromancy in this world has one source, Yndar, and every working she set aside is a pale descendant of the one ur-necromancy that is the cold she has been hunting. She was right in every particular and wrong in the whole. The crack in the firewall is the keeper's own deepest prejudice: an elf's revulsion at the raising of the dead, so total that she kept filing necromancy under not the door without once turning to look straight at it. Her load-bearing virtue and her load-bearing flaw are the same trait.

The climax mechanism is already half-built in the existing books. Canon's rule for controlling a conflux is that the one who stands at its zero-point must be a living mind, never the dead or the undead. A frozen undead emperor cannot seize the conflux himself. He needs a living vessel, which is why he has climbed the thread toward Kaelene since the night of her nightmare, and which is what the being-ridden ordeal Bram survived in Krilloan was quietly rehearsing. The endgame is the trio doing one small human thing at the conflux's centre while the war the canon stages around it rages offstage: deny the Crowned One its living vessel, or hold the cold clock still one last time by seeing that no one takes the point at all. This supersedes the open guesses in the mythology questions, several of which are now answered there.

The back half of the series is the campaign itself, run in canon order: Svavelvinter, the arctic island where the firewall finally drops; the Trakorien material as the bridge north out of the stranded Krilloan arc; Oraklets fyra ögon, where the prophecy names the trio precisely because they have been to Marjura; Kristalltjuren, the grey halls between the worlds; and Den femte konfluxen, the split focus and the war for the centre. Kamantur, the deathless amnesiac founder of the city's magic, is the natural bridge and a live candidate for a Cruri survivor or one of the undead king's generals who fled and had the truth taken from him, to be decided before he is committed to the page.

**Book Seven, The Day of Wrath, updates the spine in four ways.**

First, the mark. What surfaces on Kaelene under the rite is the Hollow Mark, the cold's own figure, with Hemaquiel's rose set in the dead centre of it, a single mark claimed twice over. The two are rivals, not master and servant: the rose is the demon-prince's genuine claim on the same vessel the cold wants, it does not mean Hemaquiel serves the cold, and Aelthira rules it his and not the cold, correctly, which is what keeps the firewall intact. Both symbols are invisible her whole life, to a mirror and to a lover and even to Aelthira who saw her bare a season before, and they surface together only under a god's light, so Kaelene learns what is written on her own heart in the same breath the coven does.

Second, the origin. The cold cut the Hollow Mark into her in the Jorpagna tomb the night it took Tarek and let her out carrying it, which is what let out has always meant; she was marked there, not born marked, and the rose at the centre may be older.

Third, the climax. The cold reaches through the Hollow Mark once, the nearest it has ever come to the living world, a brush and not an arrival, and Kaelene does more than shut the door: holding her mind inside the borrowed god by Tarek's counting drill, she takes the thread and cuts it, closing the cold's one road into her for good as far as any sense of hers can find, so that the hunt that has driven her since the tomb is over and the corridor nightmare ends. Her personal arc with the Crowned One resolves here, in victory: she is no longer hunted.

Fourth, the pivot. Reverted to human and falling five hundred metres to a death she is sure of, she is caught, not by mercy and not by the cold, but by Hemaquiel's surviving claim, the rose warm in the place the cold had been, collecting the vessel she has just cut its rival out of. She was not saved but kept. The endgame therefore tilts off Kaelene as the cold's quarry. The Crowned One no longer has its road to her and hunts her no more, so it must seek the conflux by some other path or vessel, a series threat that no longer drives her personal fear. Meanwhile an uncontested demon-prince, the same Hemaquiel the trio met and bargained with in Book Five, now holds a claim on her, the new and separate thread Aelthira carries privately. The book ends not on a march toward the cold but on Kaelene's homecoming: free, unhunted, the nightmare over, sailing north for home. Hemaquiel's stance is fixed by this: not the cold and never its servant, its rival for the same vessel, and from here a treacherous ally, the keeper who would deal with the trio against their common enemy and turn on them the moment it is safe to collect Kaelene.

## Story structure - eight beats
<!-- sb:body -->
"Prologue: A Complete Draft", the convergence, is structured as a hook-and-pivot in eight numbered beats:

| Beat | Content |
|---|---|
| 1 to 4 The tomb | Kaelene and Tarek, three years on, delve the ruins of Jorpagna. They wake the Crowned One; it strikes Tarek down; he tells Kaelene to run; she escapes alone. "She was out. Tarek was not." |
| 5 Time skip | One year later, in a tavern, Bram performs his (ever-growing) bear story to a charmed room. Kaelene watches the fire, hollowed out; Aelthira reads her silence. The trio is established as a travelling company. |
| 6 The nightmare | Kaelene's recurring dream of the endless corridor, this time it grows a wrong right turn, and the cold that finds every crack. She wakes drenched, runs Tarek's breathing drill. |
| 7 The comfort | Bram, already awake, comforts her without prying. Their relationship and his particular gentleness are drawn. "For now, that was enough." |
| 8 The promise | Far below, the Crowned One feels the "thread" leading up to her. "She had not escaped the tomb. She had been let out." The series engine starts. |

Function: converts three character studies into one story by (a) seating all three at the same table, (b) revealing the loss that haunts Kaelene, and (c) planting the antagonist's intent. It is, structurally, the true Chapter Zero of the series.

## Where It Could Go (Proposed)
<!-- sb:body -->
This section is suggestion, not canon, a sketch of how the existing material wants to grow, offered for the author to keep, alter, or discard.

**Book One, the company forms, the thread tightens**

Assemble the origin material and the prologue into a first volume whose surface story is a series of jobs on the road and whose undercurrent is Kaelene's worsening nightmares, Aelthira's Jorvaine investigation, and the first undeniable sign that something from Jorpagna is actually following them. "The Secret of the Skeleton Village" is the working model for one of those jobs: a complete, warm, self-contained outing that earns its place without touching the cold plot. Climax: the trio is forced to confront that Kaelene's "nightmare" has a physical source, and that Aelthira's research and Kaelene's trauma are the same problem.

"What the Mist Keeps" is the second working model of that road job, and the first to run at full novel length and to let the cold clock tick. Where the skeleton village stays warm throughout, the Misty Island ends with Aelthira carrying real, physical confirmation of the Academy's buried truth, and with Bram carrying a working blade out of a sorcerer's hall. It is a good template for the volume's mid-point turn: a self-contained job that is also the first undeniable brush with the thing the series is really about.

**Mid-series, understanding the enemy**

Track the buried history: the earlier Confluxes, what the Jorvaine practitioner found, whether Tarek can be recovered or laid to rest, and what the Academy will do once a student it exiled starts proving the thing it buried. The cosmic threat scales as the trio's understanding does, the horror stays rare and weighty.

The Krilloan arc. "The Shadow of a Rose" strands the trio in Krilloan until the RhabdoRhinn blows, which turns one strange city into the natural home of a multi-book run of side stories. Keep these firewalled as side stories while letting the arc as a whole be where the cold clock can be made to tick again, through the two levers the first book planted: the Black Library, the one place that could answer Aelthira's only question, which she could return to on her own account at whatever price; and Vlad Kamantur, the deathless amnesiac founder of the city's magic, an undying thing perhaps old enough to remember the Confluxes. The arc ends, whenever it ends, when the wind finally turns and lets them sail north again.

**Endgame, the looking**

The thematic resolution should turn on the series' spine: a world that survives by not-looking cannot be saved by people who look away. The heroes' refusal to stop seeing, the trait that doomed each of their old lives, becomes the only viable defence against a thing that thrives on being unacknowledged. Whether "being let out" makes Kaelene the world's vulnerability or its one open door is the question the finale should answer.

## The Setting and the Fidelity Guide
<!-- sb:body -->
**The setting: Ereb Altor (Drakar och Demoner)**

The series is set in Ereb Altor, the original campaign world of the Swedish tabletop RPG Drakar och Demoner ("Dragons and Demons," first published 1982; now also released in English as Dragonbane). The planet is Altor; Ereb is its principal, broadly Europe-and-Mediterranean-flavoured continent, ringed by the Copper Sea (Kopparhavet). It was built piecemeal over decades of adventures and sourcebooks, which is why neighbouring lands can differ wildly in culture and technology, feudal kingdoms beside Renaissance-style city-states.

This matters for the bible: the place names that read as invented, Klomellien, Berendien, Jorpagna, the Copper Sea, are established Ereb Altor canon, and so are the Third Conflux and the "fleshbiters" that destroyed Jorpagna. The drafts are original fiction set inside that shared world, not a wholly new cosmology. Several items previously filed as "open questions" about geography are therefore answerable straight from the published setting.

**Canon vs. the author's invention (fidelity guide)**

From Ereb Altor canon: the world/continent (Altor / Ereb), the Copper Sea, Klomellien (a salt-trading northern land), Berendien, Zorakin (a feudal kingdom and the canonical home of The Shining Way), Jorpagna (a fallen empire), the Third Conflux, the fleshbiters, elves, the magic schools (animism, elementalism, mentalism), and the knightly order The Shining Way (Lysande vägen).

Original to the author: the protagonists (Kaelene, Bram, Aelthira) and supporting cast (Tarek, Orvith, Fen, Carenthal, Corvel); the local towns Poane, Brinewatch, Vesket, Morral, Mern, Adrath, Crestfall, Atrema, Jorvaine; and the specific plot, the crowned tomb-entity, the "thread" to Kaelene, and the Academy cover-up.

Recommendation: keep a running list marking which names must match a sourcebook (so canon-aware readers aren't jarred) and which are the author's to define freely. Note that Atrema and the others below are author coinages placed within canonical lands.

## History: the Third Conflux and the Fall of Jorpagna
<!-- sb:body -->
The deep history the world refuses to examine is, in canon, the Third Conflux (Tredje Konfluxen) and the Dark Time (Mörkertiden) that followed it. The drafts' version aligns closely with Ereb Altor lore:

Jorpagna was the centre of a great Copper Sea empire, in the drafts, built on copper and salt; enormous, populous, ordinary in its humanity (people argued about bread, fell in love, buried their dead).

The fleshbiters (canon: köttbitarna) came in the Third Conflux "in numbers that defied accounting." Not an army, a consumption. They ate the people, animals, wood, leather, cloth, leaving only stone, stubborn metal, and the deep places they did not reach. In canon their feast on Jorpagna's capital also released the monsters and demons made in the city's magical schools.

The result is ruin without romance, "what was left when everything beautiful had been taken", and a Dark Time across much of Ereb afterward.

The numbering, Third Conflux, is canonical and implies earlier (and potentially future) Confluxes; the modern revival even posits a later "superflux." This is the spine of the series' hidden mythology and should be parcelled out slowly. The link the drafts draw, between Jorpagna's school-made horrors and the Academy's present-day magic "touching something", is the author's, and a strong one.

## Where the trio stands now (after The Day of Wrath)
<!-- sb:body -->
By the opening of the next book, about a month on from The Day of Wrath, the three are heroes in the game sense (Drakar och Demoner, Hjältarnas handbok). This is a real change in weight, not a costume. On the road they are markedly better at the soft crafts, bluffing, bartering, talking a guard round, greasing a palm, and each carries a little more Karisma than before. It shows less in what they say than in how they hold a room: they expect to be listened to, and mostly are. Keep it light on the page. It is the difference between three drifters who talk their way out of trouble by luck and three people other people instinctively defer to.

Each has also come into a single standing knack, the kind a hero grows into, recorded in the tables as a Hero talent. Bram is Fearless (Orädd): ordinary terror no longer reaches him, and only magic, a fear laid on him by a spell or a thing of the other side, can frighten him now. Kaelene has a Sixth sense (Sjätte sinne): she is very hard to take by surprise and feels danger before it lands, written as the far edge of the situational awareness she has always had rather than a new thing bolted on. Aelthira has a Magic talent (Magisk talang): she no longer needs a teacher to learn a spell, her craft has become native to her, which is the in-world reason her schools have climbed and her list has widened since Day of Wrath.

Since then they have taken that homecoming wind north to Zorakin and cleared the haunted castle of “Griffinburg”, their first job back in their own half of the world, a clean standalone that leaves the spine untouched and the three of them a little richer by a sword each.

## The Deathbringer, the novel renders the module's summons as "living fire"
<!-- sb:field {"label":"Status"} -->
**Status:** Open

<!-- sb:entry {"id":"tcts-gloss-cold-iron-and-magic","category":"glossary","aka":[],"tags":[],"sortIndex":74} -->
## Cold iron and magic
<!-- sb:body -->
The world rule, made explicit in “Griffinburg”, that cold iron fouls a spellcaster’s craft: a working mage keeps no iron on her person, which is why Aelthira carries an elf-forged pale mithril knife rather than a steel one, and why she works the silver of a warded door and leaves its iron alone. Kaelene, no caster, uses iron picks freely.

### 610 eO, now - The Made Dragon, the sixth road job
"The Made Dragon," the sixth road job, about a month after "The Shadow of a Rose." Cold clock held still (firewalled): a conspiracy to burn the necromancer-isle of Tannatopol is taken apart from underneath. A long-dead dragon is regrown and soul-loaded and then unmade with a one-use dispel, and Bram keeps the Deathbringer's fire sheathed throughout. The made-dragon working, the spirit in the girl, and the sun-crystal are each ruled not the cold; Tannatopol is developed and its wild node revealed; Kamantur and the Black Library stay cold. <!-- sb:event {"id":"tcts-ev24","bookId":"tcts-b11","dateText":"610 eO, now","sortIndex":23} -->
### 610 eO, now - The Day of Wrath, the seventh road job
"The Day of Wrath," the seventh road job, about three weeks after "The Made Dragon." Cold clock reaches its peak, then is cut: impossible murders, forty-nine sinners in seven sevens, fulfil the Order of Saint Kleol's prophecy of the archangel Angarion. To stop him the trio help the Black Rose Brotherhood summon the abyss-being Teitan into a living vessel; the rite's crystal chooses Kaelene, surfacing the Hollow Mark with Hemaquiel's rose at its dead centre. In the sky duel the cold reaches through her once, the nearest it has ever come, and Kaelene cuts the thread it ran into her in the tomb, ending the hunt and freeing herself. She falls and is caught by Hemaquiel's surviving claim. The RhabdoRhinn turns years early, and the trio sail north for home.

<!-- sb:event {"id":"tcts-ev25","bookId":"tcts-b12","dateText":"610 eO, now","sortIndex":24} -->
### 610 eO, now - Griffinburg, the eighth road job
"Griffinburg," the eighth road job, about four weeks after "The Day of Wrath." Cold clock still (standalone, off-spine): the RhabdoRhinn wind carries the trio north to Zorakin, where they land at Pendon and clear a haunted castle in the Grindanu hills, freeing Marquis Thorvald Griffin from the demon buried beneath his own seat. Bram wins the monster with the Deathbringer’s plain enchantment and takes a warded helm; Kaelene lands the kill with the dwarf-forged Grey Fang; Aelthira shields and heals the marquis and blinds the demon at the last. No move on the cold; the Crowned One is not here. Ages unchanged from the birth years above.
