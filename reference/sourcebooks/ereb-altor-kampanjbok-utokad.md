# Ereb Altor – Kampanjbok (Drakar och Demoner) — Expanded Edition

*A long-form reference to the continent of **Ereb** on the world of **Altor**, drawn from the campaign book. Years are **fO** (före Odo / before Odo) and **eO** (efter Odo / after Odo); the book's "present" is **610 eO**. Swedish proper nouns and game terms are preserved; explanations are in English.*

> **What this book is.** Ereb is one continent among many on Altor, crowded with countries of wildly different size and importance. This volume gathers the things that only make sense seen as a whole — language families, peoples, climate belts, trade routes, religions — rather than scattered country by country. Some chapters present brand-new material (the maginoder, dark magic); others are simplified, rewritten versions of older material now released to the public. Throughout, the book stresses that **the GM (SL) is sovereign**: its guidelines are a skeleton to build on, not law. It deliberately withholds detail on some lands (e.g. Nargur's forests, the central plains) so GMs can invent freely, and keeps "mystique" around the demi-human peoples, so elves and dwarves are sketched rather than fully detailed — which signals their importance, not their irrelevance.

## 1. History of Altor (Altors historia)

### The shape of the past, at a glance
| Era | Years | One-line summary |
|---|---|---|
| Förhistorien (Prehistory) | before ~9000 fO | Earlier races degenerated; the gods erased all intelligent life. Only dragons survived. |
| Stenåldern (Stone Age) | 9000–3500 fO | Neolithic-level life; hunting, fishing, gathering; society only at the local scale. |
| Bronsåldern (Bronze Age) | 3500–1500 fO | Three high cultures rise — **Melukha, Golwynda, Yndar** — and slowly dominate their neighbours. |

### Prehistory and the second creation
Almost nothing is known of the time before today's intelligent races. The strong tradition holds that the world was once peopled by other races who developed into something monstrous, so much so that **the gods agreed to wipe the slate clean and begin again**. Between roughly **10,000 and 9,000 fO** a cascade of catastrophes struck Altor, and their scars still mark the land: enormous craters such as **Kargomväggen** on Palamux in Trakorien, petrified forests, and dried-out ocean floors. It is "not impossible," the book notes, that fragments of those doomed civilizations still lie buried beneath the earth or locked away in inaccessible mountains. The **dragons** are believed to be the only intelligent beings to have lived through that age. Around **9,000 fO** the gods made **elves, humans and dwarves**, intending a more stable world this time.

### The long Stone Age (9000–3500 fO)
The new races began at a stage equivalent to early neolithic Earth: a dim grasp of agriculture but a life still built mainly on hunting, fishing and gathering. Whatever social structures existed were strictly local — there were no realms, only bands and villages.

### The three high cultures of the Bronze Age (3500–1500 fO)
Three "högkulturer" grew up and came to dominate their surroundings. Trade between them was only sporadic — the one notable exception being early contact between the Golwynda culture and the southern Akrogal/Efaro peoples. Each high culture absorbed useful innovations from less developed folk and folded them into its own ways, so that **fruitful syntheses drove progress**.

- **Melukhakulturen** — On the island of **Melukha**, "the blooming pleasure garden," humans and elves lived together in peaceful abundance for millennia, sustained by the land's bounty. Their contact with other cultures was slight. A larger, unified nation eventually formed on a religious basis, to enable grander and more beautiful creations in honour of the gods and the people's delight; music and architecture flourished. They adopted metal late and even now keep no mines of their own, trading for metal goods instead. They worship **Moder Jord** as a primal power and beneath her a pantheon of peaceful gods watching over fertility, healing and craft; their **animist magic school** was founded here. Melukha has always been a **matriarchy** with traditionally "feminine" ideals.

- **Golwyndakulturen** — Around **Golwynda's freshwater sea** an agricultural and trading culture flourished, with a sharp population surge as early as ~4000 fO. Its founding legend tells of the swift hero **Palofar**, who beat a steppe-antelope in a race and so won a wager for the water-god **Enki**. Asked his reward, Palofar modestly wished only that the ear in his victory-sheaf bear triple grain so he could bake bread for his old mother — but instead of baking the fat ears he **planted** them, and from that day the fields around Golwynda gave a **triple harvest**. The god, feeling tricked, raged, but Palofar argued that planting was surely Enki's true intent; amused by the cunning, the god let him keep both grain and head. On this rich farming base the Golwynda people mastered **bronze-casting** and gained an edge over their neighbours; surrounded by hostile folk, they took a more **warlike** path than Melukha, conquering to protect their heartland, raw materials and trade roads. Here were founded **elementarmagin, symbolism and mentalism**, and a deep, traditional interest in mysticism, astrology and speculative philosophy.

- **Yndarkulturen** — On the far island of **Yndar** lived a peaceful, primitive people who ate what the rich land gave and farmed modestly — until the **margyler** of SE Akrogal, having learned enough seamanship, **invaded**. The margyler swiftly enslaved the natives, who had no idea how to defend themselves, and raised a high culture dominated by the conquerors but built on the labour of the conquered. Free of toil, the margyler grew **learned, proud, and cruel**: they laid the foundations of mathematics and the first abstract written language, and amused themselves with war-craft, magic and torture. On Yndar the dark arts of **nekromanti (necromancy) and spiritism** were born.

### Other Bronze Age culture areas
- **Södra Akrogal** — The great southern steppes of Akrogal were peopled by **nomads** following the herds. They were the first to tame animals: the silky-furred **stäpplama** (steppe-llama) for riding, the long-legged **vargill** as a hunting partner, and the **enhornsbuffel** (one-horned buffalo) as a draught beast. Herding spread quickly to the Golwynda farmers, who expanded their useful species and improved them through breeding; from the steppes came the **bow** as both hunting and war gear, and mounted nomads who would one day wreak havoc far away in Ereb.
- **Efaro** — In the jungles of NW Farbanda, several hunter-warrior cultures rose, flourishing largely on **slaves** taken from the lands beyond the mountains around Ormsjön. The Efaro folk perfected **seamanship and navigation** and excelled at craft and engineering in wood and stone. Their magic was a form of **shamanism** that came to influence most of the later schools.
- **Norra Erebs barbarer** — In the vast forests covering NE Ereb, well-organized **alliances between communities** appeared in the late Bronze Age. The barbarians had already been forced into fairly large villages (200–300 souls), because smaller groups could scarcely survive; the growth and pressure of **svartfolk (black-folk/orc-kin)** forced them to organize defenses, war-bands and (when needed) chosen **war-chiefs or kings** — though these never held much power, for the barbarians' love of freedom ran deep. They worshipped **nature-gods** plus a pantheon stressing manly ideals of strength and honour, and held the elf-folk in deep, superstitious respect; peaceful coexistence was the rule despite the barbarians' combative, dominant nature.

### The non-human peoples
**Moder Jord** made all her servant-races at once but scattered them across the world; they began as wandering bands of the wilds, the sea, the air, or the underground.
- **Skogsalver (forest elves)** became the most numerous of her children and the most influential on history — though elves never shaped civilization the way humans and dwarves did, since their temperament is not so dynamic and striving as the mortal races'. Elf culture, fairly uniform everywhere, is **contemplative and passive**: the elves watch and meditate rather than chase wealth or power. They have lived in Melukha and Ereb's great forests since the Stone Age; the region of **Landori** (between the Nida mountains and Berendien) was long an elven heartland and high culture, from which they spread across northern Ereb and over the Demontungan into northern Akrogal.
- A second branch of forest elves settled the jungles of the **Samkarna**, competing with the reptilian beings that otherwise dominate that continent; being more advanced than their neighbours they built realms of their own and live relatively unmolested.
- **Grott- och silveralver (cave/silver elves)** kept to lives apart, above and below the surface, and so left little mark on history — as did **Moder Jord's "ickealviska" children**, who never gathered in numbers enough to found realms.
- **Gråalver (grey elves)** are a mystery even to themselves; from the first they sailed the world's seas and never named a home. They were **crucial to spreading bronze**, their ships reaching every coast and carrying knowledge far and wide; they were generous teachers while humans still lacked the seafaring cultures that could rival them. Later, embittered by conflict with seafaring humans, the grey elves grew **cautious and withdrawn**.

#### Grynnerbergens dvärgkultur
In the natural cave-systems of the **Grynnerbergen** a great dwarf-realm arose once the dwarves had learned to cultivate **klipptryffel**, an organic, nutritious fungus that grows underground wherever certain minerals and gases occur. With food secured, labour could be freed for the essentials of dwarven life: **metallurgy and mining**. The dwarves learned to **cast copper ~3700 fO and bronze ~3500 fO**, and kept these arts secret because they were enormously profitable — but the **vättar (goblins)** stole the knowledge and quickly sold it to the humans at Golwynda. The lasting enmity between dwarves and vättar dates from this betrayal.

#### Polarkulturerna
A powerful energy-flow runs along Altor's axis and wells up at each pole, making possible a unique kind of culture at the world's ends.
- **Isherrarna (the Ice Lords)** reside in the north — a strange race kin to no other, of partial material substance but mostly **condensed energy**. By legend they appear as blue-shimmering, ~10-metre-tall anthropomorphs with sharp features and blurred outlines, though few have ever seen them. They take no food, living directly off the **north pole's energy-flow**, which binds them to the place; they do not live and die as other beings but arise and vanish with the flow itself. For reasons of their own they constantly seek to **extend the north pole's influence over the south**, and they war with the southern salamanders by unknown means. When the Ice Lords are strong, ice creeps southward and the climate worsens across all of Altor; they are worshipped as gods by the **isbarbarerna** on Orghin and the folk of **Sanithsid**, and can communicate telepathically with all intelligent races.
- **Salamandrarna (the Salamanders)** live in the south-pole lava-lands — like their northern foes a race of pure material art, but reptilian, and likewise sustained wholly by the polar energy-flow. Their psyche is highly developed (telekinesis, levitation, telepathy), and their existence probably extends into the ether and other dimensions; **transferritualer** grow riskier the nearer the south pole, since the salamanders devour all loose immateriality nearby as a change from the polar diet. When they dominate, the world grows **warmer**. They are worshipped by southern Soluna's desert nomads, the desert-princes of Ormsjön's south shore, and the **dinosaurider** of Samkarna.
- **Drakarnas arkipelag** — In the hot seas south of Yndar lies the remote archipelago where this mighty species is said to have its origin; Altor's dragons gather here perhaps once a millennium, and it's claimed the most ancient dragons feel death approaching and return to die on its golden shores. Dragons are reputedly close kin to the south-pole salamanders — both, indeed, are sometimes simply called "dragons."
- **De rasslande rasernas arkipelag (Maminira me delema)** — On the edge of the permanent fog-belt west of Samkarna lies the archipelago of the "rattling races": giant, intelligent **insects and insectoids** that built advanced societies in the Bronze Age in isolation from humanoid life. From here came the flesh-eating swarms that nearly emptied Ereb during the Third Conflux.

### The Iron Age (from 1500 fO)
There is evidence the dwarves could smith **iron** in the Grynnerbergen as early as ~**1700 fO**, but they guarded the secret even more closely than bronze. Iron-craft reached the wider world only because dwarf-chiefs used **iron weapons** against both humans and vättar. Northern barbarians and svartfolk armies made enormous efforts to capture a dwarf-city and its forges, for despite everything the dwarves remained the mountains' lords and a mighty people. Only when the war-prince **Targald Karskfot** seized power in the Grynnerbergen — at the youthful age of sixty — and marched in his pride down onto the plains of **Krun** to drive the humans into the sea did the humans gain the upper hand: they broke his monopoly. In that campaign Targald and many other dwarf-nobles were captured and forced to **reveal the iron-craft as ransom**. The dwarves did not, however, betray everything; their **alloy arts (legeringskonst)** in particular were never disclosed, and they remain Altor's supreme smiths.

### Colonization (1500–1080 fO)
After 1500 fO seafaring had matured enough for voyages across the inner seas, and colonists left the overcrowded high cultures. The old Golwynda culture's three parts now went separate ways:
- **Furgia & Yndar** — Furgia, on SW Akrogal, had to fight off relentless invasions (1500–1200 fO) by the wicked **margyler** of Yndar, who took the whole **Kamsun** coast and finally Furgia too, holding it until ~800 fO.
- **Sombatze & Efaro** — Sombatze on northern Soluna long endured small wars with the Efaro tribes; the two cultures mixed into something marked by deep mysticism and a religious world-view, and inland several mountain-realms arose whose rulers were reckoned gods.
- **Krun & Hynsolge** — Krun finally enjoyed a golden age after defeating the Grynnerbergen dwarves, learning iron-smithing as its "victory-penny." From 1500–1300 fO it founded colonies along the whole north coast of the Copper Sea; realms such as **Berendien** and **Erebos** were born. In the dwarf-war Krun had allied with barbarian tribes from the Maseva-bay shore and rewarded them with the land of **Hynsolge**, where they lived peacefully among the hynsolger for centuries, inheriting the Golwynda legacy though barbarian blood remained.
- After a decade of rising tension the **Krunska kriget** broke out (1080–1065 fO), marking the start of the Age of Armies. Krun took help from Sombatze and dominated the sea; the **hynsolger** rebuilt their old ties with the barbarian kin in Nargur's forests and allied with mighty Efaro chiefs eager to strike their ancient enemy Sombatze. After fifteen years' war the hynsolger won the decisive **battle of Grivela at Jorpagna (1065 fO)**; by barbarian belief the site gained a special power, and they built their new realm's capital there. So arose the kingdom of **Jorpagna**, which would dominate Ereb until the 500s fO. The former Krunish colonies swore fealty to Grivela.
- **Skövlingen av Sombatze** — The Efaro armies that helped subdue Sombatze had no taste for discipline; they plundered the land and dissolved into robber-bands, so that chaos ruled all northern Soluna to ~1000 fO. With the eastern campaign's collapse, the margyler in Furgia survived by sheer terror and mobilized against the new threat.
- **Melukhas fall** — In the western Copper Sea, the seafaring folk of **Morelvidyn** attacked the elf-dwellers of **Sivoa** (1040 fO) and raided beautiful coasts for months — an event the elves still mourn in song — then took the ill-defended island of **Melukha** itself. A mighty new realm might have risen had the Morelvidyn king not fallen drunk into the sea and drowned, leaving the throne to his **two hundred and sixteen sons**, of whom at least half pressed claims. Melukha bought off the invaders, who soon left to fight the ensuing civil war — the so-called **"nudelhärvan"** — which ran through the 1000s.

### The Age of Armies (1080–950 fO)
This was a discharge of long-built pressure all around the Copper Sea; out of these wars the future was reshaped.

### The Imperial Age (Kejsartiden 950–600 fO)
For centuries the **Empire of Jorpagna** dominated central Ereb. The Krunska kriget had carried old Golwynda knowledge, via the hynsolger, north to the barbarians of Nargur's forests, who took from it what they found "honourable" but refused to join the Empire they thought beneath them. At the notorious **council of Grafferburg (912 fO)**, emperor **Thamsul** had all the barbarian chiefs seized and executed after their spokesman called him "a southern dog and crone," then launched a series of campaigns against the forest-folk — wars that dragged on a century or two and only **deepened the barbarians' hatred** of Jorpagna. The Empire expanded between **1025–710 fO** over the lands of today's Zorakin, Berendien and Felicien, and west of Ereb over the barbarian woods all the way to Mirelien and Kardien at the western sea; it never directly ruled **Trakorien** or **Caddo**. The common tongue spoken over most of Ereb today is a further development of Jorpagna's language, **jori**.
- **De akrogaliska nomaderna** — From ~750–600 fO the nomads north of Kamsun grew in numbers and skill, learning to ride **hippogriffs** above all, and reorganized through a religious conversion of the whole region. Their war with Jorpagna and old Yndar was inevitable; both realms were harassed by sudden, ruinous raids — Jorpagna struck mostly via Hynsolge, Krun and Felicien, the attackers usually winged since the only land-link between Akrogal and Ereb, the **Demontungan** north of the Golwynda sea, was strongly fortified. Yndar, with its long, hard-to-defend border, suffered worse.
- **Sanithsids upphöjelse** — In the 600s civilization's knowledge spread even to the barbaric ice-folk. Dwarves crossed the ice from northern Akrogal, supplying the barbarians with metal goods. In **617 fO** the isherrarna made contact with local Orghin chiefs on Sanithsid, and a "holy alliance" was struck at once. The **isbarbarerna** on Orghin were judged unfit for such cooperation, however, and were made scapegoats and attacked in hordes — but the Ice Lords' strange powers drove them off. Reacting oddly to defeat, the isbarbarerna's chiefs declared themselves invincible and concluded that the war against the Orghin folk had simply **never happened**; ever since they have ignored that neighbouring island and plundered farther afield, worshipping the isherrarna as gods without any close contact.

### The Third Conflux and the fall of two empires (599–598 fO)
A **konflux** is a moment when the stars take ominous positions and great, far-reaching events follow; the third was foreseen with dread by astrologers across Altor, though none knew what it would mean. Both Jorpagna and Yndar expected an invasion from Akrogal's nomads — and both were wrong.
- **Jorpagnas undergång** — In the summer of **599 fO** the southern sky darkened at midday over the Copper Sea islands. A cloud of billions of **köttbitare** — an arm-long, flesh-eating grasshopper-kind that devours living things — sank from the Samkarna marshes, having spread via **Maminira me delema**. Central Ereb's population was roughly **halved within weeks**; no warning outran the swarm, and four-fifths of livestock was lost. After a couple of months the insects vanished north into an unknown fate. A curious detail: each insect bore a blue stone between its hind-parts so the ground glowed blood-and-turquoise where they passed — read by some as a sign of primitive insect-religion, a "crusade against the heathen soft-skins." The Empire of Jorpagna **collapsed like a house of cards** and never rose again; the following centuries are the **Mörkertiden**.
- **Den yndariska depressionen** — Yndar, by contrast, destroyed itself through its own recklessness, on two fronts. First, its magicians went too far in their searching and drew down a wrath like the old "Gudarnas vrede": the city of **Krau-ki** vanished in a single night of mighty thunder, and it was later proven the gods had **moved** it across the world to the island **Marjura** (in the kingdom of Cruri) — apt punishment for the city's necromantic excesses, though evil spread to new lands with it. Second, a punitive expedition north from Kamsun targeted a place the nomads held holy, **Slimpaku**. The local lord found there a long wall of never-before-seen material guarding **Slimpaku**, where no nomad dared go; a white, formless **creature** atop the wall warned the day would bring ruin on the realm, but the lord turned back too late. Two months on, pale creatures called **slempukar** welled endlessly out of Slimpaku — speechless, weaponless, but wielding supernatural power — and razed city after city across Kamsun, then walked into the sea where the threat seemed to pass. Weeks later they rose on Yndar's shores and worked on until the proud realm was utterly wiped out, whereupon they withdrew behind their wall, undisturbed since.

### The Dark Age and the Age of States (599 fO – 610 eO)
Through the **Mörkertiden (599–1 fO)** Ereb's civilizations declined; in many places both writing and metalwork were forgotten. Slowly the regions regathered their strength, rediscovering lost knowledge and forging new states. One thing held in common across the southwest: the old Jorpagna tongue, **jori**, became — in its many dialects — the folk-language of nearly every land the Empire had once ruled. In the **Statsbildningarnas tid (0–610 eO)** new states rose in old Jorpagna's ruins: **Zorakin, Kardien, Berendien, Erebos** and others. Some of the old joriska culture survived, warped by local custom and geography, developing in different directions in different regions.

### Legendary prehistory: the war of origins and the Confluxes
The deepest past survives only in myth, but the book scatters several pieces of it that together form a kind of legendary prehistory:
- **Urstriden (the war of origins)** — By legend the mountain-chain **Drakryggen** is the petrified primeval being **Tiamat**, and **Geardons gap** — the single break in the range — is the wound left when **Marduk's axe Geardon** first struck the monster in the war of origins. The hurricane that forever howls through the gap is bound up with that ancient blow.
- **The creation of day** — In the elder days the boy-hero **Elfhelm**, wielding the sword **Yrbad**, slew the **Mörkrets Demon** and so brought **day** itself into being, freeing the sun's light from the dark gods' grip (memorialized among the stars; see §4).
- **Origin-myths of the crafts and creatures** — The constellation-tales preserve creation legends: the smith-god **Mingar** and the metal-hooved goat **Aruros** (whence ore-deposits), **Túringwil** father of all wolves and hounds, **Parikila** mother of the birds, and **Ghâzil** forefather of all dwarves (see §4).
- **The Confluxes** — A **konflux** is a moment when the stars take ruinous positions and world-shaking events follow. At least three are remembered: the **First Conflux**, in which the moon **Azuria** (the Blue Moon, which once gave the sea-folk light) was destroyed; an implied **Second**; and the **Third Conflux (599–598 fO)**, which felled both Jorpagna and Yndar. Each was foreseen with dread by astrologers across Altor.
- **"Gudarnas vrede"** — Even before its final fall, Yndar had repeatedly been struck by god-sent disasters of the kind called "the gods' wrath" — the same pattern that later spirited the necromantic city of **Krau-ki** away to Marjura.

### Intellectual history & the blame for the Third Conflux
A telling thread runs through the book's treatment of learning and faith. In **612 fO** the Furgian astronomer **Dykos Barrha** presented the rationalist **Dykiska världsbilden**, with its implication that all the gods men worship are mere illusions — and barely thirteen years later the **Third Conflux** shattered Jorpagna. By **610 eO**, no serious scholar in Ereb still accepts Dykos's *view of the gods*; the common verdict is that his theories were a product of the **general godlessness that reigned before the Third Conflux** — and that this very godlessness **indirectly caused the catastrophe**. The lesson drawn is religious as much as historical: the fall of the old world is read as divine judgement on impiety, which helps explain the zeal of the post-Conflux faiths. (Dykos's cosmology is nonetheless still used as the working astronomy, even by those who reject its theology.)

- **~4,000 fO** — Population boom in the Golwynda culture.
- **~3,700 fO** — Dwarves learn to cast copper; **~3,500 fO** — and bronze.

- **~1,700 fO** — Dwarves smith iron in the Grynnerbergen.
- **1,500–1,300 fO** — Krun founds colonies along the Copper Sea's north coast (Berendien, Erebos born).
- **1,080–1,065 fO** — The **Krunska kriget**; the hynsolger win the **battle of Grivela (1065 fO)** and found **Jorpagna**.
- **1,080–950 fO** — The **Härarnas tid**.
- **1,040 fO** — Morelvidyn raids Sivoa and takes **Melukha** (king **Haieg-hamu** had died 1038 fO).
- **1,025–710 fO** — The Empire of Jorpagna expands to its greatest extent.
- **912 fO** — The council of **Grafferburg**; emperor **Thamsul** executes the barbarian chiefs.

- **612 fO** — **Dykos Barrha** presents his cosmology.
- **599–598 fO** — The **Third Conflux**: fall of Jorpagna (the köttbitare swarm) and of Yndar (Krau-ki, the slempukar).
- **599–1 fO** — The **Mörkertiden**.

**After Odo (eO)**
- **0 eO** — The **Statsbildningarnas tid** begins; Zorakin, Kardien, Berendien and Erebos form from the Empire's ruins.
- **late 100s eO** — **Den Lysande Vägen** takes firm hold in Zorakin and Kardien (Caddo has already been a century under Odo's teaching); Erebos and Berendien join the church's fellowship.
- **212 eO** — Exark **Arcival** devises the Arcivalska calendar.
- **278 eO** — The great **Schism** in the Lysande Vägen (over magic).
- **281 eO** — The final break: the exarks of **Arno** and **Ekeborg** excommunicate each other; the church splits in two.
- **306–384 eO** — Lifetime of **Cessilu** of **Atrema in Berendien**, who devised a temperature scale (one *Cessilu-grad* ≈ one °C).
- **314 eO** — Exark **Zegedorn III** founds the inquisition, **Den Ljusa Handen**.
- **370–394 eO** — An earlier **Felician war**.
- **380s–390s eO** — Illusism wins a mass following in Kardien (the surge that forces its condemnation in 403).
- **392 eO** — The **sea-battle of Selim**: the Felician fleet annihilates Zorakin's, and Zorakin's undefended coasts are plundered (why Zorakin shuns the present war).
- **400–464 eO** — Reign of Kardien's **King Vidar II** (his arms pass into Kardien's heraldry).
- **403 eO** — Exark **Pergon IV** condemns **illusism**; its followers are banished to Trakorien.
- **468 eO** — **Vicotnik Ärkemagikern** reaches **Nidland** from Akrogal and forges a dark magocracy.
- **early 600s eO** — King **Kuthegil Halvskägg** of Felicien escalates the letters of marque, kindling the **Felician pirate war**.
- **610 eO** — The book's **present**: the Felician pirate war, the Ransard threat and the Nidland purge all loom; the once-a-century **Palinor crusade** is due.
- **612 eO** — The year prophesied for **Vicotnik's crusade** against the world — "a dozen-dozen" (144) years after his arrival in 468.

## 2. Beyond Ereb (Bortom Ereb)

### Samkarna — the drowning continent
The continent of **Samkarna** lies to Ereb's southwest. Moist winds sweep ceaselessly in from the great western ocean and drown it in massive rainfall, forming gigantic swamps in its central and western parts. The warm climate grows ever more unbearable the farther south one travels, and the marshes seethe with life; here every conceivable kind of beast and plant has evolved, often armed to the teeth in a hard struggle for survival. Along the east coast runs **Drakryggen**, Altor's highest mountain chain, whose tallest peaks rise above **15,000 metres**; over a 250-mil stretch nothing is lower than 5,000 m save the passage at **Geardons gap**. The range blocks the moist western winds, so only thin drizzle reaches **Ormsjön** and western Soluna beyond.
- **Morelvidyn** — The north coast facing the Copper Sea, south of Caddo and Erebos, is a patchwork of small realms with ever-shifting borders and rulers; a pirate-chief often seizes power and claims descent from **Haieg-hamu**, the unifying king who died in 1038 fO leaving the realm to **216 sons**. The morelver have little respect for life, given to cruelty, and live partly by selling slaves taken from Samkarna's interior and northern Soluna; good sailors and fighters, but undisciplined. Besides humans, **brunankor** and **svarta piratankor** dwell here — the latter feared for raids along the Kardien and Zorakin coasts.
- **Sivoa** — West of Morelvidyn, once a forest-elf realm; long fallen from power but its noble-wood forests are still peopled by elves. The morelver hold a superstitious dread of these woods and leave the elves in peace, though some trade passes between Sivoa and Melukha.
- **Melukha** — West of Sivoa lies the great paradise-island, said to be mankind's cradle. Its many people live well off hunting and the land's rich growth; unlike most folk they seek neither gold nor power, since plenty suffices for all, and devote themselves to the fine arts. **Hospitality** is sacred — it is every villager's duty to care for guests, for pleased visitors bring the host great honour, though folk from rougher lands find melukhier exhausting; the islanders never hurry and the days pass with little event. They worship the **"Gudinnan,"** a mother-figure symbolizing the interplay of life and nature (not to be confused with the elves' Moder Jord, though the two resemble one another), and the **household spirits**; their attitude to death is matter-of-fact, since the dead live on as protective spirits, so corpses are usually balsamed and kept at home as ornaments or furniture, with children painting their faces in cheerful colours and no one taking offence. Melukha is loosely divided into stamområden with a hundred-odd villages and a few larger towns each; tribes and villages are led by **old women**, held to possess the greatest life-wisdom; women and elder men live in the villages while a young man may wander from one to another among women who'll have him. War is "a contemptible but necessary task" led by men, who are reckoned better suited to it — as the proverb runs, "Who praises the emptying of the dung-barrel?"
- **Mosgilak** — Rumour speaks of this fog-and-swamp land beyond the marshes, ruled by **reptile-men** — not the primitive lizard-folk of Ereb, but a culture older even than mankind's. They worship the south-pole salamanders, want nothing to do with other peoples, and **enslave and eat** outsiders who lose their way there once they can work no longer.
- **Paringuabukten** — The three rivers Ulimen, Ghaldoil and Maur drain the vast Morginnerskogen into this bay; in some seasons so much blossom-nectar washes down that the whole bay turns sweet as sugar. Scattered primitive human tribes live around it.
- **Morginnerskogen** — A gigantic rainforest reaching from Morelvidyn on the Copper Sea north to Paringuabukten in the west and Drakryggen in the east, fading southward into the **Juronaträsken**.
- **Jurona & Blindhavet** — South of the great forest lie huge marshlands: mangrove in the north, then primitive growth of giant ferns and horsetails; here live the great amphibians and lizards that died out ages ago in our world, and rumour tells of hidden realms of intelligent **dinosaurider** on islands in the fog. West of the Juronaträsken the sea is forever cloaked in mist — **"Blindhavet"** — where surviving sailors speak of man-eating algae that catch ships and fling grasping tentacles over the decks, hungry for flesh.
- **Drakryggen & Krystalerna** — On the western slopes above the Morginnerskogen sit isolated realms whose customs and tongues are utterly strange; the morelver reckon their folk fit only for slaves, and tell horror-stories of cannibalism and beast-god worship. Below the mountains a chain of lakes runs north to south, the **Krystalerna**, so clear one sees the bottom from the surface as plainly as a flying dragon sees the valleys below; via natural canals one can sail lake to lake even where no map is drawn, and many adventurers have vanished here hunting the legendary golden heathen-temple.
- **Geardons gap** — The single break in Drakryggen, a wide cleft running east–west. By legend the range is the petrified primeval being **Tiamat**, and the gap is where **Marduk's axe Geardon** first struck the monster in the war of origins. The western-ocean winds crowd through it in a perpetual hurricane, making it nearly impassable, though the storms have carved out cliff-formations and cave-systems that perhaps offer a hidden route through.

### Ormsjön — the sea between
The waters dividing Samkarna from Soluna are called **Ormsjön**; the winds here are seldom strong, so folk travel in two-banked galleys driven by slave-power.
- **"Vägen bort"** — Ormsjön's northern inlet, named by Copper-Sea sailors. In the strait itself the water is still sweet enough to drink, but it flows hard north→south, so one can sail from Ormsjön up to the Copper Sea only on a rare hard southerly. Such a wind blows up only about **1–3 times in five years**, and is called the **"RhabdoRhinn"** ("poison-frog's breath"); when it comes, an armada of trade-ships rides up into the Copper Sea laden with drugs, poisons and gems from Soluna's deserts. In the months after a RhabdoRhinn wind, such goods grow plentiful across Ereb. The laden ships gather to wait, sometimes for months, in the harbour-town of **Krilloan** — a haunt of idle sailors, money, thieves, traders and perverse desert-princes that makes it one of Altor's more interesting places.
- **Faryngia / "Världsvalget"** — The winds rushing out through Geardons gap spin a giant whirlpool in Ormsjön before they scatter; the vortex has been the death of many careless sailors.
- **Nivralerna** — This island group forms the trade-friendly kingdom of **Niferland** in mid-Ormsjön. The islands rise just at the Faryngia whirlpool; Copper-Sea merchants often reckon them the world's edge, for beyond them rule the lawless pirates of the south. Niferland has a strong galley-fleet ruling northern Ormsjön and thus the sea-trade with the Copper Sea, while the caravans of its rival Thelgul fetch goods from Soluna's interior.
- **Södra Ormsjön / Pokti** — South of the Nivralerna the sea grows highly uncertain. Soluna's coast beyond Thelgul is a clutter of petty princely states of shifting rule; some call the region "Pokti," from a felician word meaning 'disorder, barbarism.' The realms live by selling goods northward and by plundering one another. On Samkarna's east coast below Drakryggen live only isolated tribes wanting no contact; it's said folk grow stranger the farther south one goes, and that the borders of the serpents' land **Mosgilak** are peopled by hideous crossbreeds of the two species.
- **Horyns gap, Maar Lacra & Amargoh** — One who sails too far south in Ormsjön can be caught by the strong currents near **Horyns gap**, the sea's southern outflow; a ship not crushed in the rapids is washed out into **Maar Lacra**, "the Ocean of Tears," where no rain falls and no wind blows and the still heat is murderous — the only way back is to paddle ashore and try to walk north. The dead island **Amargoh** is said to be home to grey-skinned **titans** who feed on the fossils in the stones but gladly switch to human flesh.

### Soluna
- **Efaro** — Soluna's northwest, bounded by the Copper Sea, Ormsjön and the Gizur mountains, is **Efaro**: ancient, proud, tragic. Its people split into **eight tribes** that endlessly betray one another, the tribe currently in power selling its rivals' best members as slaves to the morelver and felicier; yet the land stays fairly prosperous, rich in precious ore and good soil. The efarier are tall and dark, their hair light so as not to draw the sun's heat; warlike at heart but also drawn to art.
- **Sombatze** — One of the original Golwynda culture-areas around the freshwater sea; it has weathered history's blows from its sheltered spot behind Efaro, farming, raising maize and tapping its sugar-maples in peace while Ereb burned. When efariska chiefs took it into their heads to plunder the land, Sombatze used skilled diplomacy to set the other tribes against them and stay out of it. Many of the world's original crops and much else of life's good things come from here; the land is ruled by rich landowners under an elected **stormogul**.
- **Traxilme** — Beyond the Gizur passes from southern Sombatze lie the **Traxilme** desert-plains, once said to have bloomed with gardens, cities and strange folk, now centuries dry and dead. Its mountains are of many-coloured rock that makes the landscape shimmer like a rainbow — plains powdered violet with shifts of green and gold, peaks of sharp purple-red and other clear hues; even the air seems to change colour, probably as magnetic currents lift varied particles day to day, so one walks as in a dream. But Traxilme is as treacherous as it is lovely: poison-merchants gather minerals here for deadly brews, and when the wind rises, wide **poison-clouds** form; the unreachable ruin-cities of its interior are said by western Soluna's nomads to be haunted by **gastar and demons**.
- **Solunas inre** — Soluna's interior is an ill-omened place of ancient ruin-cities testifying to vanished civilizations whose perverted remains lurk in dark crypts; men speak of forbidden, poisonous plains where few set foot, and no one has dared learn what really hides there.
- **Thelgul** — On Soluna's mainland level with the Nivralerna lies the priest-state of **Thelgul**, whose folk worship the heavenly bodies. Thelgul and Niferland are ancient enemies but seldom clash in major war, since they depend on each other for trade.

### Golwyndahavet — the great freshwater sea
Between the continents of Ereb, Soluna and Akrogal an enormous freshwater spring wells up, forming **Golwynda's freshwater sea**, whose centre bulges upward like a glass mountain from the water-pressure beneath. On the glittering slopes of this water-mountain seafolk of many kinds are said to play, and to dwell in wondrous cities on the sea-floor round the spring, where no ship can sail against the strong out-current. The sea lies roughly **250 m higher** than the Copper Sea, so it is impossible to sail between them. It has three outflows:
- **Raas Narram** — In the west, billions of tons of water plunge each second toward the Copper Sea. On the north side of the falls stand the ruins of a two-thousand-step lock-stair built by emperor **Garhues of Krun**; today there is no way to sail from the Copper Sea up to the freshwater sea, though ships can be **portaged** over the mountains with great effort. The eastern Copper-Sea water is drinkable along the whole Efarisk coast; at "Vägen bort," evaporation has made it too salty to slake thirst.
- **Demontungan** — In the north the water rushes hidden through the **stone bridge** linking Ereb and Akrogal, then jets high over **Masevabukten** as a massive water-beam; where it bursts forth, the rock resembles a malevolent face and the torrent sprays from its "mouth," so primitive tribes round the bay worship the face as the god **Okasu**. The name "Demontungan" is used both for the stone bridge over the river and for the water that gushes from the 'god's' mouth.
- **Grelgifallen** — In the east the freshwater sea pours over the **Grelgifallen**, lower but broader than Raas Narram. Amid the falls lies the island **Habete Grelge**, surrounded on both sides by whirling rapids — an ancient principality said always to have been ruled by the same line of magicians, and reachable only from the air.

### De östra haven — the eastern seas
- **Glashavet** — Beyond Grelgifallen lies the **Glashavet**, whose translucent reefs and cliffs turn razor-sharp when broken; the many invisible reefs make it unsailable unless one knows the exact channels. It stretches all the way to Traxilme's coast and shields Sombatze from Valgussjön's evil folk.
- **Valgussjön** — The vast water beyond Glashavet, all but unknown to Ereb; reaching it is nearly impossible. Its islands are peopled by strange folk and creatures — **titans, cyclops, sphinxes** and others — and rumour places the dragons' breeding-grounds at the **Drakarnas Arkipelag** in its southeast, though that may be pure fancy.
- **Yndar** — Far to the east lies also the evil island of **Yndar**, the cradle of necromancy. Today Yndar luckily has scarcely any contact with Ereb's folk, but the cursed city **Krau-Ki** — moved by the gods to the northern island of **Marjura** — has had an ill influence on the continent's northwestern parts.

### Akrogal — the eastern continent
**Akrogal**, the continent east of Ereb, largely lacks firm statehood and is peopled by nomads and wandering hunter-tribes.
- **Demontungan** — Here in its sense as the natural **stone bridge** linking Ereb and Akrogal, ruled by the **nidlänningar** on the west side; lively caravan-traffic crosses it and it carries great strategic weight. A couple of mil north, in **Masevabukten**, sits the town of **Bahaz**, where trakoriska cavaliers anchor alongside barbarian ships to trade with the folk of south and east.
- **Kalmurri** — On the east side of the Demontungan; its ruler is said to be the **moon-goddess's son**. When the ruler dies, special holy troops set out to find the god's new incarnation — a newborn child with one wholly **white eye**, an inheritance of the pale moon-disc, deemed the only part of the moon-goddess visible to mortals. Life in Kalmurri swings between piety and licentiousness with the moon's phases: at full moon the kalmurrer must be very god-fearing, for the goddess sees them, while near new-moon no moral rules apply; **lunar eclipses** are special festivals, when the goddess closes her eye for a few hours as a sign that all is permitted.
- **Furgia** — Beyond the border-river Harytis lies old **Furgia**, Kalmurri's archenemy and the region's bulwark against the wicked folk from the east. Here ancient noble lineages keep their fine **knightly ideals**; the capital **Ketoriam**, at the Harytis' mouth, is one of Altor's richest and strongest cities, fallen only a couple of times in a thousand years to the wild nomads of the northeast. Early in the crusade-era, throngs of devout knights from western Ereb sailed to Furgia to "convert" the heathen, but were beaten back after hard fighting.
- **Kamsun** — Distant Kamsun is all but unknown in Ereb; certain exclusive goods — for example **kamsunsk papir** (paper used to sew rich men's garments) — sometimes find their way west to the great markets.
- **Vildmarkerna** — The lands beyond the Trimbus mountains in northern Kalmurri are held by nomad and hunter folk; between the Trimbus mountains and Kamsun stretch wide steppes ruled by horse-folk, and farther north still lie the endless forests of "indian-like" hunter-folk who wish only to be left in peace.

## 3. Altor's Universe (Altors universum)

Altor is the name of the world on which the continents Ereb, Soluna, Efaro, Akrogal, Jih-pun and others lie. No one truly knows what Altor looks like, but astronomers and learned men have of course laid down theories. The most credible — and now generally accepted — was first presented in **612 fO** by the Furgian astronomer **Dykos Barrha**, and the resulting picture is called the **Dykiska världsbilden** (Dykos's world-view).

### The Dykiska world-view
At the centre of the universe floats **Altor**, its only normal world: a perfect sphere about **1,000 mil** in diameter (circumference ~3,000 mil; for comparison Earth is reckoned ~1,250 mil across). Its climate is wholly unlike Earth's. At the **north pole** rules a paralysing cold; the farther south one goes the warmer it grows, until at the **south pole** the heat is near-murderous — why this should be, only the gods know, and no one has ever explored those regions closely. Altor has many continents of various kinds spread across the ocean that covers most of its surface; **Ereb** lies just north of the equator (a notional line midway between the poles) and so enjoys a mild, temperate climate, its yearly mean about **±0 °C**. The land of **Jih-pun** (described in *Drakar och Demoner Samuraj*) sits at about the same latitude on the planet's far side. Altor's atmosphere thins the higher and the farther from the surface one goes: at **15,000 m** an ordinary human has trouble breathing, and above **20,000 m** survival is near-impossible for any length of time. Many things float in the upper air, among them the island **Caranor**, home of the silver-elves.

### The role of the gods
By Dykos, Altor was made long ago by many gods, who furnished it with plants, animals and intelligent beings; as gods do, Altor's lords play an eternal game with the planet as their board. When Altor was made it was bound by certain laws, chief among them **Förgänglighetens Lag** — the Law of Transience — which says **nothing can last forever**; sooner or later all must pass, even the elves. If anything eternal came down to Altor's surface, the Law would break and the world be torn from its orbit, leading inevitably to its destruction: the dead would rise, time would cease, the universe itself would end. The only thing truly eternal is the gods, so were a god to set foot on Altor's surface the Law of Transience would shatter — therefore the gods can **never act directly** in Altor's affairs, but only through agents: **halvgudar (demigods)** reliant on superhuman powers, and the **Gudagåvor (god-gifts)** and **Sändebud (envoys)** that are signs of the gods' wish to intervene. Not even the very worst gods, who do desire the world's end, want the Law broken, for that would leave them no further outlet for their evil; so even the evil gods preserve Altor, the better to work their dark designs on a small scale upon its surface.

### The gods
There are endless beliefs across Ereb: some worship the gods of Water, some the gods of Darkness, others an Almighty Creator. By Dykos all these notions are mere illusions, since the gods have never shown themselves, so Altor's people imagine them in countless ways; most folk know only a handful of the gods that contend for mastery over Altor, and every faith holds that the gods it happens to believe in are the only true ones — no religion can encompass the infinite gods of the Cosmos. Many peoples worship the god that made them and so call that god the One; others keep a whole pantheon guarding the works of nature. Yet all the gods Altor's folk worship are only more or less correct images of the real ones. The central struggle is between **Ljus and Mörker** — light standing for goodness, humanity, warmth, mercy and tolerance, darkness for cold, violence, egoism and intolerance — and both sides strive to extend their power over as much of the planet's surface as possible. Ereb, Jih-pun and many lands lie near the **cold-gods'** domains and suffer when those gods wax strong; to keep a bearable climate, many gods are said to work to **balance cold and heat**, a metaphysical power-play vital to the planet's inhabitants.

### The heavenly bodies
Twelve observable, sphere-shaped bodies circle Altor: **nine small moons, the sun, Raukhra, and the fixstjärnesfär** (the fixed-star sphere). None can host normal life, all being much smaller than Altor.
- **Luna / Lûnia** — By far the largest of the nine moons and the only one visible year-round to the naked eye; it takes about **24 hours** to circle the planet (the exact period drifts by an hour for reasons the wise still can't fathom), and a month to turn once on its axis. Luna has a **dark side and a shining side**; the shining side resembles a face and the general belief is that it is a god riding an irregular orbit, sometimes lending the night light. Luna's light, unlike the sun's, **gives no warmth** — it is "free of that element" — and she is said to be near kin to the **cold-gods**, even if not of their number, for without their protection the moon would be burnt to dust by the sun's rays.
- **De övriga månarna** — The other eight moons are far smaller, the very least visible only by great telescope; several circle Altor at exactly the same speed as it rotates, so a permanent eclipse-shadow falls where they cast their shade — regions shunned by most living things, of which no credible reports exist, called the **'svarta hålen' (the black holes)**. Three are known: one in southern Soluna's jungles, one far out in the Västerhavet, and one over easternmost Akrogal where no erebier has ever set foot; some hold that the mystical **Krindenmaar** in Trakorien was once a black hole. To the common man these eight are known only through the names given to some of the year's months.
- **Solen** — The Life-giver and Light-bringer is among the most important divinities of Altor's universe, known under countless names and even denied existence by some, though most reckon the sun a warmth-deity circling Altor just outside Luna's orbit, taking exactly **24 hours** to complete a circuit. The sun is a constant chess-piece in the game of cold and heat: the **warmth-gods** push its path northward so it shines on ever-greater swaths of the northern hemisphere and weakens the cold's grip, while the **cold-gods** see the sun as their greatest foe and strive to press its path south, dreaming of quenching its power and casting Altor into eternal ice where their mastery would be unbroken and only Lûnia would lend light.
- **Fixstjärnorna** — Far from Altor's surface, at the very edge of the universe, hangs a sphere of black, unknown matter studded inside with countless bright stars. How they came there none can say, but it is widely known that now and then a star **breaks loose and falls** toward Altor's surface in a blaze of colour. Every people in Ereb has notions of what the stars mean; one common belief is that great heroes after death are borne to the fixed-star sphere to remind folk forever of their deeds and to light the world. By drawing imagined lines among the stars one makes out **constellations** of famous heroes and events (see §4). Apart from setting a person's birth-character for life, the stars have no great sway over Altor's course, but they are prized by sailors who navigate by them.
- **Raukhra** — A further important body, the **evil Raukhra**, shines with a constant, blood-red, cold light. Its orbit is a very extended ellipse that brings it into Altor's vicinity only a few times per millennium, vanishing the rest of the time into the void; but those approaches are always charged with great wickedness — for example it passed very near during the Third Conflux, when, glimpsed as **Röda Varse** on Altor's night sky, its worshippers gathered at the cult-sites and sent prayers for deliverance from Raukhra's power, while worshippers of the dark rejoiced in the red light's curses. Its orbit is so irregular that not even the finest star-watchers have an equation for its returns, which has led many to deem Raukhra a **chaos-power** bent on aimless devastation and the undoing of order; the truth lies hidden in the gods' world.
- **Shakhora** — Certain dark cults hold there is a **black moon, Shakhora**, visible only to the cult's practitioners, which lends them strength in the struggle against the Light; its existence is hard to confirm, but the wise suspect that, if it exists, it circles just outside the sun. When it "shows" on the firmament, darkness's envoys are said to gain power from its black "shine."
- **Kometer** — Comets are dread omens in the sky, by religious leaders conjured by the gods as messages to their worshippers; a comet always appears unexpectedly, sweeps near Altor's surface and then vanishes forever into the void, never to return.
- **Azuria / Blå Månen** — Tales tell of yet more bodies that once circled Altor in ages past, such as **Azuria** — also called the Blue Moon — which by sea-folk myth gave them light by night; Azuria ran the same orbit as the sun, 180° from it, and was destroyed in connection with the **First Conflux**. It is also possible, the book muses, that the gods, whose ways are unfathomable, may one day raise still further bodies into Altor's sky, to the wonder or terror of its inhabitants.

## 4. Constellations (Stjärnbilder)

Every people in Ereb has its notions of what the stars mean; a common belief is that great heroes after death are borne to the firmament to remind folk forever of their deeds. The sky illustrated in the book is the one seen **from Ereb** — elsewhere on Altor the sky differs, though some constellations are the same.

| Constellation | The story behind it |
|---|---|
| **Orcherion Krigaren & Charcheloch** | One of the brightest figures over Ereb: Orcherion with his jewel-studded sword-belt and silver-shimmering scabbard. He was an ancient hero who won his greatest fame slaying the two-headed serpent **Charchaloch**, also immortalized — vanquished beneath Orcherion's feet. |
| **Gyllene Skäran, Kung Gesnus & Medusas Hår** | King Gesnus cut three serpents from **Medusa's** head with the golden sickle, to ransom his beloved held captive by the **Death-prince**; Gesnus, the sickle and the three serpents were all preserved among the stars in memory of the deed. |
| **Harablos (Draken) & Hagros (Skeppet)** | The dragon Harablos spread terror across Ereb in the distant past until the hero **Akkillo**, in his ship **Hagros**, hunted it out over the sea, where the dragon plunged down; Harablos's body formed the island **Caddo**. |
| **Lutan** | In the tale of emperor **Leodorn** and empress **Farimah**, the magic lute **Alofon** played the prominent part: Farimah's playing softened the giant who held Leodorn captive in Altor's deepest chambers, so the emperor could flee. Farimah died when the giant realized his prisoner had escaped, but Leodorn later atoned for her death when he himself returned to the underworld. |
| **Turingwil (Vargen) & Vilévin (Ryttaren)** | The giant wolf-beast **Túringwil** is, by legend, father of all Altor's wolves, dogs and hounds — as big as two oxen and invulnerable beneath his dragon-scaled pelt. His greed undid him: the cunning hero **Vilévin** clad himself in hare-skin and wagered the wolf could not swallow him whole; Túringwil managed it, but once inside the belly Vilévin slew the wolf. |
| **Staven** (also **Spjutet/Lansen**) | A straight streak of four stars, by most seen as the famed staff of the archmage **Kossel**, with which he meant to topple the firmament; for his arrogance the gods took the staff and fixed it among the stars, out of the wicked magician's reach. |
| **Svärdet, Demonen & Elfhelm (Demonens Bane)** | The sword **Yrbad** is the legendary weapon with which the little boy **Elfhelm** slew the Demon of Darkness in the elder days and so **created day**. To win the sword Elfhelm had to perform five great deeds: steal dwarf-king **Ölavo's** golden strength-belt; slay the sea-monster **Yngarsil**; gather fruits from the immortal garden of the moon-elf queen; find a way to tame the mountain-giant **Nidar** (whose dwelling became **Nidabergen**); and at last choose the fairest maiden in all the world. Succeeding, he freed the sword from its prison of iron and ice where the dark gods had shut it. |
| **Ghazil (Dvärgen)** | **Ghâzil** is held to be the forefather of all dwarves, and so is immortalized in this little but bright-clear constellation. |
| **Gudarnas Hjälm** | The tale of the barbarians and the Gods' Helm is a long, tragic saga much-loved by Ereb's barbarian folk; it explains, among other things, why the barbarian folk are often fair-haired and bearded and why they shun magic. In brief, **Ilfdanúr**, son of the barbarians' king, stole the gods' golden helm to reach the gods' realm himself, but his plan came to nothing when he became aware of the gods' magical powers. |
| **Aruros (Geten)** | By old joriska legend, **Mingar** was the smith among the gods; he long refused to grant Ereb's folk the art of smithing metals, until the hero **Hôrgar-Nîr** managed to capture his livestock, the goat **Aruros**, who with cloven hooves and horns of six different metals leapt from peak to peak, creating ore-deposits of those metals. |
| **Harpan** | The god-smithed harp **Belerofon** belonged to **Parikila**, mother of the birds, and with it she made the birds' song so they might have some shelter against the Lord of Darkness. One of the birds — the crow, **kråkan** — was for the moment captive of that same Lord of Darkness, and so the crow cannot sing. |
| **Solen** | The bright star-figure at the middle of the firmament is the symbol of **Den Lysande Vägens kyrka**, and so has been given this name. |

## 5. Conflicts in Ereb (year 610 eO)

As in all civilized worlds, the folk of Ereb fall in and out of bitter quarrels; sometimes nothing comes of it and matters are settled amicably, but sometimes the consequences turn concrete and grave. For adventurers, war means **work** — as mercenaries or as plunderers — but also a more dangerous world: enemy troops may appear anywhere, law-keeping usually fails in wartime, and thieves, brigands and other criminal elements run fairly free. On the other hand, victorious generals grow rich on spoils and merchants on extortionate prices for scarce goods. The greater unrest-centres of **610 eO** are three.

### Feliciska piratkriget — the Felician Pirate War
Felician shipowners have long been able to buy royal **letters of marque** "allowing" them to seize "enemy" merchant ships in the king's name. In the first decade of the 600s, king **Kuthegil Halvskägg** issued ever more such letters against Erebos vessels — to the displeasure of erebosier, berendier and, to a degree, dalker. Erebos answered by sending its ships in **armed convoys**, which gave Felicien an excuse to openly rearm its fleet; foreign warships now stir in the neighbouring waters. On Felicien's side stand the **hynsolger** too, however much help can still be had from a realm split by civil war — though nobles and king there at least agree that war against a foreign power (read: Berendien) could reunite the broken realm, the nobles wishing to expand westward and seize sole control of eastern trade. On Erebos's side stand **Berendien and Caddo**, militarily and economically strong powers, but with a flaw: old grudges run deep, and the three have never managed a common line of action, which could give Felicien a chance to crush them one by one. Above all, **Caddo and Erebos** are bitter archrivals and rarely fully on the same side; most war-councils end in total deadlock, and some hold that Caddo could **switch sides** at any time to crush its old enemy once and for all. Erebos's foremost trade-partners, **Kardien and Zorakin**, show no interest in joining; both have weak fleets, and the zorakier have not forgotten the bloody nose they took in the last felicisk war.

### Ransarderröjet — the Ransard Threat
**Ransard** has long been an unrest-centre in northern Ereb through its aggressive policy. It seeks **revenge** on Trakorien and has prepared for a war of aggression for years; in the last decade it has subdued the neighbour-realm **Alerien** while souring relations with its other neighbours. Ransard is short of allies — no land wants to openly back the expansive, overbearing ransarder — but its army is fearsomely well-drilled and well-equipped, and with its flying mounts, the **gripar (griffins)**, it can easily throw disciplined cavalry into panic, and even the best-trained infantry quail at the sight of the flocks. (A rule-footnote: griffins make **disciplined troops count as undisciplined** in attack; undisciplined troops take **−1** to troop-value, minimum 0.5.) A further factor is the **crusade from Palinor** east of Ransard, which returns every hundred years; signs point to a crusade setting out around **610**, despite nothing being said outright, since it has come regularly for four centuries. There is thus a real risk Ransard must fight on **three fronts**: west against Trakorien, north against the alver, and east against Palinor. Trakorien's war-readiness is low — its war-fleet not in especially good order and its regular army untrained — so it will likely lean on **mercenaries**, which can be risky; but the island-realm can count on moral support from the Copper-Sea lands, who would hate to see Trakorien fall, with the exception of **Kardien**, which is not on especially good terms with it. The folk of Kardskogen and the crusader-knights in Palinor are likewise poorly equipped; the **alver** have never relished offensive war and show no sign of special preparations this time. The palinorer, too: they once swore a holy oath to crusade every hundred years, and the next is due in 610 — but nothing says this crusade will be better-organized than earlier ones, which often looked more like statements of principle than real campaigns.

### Nidländska reningen — the Nidland Purge
Another land said to be arming for war is **Nidland**, the realm of the evil magician **Vicotnik**, which in recent decades has shown an ever more unfriendly attitude to the outside world; though never open to outsiders, it is now worse than ever. Spies tell of a frightening religious fanaticism that makes the nidlänningar follow their leader's slightest wish; war-readiness is reckoned good, and by the prophecy Vicotnik uttered when he first came to Nidland, his crusade should set out against the rest of the world in **612**, "a dozen-dozen years" after his arrival. A likely problem for Nidland is its **shortage of weapons and armour**, for there is no iron in the land and, so far as is known, the nidlänningar have not bought much iron lately, though they keep a close eye on how Nidland's trade with Akrogal goes and may have gotten weapons from there. As for the neighbours' readiness: **Hynsolge**, as said, is tangled in a civil war of uncertain outcome, and would be on its knees if the nidlänningar attempted an offensive westward — but there'd be no lack of troops in the area either. **Cereval** can defend itself dutifully; especially the **trolls of Cer-skogen** are dangerous foes, and how the **drakmästarna** would stand in a war is uncertain — though probably they would rather not see Vicotnik master of the moon-silver. Whose magic is stronger, Vicotnik's or the drakmästarna's, is anyone's guess; most likely Vicotnik simply avoids the Cer-mountains and Cer-forest if he wishes to march on Cereval. **Krun**, south of Nidland, has **no war-readiness worth mentioning**.

## 6. Climate (Klimat)

*These rules are a shortened, simplified version of the climate rules in* Drakar och Demoner Gigant.

Like our own world, Ereb-Altor has several climates. Far in the north rule the **isherrarna**, striving to spread their deadly cold over Ereb; far in the south the salamanders try to consume all living things with their fire and murderous heat. Roughly midway between the poles lies Ereb, which therefore has a fairly mild, temperate climate, customarily divided into five zones.

### The climate zones
| Zone | Where | Character |
|---|---|---|
| **Arktiskt klimat** | Far north — **Orghin, Sanithsid**, the ice-barbarians' realms | Marked by ever-biting cold, snow and ice; only the ice-barbarians manage here without spells or very special gear. |
| **Subarktiskt klimat** | **Jorduashur, Barbia**, and the treacherous waters between Ereb and Orghin | Cold, harsh and windy; only a few short summer months are snow-free. Proper clothing required. |
| **Tempererat kustklimat** | **Trakorien, Ransard** | Chilly, rain-rich, windy winters and mild summers, at times very warm and pleasant. Those from the exotic backwater of Sweden would feel quite at home; for southerners it can seem dreadfully cold. |
| **Tempererat inlandsklimat** | The clearly dominant zone in Ereb | Year-mean a few degrees above zero, with moderate rainfall. The coasts can at times be very windy, but mostly neither storms nor downpours cause much trouble. |
| **Kopparhavsklimatet** | Around the Copper Sea | The most pleasant climate in Ereb for humans: warm, sunny summers; short, mild winters with more rain and sleet than snow. The light winds make it ideal for shipping, though mighty storms can blow up now and then — usually vanishing as fast as they came. |
| **Subtropiskt klimat** | The southern continents **Soluna and Samkarna** | Long dry spells alternate with rain-periods that barely suffice to water the parched soil; year-mean ~**+10 °C**, chiefly because of the often very cold nights. |

The difference between **climate** and **weather**: weather shifts day to day, more or less at random, while a place's climate is constant. A climate, like a roleperson's basic attributes, has typical traits: **Medeltemperatur, Nederbörd, Vind**.

### Effects of the weather
- It is always impossible to **shoot a bow** in rain, and likewise in Hård bris, Kuling or Storm.
- Ships at sea in **stiltje** lie wholly still; in stiltje, subtract **5** (not 10) from the 1T20 roll used to modify the mean temperature.
- A ship caught in a **storm** drifts with the wind until the storm abates; each hour, an on-deck roleperson must pass a **Sjökunnighet** check or be washed overboard — unless below deck or lashed down.
- If temperature climbs above **+35** or below **−25 °C**, a roleperson takes **−10%** on all skills requiring movement of any body-part other than the mouth, with a further **−10%** for every 5° further over/under — negated if the RP is protected by spells or wears extremely well-adapted clothing.
- In any precipitation, **CL doubles** on Smyga and Gömma sig.
- In Storm, Kuling, Kraftig or Massiv precipitation, **CL halves** on Lyssna.

Distinct dial value-sets printed on the map include `40/20/30/10`, `25/30/25/20`, `35/15/35/15`, `15/40/15/30`, `30/20/30/20`, `50/5/30/15`, and `20/30/20/30`. Use the dial nearest the party. *(The quadrant→compass orientation is not labelled in the scan; read it off the printed map.)*

In some lands merchants are commoner than artisans; in feudal states there are plenty of landless folk and serf-farmers but also relatively many nobles. ## 8. Calendar & Time (Tideräkning)

Ereb's many lands have developed different ways of measuring time; according to their religious and cultural differences they hold different notions of what marks the reckoning's beginning and its intervals. The commonest method is described here.

### Den Arcivalska tideräkningen
This calendar was "devised" in **212 eO** by **Arcival**, exark of Den Lysande Vägen, and applies in all lands that hold the Lysande Vägen as state religion, plus a few others; it is by this calendar that the Ereb-Altor box's timelines are built. It rests on the **old joriska reckoning** with some modifications — the months, for instance, were renamed, since the old joriska names were the names of Jorpagna's gods, and one preferred not to mix old heathen ways into the Lysande Vägen's calendar. The year divides into **twelve months of thirty days each**; at year's end come **five extra days** in memory of Odo's birth and to mark the new year, a span called **festeveckan**, during which one celebrates Odo's birth, the year past, and the year's start. Month-names vary a little with the language spoken, but all mean the same. In **Zorakin and Kardien**, where the **Enkla** (see Religion) hold a strong position, the so-called festeveckan at year's end has been rejected; instead, days are added at the end of every second month and at the end of every fourth quarter.

The Arcivalska calendar is in force in **Kardien, Zorakin, Klomellien, Magilre, Trakorien, Jorpagna, Berendien, Felicien, Hynsolge, Erebos and Caddo**; time is reckoned the same in all, even where days and months bear other names to honour whom one wishes. **Ransard, Jorduashur, Barbia and Cereval** keep their own **moon-based** calendar; **Nidland** uses the calendar Vicotnik Ärkemagikern devised; alver and dvärgar have their own calendars built on their creation-tales, and some religions keep deviant reckonings of their own.

### What the high days mean
- **Vårdagjämningen** is kept with great markets where folk meet and feast all day; no work is done, all gather and celebrate, and weddings are often held on this day, which marks that Light has regained power over Darkness.
- **Odos död och återuppståndelse** is a four-day feast kept in every land. On the first day all must fast and mourn; no work may be done, and from sunrise to sunset one may do nothing but pray for Odo and for dead kin. This lasts three days, but on the fourth all hold a great feast where the kin take part, eating and drinking in plenty to celebrate that the dead win a better life after death.
- **Odos återförening med Etin** is kept 54 days after his resurrection; on this day all good followers of the Lysande Vägen should give something to the church and attend the mass always held.
- **Soldansen** (see Religion) is the greatest yearly festival, kept at the summer solstice when the day is at its very longest.
- **Stora skördedagen** marks the formal start of the harvest of what the year has given, even if one has already begun, is in the middle of, or has not started reaping at all; on this day no evil words may be spoken and folk exchange fine gifts, with a great feast in the evening.
- **Ångerdagen** falls at the autumn equinox, when day and night are equally long; one looks back on the year past and repents one's sins.
- **Sköna damers dag**, kept the first vile, is a feast-day in honour of all fair ladies, when all men show their wives extra respect and gentleness and give fine gifts; weddings are often held, and since legend says Odo's mother was born this day, it is reckoned very lucky to bear a girl on it.
- **Odos födelse**, the 20th of Etinsmånad, is kept with masses to Odo's memory and gifts to both the church and one another.

### The weekdays (Veckodagarna)
The year is, by the Arcivalska reckoning, divided into a good **50 weeks of seven days** each. Every **Odosdag** is a rest-day on which no physical work may be done. Each saint and martyr of the Lysande Vägen's church has a yearly Odosdag named for them, and since every guild, society, town and order has its own patron-saint, one or more days a year are given to celebrating it.

## 10. Religion

Ereb-Altor holds several religions of differing origin and scope; most are sketched country by country, but the book describes in full only **Den Lysande Vägen**, the faith of **Zorakin, Kardien, Caddo, Berendien, Magilre** and large parts of **Klomellien**.

### Den Lysande Vägen — history
About six hundred years ago a son was born to a potter in **Arno on Caddo**, named **Odo**; he was given a good upbringing in his father's craft but spent much time wandering in nature and pondering, and was widely held a very strange boy. At twenty-seven came the turning-point: a revelation from **Etin** bidding him preach of Etin and the Lysande Vägen. Within a few generations Odo's new thoughts wholly dominated the Copper-Sea lands; in the 100s the faith took firm root in **Zorakin and Kardien**, Caddo had been fully under Odo's teaching for a century, and soon **Erebos and Berendien** joined the church's fellowship.

In **278** came the great **Schism** within the church, over whether the practice of **magic** was compatible with Odo's teaching or not. **Caddo** held all magic destructive and forbade its use, with support of Odo's letter to the ereber in which he wrote *"I shall not warp Etin's creation, for then it would become mere illusion."* On the mainland one held instead that all magic was **neutral**, its powers usable either against the teaching or in accord with it, by the magician's free choice — only **necromancy** and possibly **illusionism** counting as clearly against. In **281** came the final break, when the exarks in **Arno** and **Ekeborg** condemned each other as heretics and apostates from the True Way; the church split into **two rival organizations** and lost much of its force and momentum, and the mission has never since borne the fruit it once did.

### Doctrine (Lära) & Creation (Skapelsen)
Odo's basic teaching was that the sun-god **Etin** is the one true God and the only one worth worshipping; other gods are mere misconceptions of Etin or lesser spirit-beings. As Odo saw it, mankind in life shall honour Etin with merciful deeds and follow his commandments; Etin is almighty and sees all, and at death each soul is taken to the Death-realm to be judged for its deeds by Etin himself. Those who served best become saints and stay at their creator's side to watch over the world and live in endless bliss; the very worst, who broke Etin's commandments, are cast into the hell of chaos and darkness; those in between must undergo a long and painful **cleansing** before they can win eternal rest.

In the beginning Etin floated in the void and filled it with his light; there he made Altor and turned the world into a home for living beings. The foremost beings he made in his own image and called **humans**; through the meddling of other, lesser god-beings, some humans were changed and new races arose, but all natural forces and all life spring ultimately from Etin, who alone holds **creative power** — other beings calling themselves Gods can only modify and alter what Etin has made. With Odo as his tool, Etin revealed his Teaching to set right all that had gone wrong among the intelligent races since their creation: those who follow the Teaching will live a better, more giving life; those who trust in Etin and confess his Teaching are promised protection and the chance of salvation. The goal is a life of respect for other beings and for the Teaching — a basic tenet being that one should always show others the regard one wants for oneself, and that respect for society, for law and order, is respect for one's fellows.

### The six commandments (Buden)
1. **Etin är störst!**
2. **Missbruka inte Läran!**
3. **Hedra din familj!**
4. **Begå icke grymma handlingar!**
5. **Stjäl icke!**
6. **Håll ditt ord!**

### Custom (Sedvänjor)
Odo's words were set down by his followers in the **Gyllene Boken**; this scripture and learned men's interpretations around it make up the **Läran**. The Gyllene Boken was written in **dalkiska**, the tongue spoken on Caddo, so all the church's men must know that language well enough to read the writ; those who don't have had the Book's content explained so all can know it in broad strokes. Every thinking being is welcome to follow the Lysande Vägen and become a church-member — Odo stressed it would be a cruel deed to bar any soul from approaching Etin — and one is taken in through the **Solceremonin**, in which a priest calls down Etin's blessing on the new walker of the Way. The church's symbol is the **sun**, since Odo said the sun was Etin's sign before the world; through the sun Etin channels his life-force across the world and lets it live, so the sun plays a great role in Lysande Vägen thought and rite. The chief religious feast is the **Soldansen** at the summer solstice when the day is longest: by tradition Odo had his very first revelation then and began to dance for joy, so members gather every year at the various holy sites that day. They meet just before dawn for a hearty breakfast (the church feeds all the poor), and as the sun rises they begin a traditional long-dance on the holy place's open ground until the sun reaches its zenith. Should the solstice sky be overcast, it is read as a sign of Etin's displeasure with the year's deeds, and all gather in prayer until Etin is appeased and the sun shows again — a prayer that may last days, with only short breaks for food and sleep.

### Organisation
The book describes the church's organisation in **Zorakin, Kardien, Magilre and Berendien**; the **caddiska** organisation is similar but not identical.
- **Basnivå** — At grass-roots the church is organised in **församlingar (parishes)** that handle daily contact with the faithful; each parish covers all church-members within a bounded area and is led by a **riark (parish-priest)** who keeps to a temple at the parish centre. There the riark holds regular services and masses in which one prays to Etin and is taught the Läran; since masses are held in dalkiska, only those who understand something attend. Riarken cannot devote himself to the church full-time and is often a royal official besides, to make a living; the post is **not hereditary** — a riark's son must first train as riark at a seminary and then seek the post. After some years a riark may, for a small sum, buy free of celibacy and start a family.
- **Ledning** — The church is led by the **exarken** in **Ekeborg**, whose position answers to that of a pope and who is held the holiest and foremost interpreter of the Läran. At his side is a **kuria** of six **tots-kerîgasser**, who form a counterpart to the church government, each responsible for a function. Below them the church is divided on a hierarchical model: each land forms a **kerisât** led by a **kerisgass** (archbishop); a kerisât may be split into several **bontisât (stift)** under a **bontisål (biskop)**; below the bontisålar come the parishes and parish-priests.
- **Finanser** — The church is funded by the **church-tithe**, a tenth of the lands' total production, levied at the same time as the king's and nobles' taxes. Since the church and all its employees are **tax-exempt**, it has very great income and very few fixed costs, making it **enormously rich** — reflected in lavish cathedrals (one can fund a cathedral's building and pay hundreds of labourers for years on end) and in the luxurious lives of its leaders. The original ideals of asceticism and poverty have been entirely forgotten, and the church is now too mighty to be changed from outside; change from within is excluded, since only the deciders would lose by it. In recent centuries this luxury has badly shaken trust in the church and spawned several popular protest-movements, such as the **Ljusa Handen**.
- **Den Ljusa Handen** — One of the tots-kerîgasser leads the **Ljusa Handen**, the counterpart to an inquisition (not present on Caddo). It was instituted by exark **Zegedorn III in 314** as a tool to guard the Läran's purity; its task is to oversee the church's officials and priests, ensure they preach in accord with the exark's interpretations, scrutinize and possibly condemn deviant doctrines, and see that the folk are not led astray by dark powers' preachers. It struck swiftly and brutally; for **heretics** — those who preach anything other than the Lysande Vägen — there is only one punishment: death. The church also owns great landed estates in both realms and lets its orders live off them.
- **Maktposition** — The church holds an enormously strong position in **Zorakin and Kardien**, chiefly because those kingdoms' leaders and nobles belong to its ranks; it is otherwise rich and mighty enough to silence critics and uses that power when needed. It enjoys favoured standing on taxes, temple-building and the right to own land. In **Erebos and Berendien** the faith is official and strong, though not as strong as on the **Aidne-halvön**. By law the four kingdoms' kings must confess the Lysande Vägen — but for many rulers it has likely been only a lip-confession; the exarken still ranks always among the royal counsellors, and few rulers would dare ignore his counsel entirely.

### The church and other religions; magic
The Lysande Vägen of course holds itself the **one true religion** and Etin the one true god; others have seriously misunderstood the divine world, and their members ought to be drawn from their error by active preaching — how energetically depending on the local church-leaders' bent. Spreading the Läran sword in hand is **wholly acceptable**, which among other things has led to several crusades.
- **Odöda och magi** — By Odo, **undead** are an abomination and a repugnant, warped version of the life Etin instilled in the world; it falls to the church's servants to destroy undead wherever possible and so grant their unblessed souls rest. Spöken and gastar are reckoned pitiable beings unable to find rest after death. As for **magic**, on **Caddo** the orthodox holds (citing Odo's letter, *"I shall not warp Etin's creation..."*) that **all magic, including alchemy, is forbidden** — only the **god-gifted** may use such powers. On the mainland one holds magic **neutral**, usable for or against the Läran by the magician's free choice; but certain spells count as clearly opposed to Odo's teaching and may be used **only against the unfaithful**: **Kontrollera varelse (A), Blindhet (S), Epilepsi (S), Fruktan (S), Mörker (E), Förbanna vapen (E), Mörkerväg (E), Illusion (I)** and **Fata Morgana (I)**. All **nekromantiska** spells are forbidden outright, whatever their purpose.

### Heresies (Kätterier) & dissenters
- **Caddism** — To join the **caddiska** standpoint that magic is ungodly, destructive and must be banned. Since the great Schism this view has been **forbidden on the mainland**, though zealous caddiska missionaries still come to preach the hidden faith.
- **Illusism** — Notably rarer, this false teaching springs from **Trakorien**: several doctrines preaching that the world does not really exist but is in fact one great **illusion** — the human consciousness only a part of it, and all events wholly random and chaotic. One may still enjoy what seems pleasurable, since in eternity's perspective it matters little what happens or what one does; much "religious" activity consists of sensual pleasures. Other faiths see illusister as totally destructive, anarchistic, chaotic and incalculable. When this uncomplicated, pleasure-promoting teaching won a great following in Kardien in the 380s–390s, exark **Pergon IV** was forced by decree in **403** to declare it a defilement and an abomination wholly irreconcilable with the Lysande Vägen's core; all illusister were at once banished to **Trakorien**, where the teaching has many adherents. Ever since, the magic-school **illusionism** is viewed with suspicion, since several leading illusister practised this form of magic.
- **De Enkla** — This group preaches that Odo never meant a church to form for his teaching: each person is equal before Etin, so priests and church-organisation are a pointless barrier to contact between worshipper and god. The enkla ignore the church and its commands and strive instead to live strictly by what the **Gyllene Boken** bids; this has made them very suspect in the church's eyes, and the Ljusa Handen spends much effort watching them. Many of the movement's more enthusiastic members have been exiled to more tolerant states around the Copper Sea.

## 11. Trade (Handel)

In Ereb-Altor virtually all goods-transport is **by sea**; travel overland is slow and needs many draught- or pack-animals, while a single ship can carry as much as five hundred pack-animals — and though sailing a ship is costly, it pays handsomely in the long run. Land transport is purely a local affair and only for smaller quantities of goods.

### Sea trade (Sjöhandel)
That said, sea-travel is not without trouble. A crossing from one side of the Copper Sea to the other takes **at least a month** (usually one to one-and-a-half) and is at least as risky as overland travel through the remote mountain tracts where svartfolk and robbers hold sway. Besides all the pirates cruising for prey, storms and treacherous reefs lie in wait for the careless sailor. Some sea-routes are simply **impossible**: Golwynda's freshwater sea is sealed to all shipping, partly by its very strong currents and partly by the **Raas Narram** falls where the freshwater sea pours into the Copper Sea; the freshwater sea lies roughly **250 m higher** than the Copper Sea, so no ship can sail between them. The same holds for the freshwater sea and **Masevabukten**, where the **Demontungan** cuts the water-roads. To get round this, most trade happens in the cities by the falls: at **Stabul** beside Raas Narram, erebska and akrogaliska merchants swap goods; in **Ircront** in Nidland there is matching barter, though far less frequented, chiefly because all the Copper Sea's merchants focus instead on Stabul, which lies far nearer and is far easier to reach — which is how the state of Nidland arose.

Other truly great trade-metropolises in Ereb are **Gringul** in Erebos, **Arno** on Caddo, **Tricilve** in Trakorien and **Mastika Agga** in Barbia. Arno and Gringul, by their very central positions in the Copper Sea, are natural barter-markets for all kinds of transactions, where merchants from east and west meet to swap goods rather than sail all the way to a good's source (cheaper at source, but time is money too). **Tricilve** is the hub for trade between rich Trakorien and the Copper-Sea lands, and the centre of trade among the islands; everything, all know, can be bought or sold in Tricilve, and folk quite often try, without contract, to hawk what they have or buy what they want, at risk of profit or loss. **Mastika Agga** is above all a great timber-port for the Nargur forests, but also handles much trade from Akrogal and Cereval. A city right in the thick of it, through which in effect all trade-ships pass, is **Sterborg** in Kardien — though this city never won any real control of the trade, chiefly because of its shallow, fairly small harbour.

Why nearly all ships sail **inside Caddo** rather than outside is, naturally, the notorious **morelvidynska pirates**: most merchants reckon sailing between island and pirates the lesser risk, which gives a fairly seeming-safe shelter. The pirates do sail round the island when they think they have something to gain — but they know that if they plunder too much, the campaign against them will harden, and both merchants and pirates lose by that.

## 12. Languages (Språk)

Many tongues are spoken in Ereb, but some matter more than others. The largest family is the **joriska**, possibly rivalled only by the barbarians' family.

### Jori
**Jori** is the tongue spoken in the old **Jorpagna** and, during the Empire's heyday, spread across all southern and western Ereb; it became the mother-tongue in every land from Felicien in the southeast to Trakorien in the northwest and the region between. As Jorpagna fell and the jori-speaking folk were cut off from one another by mountains and seas hard to cross, the dialects lost contact and, over centuries, drifted ever farther apart; in the end three great groups split off, and these split further, until the original imperial jori had become more than ten wholly distinct languages — some alike, others mutually unintelligible. The differences within a group are roughly those between **Norwegian, Danish and Swedish** (mutual understanding possible with a little effort); the differences between groups are roughly those between **German and Swedish**.
- **Kejserlig jori** — the original imperial jori; mother of all modern dialects and now reckoned extremely high-flown and archaic, used today only by scholars and in diplomatic correspondence.
- **Nyjori dialekter** — spoken in the remnants of old Jorpagna, the eastern parts of Zorakin, some erebosiska small-islands, and northern Berendien.
- **Västjoriska dialekter** — spoken in Kardien, Felicien, Zorakin, Magilre, Mereld and Bzegusta, and in parts of Klomellien and Trakorien; the jori of Saphyna, Klomellien and Magilre forms a special group, the **continental jori**. The other trakoriska dialects are influenced by **barboski**, an old barbarian tongue; klomeliskan has drawn on **hamuriskan**; feliciskan has borrowed many words from the felicier's old tongue **kîmzon**. Northern Kardien speaks a dialect leaning more toward trakoriskan and kardiskan.
- **Östjoriska dialekter** — spoken in Berendien and Hynsolge and on the erebosiska isles; hynsolgskan has taken great influence from **prangi**, the original barbarians' tongue, and the nordberendiska dialects have been swayed by **satenu** from Landori.

### Other tongues
- **Erdir (Forntunga)** — this ancient tongue is dead today, but was spoken in western Ereb before Jorpagna's rise; it is still used in age-old rituals and in connection with magic, but by very few people, and is written in an alphabet of some 60 signs.
- **Barbarspråk (narguri)** — the barbarians of northern Ereb all speak related versions of one root-tongue, **narguri**; the dialects have drifted apart and are now hard for the uninitiated to follow. The branches: **Sydnarguriska** (covering **hamuriska**, spoken in SE Klomellien, and **kaseni**, spoken by the barbarians of northern Aidne); **Tjugiska** (spoken by the ice-barbarians on Orghin and Sanithsid; mother of **barboskin**, spoken in Ziddis on the trakoriska isle of Palamux, and of **rungli**, spoken on the trakoriska isle of Trinsmyra); **Barbiska** (spoken in Barbia); **Västnarguriska** (covering **jordiska/Jordu-ashur**, **ransardiska**, and **timoleriska** — which split into **palinorska, timoriska** and **aleriska** after Ransard broke up the realm of Timolerien); and **Östnarguriska** (covering **cerevaliska** and **prangi**, the tongue of the Nargur-forest barbarians, also spoken by folk who once lived in Hynsolge before the jorer came). An offshoot is **howathiskan**, spoken in Torshem south of Nidabergen, heavily swayed by östjori and satenu and now wholly unintelligible to the other barbarian tongues. The barbarian tongues are written in an alphabet differing from both the joriska runrad and the elven script, descended from the alphabet found in Akrogal and much resembling the felicier's kîmzon.
- **Dalkiska** — this distinctive tongue is spoken on **Caddo**. Some learned men hold it kin to **erdir**, but the matter is unsettled. Dalkiska is very song-like, with a marked sentence-melody and pitch-differences, which makes it very hard for outsiders to learn well (EDD: FV B4 and B5 cost double). It is written with a variant of the joriska runrad, certain needless signs struck and others added. Dalkiska is the holy tongue of **Den Lysande Vägen** and all its sacred writ; the church's priests and higher dignitaries therefore command dalkiska fairly well, whatever land they come from.
- **Andra människospråk** — there are plenty of small human tongues spoken by various folk-groups, e.g. **ker-bosh**, spoken by barbarians in NW Kardien; such tongues will be treated when they matter.
- **Dvärgiska språk (gryndur)** — dwarven tongues are relatively easy to grasp, since dwarves and humans are fairly alike in temperament, though dwarf-speech is usually more fitted to concrete things than to abstractions, mirroring the dwarves' bent for craft over philosophy. Dwarves speak many widely different tongues — some as different as Swedish and Russian — and to bridge the gap they have over time raised one tongue, **gryndur** (originally the speech of the Grynner-bergen folk), to a general lingua franca; when those folk were driven from their homes they spread across Ereb, often as travelling merchants, carrying their tongue with them. All dwarf-tongues are written in similar **dwarven runes**, each language modifying them for its own needs; sometimes the joriska runrad is used too.
- **Drakspråket** — those who study the dragon-tongue soon find it harder than any other; not for difficult grammar, word-wealth or dialects, but because masses of words must be **redefined** to translate into another tongue! Roughly a tenth of all words one can simply learn by definition — and even then it's often hard to manage without using other dragon-words. Hence dragons have a wholly different conceptual world from all other beings; dragons and humans can never fully understand one another, the dragon-tongue being even more foreign to a human than insect-speech. Magicians gladly study the dragon-tongue, since it readily expresses mighty magic in its terms; the trouble is finding a teacher, for dragons are notoriously unhelpful. By some legends, all living things have a **"sanna namn"** in the dragon-tongue — a full description of the being's true self, **preshashn** — and one who knows this name and can invoke it rightly gains **full power over the being**, able to compel it to anything or to kill it. Nothing is documented about this, but it's not unlikely a being's ego could be fully described in the dragon-tongue.
- **Svartiska** — the black-folk speak a host of tongues by race and geography; though all sprang from one stem, millennia of splintering and development have made the dialects nearly unintelligible to one another (a troll in Cereval, for instance, has no way to understand a kardisk orch). So far as is known, svartiskan has **no script of its own**; when needed, truly learned black-folk have used joriska or dwarven runes learned from some poor captive. Common to all svartiska tongues is that they are harsh and unmusical, with many throat-sounds and hard noises; it's nearly impossible for a human to speak svartiska fluently (slightly easier for a dwarf), and the black-folk themselves struggle with voiced consonants and diphthongs.
- **Satenu (alviska)** — this tongue is spoken by **teni**, all of Altor's elf-folk, and was created by **Tevatenu**, Moder Jord (goddess of the life-force). Satenu has **no dialects** and has changed very little over time, mainly by adding new words; its stability has made it very popular among Ereb's learned, since texts in it are fully intelligible everywhere and in all ages. It is a soft, pleasant tongue with few harsh sounds and a high frequency of vowels; its pronunciation, stress-rules and sentence-melody recall Italian a little. It is written in an ornate, pleasing alphabet of ~30 signs, each standing for one sound, with simple, immemorial spelling-rules that hew close to pronunciation.
- **Ödlespråken** — reptile-men, insectoids, dinosauroids and other intelligent reptiles speak a group of closely related, primitive tongues fitted to the relatively primitive lives these races lead; they are hard for other races to pronounce, holding a wealth of hiss-sounds scarcely reproducible by non-reptilian throats, and are fairly unimportant since spoken only by very limited groups.

### Writing systems (Skriftsystem)
Hundreds of tongues and dialects are spoken in Ereb, all with their own pronunciation and melody, and setting these sounds to signs on paper is not always easy. There are **three major** writing systems. By far the largest is the **joriska runrad**, used by all who speak joriska tongues and some others; the **barbartecknen**, used in Ransard, Barbia, Jorduashur, Cereval and by the few literate Nargur-forest barbarians, is the next largest, closely followed by the **alvtecknen**, the elves' beautiful script, used besides the elves by learned men across Ereb. Lesser systems exist too: the **dwarves' runes** (resembling the joriska); the felicier's **kîmzon** alphabet; the black-folk's alphabets; and the **nidländska** alphabet **Vicotnik Ärkemagikern** uses, descended from the signs used in Akrogal.

### Language-learning (Språkinlärning)
Different folks' and cultures' linguistic traits make some tongues easy and others hard: a kardier learns zorakiska quite easily, erebosiska a bit less so, and finds dragon-tongue nearly impossible. This "carry-over" of experience between related tongues works differently in EDD and DoD, so it's handled separately.
- **Grundreglerna:** A person with CL in one tongue automatically gets **half that CL** (round down) in every other tongue in the **same dialektgrupp** (for both Tala and Läsa/Skriva); if one dialektgrupp lies between, divide by three (round down) instead. *(Ex: 75% berendiska → 37% erebosiska & hynsolgska; 60% feliciska → 20% klomelliska, saphynska & magillska.)* Tongues that have **borrowed** from one another share kinship: a **quarter** (round down) of one's CL carries to the borrowing tongue (Läsa/Skriva only). *(Ex: ransardiska 80% → 20% on Tala timoriska & tala aleriska.)*
- **Expert (DoD):** A person has **FV −2** in all tongues of the same dialektgrupp (Tala and Läsa/Skriva); with one dialektgrupp between, **FV −3**. *(Ex: FV B4 berendiska → FV B2 erebosiska & hynsolgska; FV B5 feliciska → FV B2 klomelliska, saphynska & magillska.)* Borrowed tongues give **FV −4** for Läsa/Skriva. *(Ex: ransardiska FV B5 → FV B1 on L/S timoriska & L/S aleriska.)*

## 13. Magic (Magi)

### Dark Magic (Mörkermagi)
*The dark-magic rules are written chiefly for* Expert Drakar och Demoner*; to use them under Grundreglerna, convert spells' school-values to difficulty-grades (below).*

In **468 eO**, by the westerners' reckoning, a powerful magician came wandering into eastern Ereb from Akrogal's unknown lands. He called himself **Vicotnik Ärkemagikern** and settled in **Nidland** south of Cereval. The nidlänningar were an unorganized folk, but under Vicotnik's leadership a firm state was forged — a **magocracy** with Vicotnik as absolute ruler. Nearly all the area's magicians came to study at his **Akademin i Mörke**, the strange magic Vicotnik had brought from the east, and those who resisted his teachings fled or were imprisoned. Vicotnik thus became **Storkejsare of Nidland**, and none dare oppose him for fear of his magic; to help govern, he keeps a score of other dark-mages, the **domarna**, who are otherwise the only magicians allowed in Nidland — all other magic is forbidden. Vicotnik is a very cruel ruler: nearly all crimes against his hard laws are punished with death, and his twenty judges may, with his nod, decide a defendant's guilt directly. His two slogans are *"Du skall dö!"* and *"Han skall dö!"*, and he has a reputation as one of the world's most evil men.

Where the dark magic comes from is hard to say, but in any case Vicotnik brought it to Ereb from the east. The magic-school's chief mark is that it prefers the **powers of Darkness** over those of Light. A power all dark-magicians gain at their initiation is **mörkerseende**: their eyes turn for the night, set to wake by night and sleep by day, making them near-wholly nocturnal beings working almost only at night, which has further worsened their bad name — many think them a kind of **vampire**. The truth, by many learned men, is rather that they draw their magical force from **Luna**, Ereb-Altor's moon; their magic is therefore **stronger than usual at full moon** and weaker at new moon — a dark-mage's PSY rises to **double at full moon** and falls to **half at new moon**.

**School-value → difficulty conversion (for Grundreglerna):** Skolvärde **1–4 → grade 1**; **5–9 → 2**; **10–14 → 3**; **15+ → 4**.

### Magic nodes (Maginoder)
Two magical phenomena in Ereb-Altor can greatly strengthen or limit a magician's power: **maginoder** and **magistormar**; the SL decides when, where and how they occur, and is urged to use them with **great caution**, since otherwise node- and storm-effects can unpleasantly ruin a roleperson's hopes. *(Under Grundreglerna these may all be ignored.)* The magical force-flows of Altor's universe cannot be used in their raw form; only when a flow meets a certain kind of matter — usually called a **defluxer** — can the force be channelled and made usable. The magician then compares the flows to a ray of light: it only "shines" when it strikes the impenetrable defluxer, where it can be "reflected" or "refracted" by spells in the direction the mage wishes. Defluxers cannot be proven save by trial; one may liken them to a atom's electrons and protons — never visible to the naked eye, but their existence shown by their electrical effect on the world. Defluxers are assumed to be **everywhere**, since spells work anywhere, but in some places it is easier to lay magic of a certain kind and harder in others; it also takes varying time to recover magical force depending on where one is. Erebs learned call places where deflux-concentration is far above average **maginoder**.

A node's strength depends on **two factors**: the concentration of defluxers and the node's size. There is a known link between node-types and three environmental factors:
- **Jordskalets tjocklek, sammansättning** and **klimatet** govern where the various defluxers gather. It is generally known that **animism-spells** are easiest at sea-level and **necromancy-spells** at the same depth/height oppositely — the **topomagiska fenomenet**: 'animism-defluxer' lie mainly near sea-level, 'necromancy-defluxer' mostly at great heights and depths.
- **Köldbaserade elementarbesvärjelser** are harder in warm climates, and warmth-based spells harder in cold climates — the **klimatomagiska förhållandet**, an upshot, no doubt, of the cold- and heat-gods' eternal struggle.
- **Mentalistiska besvärjelser** work better in mountainous, rocky tracts than at sea, where the soil is thick — mentalism-defluxer lie in the earth-crust's minerals, easiest to use where there is much stone underfoot: the **geomagiska sammanträffandet**.

**Utseende & verkan:** A maginod is normally invisible, detectable only by truly skilled magicians or "by chance"; nodes are near-always circular, diameters from a few centimetres up to ~ten metres (typically ~three metres). The really big ones, **meganoderna**, have turned up in about a dozen places in Ereb. Nodes have three commonly-known effects when one is **inside one of the right type**: the **chance of spell-success rises**; the spell **draws less force** from the mage; and the mage **recovers magic faster** than normal. In a node of the **wrong** type, the opposite holds — success-chance falls, PSY-cost rises, and PSY-recovery slows. Some natural omens accompany nodes: a tree growing by a very strong animism-node may come to look like anything but a tree; folk buried near necromancy-nodes may become **gengångare** whether they will or no; lightning strikes oftener near strong elemental-magic nodes.
- **Speldata:** A node's size **NSTO** = 2×T10, multiplied (a number 1–100, in decimetres of diameter; e.g. 4 and 9 → 4×9 dm = 3.6 m). A node's strength **NSTY** = 2T10 − NSTO (in metres); the higher NSTY, the stronger the node. To detect a node, be within NSTY metres; if the mage's PSY in the node's school exceeds NSTY, a **hidden skill-roll** in the right school is allowed, repeated every ten minutes while in range.

### Mage-storms (Magistormar)
All magicians' great dread are the so-called **magistormarna** that sweep over Ereb at fairly regular intervals. None knows their cause; one common explanation is a kind of "discharge" of magic energy occurring this way, another that the gods use the storms to keep the balance between used and unused magic forces. A magistorm cannot be seen with the naked eye, only by the spell **Väderförutsägelse**; one notices nothing special as it passes, save that it becomes **impossible to cast spells** while in the storm's path. In nine cases of ten the magistorm is accompanied by violent **thunderstorms**, so most magicians flee from thunder, since they can always suspect a magistorm. The storm's effect is fearsome: all **magic items** in its path must pass a resistance-roll against the storm's strength or be **"avmagiserade"** (de-magicked) wholly, losing all the magic forces they once held; one uses the item's weakest spell's effect-grade as the passive part and the storm's strength as the active part. The storm's strength varies by SL's choice, but if uncertain he can roll **3T10**.

### Beings & creatures glossary
- **Drakar (dragons)** — only intelligent survivors of prehistory; gather at the Drakarnas Arkipelag; masters of the "true name" (preshashn); near kin to the south-pole salamanders.
- **Isherrarna** — north-pole beings of condensed energy; bring the ice age when they prevail; worshipped by Orghin/Sanithsid ice-folk.
- **Salamandrarna** — south-pole reptilian energy-beings; bring heat when they prevail; worshipped by southern nomads & Mosgilak; devour loose immateriality near the south pole.
- **Köttbitare** — flesh-eating grasshopper-swarm from the Samkarna marshes; destroyed Jorpagna in the Third Conflux.
- **Slempukar** — supernatural creatures welling from **Slimpaku**; razed Kamsun and Yndar.
- **Margyler** — cruel conqueror-folk of SE Akrogal who took Yndar (and long held Furgia).
- **Vättar** — goblin-kin who stole the dwarves' metal-craft and sold it to the Golwynda humans.
- **Raugoner** — intelligent insectoids that troubled Melukha in the early Iron Age.
- **Titaner, cykloper, sphinxer** — strange folk of the Valgussjön islands; titans also dwell on Amargoh.
- **Gripar (griffins) & hippogriffer** — flying war-mounts; griffins are Ransard's edge, hippogriffs the old Akrogal nomads'.
- **Reptilmän / insektoider / dinosaurider** — the "rasslande raser" and southern lizard-folk (Mosgilak, Maminira me delema, Samkarna).
- **Gengångare, gastar, slempukar** — undead/spirits; an abomination to Den Lysande Vägen, haunting Yndar and Traxilme's ruins.

### Schools of magic referenced
**Elementarmagi, Symbolism, Mentalism** (founded by the Golwynda culture); **Animism** (developed with the forest-elves); **Nekromanti & Spiritism** (born of the Yndar culture); **Shamanism** (the Efaro tradition that influenced later schools); **Illusionism** (Trakorien; condemned as heresy in Kardien); **Mörkermagi** (Vicotnik's dark school, drawing power from Luna); **Alkemi** (widespread; forbidden on Caddo except to the god-gifted). Magic is also shaped by **maginoder** and disrupted by **magistormar** (see §13).
