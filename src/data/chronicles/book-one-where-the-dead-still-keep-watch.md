## Prologue

Kaelene Fenholt grew up alone on the streets of Poane, surviving through careful observation and quick hands. After a failed theft, she was rescued by Tarek, a seasoned adventurer who recognized her remarkable ability to read people and situations. Under his guidance, she learned to turn the instincts that had kept her alive into the skills that would carry her far beyond the streets where she was raised.

Far to the south, Bram Kestrel left behind his family's fishing business in search of a life beyond the horizon. Joining a merchant caravan, he discovered that the road offered hardship, danger, and purpose in equal measure.

Far to the east, the elf Aelthira Moonveil devoted years to studying history and ancient magic in one of the great human cities. There she uncovered truths that powerful people wanted buried. Refusing to remain silent, she left the academy behind and set out with knowledge that others would do anything to suppress.

Tarek's final expedition led him and Kaelene into an ancient tomb in search of an ancient artifact. The discovery came at a terrible cost. Tarek gave his life so Kaelene could escape, leaving her with a burden she did not yet understand.

In the months that followed, Kaelene's path crossed with Bram's and Aelthira's. Though each came from a different corner of the world, they found common purpose on the road, choosing to face its dangers together.

## Chapter One: Life on the Road

The road had not been repaved in living memory, which suited Bram Kestrel just fine. Uneven stones gave a man something to watch his footing on, and watching his footing gave him an excuse not to maintain eye contact with the people he was talking to. This was useful because Bram was in the middle of a story, and the story required a certain amount of creative latitude.

"She was enormous," he said, with the conviction of a man stating a geological fact. "Biggest river serpent I have ever seen. Three coils above the waterline before you got to the head. And the head, I want you to understand, was the size of a decent-sized cart. Maybe a good-sized cart. The point is it was very large."

Aelthira Moonveil walked two paces to his left, her wand tucked into her belt in the easy way of someone who had stopped thinking about it years ago. She was watching a hawk trace slow circles above the treeline and had been for some time.

"You have told this story four times since Merrath," she said, not looking at the hawk or at Bram. "The serpent was large-sized the first time. Cart-sized the second. You are making progress."

"I am remembering more accurately as the distance increases. This is a known phenomenon."

"It is not."

Kaelene Fenholt walked a half step behind them both, which was where she tended to walk, and which she had given up explaining. It was not that she lacked confidence. It was that walking at the back of the group meant she could see everything ahead without being required to comment on it. The old road curved between fields gone to seed, bordered on both sides by ancient stone walls with grasses growing from the cracks. The sky was pale and wide. Nothing moved along the road in either direction.

She had been in livelier places. She had also been in worse ones.

Bram had moved on from the river serpent and was now deep into what he described as the Incident at the Crennford Mill, a tale Kaelene was only half-familiar with, which meant either this was a new embellishment or she had stopped listening before it ended the previous time. She suspected both were true.

"The miller," Bram was saying, "had been warned. I told him specifically: do not open that door. I was very clear about the door. And he looked me in the eye, this man, and he opened the door."

"What was behind the door?" Aelthira asked. She had taken out her wand and was examining the tip of it with the focused attention of someone who had found something interesting and was deciding whether to be concerned.

"That is the thing. Nobody ever knew for certain. Because whatever it was, when the door opened, it left by the window. Very quickly. And then the miller sat down on the floor and refused to get up for the better part of an afternoon."

"An eventful story that resolves into nothing."

"All the best ones do."

Aelthira put her wand away. The hawk had dropped below the treeline and was gone. She looked at the road ahead with the expression she wore when she was thinking about something she had not decided to share yet, which Kaelene had come to recognise over eighteen months of travel as one of Aelthira's more common expressions.

The road was old, as roads in this part of the world tended to be, worn smooth by centuries of feet and wheels and the occasional cart-axle that had given up mid-journey and been left where it fell. The fields on either side had been farmed once, long enough ago that the furrows had gone soft and shapeless. A crow sat on one of the stone walls and watched them pass with the professionally bored expression of a crow that had seen a great many travellers and found them uniformly disappointing.

Bram noticed the crow.

"Good morning," he said to it.

The crow said nothing.

"Unfriendly country," Bram said, to no one in particular.

Kaelene had grown up hearing that silence was an absence. She no longer believed this. Silence had texture, had weight, had different qualities depending on what produced it. The silence of an empty room was nothing like the silence of a room someone had recently left. She had learned to pay attention to what kind of quiet surrounded her, and the quiet of this road was the ordinary quiet of midmorning in the countryside: insects somewhere in the long grass, a faint wind moving through the tops of the nearest trees, the rhythm of three sets of footsteps on uneven stone.

Bram's footsteps were the loudest. This was partly the heavy armour, partly the way he planted each foot as though he intended to keep that particular patch of ground from moving, and partly just Bram.

Aelthira's were quieter, the light step of someone accustomed to moving through spaces without advertising the fact.

Kaelene's she did not think about. She had stopped thinking about her own footsteps somewhere around Caldorene, when she had learned that moving quietly was not always enough and that the distance between alive and not-alive could close very quickly. She still moved quietly. She just no longer congratulated herself for it.

She touched the hilt of her sword, briefly, the way you touched something familiar in an unfamiliar mood. The blade was light and narrow, good for speed and reach and getting between things that did not have obvious places to put a sword. Tarek had said she had quick hands and a slow temper, which was the right combination. She had been seventeen when he said it. She still thought about it sometimes, usually at unexpected moments, the way you remembered the specific words of people who were gone.

Up ahead, Bram had moved on to a new tale. She caught the words "a quantity of honey" and "the mayor's horse" and decided she would not ask for the connecting narrative.

"You are very quiet today," Aelthira said, dropping back to walk beside her.

"I am often quiet."

"Quieter than often, then." Aelthira said this the way she said most things: directly, without softening it, which somehow made it easier to hear than if she had tried to soften it.

Kaelene looked at the road ahead. The stone walls continued. The fields continued. The sky stayed pale and wide.

"I was thinking," she said.

"About?"

"Nothing in particular. Everything in the same general direction."

Aelthira considered this. "That is either very philosophical or you are going to tell me it is nothing and change the subject."

"It is nothing," Kaelene said. "What were you looking at? With the wand?"

There was a brief pause that told her Aelthira had noticed the subject-change and was deciding whether to honour it.

"There is a small degradation in the resonance at the tip," Aelthira said. "It is not significant. I am monitoring it."

"You are always monitoring something."

"Yes. It saves time later."

Ahead of them, Bram laughed at his own story, a big, uncomplicated sound that startled the crow from the wall and sent it flapping into the sky with its own objections.

"The honey!" he said, apparently to the road, since neither of them were close enough to have been listening. "That part always gets me."

Kaelene felt something that was not quite a smile but was in the same neighbourhood. She had not chosen Bram and Aelthira deliberately, in the sense of sitting down and considering her options and selecting two travelling companions from a list. She had ended up with them the way most people ended up with the people they travelled with: through proximity, and circumstance, and the particular way that shared danger makes some people less strange rather than more. She trusted them. She did not say this often because it did not need to be said and Bram would have turned it into a toast at the next available tavern.

The road curved ahead between two stands of oak, their branches touching overhead and making a brief tunnel of green-grey light. On the far side the fields opened again and the sky came back, and the road stretched out before them long and pale and entirely unremarkable.

Nothing moved.

Nothing called out.

Bram settled his shield on his back, adjusted the strap across his chest, and launched into what appeared to be the epilogue of the honey story.

Kaelene listened to the sound of the road and thought about nothing in particular.

For a little while longer, at least, there was nothing that needed thinking about.

## Chapter Two: A Ghost on the Road

The figure appeared in the centre of the road without announcement.

One moment there was nothing. The next, there was something, and the something was a man, or the shape of a man, standing very still in the middle of the worn stone path with his hands open at his sides. He was translucent in the way of old glass, faintly blue-white in the pale morning light, his edges soft where they met the air. His robes were ancient: long, plain, belted at the waist, the style of a religious order that had not existed for a very long time.

Bram stopped talking mid-sentence. This was notable enough that Kaelene looked up from the road immediately.

The three of them had gone still. Aelthira's hand was on her wand, not drawing it, just resting there, which was its own kind of statement.

The ghost looked at them. His face was the face of an older man, lined and tired, and whatever expression it carried was not malice. It was something much heavier than malice.

"Travellers," he said. His voice came from somewhere slightly wrong, not from his mouth exactly but from the air around him, like a sound remembered rather than made. "I would ask for a moment of your time. I have been asking for a very long time."

Bram's hand had gone to his sword without him appearing to decide to put it there. He did not draw. He was watching the ghost with the focused attention he usually reserved for things that were about to hit him.

"We are listening," Aelthira said.

The ghost looked at her with something that might have been gratitude. He breathed in, though Kaelene was not sure ghosts breathed, and then he began.

His name, he told them, had been Father Aldric, though he asked them not to worry about names because names were not the important part.

A hundred years before his own time, he said, this road had been busier. There had been a village nearby, a small one, a few dozen families and a church that had stood for three centuries. The village had been unremarkable in all the ways that made a place worth living in: it had a mill, a market day, an inn that served bad wine cheerfully. The people who lived there had done so for generations and expected to continue.

Then goblins came.

He said this simply, without dramatic pause, which somehow made it worse. A raiding party, large enough to be serious and fast enough to be dangerous. They came at night and they came with fire, and by the time the village had understood what was happening the mill was burning and the road out was cut off.

"The records my order kept said the villagers believed they would die," the ghost said. "There was no reason to think otherwise. And then someone arrived."

A knight of the Brotherhood of the Eternally Shining Star, travelling alone, whose name no one had ever learned. He had come from the north and had not stopped to ask questions or make demands. The accounts written down by the surviving congregation, accounts that Father Aldric had read and tended for the whole of his priesthood, said only that he had placed himself between the fire and the people and held that position through the night.

"The records do not say much about what it looked like," Father Aldric said. "They say it took most of the night. They say that when it was over he sat down in the road and did not get up again." He paused. "The people who wrote those words had seen the whole of it. I think they did not have the language for the rest."

Bram's hand was no longer on his sword.

They had buried the knight beneath the church, the ghost continued. Beneath the altar, in a chamber the original builders had constructed for exactly that purpose, as if they had known someone worth honouring would eventually need it. His arms and armour they had arranged on a shelf in the grave chamber with the care people give to things that deserve better than to be forgotten. He had asked for nothing. They had given him the best they had.

"No one knew his name," Father Aldric said. "They called him the knight of the Brotherhood of the Eternally Shining Star, and they kept his grave and lit candles beside the altar. In time the people who had been there that night died, and their children remembered only that there had been someone, once, who had come when they needed someone. I tended that grave for thirty years. I read those records so many times I could have recited them in my sleep." Something shifted in his expression. "I wish I had known him. I think about that sometimes, even now."

A silence settled over the road. Somewhere in the distance a wood pigeon called once and stopped.

"A hundred years after the raid," the ghost said, "a necromancer came. That part I can tell you myself."

He had arrived in winter, Father Aldric said. Alone, unremarkable-looking, the kind of man you might not notice in a market. He had asked to stay the night and had been given a room, because the village gave rooms to travellers and had no particular reason not to.

He had not been alone, it turned out.

Twenty skeletons had come with him, held in some dormant state until he chose otherwise, and in the night he had chosen otherwise. They had emerged from the road and from the fields with the patient mechanical precision of soldiers following orders, and their orders were simple: strip the knight's grave and take whatever treasury the church was hiding behind its walls. Which was, it turned out, considerable. Three generations of parish donations, a few items of genuine value, and things the congregation had trusted to the church because it was the safest place they knew.

"I heard them," the ghost said. "I came down from my quarters and into the nave and there he was, standing at my altar, directing them. Very calm. As though it were a business transaction."

Aelthira had gone very still in the way she went still when she was paying close attention. Kaelene could see her cataloguing something, filing it away.

Father Aldric had not, he admitted, been a violent man. He was a priest. He had no sword and no training with one and no particular instinct for combat. What he had was a very specific piece of knowledge: that his order's church had been built with certain protective measures worked into its stones by craftsmen who understood both architecture and defensive magic, and that those measures could be invoked from the altar by someone who knew the words.

He had known the words.

"I told the necromancer to leave," Father Aldric said. "He told me I was welcome to stop him. I think he found it amusing. A priest, alone, against twenty skeletons."

He paused.

"The magic was meant to sound an alarm and seal the doors. I may have said the words rather forcefully. There was a great deal of lightning. The north wall came down."

The north wall, Kaelene thought. She filed this away without knowing quite why.

"The necromancer and I were both buried in the collapse," the ghost said. "The skeletons he had sent below reached the outer crypt, but they could not pass the grave chamber threshold. That place was consecrated long before my time, and what he had made could not enter. His other skeletons survived the collapse. They have been there ever since. Still patrolling. Still following orders given a century ago by a man who is now dead and buried under his own rubble."

He looked at them. The morning light had not changed but he seemed, somehow, slightly less solid than he had been a few minutes ago. As though the telling of it had cost him something.

"I have been on this road for one hundred years," he said. "I have asked every traveller who passed to go into the village and cleanse what remains. Most of them could not hear me. Some could hear me but would not stop. A few stopped and listened and walked on anyway."

He did not say this bitterly. That was the part that was hardest to hear. He said it the way you stated a thing you had already made peace with because you had had a very long time to make peace with it.

"The knight deserves to rest without the undead walking above him," Father Aldric said. "The village deserves to be only a village again. And I would very much like to stop standing on this road."

He looked at each of them in turn: at Bram, at Aelthira, at Kaelene.

"I am asking," he said quietly. "I am only asking."

## Chapter Three: Old Wounds

Nobody said anything for a moment.

The ghost waited. He was good at waiting. He had had, as he had mentioned, considerable practice.

Bram was the first to move. He cleared his throat and shifted his weight and glanced sideways at Kaelene in the way he had, the way that looked casual and was not, the way that meant he had already read the room and was deciding what to do with what he had found there.

Kaelene was looking at the road past the ghost's shoulder.

She was not afraid. She wanted to be precise about that, at least in her own head, because she had been afraid in Jorpagna and she knew what it felt like and this was not the same thing. Fear was quick and bright and made you want to move. This was slower and heavier and made you want to stand very still and think carefully about what you were being asked to walk into.

Undead. A ruined village. A church with a collapsed wall and skeletons still following orders from a dead man.

She had heard a description like that before, except it had been a tomb instead of a church and the skeletons had been the least of it. Tarek had described the job in the same matterof-fact tone that experienced adventurers used for things that were dangerous but manageable. He had been right about the dangerous part.

She became aware that Bram was still looking at her. She did not meet his eyes.

"Well," Bram said, to the general air, "it sounds straightforward enough. Twenty skeletons, you said?"

"That was the number he sent to the village," the ghost said. "I cannot tell you how many remain after a century. I have never been able to make myself go back, not while they still walked there."

"Sure, sure. And they are, what, patrolling? Just walking around in there?"

"As far as I am aware."

"So they are not going to come pouring out of the village at us the moment we knock on the door. They are just in there doing their rounds." Bram nodded, in the considering way of a man building a case he had already decided to make. "That is practically a normal Tuesday. We have done worse on a Tuesday. Remember the thing with the hobgoblins? That was a Tuesday."

"That was a Wednesday," Aelthira said.

"It felt like a Tuesday."

The joke sat in the air for a moment. It was not a bad joke. It was the kind of joke Bram made when he was trying to carry weight for someone else, which Kaelene knew because she had watched him do it before. He made it land lightly so there was room to breathe around it. The intention was good, and the intention was visible, and she felt a complicated mixture of gratitude and the particular exhaustion of being known.

Aelthira turned to look at Kaelene directly. She did not say anything. She did not offer reassurance or push or frame the silence as a question. She simply waited, which was its own kind of statement: she had noticed, and she was not going to pretend otherwise, and she was also not going to make it into something it did not need to be.

Kaelene let out a slow breath.

The thing about Jorpagna was not the undead, in the end. The undead had been terrible, but terrible things had a shape you could understand. The thing about Jorpagna was the moment when Tarek had turned to her in the dark of the tomb and said run, with a certainty in his voice that had left no room for argument, and she had run. He had not, and the gap between those two facts had never fully closed.

She had kept moving since then because stopping felt like letting it win. She was not sure what it was. The guilt, maybe, or the grief, or the simple blunt fact of the world being a place where people you loved could be there and then not be there and the road kept going regardless.

And here was a road that went toward undead and tombs.

And here was a ghost who had been standing on it for a hundred years, asking in the only way he had left to ask.

"The skeletons are there," Aelthira said. Her voice was level and thoughtful. "They are not going to stop being there because we walked past. Someone will have to go in eventually, or no one will, and the village will stay as it is for another century. That is the choice. It is not a comfortable choice but it is the actual one."

She paused.

"I would rather it be us than no one."

This was not sentiment from Aelthira. Sentiment was not her register. It was an assessment, offered plainly and without decoration, and it was exactly the right thing to say because it did not ask Kaelene to feel differently than she felt. It just named what was true.

Kaelene looked at the ghost. Father Aldric was watching her with the patient, careful attention of someone who understood that this was not a small thing to ask and had never pretended otherwise.

She thought about Tarek saying run.

She thought about the knight in the grave beneath the church, who had arrived on a road not unlike this one and had not turned back either.

"All right," she said. "Give us a moment."

## Chapter Four: They Agree

The moment she had asked for passed quickly.

The ghost waited at a respectful distance, which was to say he had drifted a few metres to the side of the road and was looking at the fields rather than at them, with the practised tact of someone who had learned over a long time that the answer was more likely to come if he did not press for it.

Kaelene looked at the road. Not at Bram, not at Aelthira, not at the ghost. The road itself, worn and pale and running off between its stone walls in both directions.

The priest had tended that grave for thirty years. He had read the accounts of the knight's death so many times he could have recited them in his sleep. He had died trying to protect a man he had never met, whose name he had never known, and he had been standing on this road for a century waiting for someone to finish what he had started.

A knight buried under skeletons.

A priest who only wanted to stop standing on a road.

"We will do it," Kaelene said.

It came out more simply than she had expected. No qualification, no caveat. Just the words, which were the right ones, and the decision behind them, which had apparently been made while she was looking at the road.

Father Aldric turned back toward them. Something in his expression shifted in a way that was hard to name: not relief exactly, or not only relief. It was the look of someone who has been carrying a thing for a very long time and has just felt the first suggestion that they might be allowed to put it down.

Bram grinned. It was his good grin, the unguarded one rather than the one he deployed for an audience, and he reached up to settle the strap of his shield across his chest with the practiced ease of a man who had done it ten thousand times. "Right then," he said. "Where is this village?"

Aelthira had already drawn her wand. She was examining it with focused attention, turning it in her fingers, and Kaelene recognised the expression: not concern, but preparation. The small ritual of someone making sure their tools were ready before they needed them.

The ghost gave them directions. The village was half a mile up the road, he said, then left through a gap in the stone wall where a gate had once stood. They would see the church before they saw anything else; it was the only stone building and it stood at the far end of what had been the main street. He told them about the layout of the outer buildings as best he could remember it, which was not perfectly, given that a century had passed since he had last walked through it.

"The skeletons will be patrolling," he said. "They were soldiers when he made them and they have not stopped being soldiers. They will engage anything that enters. I am sorry. I wish I could tell you more."

"It is enough," Kaelene said. She paused. "I am Kaelene Fenholt. This is Bram Kestrel. And Aelthira Moonveil."

"Then I will remember you," Father Aldric said quietly.

The ghost was quiet for a moment. Then he said: "Thank you." Two words, plain and direct and carrying the particular weight of something that had waited a very long time to be said. He did not elaborate. He did not need to.

He faded as they turned toward the road, not disappearing exactly but becoming less present, his edges softer, until he was somewhere between visible and not. Watching, perhaps. Or simply waiting, which was what he knew best.

The gap in the stone wall was ahead on the left. Beyond it, through the trees, the upper edge of a stone building was just visible against the pale sky.

Bram rolled his shoulders, settled his weight, and started walking. Aelthira fell in beside him. Kaelene took her usual half step back, her hand resting near but not on the hilt of her sword, and followed them both off the road and into whatever came next.

## Chapter Five: Into the Ruined Village

They heard it before they saw it.

Not a sound exactly. The absence of one. The path through the trees was only a minute's walk from the road and the trees were not dense, but somewhere between stepping off the road and reaching the gap in the far treeline, the world went quiet in a way the world was not usually quiet. No birdsong. No movement in the branches overhead. Not the hush of early morning, which had its own texture and its own life underneath it, but something flatter. Something that had been quiet for a long time and had settled into it.

Bram noticed first. Kaelene saw his pace change, just slightly: the same speed but with more weight behind it, the way he walked when he was paying attention to more than the ground in front of him. His hand did not go to his sword. Not yet. But his shoulders had shifted under the heavy armour in a way that meant they were ready to.

Then the trees thinned and the village came into view and all three of them stopped.

It had been a real place once. That was the first thing Kaelene thought, and it surprised her a little, the plainness of it. She had expected something more dramatic, something that looked the part. Instead what she saw was simply a village that had been empty for a very long time. Stone and timber buildings in two rough rows on either side of what had been a main street, their roofs long collapsed inward, their walls standing in varying degrees of completeness with climbing plants threading through every crack and gap. Moss had softened the angles of the stonework. The cobblestones of the street were cracked down the middle by decades of frost and root, and the gaps between them had filled with soil and thin pale grass.

A well in the centre of the street still had its stone surround intact. Whatever had been above it, the frame and the roof and the rope and the bucket, was gone.

The church was visible from where they stood: straight ahead at the far end of the street, impossible to miss. It was the only building whose walls still stood to their full height on three sides, its stone a darker grey than the timberframed houses around it, older and more solid. The fourth side was the problem. The north wall had come down exactly as Father Aldric had described: not blown outward but collapsed inward, a ragged cascade of stone that had taken part of the roof with it and left a gap as wide as a cart. Through it Kaelene could see the pale interior, the suggestion of a nave, the dark at the far end where the altar would be.

Aelthira was looking at the buildings along the street, moving her gaze from left to right the way she read a page. Cataloguing, Kaelene thought. She was counting doorways and windows and calculating angles. Looking for places something could stand without being seen.

There were a great many such places.

She looked at the gap and thought about lightning and a priest who had said the words rather forcefully.

"No birds," Aelthira said.

"I noticed," Kaelene said.

"Unusual. There is sufficient cover and no active predators visible. Birds avoid places associated with undead in some circumstances, though the mechanism is not well understood. The animist tradition has several competing theories." She paused. "I mention it because it supports the ghost's account, not because the birds are the concern."

"Very reassuring," Bram said.

"I thought so."

They stood at the edge of the village for a moment longer. The street ran straight between its ruined buildings to the church at the far end, open and unobstructed, which should have been reassuring and was not. The silence pressed in from all sides. Somewhere inside one of the collapsed buildings a loose timber shifted with a sound like a held breath being let out, and all three of them moved at once before they registered it was nothing.

Bram let out a slow exhale. "Right," he said quietly. "Let's go."

They stepped onto the cracked cobblestones and the sound of their footsteps filled the silence like stones dropped into still water.

They had made it perhaps ten metres down the street when the movement came from the far left: a shadow detaching from the interior of a roofless building, angular and deliberate, resolving itself in the thin morning light into the shape of something upright, something that moved with mechanical precision, and then the dry unmistakable clatter of bone against bone, and then another shadow behind the first, and a third behind that.

Bram already had his sword out.

## Chapter Six: The Skeletons at the Gate

There were five of them.

They came out of the ruined building in a line, which was the first thing that struck Kaelene: not a shambling rush, not the lurching weight of something driven by hunger or rage, but a line. Evenly spaced. Spears levelled at the same height. The two at the ends of the line were carrying crossbows already drawn, the bolts seated and ready, and those two had stopped at the threshold of the ruined doorway while the three spear-carriers continued forward onto the cobblestones without breaking stride.

A hundred years of patrol had not made them slow. If anything it had stripped everything away except the essential: advance, engage, neutralise.

Bram was already moving.

He stepped forward and angled left, putting himself between the crossbowmen and the other two, his shield coming up in one motion that had been repeated so many times it no longer required thought. The broad face of it caught the first bolt with a sound like a hammer on an anvil, a sharp metallic clang that rang down the empty street and off the stone walls on either side. Bram did not flinch. He did not even rock back. He planted his feet and the bolt fell away and he grinned at the skeleton that had fired it.

"Good shot," he said. "I mean that. You have kept your form up. Most people let things slide after the first century."

The skeleton did not respond to this, being a skeleton, and began reloading with the same unhurried mechanical efficiency it had fired with. Bram respected this. He also moved a meter to the right so it would have to adjust its aim, which cost him nothing and cost the skeleton a full second it did not have.

Kaelene had already gone left.

She moved the moment Bram moved, not because she had to watch him to know when to go but because she did not. Eighteen months of working alongside someone taught you their rhythms whether you intended to learn them or not, and she knew that when Bram stepped forward he would take the centre and the attention with him, and the flanks would briefly open, and briefly was enough. She went wide around the left edge of the advancing spearmen at a pace that kept her outside the arc of the nearest spear while she closed the distance.

The lead skeleton tracked her with its spear. She let it. Tracking was not the same as hitting, and tracking her meant the spear point was moving, which meant it was not set, which meant it could not brace.

She stepped inside the point as it swung toward her, let it pass her left shoulder by a hand's width, and brought her blade across the joint where the skeleton's forearm met the elbow. It was not a powerful cut. It did not need to be. Bone held together by something other than living tissue still needed its joints intact, and an elbow joint that had taken a clean lateral strike at the weakest point of its articulation was an elbow joint that no longer held. The spear dropped. The arm hung at a wrong angle and the skeleton looked at it with the blank patience of something that had not yet updated its understanding of the situation.

Kaelene stepped back and moved to the next one.

Behind her she heard Bram engage the spearman nearest him: not the crash of a full shield charge but a controlled collision, the bass thud of his shoulder behind the shield driving into the skeleton's leading leg and breaking the advance before it could gather into anything. She heard the skeleton go down onto the cobblestones in sections and heard Bram's sword follow through before the pieces had stopped moving.

"Two," he said, to no one in particular.

The first crossbowman was still reloading. Bram had moved a meter to ruin its aim and the one meter had become three and now he was close enough that the crossbow was the wrong tool for the distance and the skeleton had not yet updated its assessment of this problem. Bram’s sword settled the question before it could. He stepped past the falling pieces without breaking stride.

"Three," he said.

The second crossbowman fired. The bolt skipped off the cobblestones a meter to Kaelene's right, which meant it had been aimed at Bram and he had moved at exactly the right moment, as he tended to. She did not look back to confirm this. She was already dealing with the second spear-carrier, a taller skeleton that had adjusted its approach the moment the first one fell, coming at an angle rather than straight in, trying to use the reach of the spear to keep her outside striking distance.

It was a reasonable tactic. It would have worked on someone without the patience to wait.

Kaelene waited.

She gave ground slowly, not retreating but measuring, letting the skeleton commit to the angle, letting it believe the distance was being maintained on its terms. Then Aelthira spoke a single word from somewhere behind and to the right, and the world lit up white.

The crackling bolt of energy that crossed the street took the skeleton that had been advancing on Kaelene directly in the chest and removed it from the equation in a single sharp report that left the smell of scorched bone hanging in the cold air. The bones did not scatter so much as cease, dropping straight down onto the cobblestones in a loose pile with the abrupt finality of something that had run entirely out of whatever had been animating it.

In the brief silence after the crack, Kaelene heard her own breathing.

"One remaining," Aelthira said, in the tone of someone reading from a list.

The last crossbowman had reloaded. It was standing in the ruined doorway where it had started, bolt aimed, making the only calculation available to it: which of the three people in the street presented the clearest shot. Bram was already facing it with his shield up. Aelthira was moving laterally, which complicated the angle. Kaelene was the closest, and had no shield, and the skeleton made its decision.

It fired.

Kaelene dropped to one knee and the bolt passed through the space where her shoulder had been and buried itself in a crack between cobblestones. She was back on her feet before the skeleton had processed the miss, crossing the remaining distance at a run, and by the time it had begun to reach for another bolt she was through the doorway and her blade had found the joint between the third cervical vertebra and the fourth and the skull came free and bounced once off the stone floor and rolled into a corner.

The body stood for a moment in the way of things that had not yet registered they were done. Then it sat down.

Kaelene straightened. Her knee from the drop was going to complain about that later. She noted this and set it aside for later consideration.

Outside, the street was quiet again. Quieter than before, if anything, the silence of a place where something had just happened settling back down over the cobblestones. Scattered bone and two intact spears lay at intervals between the well and the far wall. One of the crossbows had landed near the well's stone surround and lay there with its bolt still seated, its owner no longer present to make use of it.

Bram was surveying the street with the satisfied expression of a man reviewing work well done. "Nice drop," he said, when Kaelene came back out through the doorway.

"My knee disagrees."

"Your knee will get over it." He glanced at Aelthira. "Good shot."

"It was the obvious shot," Aelthira said, already looking toward the far end of the street and the church beyond. "The church is still ahead."

"It is," Bram agreed.

The three of them looked at it together for a moment: the dark stone, the collapsed north wall, the pale rectangle of the open doorway at its near end. The silence around it was the same as the silence that had pressed in from all sides when they arrived, but something about it felt different now. Less like a held breath. More like a door that had already decided it was going to open.

Bram rolled his sword wrist once, loosening it, and started walking.

## Chapter Seven: Inside the Church

The doors were heavy oak, swollen with years of damp, and they opened with the kind of resistance that suggested they had not moved in a long time and had opinions about moving now. Bram put his shoulder into it. The hinges gave a groan that filled the whole doorway and sent something small skittering away in the dark above them, and then the doors were open and the three of them stood at the threshold and the church breathed out at them.

Cold first. Not the clean cold of open air but something staler, still, the cold of a space that had been sealed from the weather for so long that it had developed its own climate. Then the smell: old stone and damp and the particular mineral weight of a place that had not had a living thing move through it in decades. Something underneath that Kaelene did not have a name for and did not particularly want one.

The nave stretched ahead of them. It was larger than it had looked from outside, or felt larger, the way stone ceilings and stone floors and stone walls had of taking whatever space existed and making it feel deliberate. The pews were gone, or nearly: a few rotted stubs remained in their rows, but time had reduced most of them to soft grey matter indistinguishable from the floor. The great hole in the north wall let in a rectangle of pale morning light and a thin, steady draught that moved through the nave like something looking for a way out.

At the far end, where the altar would have been, was only shadow. The hole in the wall threw its light at an angle that lit the middle of the nave clearly and left both ends in a dimness that the eye wanted to resolve into shapes and mostly could not.

Four shapes it could resolve into.

Four skeletons stood in the body of the nave, spaced across its width in a line that was not quite even, two carrying spears and two carrying short swords. They had been standing there, Kaelene thought, for a hundred years. Waiting for a door that had never opened. And now it had.

The nearest one turned its head toward them with a sound like a key turning in a dry lock.

"Four," Bram said, and moved.

He took the centre two, which was not discussed and did not need to be. He crossed the space between the door and the nearest skeleton at a pace that was neither a run nor a walk but the particular controlled advance of someone who had calculated exactly how much momentum was useful and how much was wasted, and he brought his shield up into the face of the first spear-carrier before it had fully committed to its thrust.

The collision was loud in the enclosed space. The skeleton went back three steps and Bram followed it, keeping the pressure on, not giving it room to reset. His sword found the spine on the second step, a single clean stroke, and the skeleton came apart at the waist and both halves fell separately.

The second of his two was already closing. It had not been deterred by watching the first go down, because deterrence required a kind of reflection that a century-old patrol did not include. Bram turned into it without pause, caught its sword and on the face of his shield before the stroke could develop, and drove forward with his full weight behind the shield until the skeleton’s back met the nearest pew stump and the stump settled the argument. He left it there and stepped back.

Kaelene had taken the left.

Her skeleton was the faster of the two sword-carriers, moving toward her at an angle that used the remnant pew stumps as cover, keeping something solid between them as it closed. It was smart, or had been programmed smart, and she gave it credit for that as she redirected her approach to cut off the angle it was using. It adjusted. She adjusted. They covered the width of the nave in this way, trading ground and geometry, until she let it believe it had cornered her against the wall and it committed to the lunge.

She sidestepped and the short sword struck stone and the impact shuddered up the skeleton's arm in a way that living fighters could manage and dead ones could not feel but were still subject to. The hand opened. The sword dropped. She put her blade through the joint at the base of its skull before it could recalculate and it folded.

Across the nave, Aelthira had dealt with the far right, a single precise bolt of energy from her wand that had taken it mid-stride while it was still assessing which of the three intruders presented the greatest threat. The assessment had taken too long. It was a known problem with soldiers who had been given no updated orders in a century: their threat evaluation ran on information that was a hundred years out of date, and Aelthira did not look like anyone's idea of the greatest threat until the moment she was.

Three seconds, perhaps four. The four skeletons were down and the nave was quiet and it was almost clean and then the shadows moved.

Not from the front. From behind.

The alcoves on either side of the entrance were deep, cut into the stone for what had once been devotional statues or side chapels, and the two skeletons that had been standing in them had been standing there since the necromancer had positioned them, still as the stone around them, waiting with the inexhaustible patience of things that did not experience the passage of time. They had waited through the sound of the doors opening. They had waited through the sounds of the fight. They had waited for exactly this: the moment when the intruders were at the far end of the nave with their attention forward.

And now they came out.

Kaelene heard the footstep behind her and the sound of the alcove's shadow changing and she was already turning when the spear came down, and she got her blade up but it was not a clean parry, just enough to deflect the point from where it had been aimed to somewhere less critical, and the shaft caught her across the left shoulder with enough force to stagger her sideways into the wall.

At the same moment she heard Bram.

Not words, not exactly: a short, sharp, colourfully profane expression that she had heard from him in tight spots before and that meant I did not see that coming, which from Bram was a significant admission. Then the ringing bass impact of a spear shaft caught square on his shield, hard enough that she felt the echo of it through the stone floor.

They were surrounded. The two alcove skeletons between them and the door, the scattered remains of the four in front of them, Aelthira somewhere to her right. The draught from the hole in the wall moved through the nave and the pale morning light lay across the floor in its rectangle and for one long second everything was very still and very clear.

Then Bram shoved.

He put the full weight of his shield into the skeleton that had hit him, not a fighting move but a raw physical assertion, and the skeleton went back two steps and those two steps were enough for him to get his sword around into the space between them. He was not fast, he had never been fast, but he was very difficult to move and very difficult to hurt and he had survived long enough to understand that surviving was often more useful than speed.

Kaelene pushed off the wall. Her shoulder was going to be a problem; she could already feel the particular hot numbness that came before the ache set in, and she noted it and made herself small. Left arm was unreliable. Right arm was fine. She adjusted her grip, brought her blade across her body to compensate, and went at the skeleton that had hit her before it could recover and set for a second strike.

Close-in was wrong for a spear and right for her blade. She stayed close-in.

The spear could not find the angle it needed and the skeleton could not generate the leverage to push her out, and she worked the problem the same way she worked every problem that had too much going on: small, precise, patient, one thing at a time. A joint here. A fulcrum there. The wrist first so it could not grip, then the knee so it could not advance, then the neck.

It was not elegant. It was effective.

Across the nave Bram had driven his skeleton back through three rows of pew stumps and into the far wall and finished it there with the flat economy of a man who had run out of patience for the whole enterprise. The impact when it hit the wall was the loudest thing that had happened since the doors opened and small fragments of ancient mortar rained down from the ceiling.

"Sorry," Bram said, to the ceiling.

Silence.

Complete and final, the kind that did not feel like it was going to be interrupted. Kaelene lowered her blade and took stock. Her shoulder announced itself with the full force it had been saving while she had been too busy to listen to it. Bram was breathing in the controlled, deliberate way of a big man who had been working very hard and was not going to show it more than necessary. Aelthira stood in the middle of the nave with her wand at her side, her expression the careful neutral of someone running an internal assessment.

"Are you injured?" Aelthira asked. She was looking at Kaelene.

"My shoulder took a hit. It is not serious."

"Can you confirm that or are you estimating?"

"Estimating with reasonable confidence."

Aelthira accepted this with a small nod that meant she had filed it under things to revisit. She looked around the nave: scattered bones, the pale light, the dark at the altar end, the two doorway alcoves standing empty now.

"We should have checked the alcoves," she said.

"We should have," Kaelene agreed.

"The good news," Bram said, from the far end of the nave where he was leaning against the wall with rather more weight than he would normally admit to, "is that the church appears to be otherwise empty."

"Appears to be," Aelthira said, with precise emphasis.

"Yes." He straightened, with some effort. "Appears to be. Which means we should probably have a look at that altar end before any of us have a rest."

Kaelene rolled her shoulder experimentally. It objected in the expected ways and no unexpected ones. Manageable.

She walked toward the altar end of the nave and the other two followed, their footsteps loud on the stone, the draught from the ruined wall moving against their faces, cold and steady and carrying the faint smell of the world outside: grass and sky and open air, all the things the church had been without for a hundred years.

## Chapter Eight: The Mark on the Wall

The altar end of the nave was empty.

No skeletons, no movement, nothing that had not been there for a hundred years. The altar itself was a long stone table, plain and heavy, its surface bare except for a century of accumulated dust and a single brass candlestick lying on its side. The stone floor around it was uneven where the collapse of the north wall had sent tremors through the foundations, and a long crack ran from the base of the altar to the far wall, wide enough to put a finger in.

Bram lowered himself onto the second altar step with the careful deliberateness of a large man whose knees had opinions about the morning's work. He rested his shield against the stone beside him and set his sword across his lap and did not say anything, which was, in its way, its own kind of loudness.

Aelthira stood in the middle of the nave and held her wand up at eye level, turning it slowly between two fingers in the thin light from the ruined wall. Her expression was the focused, slightly dissatisfied one she wore when something was not performing to specification. Kaelene had seen it often enough to know it did not mean anything was wrong, exactly. It meant Aelthira was running numbers.

Kaelene walked the perimeter.

It was a habit Tarek had given her, years ago: after a fight, before you rest, walk the edges. Not looking for anything in particular. Looking for anything at all. She had done it in the aftermath of enough difficult rooms that it had become reflex, the same way checking exits was reflex and keeping her back to the wall in unfamiliar taverns was reflex. The things he had taught her had settled so deep they no longer felt like lessons. They felt like the way you moved through the world.

She went along the south wall first, stepping over the fallen bones of the skeleton she had taken apart near the pew stumps, working her way from the entrance end toward the altar. The wall here was plain dressed stone, the mortar between the courses gone soft and crumbling with age, a few places where the stonework had bowed inward under the weight of decades. Nothing carved. Nothing significant. A row of iron hooks set high up, two still holding the corroded remnants of something that might once have been a lamp chain.

She turned at the altar end and began along the north wall, which was a shorter walk because of the hole. The collapse had taken a full third of it: a rough-edged absence that opened onto the grey sky and the weeds and the broken stone rubble the village had been looking at for a century. She stepped around the edge of the fallen masonry and kept going.

Past the altar. Behind it.

She almost missed it.

The wall behind the altar was darker than the rest, sheltered from the light that came through the hole and accumulating shadow the way north-facing stone did. A hundred years of grime had settled into every irregularity in the surface and made the whole wall look uniformly grey, uniformly old, uniformly featureless. And in the centre of it, at about chest height, a circular carved emblem that the grime had filled in so thoroughly that at the wrong angle it disappeared entirely into the surrounding stone.

Kaelene stopped.

She leaned closer. Tilted her head. Let the light from the ruined wall catch the surface at an angle rather than straight on.

A sunburst. Eight rays, evenly spaced, carved with care. And in the centre of the sunburst, a sword pointing upward, its crossguard level with the inner ring of the circle.

She knew this emblem. She had last seen it in a book that Tarek had kept in the bottom of his pack, a battered thing with half the spine missing whose cover he had replaced twice with pieces of saddlecloth. He had shown her the page it was on when she was still new enough to adventuring to think that knowing things was the same as knowing what to do with them. The Brotherhood of the Eternally Shining Star, he had said. Old order. Very old. You find their mark in places where someone thought something was worth protecting.

She had not thought about that book in a long time.

She reached out and set her fingers on the emblem, feeling the carved lines under her fingertips, the soft grit of old stone. Then, without quite deciding to, she pressed and turned it clockwise. It offered the resistance of something that had not moved in a very long time, and then it moved.

The sound it made filled the church.

Not a grinding, exactly, more the deep voiced protest of stone moving against stone in a mechanism that had been waiting a hundred years for someone to find it, every surface complaining at once. The altar shuddered. Bram was on his feet before the sound had finished, sword in hand, scanning for a threat that wasn't there. Aelthira had her wand levelled at nothing in particular and was looking at everything with rapid precision.

The altar moved sideways.

It went slowly, very slowly, driven by some counterweight system buried in the floor beneath it, the stone base scraping across the flagstones in a straight line with the inexorable patience of machinery that did not know it had been waiting and did not care. It revealed, as it moved, first a darkness and then the top of a stone step and then another, and by the time it had reached the end of its travel there was an opening in the floor the width of a man's shoulders, and a staircase descending into complete black.

The three of them stood around the opening and looked down into it.

Cold air rose from below. Staler even than the church, untouched since the mechanism had last been used, carrying the mineral stillness of deep stone and sealed dark.

"Tarek," Kaelene said, quietly.

Bram glanced at her. He did not ask. He understood well enough what she meant: that she had not found this alone, not entirely.

"Nicely done," he said, just as quietly.

Aelthira had already crouched at the edge of the opening, studying the mechanism where it disappeared into the floor. "The counterweight channel runs under the foundation," she said. "This was built at the same time as the church, or very close to it. Whoever designed this intended it to last." She looked up. "It is good work."

From her that was close to reverence.

They stood there a moment longer: the cold breath from below, the pale light from the ruined wall above, the silence of the church settling back around them now that the great groaning protest of the mechanism had finished. Somewhere under their feet, in the dark at the bottom of those stairs, the knight's grave was waiting.

Bram pulled a torch from his pack, struck a light, and held it out over the opening.

## Chapter Nine: Descending

Bram went first.

He did not announce this or look to see if anyone objected. He simply turned to the opening, set his torch out ahead of him, and started down. The staircase was narrow enough that his armoured shoulders nearly brushed both walls at once, and he descended at a measured pace, one step at a time, his torch held low to light the treads rather than the ceiling, and he did not say a word.

Kaelene went second and Aelthira followed. The walls pressed close on either side, cold dressed stone with no decoration and no mortar gaps large enough to worry about. The stairs were steep and the treads shallow, built for access rather than comfort. Whoever had designed this entrance had wanted it functional and discrete and nothing else.

The torch lit perhaps eight steps ahead and left the rest in dark, and the dark below had a quality to it that was different from the dark at the top of the stairs. Thicker, almost. Older. The kind of dark that accumulated over a very long time in a space that nothing had entered and nothing had left.

Fourteen steps, Kaelene counted. Then the walls fell away.

The crypt was not what any of them had been expecting.

It was low-vaulted and wide, wider than the nave above, its ceiling a series of shallow stone arches running the length of the chamber in pairs. And across that ceiling, over every arch and into every angle between them, something lived. A lichen, pale blue-green and faintly luminous, had spent a century colonising every surface the stone would give it purchase on, and what it had made of the ceiling was a map of cold light: not bright enough to read by, not bright enough to cast shadows, but enough to see by, enough to understand the shape of the space, enough to stop Bram on the last step with his torch held out to one side so it would not drown the glow.

All three of them stood at the base of the stairs and looked up.

The light shifted slightly as the air moved, or seemed to, the luminescence deepening in some patches and softening in others in a way that was either the lichen responding to the disturbance of their arrival or a trick of the eye. Either way it moved like breathing. The whole ceiling moved like breathing.

"A hundred years," Bram said, almost to himself. He was not looking at anything in particular. He was just looking.

Aelthira stepped off the last stair and moved to the nearest wall, tilting her head to examine the lichen where it met the stone. She brought her face very close to it without touching it.

"Foxfire family," she said. "Possibly a subspecies I have not catalogued. The luminescence in this genus is typically a by-product of metabolic activity, but the consistency here is unusual. Most foxfire colonies pulse. This one does not." She straightened. "It may have adapted to the environment over the course of the century. Stable light source, no seasonal variation, no predation. It has had no reason to change."

"It is beautiful," Kaelene said.

Aelthira considered this as if it were a separate piece of data, distinct from but not incompatible with her own assessment. "Yes," she said. "It is."

Bram said nothing. He extinguished his torch against the floor and the crypt settled into the lichen's glow alone, and it was enough. More than enough. The vaulted ceiling floated above them in its cold blue-green light and the stone around them held the quiet the way good stone held everything: completely, patiently, without comment.

Kaelene let herself have it for a moment. The morning had given them a great deal that was not this, and this was worth pausing for.

Then she turned her attention to what lay ahead.

Two corridors opened at the far end of the crypt, one to the left and one to the right, their entrances framed by the same low stone arches as the ceiling. Both were dark, the lichen thinning toward them and ending a few metres inside each opening, and both were quiet. The crypt itself was quiet, the stillness of a space that had been sealed for a century, and against that stillness any sound would carry clearly.

Kaelene stood and listened.

Not for long. Ten seconds, perhaps fifteen. Long enough.

"Left," she said.

Neither Bram nor Aelthira asked how she knew. Bram relit the torch from the tinderbox at his belt, the small scrape and spark of it loud in the hush, and held it up. The flame settled and steadied and threw its warm light forward into the left corridor and the shadows it made were the shadows of stone walls and a stone floor and nothing else.

Nothing else yet.

He stepped forward and they followed him in, and the lichen's glow faded behind them as they went, and then there was only the torchlight and the dark ahead and the sound of their footsteps careful on the stone.

## Chapter Ten: The Last Two

The corridor was long and low, the ceiling close enough that Bram moved with his head slightly forward in the way of tall people navigating spaces built for someone shorter. The torchlight reached perhaps three metres ahead of them, enough to read the floor and the walls but not enough to see the end. The stone here was older than the church above, or felt older, the dressing rougher, the courses less even, the work of builders who had been more concerned with permanence than with finish.

They moved slowly. Not from caution, exactly, though caution was part of it. More because the passage demanded it: the floor was uneven underfoot, the stone worn smooth in the centre by centuries of foot traffic that had stopped a hundred years ago, and there were places where the ceiling dipped further and required attention. Bram navigated these without comment. The torch flame leaned back as he walked, steadied, leaned again.

Kaelene was listening. The corridor had its own acoustic, the way enclosed underground spaces did, and sound moved through it differently than it moved through open air: flattened, directional, stripped of everything soft. Whatever was ahead of them, if it was moving, she would hear it before she saw it.

She heard it at perhaps ten metres.

Two sets of footsteps, steady and mechanical, the dry hollow sound of bone on stone. Moving away from them. A patrol route, she thought, the same route walked so many times over a century that the pattern of it had become the whole purpose. Advance to the end of the passage. Turn. Return. Repeat.

They had reached the turn and were coming back.

"Two," she said, barely above a breath. "Ten metres. Coming toward us."

Bram stopped.

He did not reach for his shield. He did not set his feet in a fighting stance or angle himself for impact. He simply stood in the corridor with the torch in his left hand and his sword in his right and waited, and the particular quality of his stillness was different from the stillness of the crypt above: that had been a wonder. This was readiness, and the two felt nothing alike.

The footsteps came closer. Steady. Unhurried. A hundred years of this same corridor, this same route, this same turning in the dark. Whatever had once animated these skeletons with purpose had long since been reduced to pure repetition, a habit without a habitkeeper, a patrol without a cause.

Six metres.

Four.

The torchlight found them: two skeletons side by side in the narrow passage, spears held upright in the carry position, their empty eyesockets registering the light a half second before their programming registered the threat. Kaelene watched that half second. It was enough.

Bram stepped forward and his sword swung once.

It was not a dramatic stroke. It was not meant to be. It was the swing of a man who had spent six years learning exactly how much force a particular kind of target required and had stopped using any more than that a long time ago. The skull left the spine with a sound like a latch being thrown and bounced once off the corridor wall and came to rest against the base of the stone, and the body stood for the single moment it took the remaining animation to understand what had happened, and then it sat down in sections.

The second skeleton had its spear coming down when Aelthira raised her wand.

The jet of fire that left it was narrow and precise, the width of the corridor but no wider, roiling forward in the confined space with a sound like a drawn breath reversed. It hit the skeleton squarely in the chest and the fire did what fire did to things that had been dry for a hundred years: it took them completely and immediately, and what dropped to the floor of the corridor a moment later was not so much scattered as reduced, a clatter of scorched and blackened remnants that rolled a little way and stopped.

The smell of it hung in the passage: char and old bone and the clean bitter edge of spent magic.

Silence.

Bram lowered his sword. He was not breathing hard. He looked at the remains of the two skeletons in the corridor with the dispassionate attention of a man tallying, confirming, moving on.

"That's the last of them," Kaelene said.

She did not say it as a question and Aelthira did not treat it as one. Aelthira was still for a moment, her wand held loosely at her side, her attention turned inward in the way she had when she was reading something that was not visible. "Yes," she said. "There is no active binding remaining anywhere in the crypt. Nothing in the church above either. Whatever he set in motion a hundred years ago, it is finished."

Aelthira lowered her wand. The smell of scorched bone was already thinning in the corridor’s cold air, displaced by the older mineral stillness that had been here long before them and would be here long after. She looked at the remains for a moment with the expression she wore when something had gone exactly as expected and she was deciding whether that was satisfying or merely correct.

The crypt belonged to them now. All of it: the corridor and the chamber above with its breathing lichen ceiling and somewhere at the end of the passage ahead, behind whatever door waited there, the grave of a knight who had come when he was needed and asked for nothing after.

Bram looked down the corridor past the remains, toward the dark at its end. Then he looked back at the other two, and something in his face was quieter than usual. Not solemn, not quite. More like the expression of a person who understood, without needing to say so, that what came next was a different kind of thing from what had come before.

"Right," he said, and held the torch up. Then they walked on.

He held the torch up and they walked on.

## Chapter Eleven

The Knight's Grave

Father Aldric was waiting at the end of the corridor.

Not as he had been on the road: not the pale urgent figure with his hands open and his account ready. He was standing with his back to them, facing the door at the passage's end, and when they came close enough to see him clearly Kaelene noticed that something about him was different. The flickering agitation that had been in him on the road, the quality of a candle in a draught, was stilled. He was not calm, exactly. He was something older than calm. He was a man who had been waiting for a particular moment for a very long time and could feel it arriving.

He turned when he heard their footsteps.

"You came," he said. And then, as if he were still surprised by it: "You came all the way."

"Did you doubt us?" Bram asked.

The ghost considered this with the seriousness it deserved. "I hoped," he said. "I have learned not to assume that hoping and receiving are the same thing."

He turned back to the door.

It was heavy oak, dark with age, bound with iron straps that had rusted to the colour of old blood. There was no handle, no visible lock, no mechanism. It was simply a door set into the stone at the end of the passage, and when Father Aldric raised one translucent hand and pressed it flat against the wood, the door swung open without resistance, without sound, as though it had been waiting for exactly this and had decided not to make a fuss about it.

The ghost stood aside.

Bram looked at Kaelene. Kaelene looked at the open doorway. Then she stepped through, and the others followed.

The grave chamber was small.

This was the first thing she noticed, and it stayed with her: not a grand vault, not a cathedral space, not the kind of room that announced its own importance. A low ceiling, plain stone walls, a floor that had been laid with more care than the corridor outside but was still just stone. A single iron bracket on the far wall held the remnant of a torch long burned away. There were no carvings, no inscriptions, no decorations of any kind.

In the centre of the room stood the sarcophagus. It was stone, like everything else, its lid a single flat slab with no effigy and no epitaph. Someone had taken care with the edges, smoothed and squared them with patience, but there was no name cut into the stone. No name anywhere in the room.

To the left of the sarcophagus, on a low shelf built out from the wall at waist height, the knight's arms and armour had been arranged with the unhurried attention of people who understood they were doing something that would outlast them.

A shield, large and heavy, its face bearing the sunburst of the Brotherhood of the Eternally Shining Star in raised iron that had gone brown with age but held its shape completely. A longsword laid flat beside it, its blade clean, its crossguard simple, its grip wrapped in leather that had dried and cracked over two centuries but still showed, in the worn patches at the hilt and at the balance point, where the hand had rested most. A suit of plate beside that: not displayed on a stand but laid out in order, piece by piece, the way you would lay out a person's clothes if you were doing it with care. The gorget. The pauldrons. The breastplate. The gauntlets set at the end with the fingers together as if folded.

None of it gleamed. All of it commanded.

"The necromancer never reached this room," Father Aldric said from the doorway. His voice was very quiet. "He died in the church above, buried with me in the collapse. The skeletons he sent ahead of him could not enter. This chamber is consecrated ground. Things made the way they were made cannot cross that threshold. I have always believed the builders knew exactly what they were doing."

No one responded to this. There did not seem to be anything to say.

Kaelene stood at the foot of the sarcophagus and looked at the armour on the shelf. The grip of the sword with its worn patches at hilt and balance. Two centuries old and you could still see exactly where the hand had been. She thought about what that meant: that a person had held this object so many times, in so many different situations, that the leather had learned the shape of them. That the sword remembered the hand even when the hand was gone.

She thought about a battered book with its spine replaced twice in saddlecloth.

"Show reverence," Father Aldric said. Not as an instruction. As a simple statement of what the room required, offered to people he trusted to understand it.

Bram had not moved from the doorway. He was standing with one hand on the frame and his torch held out to one side so its light fell into the room without him needing to step further into it, and he was looking at the sarcophagus with an expression Kaelene had not seen on him before. Not solemn. Not performative. Something quieter and more private than either of those things, the expression of a man confronted with something that had reached past the part of him that usually had something to say and found the part that did not.

He said nothing. He stayed in the doorway and he said nothing and that was its own kind of presence.

Kaelene bowed her head.

She was not a ceremonial person. She did not attend services or observe feast days or keep the small rituals that other people kept to mark the turning of things. But she knew what this was. A person had come to a place where they were needed and had given everything they had and had not asked for anything in return, not even a name on a stone. The least you could do, the very least, was to be still for a moment and let that be what it was.

She held the bow until it felt complete. Then she straightened.

At the shelf, Aelthira stood with her hands clasped in front of her, her wand at her belt, her eyes moving slowly over the arranged armour with the attention she gave to things she was trying to understand fully. She did not touch anything. She did not speak. For Aelthira, who was almost always either speaking or visibly preparing to speak, the silence was its own complete statement.

Nobody reached for the sword.

Nobody picked up the shield.

These things belonged here. That was not a thought any of them had needed to arrive at through consideration. It was simply the first and only thought, self-evident in the way that very few things were self-evident, the way that certain facts about the world presented themselves so plainly that reasoning your way to them felt beside the point.

He had come. He had done what needed to be done. He had asked for nothing.

The room held him. That was enough. That was the whole of it.

Father Aldric made a small sound from the doorway, not quite a word. When Kaelene looked at him his outline was softer than it had been in the corridor, his edges gentler, the blue-white translucence of him shading toward something almost warm. He was looking at the sarcophagus with the expression of a man who has, after a very long time, been allowed to put something down.

"Thank you," he said. To the room, perhaps. To all of them. To the knight in the stone. It was not entirely clear, and Kaelene thought the ambiguity was probably right.

Bram cleared his throat once, very quietly, which was the closest he was going to come to acknowledging that anything had affected him, and pushed off the doorframe.

"We should go back up," he said. His voice was steady and normal and it cost him something to make that way. "Give him some quiet."

## Chapter Twelve

The Village Is Free

The stairs felt shorter going up.

They always did, Kaelene had noticed over the years. The descent into uncertain places had a weight to it that the ascent did not, and once the uncertain thing had been resolved the same distance felt different under your feet, as though the stone itself had relaxed. She did not think this was literally true. She thought it anyway.

Bram came through the altar opening first, hauling himself up into the nave with a grunt that he attempted to make sound effortless and did not quite manage. Kaelene followed. Aelthira last, stepping through neatly and pausing to look at the mechanism where the altar had settled at the end of its travel, as if she were considering whether to examine it more closely. She set her hand to the carved emblem behind the altar and turned it back. The altar moved with the same patient weight as before, scraping slowly across the flagstones until the opening was gone and the floor was only floor again. Then she looked at the rectangle of pale sky visible through the ruined wall and decided against any further examination.

The church was as they had left it. Scattered bones, grey light, the cold draught moving through the nave. The remains of the alcove skeletons still lay in their doorways. Nothing had changed while they were below, and nothing was going to change now: the church's work was done and it knew it, or seemed to, in the way that spaces sometimes seemed to know things.

They walked the length of the nave without speaking and out through the main doors into the morning.

The light had changed.

When they had entered the village the sky had been pale and flat, the kind of overcast that promised nothing in either direction. Now the cloud cover had broken to the east, and through the gap a full morning sun was throwing long warm bars of light across the ruined village, the kind of light that made even broken things look considered. It lay across the cracked cobblestones and the roofless walls and the stone surround of the well and the scattered bones of the skeletons they had dealt with in the street, and it made all of it look simply like what it was: an old place, unlived-in, waiting.

Father Aldric stood at the threshold of the collapsed north wall.

He was more visible than Kaelene had ever seen him, which she spent a moment trying to account for before she understood: it was not that he was more solid. It was that the light had found him. He had spent a hundred years on a shadowed road and now the morning sun was falling on him and he was standing in it with the stillness of someone who had not expected this particular thing and was allowing himself to receive it.

He looked at the three of them as they came out of the church and crossed the cobblestones toward him, and his expression was the one she had last seen in the grave chamber, the one that had no clean name: relief and grief and gratitude and something underneath all of that which was simply the face of a person being released from a very long obligation.

"It is done," he said.

"It is," Bram said.

The ghost was quiet for a moment. He looked out over the village, the sunlit ruins, the weeds growing up through the cobblestones, the walls with their moss and their creeping vegetation.

"People can come back here now," he said. "Rebuild it. There is good land around it and the church still stands on three walls, which is more than some start with. Someone will come eventually. They always do." He paused. "I would like to think there will be children running in this street again one day. It seems like the right kind of ending for it."

Nobody said anything to this. It seemed like the right kind of ending too.

Then Father Aldric looked at each of them in turn, and what he did next none of them had anticipated.

"Bram Kestrel," he said. "Kaelene Fenholt. Aelthira Moonveil."

Their names, said plainly and with the weight of someone who had taken care to learn them and was now choosing to use them. Not a formula. Not a courtesy. Something deliberate, the act of a man who understood that names were a way of saying: I see you. I know who you are. What you did here was done by you specifically, and I will not pretend otherwise.

Bram's expression shifted in a way he did not manage to conceal before it had already happened. He covered it by looking at the sky, which was the oldest trick there was and which Kaelene had seen him use before, in moments when something had reached him more directly than he had prepared for.

Aelthira was still. Not the clinical stillness of observation but something softer and more private, her eyes on Father Aldric's face with an attention that was entirely undisguised.

Kaelene found she had nothing to say and did not try to find anything.

"Thank you," the ghost said. "All of you. For stopping. For listening. For coming all the way."

He did not say anything after that.

He was simply gone.

No fading, no final gesture, no last words. One moment he was standing in the morning light in the gap in the north wall, and the next moment the gap in the north wall was empty, and the light fell through it onto the cobblestones below as if nothing had ever interrupted it. The absence was total and it was peaceful in the way that completed things were peaceful, the way a door closed gently was peaceful, the way a held breath finally let go was peaceful.

The three of them stood in the sunlit street and let it be what it was.

Bram was the first to move. He rolled his shoulders, resettled his shield, and looked at the road back through the trees with the expression of a man doing an inventory and finding everything present.

"Well," he said. "That was a Tuesday."

It was not quite a joke. It was the shape of one, the rhythm and the delivery, but underneath it was something else: an acknowledgement, offered sideways and without ceremony, that the morning had been what it had been and that all three of them had been in it together and that this was not nothing. From Bram, who had a joke for everything and meant most of them less than they sounded, this one meant more than it sounded, and Kaelene thought he knew she knew it.

"It was a Monday," Aelthira said.

She said it completely straight, her eyes already on the path through the trees, and said nothing further, and the nothing further was its own kind of warmth.

They walked back through the village together. Past the well, past the ruined buildings with their moss-covered walls, past the scattered remains in the street that the sun was now falling on without distinction, bone and cobblestone and old mortar all receiving the same light with the same patience. The village was quiet, but it was a different quiet now. Not the held-breath quiet of a place waiting for something. Just the quiet of an empty place on a Tuesday morning in the sun.

Or a Monday. Depending.

They passed through the gap where the gate had been and back onto the path through the trees and then out onto the road, the old road with its worn stones and its stone walls and its fields going slowly to seed on either side. The road ran in both directions and there was nothing on it in either direction, which was how they had found it and how it was supposed to be.

Kaelene looked back once.

Through the gap in the treeline she could see the upper edge of the church's remaining walls, the solid grey stone of it against the sky, and above the collapsed north side the open air where the wall had come down a hundred years ago and a priest had said the words rather forcefully. The sun was on it. Just the sun, and the sky, and the old stone doing what old stone did: standing.

She turned back to the road.

Bram was already talking, something about breakfast and the distance to the next village and his considered professional opinion that the universe owed them something hot after a morning like that. Aelthira was listening with the expression she wore when she was not listening but was comfortable enough with the sound to let it continue. She had taken out her wand and was examining the tip of it, a brief and focused assessment, and then she put it away again with the small, private expression of someone adding an item to a list. She would need to have it looked at before the next job. A minor thing. But worth attending to.

Kaelene fell in half a step behind them both and let the road come up to meet her feet, and behind them the sun shone on the ruins of the village, and the ruins were only ruins now, and nothing else, and that was exactly enough.
