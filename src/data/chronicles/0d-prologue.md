## Chapter One: The Argument with the Dark

The torch was losing the argument with the dark.

Kaelene watched it from the corner of her eye as she moved, the flame bending sideways in a draft that had no business being down here, this deep, this far from anything that moved or breathed on its own. The light held. It always held, right up until it didn’t, and that was the part she tried not to think about.

Ahead of her, Tarek walked without hesitation. He had that quality, the forward motion that made you believe the ground was solid even when you hadn’t checked. She had been watching him walk like that for three years and still wasn’t sure whether it was confidence or a very convincing performance of it. Either way, it worked. She kept moving.

The tomb breathed cold. That was the only word for it. Not just cool, the cool of deep places far enough from the sun. This was older. It pressed against the skin and made the body remember it was small. The stone around them was pale grey shot through with dark veins, carved once, long ago, though whatever had been etched there had worn past meaning. Shapes that might have been figures. Patterns that might have been language. Now they were only the suggestion of intention, and even that felt like more than the place deserved.

Jorpagna had not always been ruins.

That was what people forgot, or preferred not to remember. A thousand years ago, before the Third Conflux turned everything north of the Varkesh Sea into a place where rational people declined to live, this had been the centre of something. An empire built on copper and salt and the kind of organisational brutality that makes empires possible. The city had been enormous. The surviving records, fragments mostly, gave population numbers that made historians sceptical even now. People had been born here. They had argued about the price of bread, fallen in love, buried their dead.

The large insects called Fleshbiters came during the Third Conflux in numbers that defied accounting. Not an army. A consumption. Jorpagna was not conquered or burned or starved into surrender. It was eaten. The people and the animals. The timber, the cloth, the leather. What remained was stone, and metal too stubborn to interest them, and the deep places the swarms had not thought to look, or had not needed to.

The ruins were not romantic. Kaelene had made that mistake the first time Tarek brought her somewhere like this, expecting beauty in the decay, the haunted grandeur you got in old stories. What you got instead was the residue of catastrophe, preserved in the absence of everything that had once made it matter. Ruins were not beautiful. They were what was left when everything beautiful had been taken.

She ducked beneath a lintel that had dropped three inches on the left, settling over centuries into a slow diagonal lean. Tarek ducked without breaking stride, taller than her by half a head, cataloguing every low ceiling in the place before his body needed to know about it. He was meticulous like that. It was one of the things she had resented at the start, at sixteen, certain that instinct beat preparation. It was one of the things she had come to lean on since.

“The chamber should be just ahead,” he said. His voice was low, not quite a whisper. Down here whispers felt wrong, as if they implied something was listening. A normal speaking voice kept the world ordinary. “Another fifty feet, maybe sixty.”

“What are we expecting?”

“Funerary goods. Records, if the preservation held. Maybe a sealed inner chamber, though the outer walls don’t suggest anything elaborate enough to warrant one.” He paused. “Nothing we haven’t managed before.”

She noted the pause. She had learned to note his pauses. “But.”

“But this place mattered to people who believed in protecting what mattered to them.” He glanced back, his face all shadow and angle in the torchlight. “Keep your hand close.”

She already had. The hilt of her short blade was warm from her grip, which meant she had been holding it longer than she realised. That happened down here. The body knew things before the mind had decided them.

The corridor narrowed, then opened again, the way old construction did when it was built for purpose and not for looks. Functional. Severe. The floor was cleaner than it had any right to be, and that was what finally set the back of her neck prickling. No debris. No centuries of dust and fall. The stones were bare.

Something had cleared them.

She opened her mouth to say it.

Then, from beneath her feet, beneath the floor, beneath the foundation, from somewhere that should not have held anything capable of sound, she heard it.

Stone. Moving against stone. Slow and deliberate, like a door finding its way open after a very long time.

Not a collapse. Not the settling of old weight. Something with purpose behind it.

Tarek stopped walking.

The sound lasted three seconds, maybe four. Then it stopped. The silence afterward was a different silence than before, heavier, more aware of itself, and Kaelene stood in it with her hand around her hilt and the torch burning steady overhead and understood, without being able to say why, that something had changed.

The tomb had noticed them.

## Chapter Two: Three Was Enough

Neither of them moved.

The sound was already gone. Four seconds, maybe less, then nothing, only the ordinary quiet of underground places, the quiet that pressed in from all sides and made you aware of how much stone there was in the world and how little of it you were. Kaelene stood with her hand on her hilt and waited to feel sure the sound had been real.

She did not feel sure.

“The foundations,” Tarek said. Measured. He said it the way you said a thing when you had already weighed and dropped the other explanations and wanted the one left standing to sound more solid than it felt. “Old stone settling.”

“That wasn’t settling.”

“No.” He did not start walking again. “It wasn’t.”

He was quiet a moment. She watched him look up the corridor, then back the way they had come, giving it the attention he saved for decisions that mattered. The floor too clean. The tremor that was not settling. The draft that had changed direction. Three reference points. Three was enough.

“We’re going back,” he said.

She did not argue. She did not feel relief either, exactly. She felt the shape of the decision, its weight and its rightness, and she turned because he was already turning, and they started back the way they had come.

## Chapter Three: Run

She heard it before she understood what she was hearing.

Not from ahead. From behind. From the chamber they had never reached, from the dark at the corridor’s end, from whatever had waited down there in the patient stillness of something that did not have to work at being patient.

There were no footsteps. Nothing so ordinary. The air simply changed, the same reversal as the draft, that same wrongness of something natural running backward, and then the cold arrived. Not the cold of underground stone. A cold that moved with intention. Tarek stopped walking.

He turned to face back down the corridor.

She understood what that meant before he spoke. Three years of learning to read the decisions he made with his body before he made them with words, and this one was plain.

“Kaelene.” Her name in his voice, exactly as it always was. Calm. Deliberate. Pitched to carry without being loud. “Run.”

She ran.

She heard him behind her for four steps.

Then she didn’t.

## Chapter Four: The Grey Light

The corridor. Back through the corridor.

She knew the way. She had walked it twenty minutes ago with a torch in her hand and Tarek at her shoulder and all the time in the world. She ran it now with the torch somehow still lit, gripped so hard her fingers had gone numb, and no Tarek, and no time at all.

Behind her: sound. Not footsteps, nothing so ordinary. A displacement of air, a presence moving through the dark the way cold moves, filling the space ahead of itself, announcing nothing, arriving everywhere. She did not look back. Looking back meant slowing. Slowing meant stopping. She knew exactly what stopping meant.

Left. The corridor branched and she went left because left was what her body remembered, the lean of it, the shoulder that had brushed the wall. Her shoulder brushed it again now, harder, and she didn’t slow, let the impact ride down her arm and kept her feet under her.

The ceiling dropped. She felt it before she saw it, the air compressing, the dark overhead coming closer, and she ducked without stopping and the lintel scraped her back, scraped the top of her pack, and she was through and still moving, her heartbeat loud enough to fill the tunnel.

She was not a fighter. She knew that about herself, not as a failure but as a fact, the same way she knew she was better with languages than with a blade, and better with a blade than she would ever be at staying calm in tight spaces. Tarek had been teaching her. Had been. Past tense. She put that thought in a box, threw the box down a well, and kept running.

Left. Left again. The floor was rising, which meant up, which meant toward the entrance. She could not see the entrance. She could not see anything past the narrow reach of the torch, and the torch was guttering now, the flame flattened against the rush of her movement, more shadow than light.

It was closer.

She felt it as she had felt the draft, that reversal of something natural, the cold coming from behind now instead of below. It was not running. Whatever it did, it did not run. It closed the distance by some means that cost it no effort, and the closing came faster than her legs could answer.

Stairs. She caught the first step with her toe, caught herself on the wall, palm flat on cold stone, and went up two at a time, three at a time, the torch swinging wild. Steep and narrow, these stairs. On the way down she had complained about them, said something to Tarek about the architectural priorities of a people who built tombs you needed a ladder to reach.

He had laughed.

She hit the top step and there was the door, the outer door they had come through, still open the way they had left it, and beyond it: grey. The flat grey light of an overcast afternoon, the most beautiful thing she had ever seen.

She threw herself through it.

Cold outside air hit her face and she kept moving, down the broken steps, across the rubble, ten feet, twenty, before something stopped her. Not a sound. The absence of one. The cold presence behind her had gone quiet.

She turned.

The doorway was dark. Only dark. Stone and shadow and the mouth of a place she had just run out of, and nothing in it. The threshold sat in the grey afternoon light like a frame around an empty painting.

Nothing came through.

She stood there with the dead torch in her hand, smoke rising off the wick in a thin useless line, and breathed. In and out. Her legs shook. Her back, where the lintel had caught her, would be a spectacular bruise by morning. The grey light lay over the ruins and the wind moved through the broken stones without caring one way or the other, and the doorway held its dark and gave nothing back.

She was out.

Tarek was not.

## Chapter Five: Watching the Fire

One year later, the bear was twice the size it needed to be, and Bram was only getting started.

“Now at this point,” he said, leaning over the table with the gravity of a man whose story had reached its most important lie, “the bear is standing. On its hind legs. Which puts it, I want you to understand, at roughly the height of a sensible man’s ambitions and a foolish man’s doorframe. And Corvin, that is my former employer, a man of many virtues, none of them courage, Corvin is behind me going, Bram. Bram. I think we should reconsider the route.”

The table nearest the fire had abandoned its own conversations. Three merchants in travelworn coats and a woman whose hands marked her for a farrier had all turned quietly toward Bram’s voice, the way people turn toward warmth.

“And what did you do?” one of the merchants asked.

Bram spread his hands. “What any reasonable person would do. I told Corvin the bear was surely more frightened of us than we were of it.”

“And was it?”

“Not remotely. That bear had never been frightened of anything in its life, and it had opinions about people who suggested otherwise.” He picked up his cup. “We took the long way round. Added four days. Corvin billed the client for them, and I want it noted that I saw none of it.”

The table laughed. The farrier laughed and said something to the man beside her. Bram took all of it with the easy satisfaction of a craftsman whose work had landed, and sat back.

Across the table, Kaelene was watching the fire.

Not the room. Not the story. The fire in the hearth on the far wall, the way it kept finding new shapes in the same wood and losing them before they could be named. The tavern was warm and low-ceilinged and smelled of pine smoke and something cooking, and by any reasonable measure it was exactly where you wanted to be on a cold evening in early autumn. She had a full cup. She had a plate that had been full. The bench was solid under her, the company was good, and she was watching the fire the way people watch fires when the room can’t quite reach them.

“The caravan guard story again,” said Aelthira, at Kaelene’s left.

Her voice was low, pitched for the two of them only. She could speak in a crowded room without carrying: precise placement, least effort, most delivery.

“It gets longer every time,” Kaelene said.

“Last week the bear was merely large. Impressive, but mortal.” Aelthira turned her cup in a slow, considered circle. “He’s added at least two feet since Crestfall.”

“He adds more when the audience is bigger.”

“A story that earns its keep.” Aelthira glanced sideways at her, unhurried, the kind of look that gathered information without seeming to try. “You haven’t touched your cup in eleven minutes.”

“I’m pacing myself.”

“You’re somewhere else.”

Kaelene picked up her cup and drank. It was good. Dark and a little bitter, the kind of ale that rewarded attention. She set it down and looked at the fire again, because that was easier than looking at Aelthira, whose eyes missed nothing and asked after all of it.

“I’m here,” she said.

Aelthira said nothing. She was good at silences that did not feel empty.

At the head of the table Bram had taken a follow-up from the farrier and was deep in the defence of his claim that bears are, categorically, more dangerous in autumn than in spring. He had a theory. He always had a theory. It was probably wrong and certainly entertaining, and the farrier was laughing again, and the merchants had called for another round.

Kaelene watched him work and felt the thing she had been feeling most of the past year, the thing she had no clean word for. Gratitude, maybe. Or something next to it. Something that saw what Bram did in a room, how he filled it, how he made people feel the evening was going well and the company was good and there was no reason to look too hard at what waited outside the light.

He was very good at that. So good that sometimes she forgot to be grateful and simply let it work on her, let the warm room and his voice and the weight of a full cup be enough for one evening.

The fire shifted. A log settled, the shape it had held collapsing into a new one, and she lost the thread of whatever she had been thinking.

A year. She had been moving for a year, one foot after the other, one town after the next, new roads and new work and new people who did not know her face or her name or what had happened in the ruins to the woman she had been before. She had not healed. She knew the difference between healing and moving, and she was not confused about which she was doing.

But she was here. She was warm. Bram was mid-sentence about a tollgate and a goat, the room was laughing, and for this one hour of this one evening, that was enough.

She reached for her cup.

She did not look at the fire again.

## Chapter Six: The Right Turn

She is in the corridor.

She knows it is the dream because the corridor is too long. She walked it in twenty minutes; she has been running it far longer than that, long enough that her legs have gone from burning to numb to something with no word for it. The torch is already dead. She runs in the dark and knows the way, knows every turn, has been here before and before and before, and still the corridor keeps finding more of itself ahead of her and she keeps running into it.

The grinding starts.

Below her feet, the same sound. Stone on stone, slow and deliberate, but closer now, louder, the way sounds in dreams run louder than they should, as if the air here is thinner and carries more. She does not slow. Slowing is not something her legs will agree to.

Left. She goes left because her body remembers left. The wall is there where it should be, cold and close, the stone under her palm as real as anything she has ever touched, and she thinks, as she always thinks here, maybe this time. Maybe this is the time the corridor ends where it should and the stairs are where she left them and the door is open and the grey light is waiting on the other side.

The corridor turns right.

She does not remember a right. There was no right. She has walked this route in daylight and in memory and in this dream more times than she can count, and there has never been a right. But the wall curves, certain and solid, the way she needs to go is not the way on offer, and behind her the cold is already coming.

Tarek.

She stops, which she never does, which even as she does it she knows is the one thing she must not do. She stops and looks back down the corridor and he is there, at the far end of the dark, barely visible, barely a shape. Standing. He is standing, which means he is alive, which means she never had to run, which means.

“Run,” he says. Exactly as before. Calm, deliberate, pitched to carry.

She takes one step toward him.

The cold hits her back.

It is not a hand. It is not anything she can name. It is the end of warmth, total and at once, and it moves through her like water through stone, finding every crack and filling it, and she opens her mouth to scream and what comes out is not a scream, it is only air, her breath leaving her body in one long terrible exhale, and the dark closes from every side at once, and she knows she will not reach the stairs this time, will not reach the door, she will be here, in the corridor that does not end, with the cold and the grinding and the shape of Tarek at the far end saying run, run, run, and she cannot.

She cannot.

She woke sitting upright.

The room came back in pieces. Wood ceiling. Low rafters. The smell of the tavern, banked fire and old ale, the ordinary furniture of a place where nothing was buried and nothing had waited a thousand years. Outside the window the dark was the plain dark of a cloudy autumn night, no moon, no depth to it, only absence.

She was drenched, her shirt stuck to her back. Her heart hit her ribs with the single-minded urgency of something that had not been told the running was over, and she sat in the dark and pressed one hand flat to her sternum and breathed, deliberately, the way Tarek had taught her. In for four. Hold for four. Out for four.

He had called it a soldier’s trick. Something for when the body has decided danger is present and needs to be told otherwise. She had practised it on small things, high ledges, tight spaces, and thought she understood what it was for.

Her hand shook against her chest.

The room was warm. The room was safe. The corridor had never had a right turn in it, the cold was not here, and the dark past the window was only a cloudy night over a town whose name she had already half forgotten.

She breathed. In and out.

The shaking slowed. Her heart found a pace that was fast but no longer frantic. She sat in the dark with her hand on her chest and the sweat cooling on her skin and the dream going soft at its edges the way dreams do, imprecise now, losing the awful realness that had made it unbearable half a minute ago.

The cold, though. The moment it had found every crack.

That stayed.

## Chapter Seven: The Bad Nights

Bram was already awake.

Not sitting up, not alarmed, just awake, the way he was on the bad nights, present and quiet in the dark beside her. She never knew how he knew. She had asked him once and he had said he slept lightly, which was true and not the whole truth, and she had let it go, because some things are better received than examined.

“Hey,” he said. Just that.

“Hey.”

He did not ask what it was. He knew what it was. It was always the same thing, more or less, and he had learned early, or maybe always known, that asking someone to describe the inside of their worst memory at three in the morning is not a kindness. So he did not ask. He shifted closer, and she felt the warmth of him, solid and ordinary and entirely real, and some part of her that was still in the corridor understood, slowly, that she was not.

She let out a breath she had been holding since she woke.

“Bad one?” he said, after a while.

“The corridor had a right turn in it this time.”

A pause. He was thinking about it; she could tell. Bram thought carefully about the things she said in the quiet, even when he answered lightly. Especially then. “Seems unfair,” he said. “Changing the layout.”

“It is extremely unfair.”

“Someone should file a complaint.”

“I’ll make the right enquiries.”

He made a sound that was not quite a laugh, warm and low, and she felt it more than heard it. She had not laughed. She was not ready to, and he had not tried to get her there, only set the possibility down where she could reach it if she wanted. That was the thing about Bram, the real one, underneath the stories and the crowd work and the bear that grew two feet between towns. He knew where the edge was. He never pushed past it.

She settled back against him and looked at the ceiling. The low rafters. The ordinary dark. Her heart had come down to something reasonable. Her shirt was still damp at the back, but the room was warm and the warmth reached her now in a way the tavern fire had not quite managed earlier.

“I’m alright,” she said, because it was mostly true, and because she wanted it to be true, and because saying it to Bram was different from saying it to herself in the dark.

“I know,” he said.

Not: I know you will be. Not: it gets easier. Just: I know. Present tense. The version of her that existed right now, damp-shirted and shaking-handed and a little less afraid than five minutes ago. He knew that version. He had been here for most of her worst ones.

She closed her eyes.

Outside, the night was still cloudy and moonless, the town quiet, and the corridor with its wrong right turn was receding into the soft blur of a dream that had done its damage and was becoming only the memory of a dream, which was a thing she could carry.

She slept.

The fear did not leave. It settled, as it always settled, down into the low place where it lived between the bad nights. Patient. Waiting for the next corridor, the next wrong turn, the next time the cold would come at her back and she would understand all over again that the tomb was not finished with her.

But that was later. For now there was only the room, and Bram, and the slow return of her own breath.

For now, that was enough.

## Chapter Eight: Let Out

Kaelene slept with Bram’s arm across her and her breathing slow and the dream already soft at the edges. The cold did not go with it. That was the thing about the nightmares from Jorpagna: everything else faded as nightmares do, the images dissolving, the sounds going first, but the cold stayed. Specific. Patient. The cold of a place that had been underground a very long time and had learned to need nothing from the surface.

She shifted in her sleep. Bram’s arm tightened a little without waking. She did not feel the stillness that had settled over the room, the air gone careful, the candle on the sill no longer guttering but standing straight and very still, as if something nearby had stopped moving.

She had not escaped the tomb.

She had been let out.

The distinction had not yet become important to her. It would.
