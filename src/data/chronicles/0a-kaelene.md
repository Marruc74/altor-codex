## Chapter One: A Normal Morning

The fish market on Pellar Street opened at first light and smelled the way it always did: cold water and old salt and something underneath both of those things that no amount of wind off the channel ever quite carried away. Kaelene had stopped noticing it years ago. She noticed other things instead.

She noticed, for instance, the butcher's boy at the end of the second row, who had arranged his display of smoked sausages with the particular pride of someone left in charge for the first time. He kept straightening them. He kept glancing back toward the covered section where the main stall was, checking and reassuring himself that the person who had left him here had not yet returned to find fault. His attention moved in a predictable rhythm: the sausages, the crowd, the covered section, back again. She had timed it without meaning to. Eleven seconds, more or less, between each glance.

She noticed the couple arguing in low voices by the salt barrels, both of them too absorbed in each other to pay attention to anything else. She noticed the old woman with the handcart who stopped every thirty feet to shift her grip, blocking the flow of foot traffic in a way that made people move around her and in doing so briefly lose track of whatever they had been attending to. She noticed the dock workers moving through the far end of the market in a loose group, loud enough to pull eyes without meaning to. She noticed the gap between the rope coils near the harbour steps where a person could stand and be partially screened from the main walkway, and the narrower gap between two stacked crates that would serve better if things moved quickly.

She moved through the crowd the way water moves through a drain: finding the path of least resistance, adjusting without deciding to. The grey morning pressed down over everything. The sky was the colour of old pewter and the cobblestones were wet and the boats along the quay knocked softly against each other in the dark swell. Poane looked the same in the morning as it did in the afternoon, which was to say it looked like it always had and always would, indifferent to its own sameness. She had grown up inside that sameness and the city had never once suggested she was anything other than one more inconvenience moving through it.

She was sixteen and she had lived here her whole life and the city had never once noticed.

Poane was a working city. That was how people described it, the ones who described it with any pride, working, as if the word were sufficient justification for everything that was hard about it. There were larger cities in Klomellien, grander ones, cities with wide avenues and covered markets and the kind of architecture that suggested someone had once believed the place deserved to be beautiful. Poane had the harbour and the fish and the coal that came down from the northern mines, and it had the people who processed all three, and it had been doing this for long enough that it had stopped imagining anything different. She had grown up understanding, without anyone ever saying it directly, that a city like this did not produce people who left. It produced people who stayed and worked and were used up by the work and then were replaced by other people who did the same. She had never found a way to feel anything in particular about this. It was simply the shape of the world she had been handed.

Three exits from this section of the market. Left toward the tannery district: slow, the smell was worse than the fish market, but the narrow streets broke sight lines quickly and she knew three routes through that most people did not. Straight ahead through the covered arcade: faster but more people, more chances to be remembered by a face she would rather not remember her. Right and down the harbour steps to the quay: risky if the tide was up, clean if it was not. She checked the steps without appearing to look at them. The tide was low. Good.

She let herself drift toward the second row, moving the way people move when they have no particular purpose: a little slower than the crowd's main current, pausing occasionally to look at things she had no intention of buying. A bundle of dried herbs. A stack of salted fillets wrapped in grey cloth. She examined them the way a person examines things when the price might be negotiable and the day is young enough to bother. This was the part that people never understood about what she did, the ones who noticed her at all. They thought the skill was in the taking. It was not. The skill was in the waiting, in the quality of presence that rendered you unremarkable, in the patience that let you move only when the moment had already agreed to receive you.

The butcher's boy had straightened the sausages again. He was perhaps fourteen, with a round earnest face and a bruise along his jawline that she recognised as the kind delivered by someone who felt they had cause. He was working hard at being trustworthy, which meant his attention was split between performing the role he had been given and checking, constantly, that he was performing it correctly. She had known boys like him. She had been, in certain ways, a version of him, before she had understood that performing trustworthiness for people who had already decided not to trust you was a waste of energy.

The sausages hung from a hook rail at the front of the display, fat and dark-cased, smoked to the point where the skin had gone tight and glossy. She could smell them from where she was, even over the fish and the coal smoke and the salt. The smell hit the empty place in her stomach and the empty place responded with the particular insistence of something that has been patient for a very long time and is running out of patience.

She had not eaten since the day before yesterday. Not the sharp complaint of a missed meal but the quieter, more settled kind of hunger, the kind that had been there long enough to stop announcing itself and start simply being the condition of being awake. She knew it well. It was not dramatic. It was just true, the way the cold was true and the grey sky was true and the fact of being alone in a city that had never once looked at her directly was true.

She had tried, in the early days, to think of other ways. Work that did not require stealing. There were things she could do: shift work at the cannery, if they were taking on, which they were not always. Sweeping out taprooms. Running messages. She had done all of these things and they paid enough to eat on some days and not enough on others and they could all be ended at any moment by a shift foreman in a bad mood or a publican who decided she reminded him of someone he did not like. Stealing was worse in certain ways and better in one specific way: it was hers. The skill of it was hers, entirely, and nobody could take it away by deciding they did not feel like paying her today.

She had also tried, once, asking for work at the harbour authority offices, which had felt like the most humiliating thing she had ever done and had produced exactly the result she should have predicted. The man behind the desk had looked at her the way people looked at things they wanted to move out of the way without touching and told her they were not hiring. She had thanked him, because she had not yet learned that thanking people who did not deserve it cost her something she was not getting back.

The butcher's boy glanced back toward the covered section. She counted. Eleven seconds.

The dock workers were going to clear the end of the row in roughly four minutes. The couple by the salt barrels had reached the particular pitch of argument where both parties were too invested to pay attention to anything else. The old woman with the handcart had stopped again. The window existed. It would not stay.

She was already moving.

## Chapter Two: The Catch

She was almost fast enough.

Her fingers had closed around the sausage casing and she had already begun to shift her weight when the butcher's boy turned around. Not after eleven seconds. After seven. Something had changed his rhythm, a sound from the covered section or a flash of instinct she could not account for, and he turned and his eyes went immediately to her hand and the sausage it was holding and the small remaining distance between that sausage and the hook it had been hanging from.

He was fourteen and he had a bruise on his jaw and he shouted the way people shout when they are frightened and want not to be: loud, to fill the space before fear does.

What happened after that was quick and public and not unusual for Thalen. People turned. Someone used the word thief and it moved through the crowd the way such words do, passed along and amplified, each repetition stripping a little more of whatever complicated truth sat underneath it. By the time two men stepped forward Kaelene had already stopped being a sixteen-year-old girl who had not eaten since the day before yesterday and had become something simpler: a problem to be dealt with. Poane was good at simplifying things. It had a great deal of practice.

The cobblestones were cold and wet. That was the first thing she registered when she went down, the specific cold of wet stone against her cheek, and the second was the sound the crowd made as it closed around her, not angry exactly, more like satisfied, the sound of a situation resolving in the direction it had always been going to resolve. She pulled her arms over her head the way she had learned to and tucked her chin down and made herself as small as the situation allowed, which was not very small.

Nobody asked why.

That was the thing she had never quite gotten used to, even now. Not the pain itself, which was bad but familiar in the way that anything survived enough times becomes familiar. It was the absence of curiosity. The complete lack of interest in the question of how she had arrived at this particular moment on this particular morning. There was a thief on the ground and therefore there was a correct response to a thief on the ground, and the people around her were performing it with the clean conscience of people who believe they are doing what the situation requires. She understood, in an abstract way, that this was not personal. That made it worse, not better. Personal would have meant she existed to them as a person.

She kept her jaw set. She did not beg. Begging had never worked and she had a theory that it made things worse, gave people permission to feel something about you that was not contempt, and contempt at least kept a certain distance.

She went somewhere else while it was happening. She was good at this. She thought about the market in the abstract, about the dock workers and whether they had cleared the row yet, about the angle of the tide coming in and whether she had correctly estimated midday or whether the swell that morning suggested it would come earlier. She thought about a loose cobblestone she had noticed near the rope coils that she would need to remember if she ever used that gap as an exit route. She thought about anything that was not here, on the ground, in the cold, in the specific and impersonal unkindness of strangers who had decided she had earned this.

The butcher's boy had gone quiet. She could not see him from her position on the cobblestones but she had the sense, from the particular shape of the silence where his voice had been, that he was standing a little apart from the men who had stepped in. She could not tell what his expression was. She set it aside. It did not change anything about her current position.

One of the men said something about the sausage. She had dropped it when she went down; it was somewhere on the cobblestones near her, which struck her as faintly absurd, or would have if she had had the spare capacity to be struck by things. All of this for one sausage. Although that was not quite right either. It was not for the sausage. It was for the wanting of it. For being the kind of person whose wanting was visible in the first place.

She pressed her cheek against the stone and looked at the grey morning sky above the roofline and the flag on the chandler's shop three doors down, moving steadily in the channel wind. She breathed through her nose. She counted. It was the same thing she did when she was reading a market, or mapping an exit, or waiting for a window of attention to open. Counting was useful. It gave the mind somewhere to be that was not here.

She did not cry. Not yet.

It stopped, the way these things always eventually stopped, when the crowd's interest drifted toward whatever came next. Someone laughed at something unrelated. A vendor resumed his prices. The two men who had stepped in exchanged a few words with the butcher's boy, who said something in a low voice that she could not make out, and then they moved away. The crowd thinned. The market processed her and moved on.

She stayed where she was for a moment. The wet had soaked through her coat at the shoulder and her ribs ached in the specific way that meant they would be worse tomorrow. Her jaw ached from holding it. She took stock the way she always did after something like this: methodical, without sentiment, as information she would need.

The wrist where the merchant had grabbed her was stiff. She rotated it carefully and decided it was not serious. Her left knee had caught the cobblestone at a bad angle and would slow her down for a day or two, which was an inconvenience she could not afford but would have to manage. She was cold all the way through, the kind of cold that took an hour somewhere warm to shift, and she did not currently have access to anywhere warm. These were the facts of it. She arranged them in order of what needed to be addressed first and set about addressing them.

The sausage was still there. Three inches from her hand, lying on the cobblestones where she had dropped it, intact. She looked at it for a moment, this small stupid thing that had set the whole morning in motion, and felt something that was not quite amusement and not quite fury and sat somewhere between the two. Then she pushed herself up off the cobblestones.

That was when she heard the voice.

## Chapter Three: Tarek Steps In

The man was not large. That was the first thing she registered, still half-kneeling with her palm flat on the wet stone. He was not large and he was not young and there was nothing about him that should have made the two remaining men go still the way they did when he stepped into the space between them and her.

He did not raise his voice. He did not raise his hands. He simply arrived, and he looked at the two men with a quality of attention that seemed to rearrange the available options without naming any of them, and both of them found somewhere else to be. The butcher's boy retreated toward the covered section. The last few onlookers drifted away in the unhurried way that crowds do when the interesting part has concluded, and then it was quiet, which was strange, because the market was never quiet.

She became aware, in the sudden stillness, that she was shaking. Not from cold, or not only.

The man crouched down to her level. He did not reach for her. He did not speak immediately. He simply waited, with the particular patience of someone who has learned that waiting is usually more useful than whatever you were about to say, and looked at her face with an attention so direct and so entirely without judgment that she found she could not look away from it. He had an unremarkable face. That was perhaps the strangest thing about him. He looked like a man who had spent a long time outdoors and had arrived, through that process, at a kind of weathered evenness. Nothing about him explained why the crowd had listened to him.

She cried. She had not planned to and she did not do it gracefully. It came out of her in one short ugly wave and she pressed the back of her wrist against her mouth and brought it under control in a few seconds, but those seconds were real and he saw all of them and his expression did not change in any of the ways she had been braced for. Not pity. Not discomfort. Not the stepping-back that people performed when someone else's feelings became inconvenient.

He just watched her. Like she was worth watching.

"Why," she said. Her voice came out rougher than she intended. It was meant as a complete question and arrived as a single word and she did not have the energy to add to it.

He seemed to understand it anyway. "Because it was going to continue for a while yet," he said, "and you had already had enough of it."

It was such a plain answer that she did not know what to do with it. "I only took it to eat," she said, meaning the sausage and the whole morning and everything behind the morning. She felt the nakedness of it the moment it was out, how small it sounded after she had held it behind her teeth for so long. The truest things always sounded smaller than they were.

"I know," he said. And then, without any particular weight on it: "I did the same thing once."

She looked at him. He was not performing honesty. It was simply what he was saying, offered with the ease of a fact that had long since stopped being difficult. She found, reluctantly, that she believed him.

He reached into his coat and produced a small cloth-wrapped parcel, which he set on the cobblestones in front of her without comment. She looked at it. Then at him. He made no gesture suggesting she should or should not open it, so she did. Bread, hard cheese, two strips of dried meat. Real food, dense enough that you could feel it.

She ate without rushing, because she was not going to let a stranger watch her desperate. When she had finished she folded the cloth the way it had been and held it out to him. He shook his head once. She put it in her pocket.

He stood, unhurried, and she stood too because staying on the ground was worse than standing, even with her ribs making their objections known. The market noise continued around them from a distance, as if he had a radius of quiet that moved with him.

"You were working that market for the better part of an hour before you moved," he said. It was not an accusation. "I watched you."

She went very still. A different kind of still than his.

"Not the way you're thinking," he said, reading it with an accuracy that unsettled her. "The way you read the crowd. The couple by the salt barrels, the dock workers timing out, the gap by the harbour steps. You had the whole picture before you committed to a single step." He paused. "How long have you been doing that?"

She did not know how to answer because she had never thought of it as something she did. It was simply how mornings worked. "Always," she said.

He nodded, as though this was the answer he had expected. "It is a skill," he said. "Not instinct. Not a gift. Something you built, whether you knew you were building it or not. And you are putting it to considerably less than it is worth."

Nobody had ever called it anything before. The reading of rooms and crowds and patterns, the mapping of exits and windows of attention. She had thought of it, when she thought of it at all, as simply the way she survived. That someone might look at it and see something with applications beyond survival was a thought she had not previously had room for.

She kept her face still. "What do you want?"

"To offer you something," he said. "I work alone, most of the time. It has suited me. But I have been thinking that it need not always be the way, and that the right person, trained properly, would be worth more than the cost of the training." He looked at her steadily. "I can give structure to what you already do. Vocabulary. Application. The work is better than this and safer than this, though I won't call it safe, because that would be a lie and you would know it immediately." A pause. "What I teach you will be yours regardless of what else happens. That I can promise."

She was quiet for a moment. Around them the market continued its noise, vendor calls and cart wheels and the particular percussion of a busy morning, and it felt very far away. "What kind of work?" she said.

"Escort. Retrieval. Investigation, sometimes. People need things moved or found or understood, and they are willing to pay for someone who can read a room and make good decisions quickly." He said it matter-of-factly, the way someone describes a trade. "It is not glamorous. Some of it is dull and some of it is dangerous and it is not always possible to know in advance which is which. But it is honest work, in the sense that matters: you earn what you are paid and you know what you agreed to."

She studied him carefully. The unremarkable face, the quality of stillness, the way he had simply arrived between her and the men who had been hurting her and the men had left. She was very good at reading people and she could not find the angle on him. That was unusual enough to be interesting.

"Why would you do that for a stranger?"

He thought about it properly. Not performing consideration but actually giving the question the respect of his attention. "I suppose," he said, "someone once did the same for me. And it seemed fair to pass it along."

The wind off the channel moved through the market street and the flag on the chandler's shop snapped once and went still. Her ribs hurt. Her jaw hurt. She was cold in a way that had nothing to do with temperature.

She thought about the harbour steps. The tide came back in by midday and after that the quay was impassable on foot and there was nowhere to sleep that was not already spoken for and she had seven coppers to her name and both of her good hiding spots had been found and emptied in the past month and it was getting colder and was going to keep getting colder.

She thought about the butcher's boy and the eleven seconds she had counted and the seven seconds that had actually passed, and she thought about what it would mean to be wrong less often, or to have someone who would tell her plainly what she had done wrong and what she might do differently, instead of just letting the morning demonstrate it on her.

She thought about what he had said. What I teach you will be yours to keep.

"All right," she said.

Not thank you. Not relief. It was a better option than the alternative and she was smart enough to take it, and he received it as exactly that, without any performance of generosity, as a transaction between two people who both understood what it was.

"Tarek," he said.

"Kaelene."

He nodded once. "There is a place on Cutter's Lane that does a reasonable soup. We can talk there properly." He started walking. She fell into step beside him, which felt both natural and strange. She was not accustomed to walking beside anyone. "Tell me," he said, "how much of that market did you have mapped before you moved?"

She considered the question seriously. "All of it. The dock workers were going to clear the far end of the row in four minutes. The couple by the barrels were too absorbed to notice anything. There was a window."

"There was," he said. "What went wrong?"

"I miscounted his rhythm." She said it without apology. It was simply the fact.

"How will you avoid that next time?"

She thought about it properly, the way she would have thought about an exit. "Count twice before moving. Use a fixed reference point, not just the person. Something external they're responding to, not just their habit."

Tarek made a sound that was not quite approval and not quite agreement but sat in the vicinity of both. She would learn, over the coming months, that it was the sound he made when she had gotten something right.

Behind them the market went on, grey and indifferent and entirely unchanged. It did not notice her leave. She was already not thinking about it.

Cutter's Lane was a narrow street two blocks inland from the harbour, sandwiched between a ropemaker's workshop and a building that might have been a chandlery at one point and was now several things at once. The place Tarek meant was unmarked from the outside, which she noted. A door, a step, a window with its shutter half-closed against the morning. Inside it was warm in the way that old buildings are warm, not from any great fire but from the accumulated heat of many years of people in a small space. A woman behind the counter looked at Tarek with the recognition of someone who serves a regular, and at Kaelene with the practiced incuriosity of someone who has learned that Tarek occasionally arrives with company and that it is not her business what kind.

They sat. Soup came without being ordered, which meant he was more regular here than occasional. She wrapped her hands around the bowl and felt the warmth move through them and tried not to show how much it mattered.

"Tell me about the morning," he said. "From the beginning. Not the mistake. The whole thing."

She told him. She was precise about it because precision was the habit she was most accustomed to, the one that had served her best, and because she found, strangely, that speaking it aloud to someone who was listening as carefully as he was listening made it feel less like a failure and more like a lesson that had been delivered somewhat forcefully. He did not interrupt. When she finished he was quiet for a moment, and she ate soup, and the warmth of the room settled around her.

"You read it correctly," he said at last. "All of it. The error was in the execution, not the reading."

She had known this. It was different to hear it said.

"That distinction matters," he said, as if he had seen her reaction to it. "Most people conflate the two. They make an error and they decide the whole approach was wrong, when what was actually wrong was a single piece of information. You counted his rhythm. The rhythm changed. That is a problem with what you were given to work with, not with how you worked with it." He looked at her. "Do you understand the difference?"

She did. She was not sure she had the words for it yet but she understood the shape of it. "The way I read the market was right," she said carefully. "I just had one number wrong."

"Yes." He picked up his spoon. "We will work on that. On building in margins. On not depending on a single count when you could use three reference points and be certain." He said it with the ease of someone describing a task that is large but entirely manageable, and she found, to her surprise, that she believed him about that too.

She looked at him across the table in the warm, unmarked room on Cutter's Lane and thought: this is either the best decision I have ever made or a very different kind of mistake. Then she thought: there is only one way to find out. And she had always been good at taking the available option.
