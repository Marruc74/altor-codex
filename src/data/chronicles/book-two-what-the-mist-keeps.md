## Story So Far...

Kaelene Fenholt grew up alone on the streets of Poane, surviving through careful observation and quick hands. After a failed theft, she was rescued by Tarek, a seasoned adventurer who recognized her remarkable ability to read people and situations. Under his guidance, she learned to turn the instincts that had kept her alive into the skills that would carry her far beyond the streets where she was raised.

Far to the south, Bram Kestrel left behind his family's fishing business in search of a life beyond the horizon. Joining a merchant caravan, he discovered that the road offered hardship, danger, and purpose in equal measure.

Far to the east, the elf Aelthira Moonveil devoted years to studying history and ancient magic in one of the great human cities. There she uncovered truths that powerful people wanted buried. Refusing to remain silent, she left the academy behind and set out with knowledge that others would do anything to suppress.

Tarek's final expedition led him and Kaelene into an ancient tomb in search of an ancient artifact. The discovery came at a terrible cost. Tarek gave his life so Kaelene could escape, leaving her with a burden she did not yet understand.

In the months that followed, Kaelene's path crossed with Bram's and Aelthira's. Though each came from a different corner of the world, they found common purpose on the road, choosing to face its dangers together.

Their travels eventually led them to the ruins of a forgotten village, where the ghost of Father Aldric asked for their help. Honoring the memory of a nameless knight who had once given his life to save the village, the companions descended beneath the ruined church, confronted the undead that still haunted its halls, and brought peace to a place that had waited a century for someone to answer its call.

With one chapter of their journey behind them, Kaelene, Bram, and Aelthira return to the road, never knowing what the next adventure will bring.

## Chapter 1. Ordinary World

The Three Stags kept a good fire. On a wet forest night, the kind that drove every soul in Outskirt indoors, that was most of what an inn needed to be famous for. The rest, lately, had been Bram.

He stood with his back to the hearth and a tankard he hadn’t touched in a quarter hour. You couldn’t drink and tell a story at once and do justice to either, and he was a long way into the part where the bear came down off the ridge. Maybe two dozen people had crowded the common room that night, which in a village the size of Outskirt was very nearly everyone who could walk, and they leaned toward him the way grass leans before weather.

“Now a bear,” Bram said, “your honest forest bear, he does not want you. I want that understood. He wants the berries and the fish and to be let alone, same as the rest of us. The trouble starts when you and the bear both want the same narrow piece of ground, and that night, friends, the narrow piece of ground was the only dry ledge on the whole cursed mountain, and it had my name on it, and it had his.”

“How big,” said the smith’s boy, who asked this every time and was never disappointed.

“Big enough that when he stood up,” Bram said, and stood up himself, which he had been sitting down to do, rising onto the balls of his feet with his arms out and his fingers crooked, “the moon went out. Just like that. A whole moon, gone, behind one bear.”

The room made the sound it was supposed to make. Bram let it. He was not, by his own cheerful admission, the cleverest man any of them would ever meet, but he understood a room the way Ulvar understood a ledger. The trick of holding one was never the words. It was the waiting. You let them want the next part.

Across the room, at the small table by the side door where she always sat, Kaelene watched him work them and felt the old thing she had no clean word for, somewhere between fondness and a thief ’s professional respect. Bram took nothing from these people and they emptied their pockets for him anyway, ale after ale, and went home richer than they came. It was the nearest thing to honest work she had ever watched anyone do well.

She sat there because it let her watch both doors at once, the front one to the yard and the smaller one to the kitchen passage, and she did it without thinking, the way other people breathed. The fire was at her left, which kept her night eye dark, in case. There would be no in case. There was never an in case in Outskirt, a forest village two days from anywhere that mattered, where the worst thing in a year was a fox in the henhouse. She knew that. She sat where she could see both doors regardless. The crypt under the skeleton village hadn’t cured her of anything; if she was honest, and she tried to be, at least with herself, it had made things a little worse. She slept badly now in a new way, a wet, closed-in way, and had stopped expecting it to pass.

So she watched the doors, and she watched Bram win the room, and she did not say much, which suited everyone fine. The village had decided early that the quiet dark one was the dangerous one, and the village was not wrong, and Kaelene let them keep the comfort of being right about something.

She was also, without making any show of it, watching Ulvar.

It had taken her about four seconds, that first night, days ago now. Bryte, the big one out front, was easy to read and pleasant to read, a broad fair man of maybe forty-four with a laugh you could warm your hands at and an open way of moving that hid nothing, because there was mostly nothing to hide. Half the unmarried girls in Outskirt found reasons to be near the bar when he was working it, and he enjoyed this in the harmless, flattered way of a man who had been handsome a long time and stopped being surprised by it. He poured a generous measure, remembered your name and your father’s name and what you drank. That was Bryte, all of him, right there on the surface.

Ulvar was the other thing entirely.

He kept to the corner near the casks, lean and hunched and dark, a good ten years older than his partner and built like something left out in too many winters. He kept the books. He did the buying, which meant the haggling, and he was good at it in a way the farmers who sold to him admired and resented in equal parts. Quick eyes that went over a room and counted it. Quick hands that he kept still on purpose. Kaelene had clocked all of it in those first few seconds the way you recognize your own reflection in a strange window: not a thought, a jolt. Oh. You. An old thief looking at a young one. He had felt it too, she was fairly sure. He’d met her eyes once that first night and something had passed between them, dry and amused and a little sad, two people who had both put a particular kind of work down and weren’t entirely certain their hands had gotten the message. His fingers still itched. She had watched them itch, once, when a wool trader passed through with a fat purse worn careless at the hip; watched his hand drift, then find a rag to wipe instead. She liked him for the drift and liked him more for the rag.

He caught her watching now and lifted his chin a quarter inch, the smallest possible greeting, and went back to his counting. She let the corner of her mouth move and went back to the doors.

At the long table nearest the fire, where the young people sat, Vagnhild and Biorn of Greatstead had the look of two cats at a window. They came nearly every night the trio stayed, which everyone understood and no one remarked on. Greatstead was the largest farm in Outskirt, a long walk out under the trees, and that two of Warge and Luvia’s children would rather sit in a smoky inn drinking small beer and listening to a stranger talk about bears was not, once you saw the children, much of a mystery. Both were lit from inside with the particular hunger of young people who have grown up somewhere safe and loved and small, and begun, just lately, to find it unbearable.

Biorn was sixteen and looked it everywhere but the eyes, which were older and very steady when he forgot to be a boy and just listened. He sat forward over his cup with his whole body aimed at the fire and the storyteller in front of it, and when Bram did the part about the moon going out, Biorn’s lips moved with the words, because he had heard the bear three times now and had the shape of it by heart. He was, Bram had said once, quietly, to the other two, the boy he had been at sixteen down to the bone, sure as sunrise that the real life was happening somewhere over the next hill and that the only sin a man could commit was to die before he got there. Bram had said it without much warmth, which was not like him.

Vagnhild was older, twenty-one or so, and steadier than her brother in the way of someone who has done a little more of the actual living, but the same fire was in her, banked lower and burning longer. She watched the trio less like an admirer and more like a scout, Kaelene thought, taking the measure of three people who had done the thing she wanted to do, which was leave. She laughed at Bram’s jokes a half-beat after everyone else, because she was watching his hands while he made them.

And at the small table beside Kaelene, leaning back against the wall with a cup she had also not touched, Aelthira kept her two columns.

She did this in her head, mostly, though there was a real book somewhere in her pack with real columns in it. The book had started, in another life, as a scholar’s instrument, a way of sorting what she learned at the Academy into what held up and what didn’t. She had filled a great many pages with the second sort before she left, which was, in the end, why she left. Now the columns had quietly become a way of reading a room. Left column: the warm things, the true things, the ones you could lean on. Bram holding the fire. Bryte’s open laugh. The boy mouthing the words. Right column: the other kind, the small wrong notes a less careful person let slide past.

There was a note in the right column tonight, and it had been growing all evening, and it concerned the lake.

She hadn’t gone looking for it. It had simply surfaced, the way her notes did, a pattern assembling itself out of a dozen things too small to mention alone. Since the trio came down off the eastern road into Outskirt, the talk in the Three Stags kept drifting toward the country round about, and more than once it had touched the lake to the west, the big still water the maps called the Mirror and the locals called the Grey, for the fog that never left it, and each time it moved off again fast, the way your tongue moves off a sore tooth. A traveler tonight had asked, idly, whether there was good fishing on the Grey, and the table had gone briefly and completely silent, and then Bryte himself, easy Bryte, had laughed and changed the subject to the weather with rather more force than the weather deserved.

Nobody fished the Grey. Aelthira had pieced that much together. Two days’ walk, a fine still water full of fish by every account, and nobody in a village that ate a great deal of fish ever went near it, and nobody would say why. The not-saying had a shape to it, an old practiced shape, the shape of a thing the village had agreed not to look at directly, the way her own Academy had agreed not to look at certain pages. She knew that shape from the inside. It was, she thought, with the small cold thrill that was the nearest thing she had anymore to joy, the most interesting thing she had run into in months.

She wrote it down, in her head, in the right column. The lake. Ask no one. Listen.

Bram finished the bear. It ended the way it always ended, with the bear and Bram parting on terms of mutual and lasting respect, neither having killed the other, both having gained a story, and the room laughed and drank and called for the skeletons. That was the new one, out of the village job, and Kaelene noticed without much surprise that there were three skeletons in it tonight where there had been two last week, the dead quietly recruiting. Bram caught her eye while he poured himself a real drink at last and shrugged, what can I do, the story wants a third skeleton, and she let him have the shrug. Some lies were only the truth in better clothes, and a third skeleton hurt no one.

Outside the wet forest dripped, and the fire snapped, and somewhere under the warmth of the room a thin cold draught came in along the floor from the yard door, the way it does in old inns, and touched the ankles of everyone near it, and went unremarked.

## Chapter 2. Call to Adventure

The yard door banged open hard enough to set the shutters jumping, and the cold and the wet and three terrified dwarves came in together.

They did not so much enter as wash in, the way water washes over a sill, all three at once and all talking, soaked through and shaking, dwarf-cloaks black with rain and clinging, beards dripping onto the floorboards. The warm full room turned toward them as one and went still. The smith’s boy half rose. Bram set down his drink. Behind the bar, Bryte’s hand had already found the heavy oak truncheon he kept below the taps without his quite deciding it should, and that small unthinking movement told Kaelene more than the dwarves’ faces did, which was saying a great deal, because the faces were telling her plenty.

They weren’t hurt. That was the first thing she checked, by habit. Whole, no blood, no wound. The thing that had them was behind their eyes. She had seen that exact arrangement of a face before, in a mirror, in the bad weeks, and she sat up a little and watched.

“Ale,” one of them managed, the oldest by his grey, “ale, for the love of stone, and a fire,” and Bryte had it for them before the words were finished, three tankards thumped onto the nearest table and the young folk scattering to make room, and the dwarves fell on the drink and the heat and for a moment said nothing at all, just shook and steamed and breathed. Outskirt did not get many dwarves. Outskirt did not, by the look of these three, much want any more.

“Now then,” Bryte said, gentle, the way you’d speak to a spooked horse, settling his weight against the table. “You’re in the Three Stags, in Outskirt, and you’re safe and dry, and there’s bread coming. Take your time.”

The grey one looked up, and the firelight got into his wet face, and he said the thing that emptied the warmth out of the room.

“We saw the island.”

It should have meant nothing. It was four small words about a piece of land. But Aelthira, watching from her table with her cup forgotten and every sense she owned standing up straight, saw the whole common room flinch as if from a struck blow. Not the travelers. The locals. The villagers of Outskirt, every one of them, all together, a single shared flinch and then that practiced silence settling over them like a lid, and she understood in that instant that the lake she had been quietly puzzling over all evening had not been the secret at all. The lake was only the road to it. This was the thing they did not look at. She felt the right column of her book go bright in her mind, the way a coal goes bright when you breathe on it.

“Nobody’s ever seen across the Grey,” said someone, low, from the back, and it was not an argument. It was a prayer, the old comfortable certainty that what the fog hid was nothing at all.

“We saw across it,” the grey dwarf said. “Once. And it isn’t nothing.”

He told it badly, which was how Kaelene knew it was true. A good liar tells a clean story; a frightened man tells it the way it happened to him, out of order, circling back, getting stuck. They’d been on the west road two days gone, the three of them, afoot, on dwarfbusiness of their own that the grey one didn’t explain and nobody pressed. The road ran a long stretch along the southern shore of the Mirror Lake, and the fog sat out on the water as it always had, the thick still fog that never lifted, that no one walking that shore in living memory had ever once seen across. Then, around midday, with no wind to move it, the fog had thinned.

“Like a curtain,” said the youngest, speaking for the first time, very fast. “Like a hand drew it. One moment grey, and the next you could see, clear across, half a league of open water that no living soul has ever once laid eyes across. The fog’s sat on it since before our grandfathers’ grandfathers. It should have been empty water. It’s always been empty water. And it was not empty.”

“An island,” the grey one said. “Where there is no island, where there has never been an island, where Karkôr and stone both would swear there could not be one. A round island, low, rock and a black shore and trees the wrong color all the way round it, and at the far end of it a tower.”

“And on the tower,” the youngest said, and stopped, and drank, and could not go on.

“On the tower,” the grey dwarf finished for him, looking at no one, looking at the middle distance where the thing apparently still stood, “a shape. Tall. Taller than a man, taller than three men, four dwarf-lengths if it was one, and standing on the very top of the tower as though it owned it. And it was burning. Not burning down, you understand. Burning and whole. Licked all over with little tongues of fire, head to foot, the way a log is licked when it first catches, but it did not burn away, it just stood there inside the fire and was not consumed, and the sky over the tower had gone red. Clear noon, grey mist all around, and over that one tower the sky was the color of a wound.”

“We ran,” said the third dwarf, who had not spoken at all until now, and whose voice was perfectly flat. “We ran from the shore harder than we have ever run in our lives, and we did not look back, and behind us the fog closed up again like a mouth. When we had gone a good way and dared to stop and look, there was nothing to see but grey. No island. No tower. Only the fog on the water, the way there has always been only fog on the water.” He looked around the silent room. “We will not go near that lake again. Not for cargo, not for kin, not for the king’s own gold. We came on east to tell you it’s there, behind the fog, where no one ever knew, and now we are going, and we are not stopping, and that is the end of what we have to say about it.”

Nobody answered. The fire ate a log. At the long table by the hearth, Vagnhild had gone forward over her folded arms with her eyes shining, and Biorn looked as if his whole sixteen years had been a waiting room and the door had finally opened, and Kaelene marked them both, the way you mark the edge of a cliff a child is wandering toward.

And it was into that silence, with the perfect terrible timing of a man who had been waiting all evening in the shadow by the kitchen passage for exactly this, that Sirter came and sat down at the trio’s table.

He came with wine on his breath and a too-friendly smile and something wrapped in oilcloth held against his chest like a baby, and he sat himself down on the bench beside Bram uninvited, the way only a certain kind of villager ever did, the kind everyone tolerated and nobody trusted, and he leaned in close and dropped his voice to the volume men use when they very much want to be overheard.

“Now then,” Sirter said. “Now then, friends. You’ll be the three from the eastern road. The bear man and the quiet woman and the clever one. I’ve watched you. I’ve a thing here might interest people who go to places.” He patted the oilcloth. “People with the nerve for it.”

“We’ve a thing too,” Bram said pleasantly. “It’s called a quiet drink, and you’re sitting on it.”

But he did not get up, and Sirter, who knew the difference between a no and a haggle as well as any man alive, grinned wider and unwrapped a corner of the oilcloth, just a corner, just enough.

It was a map. Old hide, drawn in a brown ink gone rusty with age, and the whole left third of it was burned away, the edge of the burn black and curled. What was left was the island, and only the island, no shore around it and no water, as though whoever drew it had never cared what lay outside it. It was round, low, roughly circular. Near the top a mark that might have been a tower, set toward the northern edge, and at the very heart of the island a smaller shape, an inner water, a little lake cupped in the middle of it. There was writing around the edges in a hand and a tongue Aelthira did not recognize at a glance and very much wanted to.

“You’ll have heard the tales of that island, even if you’ve not heard them put plain,” Sirter said, with the satisfaction of a man delivering a line he had practiced. “Wizards. Wicked ones, going back and back. They went out there for the quiet of it and the strength of it, to call up devils and bargain for power. And they did, and it ate most of them, the way it eats that kind. But the stories all agree on one thing, and it’s the thing that matters. Three hundred years gone there was a great one out there, the strongest of the lot. Spent his whole long life raking in gold and silver and worked things and bound treasures, a hoard to make a king weep. And when the island took him at the last, the way it took all of them, the hoard stayed. Down in the dark. Nobody’s ever brought a copper of it back, because nobody’s ever found the way in, because nobody’s ever even seen the cursed island.” He patted the oilcloth. “Until now. The dwarves saw it. And I’ve the map to it, and I’ll have fifteen gold for the map. Twenty,” he amended, glancing at the dwarves, sensing the market had just moved in his favor. “Twenty, given the demand.”

“And where did a man like you come by a map to a place no one’s ever seen,” Kaelene said. It was the first thing she had said all night, and Sirter turned to her, and something in her flat tone made his grin falter.

“My uncle,” he said. “Believed every word of it, the old fool, the hoard and all. Spent years on it. Went out to that lake to find a way across and find the island and find the gold, and he didn’t come back. The season after, his pack came down the west track on the back of a peddler who’d found it on the shore with the bones still in the coat. The map was in it.” He pushed the map an inch across the table. “That part’s no lie. I’ll lie to you about plenty, friend, I’ll grant you that to your face, but not about my own uncle dead out there for this, and not about where it sits in the water. Look at it. He drew it himself, near as I can tell, off older maps, before he ever set out. The dwarves saw what my uncle gave his life trying to see.” He looked, for a moment, almost ashamed, and almost wise. “I never went near that lake and I never will, and that’s the truth of me, and I still want my twenty gold, because a true map to a place full of dead wizards’ gold is worth twenty gold to the kind of people who’d go. And you’re the kind of people who’d go. I knew it the first night. You’ve got the look. Etin help you.”

Across the table, Aelthira reached out one finger and turned the burned map so the writing faced her, and bent over it, and for her, alone in that whole frightened room, the night stopped being a story about somewhere not to go and became something else. A place where magic had been worked strong enough to be feared for three hundred years. A shape that stood in fire and was not consumed. A lake the fog had hidden so long that no living eye had crossed it, and writing in a hand from no school she knew, around the edge of a map a dead man had carried to the water and died for. This was not a rumor. The dwarves hadn’t brought a rumor in out of the rain. They had brought a sighting, and Sirter had brought the way in.

She did not say any of that. She put her finger on the tower-mark near the top of the island, light as a moth, then let it drift to the small inner water at the heart of it, and she said, in a voice she kept very level, “Fifteen. And you keep it under the cloth on this table tonight, and you don’t sell it twice.”

Sirter looked at her finger on the map, and at Bram not getting up, and at the quiet woman who had not blinked once at devils or dead wizards, and made the calculation that men like Sirter live and die by.

“Fifteen,” he agreed. “And it stays under the cloth, and it’s yours in the morning, and may you have more luck with it than my uncle did, or the wizards, or me.”

## Chapter 3. Kaelene Decides

They did not buy it that night. Bram gave Sirter a coin to keep the map under the oilcloth and on the table until morning, which Sirter understood and accepted, and the three of them went up to the room they shared under the eaves with the rain on the roof, and there, in the small space and the candlelight, the argument that was not quite an argument happened.

Except it was not really between the three of them. Aelthira knew that, and Bram knew it, and so they did the unusual thing, for them, of not pushing. Aelthira would go. She had decided somewhere between the dwarf saying island and her own finger touching the tower-mark, and her wanting it was so plain and so simple that it needed no defending; she sat on the edge of the narrow bed with the candle and waited, holding her hunger still by main force, because she had learned that her wanting things very badly was sometimes a reason to be careful rather than a reason to be heard. And Bram, who would follow either of them into most kinds of fire and said so cheerfully and often, leaned in the doorway with his arms crossed and watched the only one of the three for whom this was not simple.

Because it was Kaelene’s nightmare. They both knew that too, without anyone having to lay it out.

She stood at the little window with her back to them, looking at nothing, at the black wet dark where the forest was, and she let herself add it up, every piece of it, because notlooking was the one sin she had decided she would not commit anymore, whatever it cost.

An island no one had ever laid eyes on, hidden a lifetime of lifetimes behind a fog that never lifted. A still grey lake under that drowning fog. Cold water. A tower. A drowned place, by the shape of the map, a hall gone down under water, and a thing in fire at the top of it, and a dead sorcerer’s writing around the edge of a stolen map. She made herself look at each piece, and each piece was a piece of the worst day of her life. This was the furniture of the dream that had been chasing her since Jorpagna and had only gotten louder since the crypt: the cold, the wet, the closed-in dark, the dead place that took someone, and the going down into it. She had been here before, not this lake but its shape, its weather, its smell, and she had gone down into a place very like this one and Tarek had not come back up, and she had.

She had walked away from the high city carrying that, the going down and the coming up alone, and she had been walking away from it ever since, down every road she could find, and the thing she had finally understood, somewhere on the eastern track or maybe just now at the window, was that the walking was not working. The dream did not get smaller behind her. It got smaller in front of her, the closer she walked toward it, and the bigger behind. Running was a way of keeping the dark exactly the size it was. She had run a long way to learn that.

“You don’t have to,” Bram said from the doorway, quietly, when the silence had gone on long enough. “I want that said out loud, so it’s said. Of everything we’ve ever pointed ourselves at, this is the one I’ll turn the boat around on with no story attached and no grudge after. There’s no shame in it. We’ll buy the map off Sirter to spite him and never use it, and tomorrow we walk south to somewhere warm with no lake in it, and I’ll tell the bear for new people.”

“I know,” Kaelene said.

“He means it,” Aelthira said, from the bed, “and so do I.”

“I know,” Kaelene said again. And she turned around.

She wasn’t crying, and she wasn’t steady. She was both at once, the way the dwarves had been wet and warm together when they came in. Her face had the look of someone telling the exact truth at some cost.

“I’m afraid of it,” she said. “I want that said out loud too, so it’s said, and so you’re not guessing. I’m afraid of it the way I’ve never been afraid of any job we’ve taken, because it is the precise shape of the thing that broke me, and I have not slept right since the crypt, and if I had any sense at all I’d be packing for south right now and thanking you both.”

“But,” said Aelthira, very softly, because she could hear it coming.

“But I want to go,” Kaelene said, and something in her face came loose when she said it, some long-held thing, not relief exactly, more like the feeling of putting down a weight you have carried so long you stopped noticing it was a choice. “That’s the part I didn’t expect. I’ve been standing at that window trying to feel the no, waiting for it, because the no is supposed to be the sensible thing and I am, whatever else, a sensible thief. And it isn’t there. Underneath the fear there’s this other thing, and it’s been there the whole time, since the dwarf said the word, and it’s curiosity, and it’s stubbornness, and it’s something I don’t have a clean name for that wants to go down into the kind of place that took him and walk back up out of it on my own two feet, in the daylight, while it’s me deciding and not the dark. I want to look at the thing that scares me. I’m so tired of only ever running away from it.”

The candle flame leaned, and steadied.

“I could be wrong,” she said. “It could kill us. The dwarves were not playing at being frightened, and Sirter’s not lying about the men this map outlived, and I’ve seen enough to know that the things that scare you sometimes scare you because they should. So I’m not saying it’s safe and I’m not saying it’s clever. I’m saying I’ve heard the call, and for the first time in a long time the thing in me that answers isn’t the thing that’s afraid. It’s the other one. And I’d rather find out who I am with the two of you at my back than keep walking south being careful for the rest of my life.” She looked at Bram, and then at Aelthira, and her voice steadied all the way down to the floor. “So. We go. If you’ll come. The yes is mine. I’m not being talked into anything and I’m not being talked out of it. I’m choosing it. Buy the map.”

Bram pushed off the doorframe and crossed the little room, and he took her face in both hands, and he kissed her, slow, once, on the mouth, the way you kiss someone you have just watched walk willingly toward the thing that could take her from you. Then he rested his forehead against hers and stayed there a moment, breathing her in, his thumbs moving once along her jaw.

“There she is,” he said, low, against her. “Right. We’re going to a haunted drowned island full of dead wizards and their gold, to spite three hundred years of better men and a man named Sirter. I love this company.” He grinned at them both, the firelit grin from downstairs, only smaller and just for them. “I’m going to need a better story out of this than the bear.”

“You’re going to need a great deal more than a story,” Aelthira said, but she was already on her feet, already reaching for her pack and the real book with the real columns in it, and there was a brightness on her that had nothing to do with the candle. In the left column she wrote, in her head, where the warm true things went: Kaelene chose it. All three. And under it she let herself add, because it was warm and it was true: We walk toward what we fear. That is the company we are.

She did not yet write anything in the right column about what waited on the water. There would be time for the right column; there always was, with Aelthira. But not tonight. Tonight the yes was Kaelene’s, and it was the warmest thing in the room, and Aelthira had learned, the hard way, in a marble hall a thousand miles east, to know a warm true thing when she finally found one and not to spoil it with the cold.

## Chapter 4. Provisioning, the Mentor, and the Chapel

In the grey morning they bought the map off Sirter for fifteen gold, and Sirter took the coins with a face that could not decide between relief and regret, and the trio spent the day getting ready and saying, without saying it, goodbye to a village they had only just met and had somehow already decided to risk their lives near.

There was provisioning to do, the dull true work of it, rope and oil and dried meat and a second waterskin each, and Bram did most of it cheerfully while Kaelene watched the village move and learned its corners the way she learned every place, in case. But the part that mattered, the part Aelthira had been quietly steering them toward since dawn, was the long climb up the wooded slope behind Outskirt to the hut of Mifalodor.

Mifalodor was tiny, and old past guessing, and angry about something that had happened long before any of them were born and was apparently still going on inside him. He was an alchemist, the only one for three days in any direction, and he lived apart on purpose and traded his brews for goods and tolerated visitors the way a cat tolerates rain. He did not stand when they ducked in under his low lintel into the close, sharp-smelling dark of the hut, and he did not offer them anything to sit on, and the first thing he said was, “Don’t touch that,” to Bram, who had not yet touched anything.

But he sold to them, which was the thing he did, and Aelthira, who had a way with difficult clever people because she had spent her whole life among them, drew him out by the simple method of being precisely as exact as he was. She did not call his brews potions and she did not gush. She named what she wanted, and named it correctly, and Mifalodor’s vinegar eyes warmed a half a degree, which on Mifalodor was practically an embrace.

He sold them a healing elixir, two small stoppered vials of something dark that he swore would close a wound that should have killed you, once, if you drank it fast and weren’t too far gone already. He sold them a draught of invisibility too, a single dose in a fat green bottle, with a great many warnings about how briefly it worked and how it did nothing for the noise a body made or the smell it left, so that a man made unseen by it was still a fool to think himself unfound. And last, most carefully, a warding draught against magic, a flat grey liquid he handled as though it bit. When Aelthira pressed him for the proper word he called it antimagi, a thing that for a short while turned a person into poor soil for a spell to take root in.

“You’ll want that one where you’re going,” Mifalodor said, and it was the first time he had let on that he knew where they were going, and he said it without surprise, which surprised them. He looked at Aelthira then, only at Aelthira, the long flat look of one practitioner sizing another.

“You feel it already, don’t you,” he said. “Standing in my hut. You with the trained hand.”

“Feel what,” Aelthira said, very level.

“It.” He gestured west, with his chin, toward the unseen lake. “The reason no one fishes the Grey, and no one has for longer than the village’s grandmothers were alive. It is not the island, girl, or not only. The island is what the place does when it is fed. The thing itself is in the water and the rock for leagues, an old, old swelling in the world out that way, where the skin of things is thin and a working bites deeper than it has any right to.” He leaned forward, and his anger, for once, was not at his visitors. “On that island your craft will run cheap. Cheap and fast and easy, easier than it has ever come to you, and that will feel like a gift, and it is the most dangerous thing there. Cheap magic is a door left standing open. Anyone can spend a little there. Anything can come through. The man who built whatever sits at the bottom of that lake found that out, I’d stake my hut on it, and so will you, if you are not very careful with your easy new gift.” He sat back. “That’s the warning, and it’s free, which is more than I can say for the draughts. Leave the goods we agreed on by the door and get out, I’ve work.”

Aelthira paid, and they got out, and on the steep walk back down through the dripping trees she was quiet in a way the other two had learned to leave alone. She was writing. Right column. The island makes magic cheap. Cheap magic is a door. The old swelling in the world. The skin of things, thin. And under it, because she was a careful woman and her care had been bought at a high price, a single underlined word she did not show the others: Careful.

The chapel they came to in the late afternoon, almost by accident, on the way back toward the inn, and it was Kaelene who stopped.

It was a small thing, the chapel of Outskirt, a single low room of grey forest stone with a slate roof gone soft with moss, set a little apart on a rise among old trees, the kind of holy place a small far village keeps up out of habit and a vague hope. The door stood open. Inside it was cool and dim and smelled of stone and cold candle, and there was an old man sweeping, who was Rabindranath, and there was, carved deep into the lintel over the plain stone altar at the far end, a mark.

Kaelene saw it and stopped as though she had walked into glass.

A sunburst. And over the sunburst, laid across it point-down, a sword. The two together, cut clean into the old grey stone above the altar, worn but unmistakable, and she knew it, she knew it the way you know a face in a crowd before you know how, a jolt under the ribs, oh, you, and her hand went out and found the edge of the nearest pew and held it.

“The Shining Way,” Aelthira said softly behind her, reading it too, but not feeling it, not the way Kaelene felt it.

Because Kaelene was not in the chapel anymore, not entirely. She was years back and a hundred roads away, on some nothing afternoon with Tarek crouched at a half-fallen wayshrine, his blunt finger tracing the very same two shapes worn into the very same kind of stone, the sunburst is the light they served and the sword is how they served it, and where you find this, girl, you find people who once decided that the dark was a thing to be walked into and not away from, and you remember them for it. He had taught her to read marks the way Ulvar read a ledger and Bram read a room, this one among a hundred others, long before the skeleton village, long before any of it went wrong. He had been alive when he taught her this. That was the thing that closed her throat.

And here it was again, breathing, in a living chapel in a warm village, with an old man sweeping the floor in front of it, and the light from the open door falling on it, and no crypt under it, no body, no cost. Just the mark. Just Tarek’s hand reaching forward out of the good years into the front of a hard new one, to put one true known thing in her way the morning before she walked back down into water.

She found, to her own surprise, that her eyes had filled, and she let them, in the cool dim of the chapel where it cost nothing, and she did not wipe them in front of Bram, who saw and said nothing, which was the kindest thing he knew how to do.

The old man, Rabindranath, leaned his broom against the wall and came down to them, unhurried, a thin soft-spoken man with the distracted gentle eyes of someone who slept a great deal and dreamed a great deal and was never entirely sure which of the two he was in. He did not ask their business. He looked at Kaelene’s wet face and at the mark she could not stop looking at, and he said, “You know the Way,” not a question, “and it grieves you,” also not a question, and Kaelene said, “Someone taught me to read it. He’s dead now,” and Rabindranath nodded as though that were the most ordinary and the most sacred thing a person could say, and let it be.

He did not have much to give them. There was only the mark and the cool and the old man, and that was the grace of it, that it asked nothing and offered only memory. But at the door, when they were leaving, with the long light coming gold under the trees, Rabindranath put his soft dry hand on the doorframe and looked out west toward the water no one fished, and a shadow went over his gentle distracted face.

“I dream true, sometimes,” he said, to no one in particular. “It’s a small gift and a tiring one and mostly I’d give it back. But I’ll tell you what I’ve dreamed this week, since you’re going where I think you’re going, and you can do with it as you like.” He paused. “I dreamed of cold water and a man in a torn shirt who has been waiting a very long time, and was glad. And I dreamed of two empty rooms. That’s all. I don’t understand it and I won’t pretend to.” He smiled at them, gentle and sad and entirely without hope of being heeded. “Go carefully. And come back, if the Way’s any use to you, because someone ought to, for once.”

It was not until they were nearly back at the inn, in the last of the light, that the other thing happened, and it happened on the road by the turnoff to Greatstead, where Vagnhild and Biorn were waiting for them, having clearly walked out from the farm to do it, and having clearly rehearsed.

Biorn did the talking, because Biorn always did, and he did it fast and bright and full, all his sixteen years pouring out at once: that they had been talking, he and his sister, that they had heard the whole of it, the dwarves and the island and the map, that the trio would need hands, that he was strong and Vagnhild was clever and they both knew the country, that they could row and carry and watch, that they would be no trouble and a great deal of use, that they would, in short, like to come.

And Kaelene looked at the boy’s shining face, and at Vagnhild standing a half-step back with the steadier, longer fire in her, and she felt the easy yes rise up, because they would be useful, and because she of all people understood the hunger, and she put it down again, because she understood the hunger too well.

It was Bram, in the end, who answered, and he took his time about it, and he got it right, and Kaelene watched him do it with something like awe.

“Sit down a minute,” Bram said, and sat himself on the low stone wall by the turnoff, and the two of them sat, because you sat when Bram sat. And he was quiet a moment, looking back down the road toward the lights of Greatstead just coming on in the dusk, the big warm farmhouse with its smoke and its dogs barking and its lit windows, where Warge would be in from the fields and Luvia would have the supper on and little Rosiall would be underfoot with Nipper and Greycoat, and a table would be laid with five places, two of them empty right now and both of them kept.

“You think we left places like that,” Bram said, nodding down at the warm windows. “You think the three of us walked out of houses like yours, full ones, loud ones, places with a fire and a table that keeps your seat, and that the leaving was the brave thing and the staying is the small thing, and that if you want to be like us you have to leave a Greatstead behind. That’s the story you’ve built, isn’t it. I built the same one at your age. I’d have sworn to it.” He shook his big head, slowly. “It’s a lie, Biorn. Not yours, mine. Listen.

“Kaelene there never had a house at all. She had a city that used her up and never once learned her name, and she left it because staying would have finished the job. Aelthira left a place she trusted with her whole heart the day she found out it was teaching her a lie, and that kind of leaving takes a piece out of you that does not grow back. And me.” He looked down at his own hands. “I had a hard man for a father in a hard town by the sea, and a life he’d built for me brick by brick that fit me like a boot two sizes wrong, and I walked out from under it because if I’d stayed I’d have become him, and I could not. None of us left a Greatstead, lad. Not one of the three of us has ever had what you’ve got down that road there with the lights on. We left because staying would have cost us ourselves. That’s the only reason worth a road.”

Biorn had gone still. Vagnhild was watching her brother’s face, and she had understood already, Kaelene saw; Vagnhild had heard it a full beat before her brother and her own restless fire had banked itself, just so.

“You don’t have to leave to be one of us,” Bram said, gentler now. “You’d have to leave to stop being something rarer than we’ll ever be, which is a person with a true home to defend. And here’s the thing nobody tells a boy with a fire in him, so I’ll tell you, because I see myself in you so plain it aches. The fire’s not the problem. The fire’s good. The world is full of real teeth, Biorn. I’m rowing toward a mouthful of them this very week, and a place like Outskirt, small and far and soft, needs someone with exactly your fire who points it at the dark coming in instead of carrying it off down a road. Be a fighter, if that’s the want in you. But be the wall this village leans on. Learn the blade so you can stand in a doorway and mean it. Train the boys after you. Be the one who’s here, between Greatstead and the things in the dark, when something finally comes. Because something always finally comes.” He smiled. “Better the wall a hundred people lean on than one more sword for hire who forgot the name of the table that kept his seat. There’s more glory in the doorway than the road. It’s just slower to arrive. Take it from a man on the road.”

For a long moment Biorn said nothing, and his face did several things, the boy’s disappointment and then under it, slowly, the older steadier thing rising up to meet what he’d been handed, which was not a no but a purpose, which is a different gift entirely and a harder one to refuse.

“You really think something’s coming,” he said at last. “Here. To Outskirt.”

“Something’s always coming somewhere,” Bram said. “Be ready for it here, where the people you love are, and you’ll have done more than I ever have with a sword in my hand.” He stood, and clapped the boy once on the shoulder, soldier to soldier. “Go home, Biorn. Your seat’s getting cold and your mother’s a better cook than I’ll eat for a week. Walk us to the inn first if you like, but go home tonight, and start being the wall tomorrow. We’ll want to find it standing when we come back.”

And Vagnhild took her brother’s arm, and the two of them walked the trio back down to the lights of the Three Stags in the last blue dusk, quieter than they’d come, the boy turning the new shape of his life over and over in his hands, and at the inn door they said goodnight and went home to Greatstead, to the warm windows and the kept seats and the dogs, exactly as Bram had asked, and that was the last warm thing the trio had to leave behind before the water.

In the morning they would walk west.

## Chapter 5. Crossing the Threshold

The west track out of Outskirt was an old road the forest had been quietly taking back for a hundred years, and the trio walked it for a day and a half before anything tried to happen to them. A generous amount of warning, by Bram’s reckoning, that they were headed somewhere the forest had stopped wanting people to go.

They made good time the first day. The weather lifted, the trees thinned and thickened and thinned again, and Bram told no stories on the road, because Kaelene had asked him not to. She’d done it the second morning, in the gentlest possible way, by pointing out that a man’s voice carries a long way under trees and they didn’t yet know what was out here to hear it. He’d taken it well, the way he took most things, and walked instead with his thumbs in his belt and his eyes on the green, and Kaelene watched him not-talking and loved him a little for how hard it plainly was.

She took the front. That was the order they’d settled into without ever agreeing it aloud: Kaelene a dozen paces ahead reading the ground and the light, Aelthira in the middle with her quiet inward look, Bram at the back where his size did the most good. Kaelene read the road the way she read a room, and what the road told her, all through the morning of the second day, was that things had been moving on it that should not have been. Tracks, not old. The prints of booted feet, scuffed and run-down at the heel, more than one set, a good many, all of them headed west the way the trio were headed, the way down to the lake. Something had gone this way before them, and not long before, and gone in a hurry. She did not like sharing a road with it, whatever it was, and she kept her eyes down and her pace easy and said nothing, and the prints ran on ahead of them mile after mile, and then, around midday, she heard the first of it: a sound somewhere out in front, faint and then not faint, a wet ragged clatter of bodies moving badly and fast, and growing. Coming back. Coming toward them.

She stopped, and raised her hand flat, and the other two stopped behind her without a sound, and the clatter swelled out of the trees ahead, closer with every breath, a great many bodies moving badly and fast and coming straight up the road at them.

“Off the road,” she breathed. “Now. Down.”

There was a stand of hazel and bramble on the high side of the track and they went into it the way water goes into sand, fast and low and without fuss, Bram folding his big frame down behind a deadfall with a soldier’s economy that always surprised people who only knew him for the bear, Aelthira tucking herself into a hollow under the roots, Kaelene flat behind a screen of fern with one eye to a gap. They had done this before. The forest closed over the sound of them and they went still, and they waited, and the clattering came on, and then the goblins were there.

There were perhaps a dozen of them, and they were a ruin. Kaelene had fought goblins, and these were nothing she’d have called a war-band; they were the survivors of one. They came down the track at a broken half-run, the fastest their hurts would let them go, every one of them bound up in rags gone dark and stiff, limping, one half-carried by two others, one with an arm strapped to its body and a face like a dropped melon. They weren’t hunting. There was no order to them and no anger, only the flat blind forward-push of things that have seen something behind them so much worse than whatever lay ahead that ahead had stopped mattering. They stank of fear and old blood. They didn’t look to the sides. They wanted only to be somewhere else, and they wanted it with their whole battered bodies, and Kaelene, watching through the fern, felt the cold settle a little deeper in her, because she had been to the place that does that to a creature, more or less, and she knew the look from the inside.

Most of them went past. And then one stopped.

It was near the back, a lean grey-green thing with a half-healed gash down its scalp and one ear gone, and it stopped dead in the middle of the track not eight feet from where Kaelene lay, and it lifted its ruined head, and it sniffed.

Nobody breathed. Bram, behind the deadfall, became a stone. Aelthira, in her hollow, did the small terrible thing of not even thinking loudly, because she more than half believed some creatures could feel a mind reaching out, and she made hers small and still and dark. Kaelene watched the goblin’s nostrils work, watched its head turn, slow, swinging toward the bramble, toward the gap, toward her. Its eyes were yellow and exhausted and afraid, and for one long held moment they seemed to look straight into hers through the screen of fern, and she did not move, did not blink, became nothing at all, the oldest trick she owned and the first Tarek had drilled into her: you are not hiding, girl, you are not there. There is no you to find.

The goblin sniffed again. Something in the wind shifted, or its nerve broke, or the terror behind it reached up and yanked, because all at once it flinched as though struck, gave a thin wretched whine, and bolted after its band, gone down the track and into the green, and the clattering receded, and was gone.

They did not move. Kaelene held up her hand again, flat, wait. Because the thing about a band of creatures fleeing in terror was that it had, by definition, something to flee from, and that something was very likely still coming.

It came perhaps three minutes later, and it came differently. Where the goblins had been noise and ruin, this was a kind of silence with speed inside it, a sense of the forest leaning and the light bending, and then they were simply there on the track, going the other way, going after the goblins, and they were wood elves.

There were six of them, tall and lean and barely touching the ground, grey and green and brown so that the forest seemed to be passing through them rather than the reverse, bows in their hands and that high cold beautiful focus on their faces that elves wear when they are doing the one thing in the world they do better than anything else alive, which is hunt. They moved at a pace the goblins could not have dreamed of and made a fraction of the noise, and they were past in moments, and would have been gone in moments more, except that the one in the lead checked, mid-stride, and turned her head, and looked straight into the bramble at Aelthira.

There was no hiding from that look. Aelthira did not try. She rose, slowly, to her knees, out of the hollow, into the elf ’s line of sight, with her empty hands a little out from her sides, and she met the elf ’s eyes, and something passed between them.

It was not friendship, and the elf ’s face made that plain in the first half-second. Aelthira was a light elf, born and raised among humans, and the wood-kin had little love and less trust for the cousins who’d gone to live in the cities of men. What crossed the elf ’s face when she placed her was not warmth. It was a cool, narrow appraisal, the look you give a relation you’ve heard unkind things about. But blood was blood enough to stay the bow, and there were goblins on the wind. Whatever a wood elf made of a light elf on an ordinary day, this was not an ordinary day. On the one thing that had ever united every kind of elf there was no daylight between them: the goblins were the enemy, and an elf who’d point the hunt the right way was, for the length of this chase, on the right side of it. The elf ’s head tilted, a question with no words in it. Aelthira lifted one hand and pointed, clean and certain, down the track the way the goblins had fled.

The elf held her eyes one more heartbeat, the wariness still in it, weighing the light elf against the help she’d just been handed and finding the help, for now, the larger thing. Then she dipped her chin, the smallest nod, the courtesy you pay an ally you’d never invite home, my thanks, and she was gone. All six of them gone, the forest closing behind them like water over a stone. The track lay empty, the afternoon light falling through the leaves exactly as before, as though none of it had happened at all.

Bram rose up from behind his deadfall, brushing leaf-mould off his front. “Well,” he said, very quietly, which was as loud as Kaelene had allowed. “That’s the first time I’ve ever been glad to be ignored by six people who could’ve put an arrow through my eye before I finished blinking.” He looked at Aelthira. “She didn’t much like the look of you.”

“She didn’t,” Aelthira agreed, and there was a small wry warmth on her face that she was trying not to show and not quite managing. “Her people think mine sold the woods for a soft bed and a roof. They’re not entirely wrong. On any other day she might have spat at my feet.” She got to her feet. She was a light elf, raised in the cities of men and schooled in a hall of men, and the wood-kin were as strange to her as she was to them. She had never belonged to her own people any more than to the humans she’d lived among, and had belonged to nothing at all but these two. But a stranger of her own old blood had looked at her, out here in the deep wood, and weighed her, and decided that whatever else she was, she was not on the side of the things in the dark. That was a colder thing than being claimed. It had landed somewhere in her she’d thought long closed all the same. She filed it, where the warm true things went. “There were goblins between us,” she said. “And there’s nothing an elf hates like a goblin, not even another elf. So she let me help, and she’ll never think of me again. It’s not the same as being known. But it’s not nothing.”

“It’s not nothing,” Kaelene agreed, and they went on.

They came to the Grey in the grey of the next dawn, and the lake was everything the dwarves had promised and worse, because the dwarves had been describing a fright and the trio were walking into a fact.

The mist owned it. It lay on the water in a thick still pall that did not move and did not lift, and it soaked everything it touched, so that within a hundred paces of the shore their clothes were heavy and their hair wet through and the world had shrunk to a circle a stone’s throw wide, walled with pale nothing. No wind. And then the thing that got under Kaelene’s skin and stayed: no sound. No birds. The whole long walk through the forest had been loud with birds, the ordinary endless racket of a living wood, and here at the edge of the Grey it simply stopped, as though someone had drawn a line, and on the lake side of the line the silence was total and waiting and aware.

“I don’t like it,” Bram said, and it was the most honest thing he’d said in two days, and nobody argued.

There was a boat. They found it drawn up among the reeds at the water’s edge, a low broad rowing boat of black wood, old and tarred and sound, with two sets of oars shipped neatly inside it as though someone had left it ready and stepped away a moment ago, and there was no sign of who, and there had not been a footprint within a mile that any of them could read. It sat in the reeds and waited, the way the boat at the bottom of every bad dream waits, and Kaelene looked at it for a long moment and then, because she had decided at the window in Outskirt that not-looking was the sin she would not commit, she walked down and put her hand on its cold wet gunwale and said, “Come on, then. It’s only a boat.”

They took their places without discussing them. Bram took the oars, because Bram’s back was the engine of the thing and because rowing gave him something to do with the fear that nattering would have made worse. Kaelene crouched in the bow, facing forward into the mist, on watch, her knife loose and her eyes moving. And Aelthira sat in the stern with the burned map across her knees, though she did not need it now, and closed her eyes, and worked the small quiet magic that was hers to work.

She felt it the moment she reached for it, and her breath caught.

It came too easily. She had a finding-sense, a small reliable thing, a way of feeling true north and true distance the way other people feel up and down, and ordinarily it cost her, a little, a tightening behind the eyes, a coin spent. Out here on the water it did not cost her at all. It came pouring up out of the reaching like a spring uncapped, far more than she had asked for, bright and cheap and frighteningly abundant, and she understood, with the cold clarity she trusted more than any warmth, that Mifalodor had told her the exact truth. The water itself was rich with it. Her craft ran cheap here, cheap and fast and easy, and it felt, God help her, wonderful, and the wonderful feeling was the most dangerous thing she had ever touched, because she could see at once how a person could drink at this spring until they drowned and call it a gift the whole way down. She took only what she needed, a heading, a thread of true direction laid out through the blind grey, and she made herself put the rest back, and that putting-back was the hardest discipline she had practiced in years.

“East-northeast a touch,” she said, low. “Hold it. I’ll keep us true.”

Bram rowed, slow and even and quiet, the oars going in and out of the still water with the smallest possible sound, and the boat slid out into the grey and the shore vanished behind them and there was nothing in the whole world but the circle of pale water and the three of them inside it, going somewhere by faith.

It happened once, about halfway across.

Kaelene saw it because Kaelene was watching, and she did not cry out, because the first rule was that you do not cry out, you report. Off the starboard bow, a boat’s length away, the still grey surface parted without a ripple, and an eye came up out of the water on a long pale stalk, an eye the size of a fist, dark and wet and slick. It rose to the height of a standing man. It turned, slow, deliberate, taking them in, and fixed on the boat. On the three of them. There was something behind it that was thinking, and it held there, patient, while Bram’s oars hung dripping and stopped, while Kaelene’s hand closed on her knife and did not raise it, while Aelthira opened her eyes and went very still. It looked at them for the length of three slow breaths, the way a man at a window looks down at people passing in the street, idle and unhurried and entirely in command.

And then it sank. The stalk drew down, smooth as oil, the eye going last, and the surface closed over it without a mark, no ripple, no wake, nothing to say it had ever been there at all, and the lake was still and grey and silent again.

Nothing else surfaced. Nothing came for them. No second eye, no rush of water, no attack. Bram looked at Kaelene, and Kaelene looked back, and neither of them said the thing they were both thinking, which Aelthira said for them, very quietly, from the stern.

“It could have taken us,” she said. “Whatever owns this lake. It looked at us, and it decided to let us across.” She turned her face forward, toward the unseen island, and there was no warmth in her voice now at all, only the cold clear reading that was her truest gift. “We are not crossing this water because we’re clever or quiet or lucky. We are crossing it because something on the far side wants visitors, and the lake has been told to let them come.” She picked the heading back up, the thread of true direction in the blind grey. “Row, Bram. We’ve been invited. The rude thing now would be to turn back.”

Bram rowed, and the cold went down into all three of them and stayed, and then a darkness gathered in the grey ahead, a denser nothing inside the nothing. It firmed as they pulled toward it, into a black shore and the wrong-coloured trees standing over it, and there was the island, exactly where the dead man’s map had set it, that no living eye had seen until two days ago.

## Chapter 6. Tests, Allies, Enemies

The island was wrong in the way Aelthira’s right column was always trying to put words to, the way of a thing that has been built and is pretending to have grown. The black shore was real rock, the trees behind it real trees, but the trees were the wrong green, a deep drowned green with no spring in it, and they didn’t move, because there was no wind, and the silence of the lake came right up onto the land and sat there. The map had a smaller lake drawn at the island’s heart. They found it where the map said, a still dark inner water cupped in the rock, and at its near edge, exactly where Sirter’s burned hide promised, the way down.

It was a flooded mouth in the stone, a low arch half drowned, black water lapping at it without a sound, the throat of something going down into the rock and under, into the dark and the cold. Bram stood at the edge of it and looked at the water going in and did not like any single thing about it.

“We can’t take it all down,” Kaelene said, already unbuckling. “You can’t swim a flooded passage in plate and mail with a slab of iron on your arm. We strip to what we can move in, we leave the rest above the waterline where we can find it coming back, and we go light.”

This was the worst part for Bram and they all knew it, and he did it anyway, which was the whole of him in one act. He stripped off his plate piece by piece, the buckles stiff under cold-clumsy fingers, and then the mail shirt under it, and he rolled the lot and tucked it up in a dry cleft well above the water with his pack and his heavy gear, and then he stood holding his shield, the iron heater shield he had carried for years and patched a dozen times and trusted with his life more often than he cared to count, and he looked at it, and he looked at the black water, and he understood that there was no version of this where the shield came too.

“You’ll want it back,” Kaelene said, gently, because she saw his face. “It’ll be right here. We’re coming back this way.”

“I know,” Bram said. He set the shield against the rock, careful, leaning it where the spray would not reach it, the way you set down something you mean to pick up again. He kept his sword, belted across his back where it would ride least in the water, and his knife at his hip, and that was all. “Right,” he said. “Lighter than I’ve gone into anything since I was a boy. Let’s not make a habit of it.” But he was smiling when he said it, the small real smile, because the only thing to do with a fear you cannot put down is to make a joke of carrying it.

Aelthira raised a magelight, and it cost her nothing, and she watched it cost her nothing with that same wary inward look. A soft cool blue-white globe, no bigger than an apple, that floated at her shoulder and lit the water and the arch and threw their shadows huge and wavering on the wet stone. She sent it ahead, into the mouth, and the dark swallowed it down to a circle, the way the mist had swallowed the world on the lake, the light eaten back to a small sphere with the black pressing in all around it.

“It doesn’t reach as far as it should,” she said. “The dark here is thick. Stay close to me and stay close to the light.” She looked at the two of them, and then she went in first, because the light was hers and the light had to lead, and they followed her down into the cold.

The flooded passage was mercifully short, a dozen strokes of hard swimming through water so cold it drove the breath out of them, the magelight bobbing ahead and the rock close on every side, and then the floor tilted up under their hands and the water shallowed and let them go, and they dragged themselves out onto a flight of worn stone steps that climbed up out of the flood into air. They lay on the steps a moment, shaking, streaming, alive, and then they went up. The stairs gave onto a dry tunnel, low and close, the rock dressed by old hands, and a little way along it the passage forked, and forked again, three or four black mouths opening off it into the dark with no mark to tell one from another. Aelthira’s magelight found the floor of each and the floor told them nothing. They went right, because you had to choose and right was as good as any, and the tunnel ran on a dozen paces and simply stopped, a blind dead end of close stone with nowhere further to go. The air there was old and still and dead. And against the end wall, where the passage gave out, lay the bones.

A man had died here, years ago, at the end of a tunnel that went nowhere, as though he had dragged himself as far from the water as his wound would let him and run out of hall before he ran out of life. The years had taken the rest, so that what remained was a long shape of old bone wrapped in the rotted ruin of a mail shirt, a fine shirt once, riveted rings gone to rust and rags, and it was torn. Across the chest, through the mail, the rings burst outward in a great ragged wound, the metal itself ripped open by something that did not care about metal, and the bones beneath it lay broken in the same line. Whatever had killed this man had reached through good mail like wet paper and torn the life out of him, and he had crawled here after, with that hole in him, into the deepest blind corner of the hall, to die in the dark with his back to the stone and his face toward the only way he had come.

And as the magelight steadied and the three of them stood dripping and staring, the man stood up.

Not the bones. The bones stayed where they were. But a shape rose up out of them, or up out of the air above them, the way mist rises off water, and took the form of the man who had owned them: tall, broad, a warrior in his prime, in a torn mail shirt with a great wound across the chest, his face grey and sorrowful and worn smooth by years of grief, and he looked at the three living people standing in his tomb, and the grief on him cracked open into something almost unbearable, which was hope.

Kaelene’s hand was on her knife. Aelthira had gone still and watchful, reading the shape of the thing the way she read everything. But it was Bram who stepped forward, dripping, unarmed but for the sword on his back he had not reached for, because Bram knew grief when he saw it the way he knew a frightened room, and there was no fight in this dead man, only the years of his waiting.

The ghost spoke, and his voice was the sound of cold water moving in the dark.

He had come here years ago, he told them, when he was the man those bones had been, a warrior with a name and a sword and a purpose: to kill the sorcerer who made this island, and to free the woman the sorcerer had taken, the woman he loved more than daylight, held somewhere below in the dark. He had crossed the lake. He had come down into the cold, and gone in after her with his good mail and his dread sword and his certainty, and he had never reached her. He had never even reached the sorcerer. Something had been waiting for him in the deep of the hall, something the sorcerer kept and could not fully control, a thing of the death-cold and the long fast reach, and it had taken him in the dark before he ever got near the one he came to save. It tore through his mail and his chest and left him to crawl here, up the stair and back into this blind end of the hall, as far from the thing as his ruined body could drag him, to die in the dark knowing he had failed her. And he had been here ever since, unable to rest, because the thing that killed him was still down there, and she, his beloved, was still down there, and he did not know, in all the years of his waiting, whether she lived or died, only that he had not reached her, and could not now, and never would.

“I cannot pass it,” the ghost said. “I tried, after. A dead man trying the same death twice. It does not even notice me now.” He looked at Bram, and the hope in his ruined face was a terrible thing to be the object of. “But you live. You have warmth in you, and weight, and breath, all the things it feeds on, and it will come for you exactly as it came for me. Unless.” He reached up, slow, to his own neck, and lifted from it something that had not been there a moment before, or had always been there, a medallion on a cord, dull metal worked with marks Aelthira’s eyes went straight to and could not quite read. “This warded me. Not enough. Magic slides off it, turns aside, finds no purchase, and the thing down there is more than half made of magic. It did not save me, because I trusted it too far and walked in proud, but it turned the worst of the spellwork that would otherwise have unmade me on the spot, and let me die a man’s death instead of a thing’s.” He held it out, and the dead hand passed it to the living one, and it was solid and cold and real in Bram’s palm. “Wear it. Let it guard a living fighter where it could not guard me. It glows when the death-magic is near, a blue light, faint and then brighter, so you will not be surprised the way I was surprised. And go where I could not, and reach who I could not reach, and kill the thing that killed me, so that I can rest, and so that perhaps you can do the thing I came a hundred miles to do and failed at in an hour.” His voice broke, water on stone. “Her name was Tuviol. If you find her, or what became of her, I would know it. I would know it at last. I have been afraid to know it for years now, and I am so tired of being afraid.”

Bram put the medallion over his head, and it lay against his bare chest, cold, and he closed his hand around it once. “We’ll find the thing,” he said. “And we’ll find her. You have my word, friend, for whatever a living man’s word is worth to a dead one.”

“It is worth everything,” the ghost of Cadal said, very quietly. “It is the only thing that has ever been worth anything.” And he sank back down into his bones, and the grief closed over the hope again, and the dead end was only a blind stub of tunnel with a dead man in it, and the three of them stood dripping in the magelight, and Kaelene was the one, this time, who could not speak, because Rabindranath in his gentle distracted voice had told them, on the chapel step, I dreamed of a man in a torn shirt who has been waiting a very long time, and was glad, and she had not understood it then and she understood it now, and the understanding was cold all the way down.

They went back to the fork and took another of the dark mouths, deeper this time, and that was where they met Okvir, and Okvir was, by contrast and by mercy, almost a relief. They smelled him first, a thick hot reek of old meat and wet fur and worse, rolling up a side passage, and then they heard him, a vast wet rumbling muttering, a creature talking to itself in the dark in a language of grunts and complaints, and Kaelene held up her hand, wait, and they crept up to where the passage opened into a wider warren of chambers, and there he was. A cave troll, ancient and enormous and bad-tempered, grey-skinned and warty and hunched, with arms like fallen trees and small mean red eyes, sitting amid a glorious sprawling mess of a hoard, coins and cups and old armour and bones and bright worthless things all heaped and scattered through the side-rooms, and the troll sat in the middle of it counting something, or pretending to count it, the way a miser pretends, and grumbling, and entirely at home.

“We’re not getting past that quietly,” Bram breathed.

“No,” Kaelene agreed, and her eyes were already moving, already working the angles, because this was a kind of problem she liked, a problem of staging rather than strength. “But look at him. Look how he’s sitting. He’s old and he’s alone and he’s been alone a long time, and a thing that’s been alone and afraid that long has rules. Watch.”

She picked up a stone and tossed it, light, into the dark of a far chamber, and the troll’s head came up like a struck dog’s, and he heaved himself half upright with a speed terrible in something so huge, peering into the dark, sniffing, and he called out, a rough bellowing challenge, how many, how many of you, and when nothing answered he settled back, but slowly, watchful, unhappy.

“There,” Kaelene said softly. “He counts. He won’t fight what he can’t count, and he won’t fight what he thinks is more than a few, because a thing alone in a hole a long time learns that the way to die is to take on more than you can manage. The dwarf-witnesses said it without knowing they said it. He attacks the moment he sees few enough to win against. So.” She smiled in the dark, and it was the old smile, the working smile, the one Tarek would have known. “We don’t be three. We be a great many. And we do it with nothing but noise and shadow, because that’s all a thing in the dark has to go on anyway.” She was already drawing the fat green bottle out of her pack, Mifalodor’s single dose of invisibility, weighing it in her hand. “And one of us he can’t count at all. He won’t see me, only hear me, everywhere at once. He’ll go mad trying to put a number to the room.”

“He’ll smell you,” Bram murmured. “Mifalodor said it doesn’t hide the smell, or the sound.”

“Good,” Kaelene said. “Let him smell a soldier moving where there’s no soldier to see. That’s worse than seeing one.” She pulled the stopper.

What followed was the loudest, strangest, most ridiculous quarter hour of the whole delve, and it was, Bram said afterward, the most fun he’d had since the bear. Aelthira split her magelight, which cost her nothing here, into three, then five, then a dozen guttering moving lights and scattered them through the warren so that the chambers all around the troll were suddenly full of bobbing lamps as though a war-band were closing in from every side at once. Kaelene drank the draught and went out of sight between one blink and the next, and then she was everywhere, a scuff of boots from the left, a cough from a high ledge, a body brushing past a stalagmite on the troll’s blind side close enough that he flinched from a thing he could not see, and she threw her voice on top of it, a trick she had off a strolling player in Jorpagna years gone, so that orders came barking out of the dark from one quarter and answers came back from another, a sergeant here, a bowman there, flank him, hold the passage, wait for the word, a dozen people who were not there and one who was and could not be found. And Bram, in the one patch of light Aelthira left on him, stood up to his full considerable height in the mouth of the main passage with his sword drawn and his chest bare and the dead champion’s medallion glinting on it, and he did the thing Bram did better than anyone alive, which was hold a room, and he addressed the troll as though he were the least of forty.

“OKVIR,” Bram roared, picking the name off nothing, betting on a troll’s vanity, and the bet paid, because the troll started at his own name. “We have you ringed, old worm. Forty blades and four wizards, and we want nothing of yours, you mangy heap, nothing but the road past. Sit down on your gold and let us by, and live another hundred years counting it, or stand up and find out how many of us there are the hard way. Your choice, and choose fast, my archers are cold and my wizards are bored.”

The troll stood up. He stood up to his full height, which was a great deal more than Bram’s, and his red eyes went round the warren counting the lights, one two five a dozen, and the voices barking in the dark, left and right and behind, and the thing he could hear and smell moving among them that his eyes could not land on at all, and the big bare-chested man in the light with the cold blue glint at his throat and not a tremor in him, and Okvir, ancient and alone and not, whatever else, stupid, made the calculation that had kept him alive in this hole a long time. He could not count them. There were too many lights and too many voices and one of them would not be seen, and he could not count what he could not see, and the first rule of being old and alone in the dark is that you do not fight what you cannot count.

He sat back down. He gathered an armful of his hoard against his chest, sullen and enormous and beaten without a blow struck, and he glowered at Bram, and he jerked his great warty head at the passage on the far side of his warren, the way on, go, then, go, and curse you, and he hunched over his gold and would not look at them as they went.

They went, quick and quiet and not pushing their luck, through the warren and out the far side, Kaelene fading back into view a few steps along as the draught wore thin, solid again and grinning, and they did not take so much as a coin, partly because Kaelene said a thing that thinks it lost to forty will tear itself apart looking for the forty later and never think of the three, and partly, Bram admitted, because he had felt a little sorry for the old monster, alone in the dark with his counting, the way you feel sorry for anything that has been afraid by itself for too long. And when they were well clear, in the cold passage beyond, the three of them looked at each other in the magelight, soaked and breathless and grinning despite everything, and Bram let out a long shaking laugh.

“Forty blades and four wizards,” he wheezed. “I’d like to have seen them.”

“You’d have liked the bowmen,” Kaelene said. “They were a fine body of men. Pity they didn’t exist.”

But the warmth of it did not last long, because the passage beyond Okvir’s warren ran downward, and the cold came up to meet them, and it was a different cold from the cold of the water, and at his bare chest, in the dark, Bram felt the dead champion’s medallion grow faintly, faintly warm, and then he looked down and saw it had begun, very softly, to glow.

## Chapter 7. Approach to the Inmost Cave

The passage gave out into a cavern so large the magelight could not find the far side of it, and Cadal’s warning, which had been a story, became a place.

It was a chamber of black water and slick black stone, and a mist hung over it, a low cold pall exactly like the mist on the lake above, as though the lake had reached down a finger into the heart of the rock. It was thick, the fog, thicker than the magelight could fight; it ate the light within a few paces and gave back only grey, so that the room did not show itself all at once but a little at a time, the way the lake had, walled close on every side with pale nothing. What they could see was this: a narrow rim of rock at their feet, no wider than a footpath, running off both ways along the wall and curving out of sight into the fog; and black water beyond it, deep and still, going down past the reach of the light when Aelthira sent the globe out low over the surface, a cold throat in the floor of the world. And out of the rim, leading away from the shore into the grey, a line of low flat stepping stones, wet and uneven, spaced for a long stride or a short jump, that went out a little way over the deep and then vanished where the fog took them. Where they led, what waited at the far end of them, whether there was a far end at all, the fog kept to itself. The cold did not come from the air. It came up off the water, a wet seeping death-cold that climbed into the bones and sat there, and Kaelene, who had crawled out of the flooded passage shaking and had not been warm since, felt it reach a place in her that the rest of the chill had not, and knew, without being told, that this was the room. This was the furniture of the dream. The cold water, the closed dark, the going-down. She had walked the whole long way toward it on purpose, and here it was, and she made herself stand and look at it, because not-looking was the sin she would not commit, and looking at it did not make it smaller but it made it hers.

At Bram’s chest, the medallion glowed. Faint blue, then steadier, then steady, a cold clear light that did not waver, and Bram looked down at it and then up into the mist over the pool and said, low, “It’s here. Whatever the ghost warned us of. It’s in this room with us.”

But it was Aelthira who stopped, a dozen paces in, at the edge of the light, where the cavern wall met the floor, and went absolutely still, because she had seen the thing that the rest of the room had been hiding.

Cut into the natural rock of the cavern floor, clean and deep and deliberate, was a figure, and it was not any figure she knew from common work. Two rings, one inside the other, and both of them broken at the same point, so that the gap ran clean through both like a corridor cut through a wall. Through that gap passed a single straight line that did not stop at the rings but ran on past them to either side, as though it marked the path of something that came from beyond the figure and went out the far side of it and was never meant to stay. Between the two rings, spaced around the band and touching neither, were seven short strokes, like tally-cuts, like teeth. And at the very centre of it, where every binding figure she had ever been shown had its locus, its heart, the node that gathered and held the power, there was nothing. Bare stone. A deliberate, pointed emptiness where the heart of the working should have been. She had no name for it. She gave it one anyway, there in the cold, the way you name a thing to keep it at arm’s length: the Hollow Mark, for the empty middle of it, because the empty middle was the whole of what it meant. Fanning out from it across the floor and up the near wall were scorch-marks, old burns, the rock itself blackened and blistered in long radiating tongues as though something had stood inside the figure and burned outward without consuming itself, and Aelthira thought, unwillingly, of the dwarves’ tale, the shape on the tower licked all over with fire and not burning away, and she understood that they were two ends of the same thing.

She crouched by the edge of the figure, not touching it, the magelight drawn down small and close to her hand, and she read it the way she read everything, and what she read frightened her more than the cold or the dark or the unseen thing in the mist, because it was familiar.

This was not a hedge-wizard’s trick. It was not the small reliable craft she had learned, the finding and the lights and the locks. It was the other thing, the deep, deliberate, structured work the Academy had pages on, pages kept in the parts of the library a student was not given a key to. Aelthira had found her way to them anyway, in the months before she left, and they had been most of the reason she left, because what was on them did not match what she’d been taught at the front of the room, and the mismatch had a name, and the name was a lie. She had seen this exact figure once before, and only once, the broken double ring, the line running through, the seven cuts, the hollow heart, drawn small on a single page behind that locked door and explained away in a careful hand as a thing that did not work and had never worked and was recorded only so that it might be known and dismissed. And here it was, cut a full hand deep into the floor of a drowned hall a thousand miles from any school, by a dead sorcerer who could not possibly have read the Academy’s books and had drawn its most carefully buried secret into the rock as casually as a mason lays a course.

Which meant the secret was not the Academy’s at all. The Academy had only buried it. It was older than the Academy and wider than the Academy, and it was real, and it worked, and someone had used it here to reach through the thin skin of the world for something he could not control, and the something had answered, and this scorched and silent hall was what answering had cost him. She could read that much in the burn-marks alone. And she could read one thing more, and it was the thing that made her go cold: the scorching here was not old. The Hollow Mark was old, cut and recut by hand after hand over a span she could not guess, but the last burning, the great one that had fanned these fresh black tongues up the wall, had happened days ago, not lifetimes. The soot had not yet settled. Whoever had spent his life in this place reaching through the thin skin had made one reaching too many, and very recently, and it had turned on him and unmade him where he stood, and the fire of it would have stood up tall and red over the island for any eye on the lake to see. She thought of the dwarves, soaked and shaking in the Three Stags, the fireshape on the tower and the sky the color of a wound, two days gone. They had not seen a monster. They had seen a death. The master of this hall had died burning the very week the trio set out, and the island, leaderless, had floated up out of the mist with its larder full and its guardian unguarded, the cheap, abundant, wonderful magic Mifalodor had warned her of, the door left standing open, and on the far side of the door a thing that came through and could not be sent back.

She said none of it. She crouched a moment longer, fixing every line of the figure in her memory the way she fixed everything, and then she rose and stepped back from it, and when Bram, watching her face, asked, “Anything?”, she said only, “Old work, freshly sprung. Whoever ran this place died here, and not long ago. I’ll want to look at it properly on the way out, but not now,” and she let the reader of her own life file it in the right column where the cold true things went, and said no more, because there was a thing in the mist over the pool waiting to kill them and now was not the hour for the longest, oldest secret she had ever found.

“We go out along these stones,” Kaelene said, reading the line of them at her feet, the only path the fog would give them. “The dead man said it can’t leave its place, that it’s bound to a stone out there somewhere in the water, and that’s the only reason he lived long enough to crawl off and die. So we trust that. Somewhere ahead of us, in the grey, there’s a thing that can’t come off its perch, and a reach that can, and we don’t yet know how long that reach is. So we string out, single file, and we never bunch, and we never stand where one swing could take two of us. Bram in front. You’re the one it’ll want, and you carry the light it can’t touch, so you keep its eyes on you. Me behind you, low, close enough to sting it and drag its head round, never close enough to die. And Aelthira.” She turned. “You stay at the back, far out near the shore, far as you can stand and still throw, and you do not take one step in past me, do you understand me, not for anything. Whatever you can do, you do it from where it cannot reach you.”

“You’ll be in its reach and you’ve no medallion,” Aelthira said, and held out the flat grey bottle, Mifalodor’s warding draught, the antimagi. “Bram has the ghost’s ward. I’ll be too far back to want one. You’re the one who’ll be close and unguarded. Drink it.”

Kaelene looked at the bottle a moment, and did not argue, because the reading was right and there was no time to be brave about it. She drank the grey draught down, and it tasted of cold iron and nothing, and she felt it settle into her like a held breath, the small wrongness Mifalodor had promised, herself turned for a little while into poor soil for any spell to take root in. “Poor soil,” she said, and almost smiled. “Let it try to plant anything in me.”

“I understand you,” Aelthira said.

So they went in along the one arm, single file over the cold black deep, three small points of warmth strung out toward the fog-drowned centre where the stone waited. Bram led, his sword drawn and low in his fist, the steady blue glow at his chest going out ahead of him into the mist; Kaelene came low and silent a few stones behind him; Aelthira held the back of the arm out near the rim, her cheap terrible power ready in her open hands. And they started in toward the middle, stone by stone, and waited for what the medallion was warning them of to show itself.

It did not make them wait long.

## Chapter 8. The Ordeal

They never saw the first blow coming, and that was the whole of why it landed.

The mist over the pool was too thick to see across. It hung in a low cold pall that ate the magelight within a few paces and gave back nothing, so that the far side of the water, the centre of it, the low stone where the four arms met, were all of them only grey, a wall of pale nothing exactly like the wall that had ringed the boat out on the lake. They could not see what waited in it. They could only feel the cold of it climbing, and watch the medallion at Bram’s chest, which told them what their eyes could not: it flared, faint blue to bright to a hard clear blaze, brighter than it had glowed in the warren or the passage or anywhere. Bram looked down at it, and then up into the blind grey, and he barely had the breath to say it was close.

And the reach came out of the fog.

There was no shape to it, no warning, no demon to see and set himself against. Only the mist tearing open as something impossibly long and impossibly fast came lashing out of the grey at the height of a man’s chest, an arm too long and jointed wrong, and at the end of it not a hand but a great pincer, a crab’s claw grown to the size of a man’s two fists, black and chitinous and snapping shut with a crack like green wood breaking, there and on him before any of the three had so much as glimpsed the thing it belonged to. The medallion did its work in that same instant, and that work was the only reason Bram lived past it. He felt the death-magic come off the strike like a wave of black cold meant to stop his heart where it beat, felt it hit the medallion and slide, turn aside, find no purchase, exactly as Cadal had promised, the spell that had unmade better men breaking apart against the dull worked metal at his throat. But the medallion warded the magic, not the blow. The long arm and the snapping claw took him across the body and lifted him off his feet, shieldless, light, with nothing to set against a thing he had never even seen, and flung him off his stone and out over the deep, and the cold black water came up and swallowed him whole.

He went down with the sword still in his fist, the sword he’d drawn and never got to swing. The cold was a fist of its own that drove the air out of him, and the dark closed over his head, and somewhere in the first wild moment of it, the wrenching and the flailing and the cold clamping his hands to uselessness, the grip turned in his fingers and the blade was gone, torn out of his hold and spinning away down into the black throat of the pool, down past the reach of any light, gone for good. He clawed. He had no shield and now no sword and no air and the cold was pulling him down, and somewhere above him in the world of air a thing was screaming, and Bram, who had walked into this island lighter than he had been since he was a boy, found himself lighter still, stripped down to a man and a knife and a will, and he used the will. He clawed up through the cold and the black toward the faint blue glow that was the medallion still at his own chest, the only light in that drowning dark, and when he broke the surface gasping he came up not where he had gone in but on the far side of the centre stone, clear across the pool from the others, where the blow had flung him. He dragged himself, hand over hand, half-drowned and shaking and weaponless, up onto the nearest stone of one of the far arms, behind the thing on its island, and he lay there for one second heaving water, and then he got up, because the others were still in it, and because he was, for the moment, the one place in the room the demon was not looking.

While Bram was under, the others saw the thing whole at last, because its own lunge betrayed it. The strike that took Bram had carried the demon out to the very limit of its reach and snapped it short there, the way a dog hits the end of a chain, and the mist tore around the recoil, and there, on the low central stone where the four arms of stepping stones met, an island barely larger than itself, stood Akrae. It stood upright on two legs, tall, taller than Cadal’s ghost had been, a man’s frame in its broad shoulders and its lean trunk and the way it set its feet, and everything above the neck and at the ends of the arms was wrong. Its head was a crab’s head, a smooth black carapace with no face in it, and from the front of it two eyes stood out on jointed stalks, swivelling, and below them a cluster of small hard mouthparts worked and worked without a sound. Its hands were not hands. Each arm ended in a great claw, a pincer of black shell longer than a man’s forearm, the kind of thing that does not cut so much as shear, and the long reach that had taken Bram was one of them. Behind the swivelling stalked eyes there was an absence worse than malice, because malice is a kind of attention and this was only hunger, vast and patient and without any bottom. Round the stone, cut into it and glowing now with a sick white light, was a binding, a working circle of close-written marks, and it was not the Hollow Mark, it was the ordinary deep craft, the kind of figure made to cage a thing and hold it, and as the demon hauled itself back from its overreach the lines flared and held it, and Aelthira had been right and Kaelene had been right and the dwarves had been right: it was bound. It could not leave the stone. Its reach was long, longer than its body, long enough to take a man off a stone before he ever saw it come, but the reach ended, and the end of it was a chain, and that chain was the only reason three living people could hope to live out the next few minutes.

So they did not waste the second he had bought them.

Aelthira, far out at the back of the arm, beyond the longest stretch of the demon’s reach, had opened her hands and begun to spend, and the island spent with her, cheap and bright and terrible. She did not have to husband anything here, and so she did not, she poured it out, fire and force and hammering light, not at the demon’s hide which she could see was made for turning such things, but at the stone under it, at the footing of its island, at the glowing lines of the binding, anything to make the thing flinch and turn and reach for her instead, anything to keep its eyeless face toward the front of the room. And it worked, because the thing was hungry and she was warm and bright and loud with power, and it wheeled toward her, arms lashing out over the black water, and was hauled up short again at the chain of its reach, screaming, a hand’s breadth short of where she stood, and she did not flinch and she did not step in, she held her ground at the very edge of its reach and burned it and held its attention forward and away, and she felt, under the fear, a thing she would have to think about later and be afraid of differently, which was how good it felt to spend without counting the cost.

And Kaelene worked the edges. She was low and silent and everywhere it was not looking, and she did the small cheap dangerous things that win fights the strong cannot win alone. A stone thrown hard from the dark cracked off the demon’s carapace from the side, and its head snapped round toward the impact, the eyestalks whipping about, toward nothing, away from Aelthira for half a heartbeat. A shout in a voice that was not where she was, the strolling player’s trick again, came barking from a black corner, and the head wheeled again, hunting a thing that was not there. Once she misjudged it, drawn a half-step too close on a slick stone, and the demon caught her there, not with a claw but with the death-cold itself, a black wave of unmaking that broke over her and should have stopped her heart on the spot the way it had stopped Cadal’s. It found poor soil. Mifalodor’s grey draught held, the spell sliding off her the way it slid off the medallion, finding nothing in her to take root in, and she felt only a deep wrong chill pass through and let go, and she did not die, and she did not waste the gift of it, she was already moving again. A flicker of motion at the very edge of the blue light, a thrown rag, a tossed pebble skittering, anything to drag that hungry attention round and round so that it never had two full seconds to spend on any one of them, so that the long fast reach was always a half-beat behind where it needed to be. She read its rhythm the way she read a room, found the count of it, the wind-up and the strike and the recovery, and she stole half-beats out of the middle of it, and every halfbeat she stole was a half-beat the demon spent confused instead of killing, and the halfbeats added up, and they were what Bram needed.

Because Bram, dragged half-drowned out of the pool on the wrong side of it, with nothing left but the knife at his hip and the medallion at his throat, looked across the centre stone at the thing screaming and wheeling and lashing toward the front of the room, its long back turned to him, hunting two ghosts and burning under a wizard’s fire, and he understood the shape of the only way through. It could not leave the stone. Its reach was long, but it had an inside, and its face was turned the wrong way. Get in behind it, close, right in against the thing where the long arms, flung out toward the others, could not fold back to catch him, in past the blades to the body, and a man with a knife had a chance a man with a sword would never need to take. It was the most dangerous thing he had ever decided to do and he decided it in about a second, the way he decided everything, and then he was moving, low and fast in from stone to stone along the far arm at the demon’s back, while Kaelene threw her stones and Aelthira threw her fire and the two of them held its eyeless face turned away from him, and from the last stepping stone before the centre, the one at the back of Akrae’s island, Bram jumped.

He landed behind it, inside the reach, in against the cold wrong body where the long arms, stretched out toward Kaelene and Aelthira at the front of the room, could not fold back fast enough to find a man at the demon’s spine. At the last instant it felt him come, and one great claw snapped back over its own shoulder and caught him, the pincer closing on his shoulder and across his back with a force that ground the bone and would have sheared clean through a thinner man, and did not stop him, because by then he was already inside it and his knife was already moving. He got an arm up over the black carapace and hauled the heavy head back, baring the soft pale seam where the shell of it met the body, the one place on the whole horror that was not armour, and he drove the knife into that seam and ripped, two-handed at the finish, the whole of his weight and his terror and his will behind it, a man who had lost his shield and his sword and come up out of the cold black water on the wrong side of a demon with nothing but a knife, killing it from behind with his feet planted on the thing’s own stone, and the screaming rose to a pressure that nearly burst their skulls, and then it stopped.

The demon came apart. Not like a thing dying; like a thing being sent back, unmade, the long body folding in on itself and going to a black smoke that the cold drew down into the binding in the stone, into the glowing lines, which flared once, blinding, and went dark, and were only old scratches in rock. The cold lifted. The pressure was gone. And Bram stood swaying on the dark stone island in the middle of the black pool, bleeding from his back and his shoulder where the claw had clamped down on him, his knife still in his fist, alive, and he looked across the still water at the two of them and tried to grin and did not quite manage it, and said, in a voice that shook, “Tell Cadal it’s done.”

It had cost them. Kaelene came out across the stones to him and bound his shoulder with strips of her own shirt, fast and tight, the deep punched wounds where the pincer had closed welling dark, and Aelthira came in at last from the end of her arm, hands trembling now with the after-shake of all she’d spent and all it had not cost her, and between them they got Bram off the centre stone and back along the stones to the rim, and gave him a pull of Mifalodor’s healing elixir, a swallow of the dark stuff, and watched the worst of the bleeding slow. He would carry the scars. But he would carry them living, and that was the trade they had come to make.

And then, because the way on led deeper, and because they had given a dead man their word, they crossed the last of the cavern’s stones and went on into the lower hall to find what Cadal could not, and the loud triumph of the killing fell away behind them, and the heaviest part of the whole book began.

The way on changed under their feet not far past the cavern. The wild rock gave way to worked stone, cut and dressed and squared by hands, the walls going from the blind roughness of a cave to the deliberate straight lines of a building, and that was the first thing, and the worst thing, because it meant the hall below was not a thing the island had grown but a thing a man had made, on purpose, for use. A short passage of that dressed stone brought them into a room, the first true room of the lower hall, square and low and empty, and out of it three dark doorways led away: one to the left, one straight ahead, one to the right. The right-hand door, when Aelthira put her light to it, gave onto a fall of collapsed stone a few feet in, a way that went nowhere, and they let it be. That left two. And from the left-hand doorway came the smell of old straw and older grief, a long row of doors going off down a corridor, and the doors were cells.

It was a prison, but not a prison meant to hold. It was a larder. The sorcerer had kept people down here not to keep them but to spend them, one at a time, fed into whatever he had been reaching for, and they went left, because of the word they had given a dead man, and the third door they came to had a name to it that they did not learn from any sign, but from the small terrible patience of the things left inside it.

It was Tuviol’s cell. They knew before they knew. There was a pallet gone to rot, and a cup, and on the wall a tally of days scratched in patient rows, weeks of them, a season and more, the marks of someone counting out a captivity one day at a time and hoping each day to be the last. And then the counting stopped. Not trailed off, the way a tally stops when the hand that keeps it grows too weak or too hopeless to go on. It stopped clean, in the middle of a row, on an ordinary day no different from the one before it, the last stroke cut as firm and even as the first. Something had ended the count from the outside. And the cell was empty. There was no body in it, and the empty cell was worse than any body could have been, because a body would have meant she died here, in her own time, in this small dark room, and the clean-cut tally on the wall meant the other thing instead: that one ordinary morning the door had opened and they had come for her, and taken her out, and she had never come back to finish her count, because there was nothing left of her to come back. They had taken her to the working. They had given her to the fire, the way the sorcerers of this place gave everything the larder ever held, fuel for the reaching, and she had gone from this cell to the Hollow Mark and was nowhere now at all, long years gone, while the man who loved her lay dead in the dark two levels above, killed before he ever reached the corridor her door opened on, the two of them ending within a stone’s throw of each other and never knowing.

And below the broken tally, low on the wall, where she must have knelt to cut it, was the other thing, the thing that named her and broke them: a message, scratched deep into the stone over what must have been hours, the letters small and careful and even, the work of someone with nothing left but time and a wall and one thing she needed to say before the end. I will always love you, Cadal. Let our souls meet in paradise. Tuviol. She had not known he was already dead two levels above her. She had cut his name into the rock as a vow and a hope, addressed to a living man she believed might still come, and he had come, and never reached this corridor, and the name she carved had been the name of a corpse before the chisel of her fingernail ever touched the stone.

Nobody spoke. There was nothing to say that the cell had not already said. Kaelene stood in the doorway and could not make herself go in. Aelthira went in, because someone had to, and crouched at the wall with her fingers under the small carved letters, the decoder of the company reading the one inscription she would have given anything not to understand, a dead woman’s last words of love to a man who was already dead when she wrote them. She read it once, silently, and then she did the only thing there was to do, which was read it again aloud, low, into the cold, so that the words had been said by a living voice at least once in a hundred years. I will always love you, Cadal. Let our souls meet in paradise. She bowed her head. Her name was Tuviol, Cadal had said. If you find her, or what became of her, I would know it. They had found what became of her. And they would have to carry it back up two levels and give it to the man who had been afraid to know it for years, and there was no version of the telling that was not a cruelty, and no version of not-telling that was a kindness either.

The next cell along made it worse still, because the next cell had a message in it.

It was scratched into the wall, low down, near the floor, in letters made by a fingernail or a sharpened stone over what must have been hours, days, the desperate patient work of someone with nothing left but time and a wall, and Aelthira read it out, slowly, in the magelight, her voice flat and careful and not quite steady. The man who had been held in this cell had known he was going to die; he had known he was being kept to be sacrificed, fuel for the sorcerer’s reaching, and before the end he had spent his last strength carving a wish into the stone. That whoever found these words would carry them out into the world, and seek out a certain warrior, a mighty man, the owner of a sword of dread, named here in the scratched letters: Cadal. And tell that warrior where his beloved Tuviol was imprisoned, in the cells beneath the island, so that he might come for her, and save her, before it was too late.

The man who carved it had been Tuviol’s brother. A prince, by the few proud words he gave himself even at the end. Emrys. He had been thrown into this cell to die so that the sorcerer might reach through the thin skin of the world, and his last act had been not a curse on the man killing him but a message for the man who loved his sister, a hope flung blind into the dark on the chance that someone, someday, would find it and carry it far enough.

And the three of them stood in the cold cell in the magelight and understood, all at once, the full cruelty of it, because they knew how every line of that hope had already failed at the moment it was written. Cadal had come. The message never reached him, but he had come anyway, on his own, and died in the dark two levels up before he ever reached the cells, long years gone. Tuviol had been taken from the next cell and given to the working, sacrificed, while her brother scratched a plea for a rescuer who was already dead. And Emrys had carved that hope into the wall begging a rescue that had failed at every link before the first letter was cut. Three people reaching for one another in the dark, the warrior and the sister and the brother, and not one of them ever reaching, all of them slain or burned a lifetime ago and a few short steps apart. The silence after them had lasted generation into generation, the hall staying hungry, the sorcerers coming and going and feeding it, until three strangers from a forest inn rowed across a haunted lake and read the whole tragedy off the walls in a single afternoon.

After the screaming and the fire and the killing, the quiet of those two cells was the heaviest thing any of them had ever stood inside, and they stood inside it, because it deserved to be stood inside, and because the dead deserved at least that someone, finally, had come, and read their names, and understood.

It was Kaelene who said it, at last, very low, in the doorway she had not been able to cross. “The sword,” she said. “The dread sword. The brother’s message names it. Cadal’s blade.” She looked back the way they had come, up the corridor of cells toward the empty room where the three doors met, and the straight door they had not yet taken. “The sorcerer took it off his body when he killed him. It’ll be down there, wherever that far door goes. Whatever the dead man came in carrying, it’s still here.” She drew a breath that shook. “Bram went into the water with a sword and came out with a knife. And there’s a sword down there that a dead prince named years ago, in a plea scratched on a wall that never reached the man it was meant for. Cadal’s own blade.” She turned to look at the two of them in the cold blue light, and her face was wet, and steady. “Let’s finish it. Let’s get him his answer, and his beloved’s rest, and his sword into the hand of a living fighter who’ll carry it back up into the daylight. It’s the only thing left down here we can still make come out right.”

And they went on, down the last dark corridor, toward the gate and the riddles and the drowned hoard and the dread blade waiting in it, carrying the weight of two empty cells and one dead man’s long-buried hope, down into the deepest part of the cold that stays.

## Chapter 9. The Last Gate

They went back up the corridor of cells to the empty room where the three ways met, and took the door they had left for last, the one straight ahead, and it ran down, and the worked stone grew finer the deeper it went, dressed and squared and then, at the bottom, polished, so that the magelight ran along the walls in long cold gleams, and the air lost the smell of the cells and took on something else, a dry hot scent of burning that had no business this far under cold water. Kaelene smelled it first and stopped, and the three of them stood at the mouth of the last chamber and looked in, and the chamber was a dome.

It was a perfect dome of dark marble, black shot through with veins of red, and it had been made, not found, every line of it true, a place built for one purpose by someone who cared a great deal how that purpose looked. And in the centre of the dome, where the floor was a circle of paler stone, sat the thing that kept the gate.

It was vast, and beautiful, and on fire. It stood upright, man-shaped the way Akrae had been man-shaped and no more truly a man than Akrae was, a towering figure half again Bram’s height, broad and long-limbed, its hide a deep red-bronze that the flames moved over without burning. Two enormous wings of fire rose from its shoulders, real flame, licking and curling and throwing moving shadows around the dome, and they did not catch the marble and did not gutter and did not consume the wings they were. Set on those wide shoulders was the head of a lion, maned in fire, the flames standing off it like a sun’s corona, the great jaws and the heavy brow unmistakable above that upright body. Its eyes were old, and worse than old, they were bored, the deep settled boredom of a thing that has sat in one place a very long time doing one thing and grown tired of how easy that one thing has become. It watched the three small wet people come into the light of its fire. It did not move. And when it spoke its voice was warm and cultured and entirely without mercy.

“Three,” said Khaa. “How generous. It is usually one, half-mad with the dark by the time it reaches me, and the conversation is poor. Three at once, and walking in on their own feet, and not yet weeping. I shall enjoy you.”

“We’re only passing through,” Bram said, because someone had to say something, and because he had learned that the way to deal with a thing that meant to frighten you was to refuse to be on its schedule. “We don’t want anything of yours. There’s a chamber beyond you we mean to reach, and then we’ll be out of your fire and out of your hair, or your flames, as it were, and you can get back to being bored in peace.”

The fire-wings rippled, which might have been amusement. “Everyone is only passing through,” Khaa said. “That is the entire trouble with being a gate. No one comes to see me. They come to see what is behind me, and they resent me for standing in the way, and I resent them for it, and so we have our little arrangement, which is this.” It drew itself up to its full towering height, the wings spreading wide, and the dome filled with the heat of it, and the moving shadows leapt. “I ask. You answer. Three questions, no more, no fewer, and you will answer them rightly, all three, or I will eat you, which I will also enjoy, and rather more, because at least it is a change. You do not get to decline. You do not get to leave the way you came; that door is mine now, you stepped through it. Answer, or be answered for. Are we agreed?”

“Do we have a choice?” Kaelene said.

“No,” Khaa said, pleased. “I find that is the most honest kind of agreement.” It folded itself down again to sit, the burning wings settling along its back, and the boredom came back over its old eyes like a film, and it said, “The first.”

And it asked its riddle, and the riddle was this:

I am the oldest guest in every house, older than the hearth and older than the door. No wall has kept me out and no lock has held me, and yet one small flame can drive me into the corners to wait. I come for the king and the beggar the same, I cover the whole world and weigh nothing at all, and however many times you send me away, I am patient, and I always come again. What am I?

It was Aelthira’s. They had agreed nothing aloud, the three of them, and they did not need to; she felt it land in her the way a question lands in a scholar, the small clean click of a thing she had read about and thought about and turned over in the long quiet hours, and she did not rush it, because rushing was how clever people got eaten, but she did not doubt it either. She thought of the forbidden pages and the long nights by a single candle, the candle that held the dark off only as far as its little circle and no further, and she had it.

“Night,” she said. “The dark. It is older than any fire and any house, it passes any wall, a candle drives it to the edges of a room but cannot kill it, it falls on everyone alike, it has no weight, and it comes again every evening no matter how many mornings have sent it off. The answer is night.”

The fire-wings stilled. Khaa looked at her for a long moment with its bored old eyes, and something moved behind them that was almost respect and almost disappointment, the look of a thing that had hoped, a little, to be fed.

“Night,” it agreed. “Yes. Well answered, and quickly. The scholar.” It let the word sit, having read her in a breath the way she read everything. “The second.”

And it asked again:

I am born in the eye and I die on the cheek. I come out of laughing and out of grieving both, and I cannot tell you which one made me. I carry salt, but I am no sea. I fall, but I break nothing. And I say the thing the mouth has decided not to. What am I?

This one was Kaelene’s, and they all knew it, because it was not a scholar’s riddle at all. It was a reading. It was a small careful watching of the thing a person tries to hide and gives away anyway, and that was Kaelene’s whole craft, the craft Tarek had drilled into her, the craft that read Ulvar in four seconds and the rigged silence of a frightened village and the wind-up of a demon’s strike. She had spent her life learning to see the thing the mouth would not say, and the riddle was describing her own trade back to her.

“A tear,” she said. “It starts in the eye and runs down the cheek and dries there. It comes from joy and from sorrow both and you can’t always tell from the tear alone which one it was. It’s salt without being the sea. It falls and nothing breaks. And it tells the truth the person was trying not to tell.” She looked the burning thing in its old eyes without flinching, because she had decided at a window in Outskirt that she was done flinching. “The answer is a tear.”

“A tear,” said Khaa, very softly, and the boredom in its eyes deepened toward something that might, in a creature with a heart, have been sorrow. “Yes. The watcher. You see the small things.” It was quiet a moment, and the fire breathed. “Two,” it said. “Two and clean, and I begin to think I shall go hungry tonight, which would make twice now, and I do not care for the number two.” Its gaze moved, slow, and came to rest, at last, on Bram. “But there are three of you. And the scholar has had hers, and the watcher hers, and that leaves the storyteller, the big one, the one with the dead man’s light on his chest and no cleverness in his face at all.” The wings flared, eager again, the boredom burning off in the hope of a meal. “I have saved my best for you, big one. And I do not think your friends can help you, for if they speak your answer, the bargain is broken, and I eat all three. So it is yours alone. Listen.”

And Khaa asked the third riddle, and it was the hardest, and it was Bram’s, and there was nothing in the world Aelthira and Kaelene could do but stand very still and not make a sound and watch him meet it alone:

Men have crossed deserts to find me, and drowned in cold water reaching for me. They have murdered their brothers for me, and sold their daughters, and broken every oath they ever swore. I do not eat and I give no warmth. I cannot close a wound or fill a winter. I have been loved more fiercely than any living thing that ever breathed, and never once, in all the long ages of the world, have I loved a single one of them back. What am I?

The silence after it was the loudest thing in the dome.

Bram stood in the firelight with the cold blue medallion glowing faint on his chest, dripping, hurt, weaponless, and Kaelene watched his face and felt her heart climb into her throat, because she could see him reaching, and she could see all the wrong places the reaching might go. It was the kind of riddle that ate clever people, she realised, because it was built to. A clever person heard it and reached for something grand and abstract, the way her own mind had leapt before she caught it: a false friend, a faithless lover, glory, beauty, power, death, the world itself. A hundred poetic answers fit some of it and not all of it, and the trap was that the longer you thought, the more of them you found. Khaa was watching for exactly that, the flicker of a mind getting lost in its own cleverness, and it would call any of those wrong, and it would be right to.

But Bram was not a clever man, and for once in his life that was going to save all three of them.

He had stopped reaching. Kaelene saw it happen, saw his face go still and then settle, and she understood that he had simply stopped trying to be cleverer than he was and gone instead to the thing he actually knew. Because Bram had spent his whole road-life among treasure-seekers and sell-swords and men who chased shining things, and he had watched, with his easy storyteller’s eye, exactly what those men would do for the shining thing and exactly what the shining thing did for them in return, which was nothing, ever, at all. He had seen it on a dozen roads. He was about to see it again in the next room. There was no poetry in it. There was no trick. There was only the plain flat thing he had watched men ruin themselves over his entire life, and he said it the way he would have said the sky was up.

“Gold,” Bram said. “It’s gold. Men cross deserts and drown for it and kill their own blood for it and break every promise they ever made, and it can’t warm you and it can’t feed you and it can’t close a wound, and they love it more than they love anything that loves them, and it has never once loved a single one of them back. There’s nothing clever in the answer. It’s just gold. I’ve watched men do all of those things for a heap of it, and I’ve never once seen the heap do anything for them in return.” He almost smiled, tired and grim. “I’m fairly sure there’s a heap of it in the next room, in fact, and I’d bet my life it’s loved no one either. The answer’s gold.”

For a long moment Khaa did not move, and the fire of its wings burned low and sullen, and the old bored eyes looked at the big bleeding storyteller who had walked past every clever trap in the riddle and gone straight to the bottom of it on plain hard sense, and the demon was, at last and unmistakably, disappointed, the way a thing is disappointed when it had set out its finest meal and the meal declined to be eaten.

“Gold,” said Khaa, and sighed, a long hot gust that stirred the magelight. “Yes. Of course. Gold.” It rose, and stepped aside from the open way, off the circle of paler stone where it had stood, and the stone darkened and cooled, and the way beyond stood open, a low arch into deeper dark. “Three,” the demon said, not looking back. “Three, and clean, and I shall be hungry and bored a while longer, and serve me right for hoping. Go on, then. Take your gold, big one, since you understand it so well. And when you are old, and you find that the understanding never once stopped you wanting it, you may come back and sit with me, and we will be bored together, you and I, two things that knew better and wanted it anyway.” It lowered itself to sit against the wall of the dome, and folded its burning wings about it, and closed its ancient eyes, and was a fixture of the place again, a guard with nothing to guard against, and they went past it quickly and quietly and did not breathe again until the firelight was behind them.

## Chapter 10. The Reward

The treasure chamber was the heart of the hall, and the sorcerer had kept his hoard the way a miser keeps a secret, behind one last difficulty.

The chamber was split in two by a gap, a black crack in the floor that ran the width of the room, narrow but deep, the magelight finding no bottom to it, only cold air rising and the smell of the deep. On the near side stood the three of them. On the far side, raised a little, on a low platform of the same paler stone Khaa had stood on, lay the hoard, and it was a true dragon’s-dream of a hoard, gold and silver heaped and spilling, cups and chains and coin and old worked things, plate and rings and the bright worthless treasures of a dead man’s greed, glinting in the magelight, and laid across the gap, from the near side to the platform, was a single broad plank of dark old wood, worn smooth in the middle as though by long use, the way over.

And on the hoard, half-buried in the gold where it had been thrown down and left, lay a sword.

Bram saw it before any of them, the way a thirsty man sees water. It was a longsword, a one-handed blade of good length and fine balance, and its blade was black, not rustedblack but made-black, a pitch-black first-class steel that drank the magelight rather than giving it back, an edge on it you could see was wicked even across the gap, and down the fuller ran a line of runes that caught what little light the blade refused, glinting a dull red, and Bram looked at it and felt the loss of his own sword in the cold pool come back like a missing tooth, and he wanted it, plainly and simply, the way he had just told Khaa men want gold.

“That’s it,” Aelthira said quietly, beside him, reading the runes from across the gap with her trained eye, and her voice was careful. “That’s a working blade, and the working’s in the runes. They’re words, Bram. A spell bound into the steel, the kind you wake by reading aloud. I can’t read them from here and I’m not sure I want to, but I know the shape of what they do. It’s a summoning. Spoken, it calls something through to fight for the hand that holds the sword, and then sends it back.” She was quiet a moment. “And the make of it is old. Old as the cells. Old as the Hollow Mark. Emrys’s message named a dread sword and named the warrior it belonged to, and that is Cadal’s blade. The sorcerer took it off his body when he killed him, and locked it down here with everything else he stole, and it has lain in this gold a long age waiting for someone to carry it back up into the daylight.” She looked at Bram, at the cold blue medallion on his chest, at the empty sheath on his back, and the symmetry of it was not lost on her, or on any of them. “The man who carries Cadal’s medallion is about to carry Cadal’s sword. I don’t think that’s chance. I don’t think much in this hall is chance.”

“Then let’s go and get it,” Bram said, and started for the plank.

“Stop,” said Kaelene, and the flatness in her voice stopped him with one foot raised, because it was the voice she used when she had read something and the something was deadly.

She crouched at the near end of the plank and looked at it the way she looked at everything, and she did not touch it. “Look at the wear,” she said, low. “It’s smooth in the middle. Worn smooth, like a thing that’s been crossed a hundred times. But there’s no other way into this room, and nobody’s been down here in years to wear a plank smooth crossing for gold, because Khaa eats them at the gate and the cells are full of the ones who never even got that far.” She traced the air above the plank’s near end, where it rested on the lip of the gap. “It’s worn smooth because the sorcerer wanted it to look used. Look there. The far end doesn’t sit on the platform. It rests on a stone that rests on nothing, balanced, and the moment a real weight comes onto the middle of this plank, the far end lifts and the whole thing tips and rolls, and whatever’s standing on it goes down into that crack, and the plank settles back the way it was, smooth and innocent, ready for the next greedy fool. It’s a deadfall. It’s the cleverest thing in this whole hall, because it’s the only one that doesn’t look like anything at all. He didn’t guard his gold with a monster. He guarded it with a plank, and the plank’s killed more men than the monster ever did, I’d wager.” She stood, and stepped back from it, and there was a grim satisfaction on her face, the craftswoman’s pleasure in having seen the seam. “So we don’t walk it.”

“It’s a fair gap,” Bram said, measuring it with his eye, “but it’s a gap. Not a chasm. With a run at it.”

“With a run at it,” Kaelene agreed. “All three of us, one at a time, clear of the plank entirely, land on the platform on the gold side, and let the trap close on nobody at all. We don’t give it our weight. We don’t give it anything.” She looked at him, hurt and tired and shieldless and weaponless and wanting that black blade so plainly it ached. “You first, big as you are, so if you come up short we’ve two of us on this side to haul you. But you won’t come up short. Run.”

He ran, and he jumped, and for one long terrible heartbeat he was out over the black crack with nothing under him, and then he came down hard on the far platform among the spilling gold, went to one knee in it, and was up, whole, on the hoard side. The plank had not so much as trembled, because no weight had touched its middle. Kaelene came next, light and clean as a cat, barely seeming to push off at all, landing soft beside him. And Aelthira last, and Aelthira cleared it most easily of the three. Whatever the long human centuries had taken from her people, they had not taken this: she crossed the gap the way the wood elves had crossed the forest floor, barely touching the ground at either end, a long unhurried stride out over the dark that made the deadly jump look like stepping over a puddle, and she landed soft and balanced and unbothered on the gold beside the other two. Behind them the deadfall plank lay smug and untouched, having closed on exactly nothing.

And Bram bent down, and took up the Deathbringer.

It came out of the gold heavy and cold and right, the grip filling his hand as though it had been waiting, which Aelthira would later think it more or less had. The runes along the black blade caught the magelight and held it, a line of dull red letters in the lightless steel. They did not wake, because no one read them, and that was its own kind of mercy. Whatever slept in the words went on sleeping. But the weight was real, and the edge was real, and the black length of it was a sword again in a sword-hand, and Bram held it up in the dark of the treasure chamber, Cadal’s blade in a living grip after a hundred years, and he laughed, low and amazed, the storyteller getting, at the very bottom of the worst day of his life, the one impossible thing.

“I went into that water with a sword and came out with a knife,” he said. “And I’m coming up out of this hole with a great black sword that a dead champion carried before me. I’ll take the trade.” He looked at it, at the dull red letters in the steel, and the laugh quieted into something graver. “Cadal’s blade. We’ll tell him it’s found. We’ll tell him a living man’s carrying it back up into the sun.”

“Don’t read those aloud,” Aelthira said, low, and Bram looked up at her. “Not for a dare, not to show a tavern, not ever, until you understand them, and maybe not then. I told you it was a summoning. As near as I can make the shape of it, the words call fire through. Living fire, things of the other side that wear the shape of flame, that do a wielder’s killing for a little while and then go home. That’s a true gift in a hard corner. It’s also a door, and you saw what came through the last door anyone opened in this hall.” She held his eyes. “So carry it. Use the edge, it’s a fine edge. But leave the words asleep until we know a great deal more than we do tonight.” Bram looked at the runes a long moment, then nodded, slow, and did not argue, because he had stood in two cold cells an hour ago and learned what this place did with the things it called through.

It was, Kaelene thought, watching him, the exact opposite of the nameless knight’s grave in the skeleton village, the grave they had found and honoured and left untouched, the sword they hadn’t taken because it wasn’t theirs and the dead had earned their rest with it. This was the other thing. A sword taken, carried out, put into a living hand, and it was right to take precisely because the last one had been right to leave. The dead knight had wanted to keep his blade. Cadal wanted his carried. The whole of being decent, Kaelene was starting to think, was telling the difference.

Only Aelthira said nothing more, for a moment, watching the dull red runes hold the light and not wake. She thought of Mifalodor in his close hot hut. Cheap magic is a door left open. Anyone can spend a little there. Anything can come through. A blade whose words summoned living fire from the other side, made in this hall, by the hand that cut the Hollow Mark, was exactly the kind of door the sorcerer had filled this place with. She filed it, in the right column where the cold things went, very small: the sword is a working of his, carried out into the world now. Then she folded it away and said no more of it, because Bram was happy, and they were alive, and the day had been long and terrible, and there would be time, in some later book of their lives, for the cold to come collect what it was owed. Not today. Today the black sword was a sword again in a good man’s hand, the words in it asleep, and that was enough to be going on with.

They took some of the gold, too, because they were not fools and there was a hoard at their feet that no one living had any better claim to. Not much, and nothing bound or worked or marked with the dead sorcerer’s hand; Kaelene saw to that, sorting it with a thief ’s quick fingers, plain coin and a few unornamented rings and a fat handful of loose stones into the three pouches they had between them, leaving the cups and chains and the strange wrong pieces where they lay. Enough to be worth the crossing and no more. They had come for the answer Cadal needed and the sword that was owed him, and the gold was only sense on the way out, the kind of sense that buys a winter’s roof and a horse and a long while of not taking the next hard job out of want. Bram weighed his pouch in his hand and grinned and said it would make the bear jealous, and they buckled the rest of it away and turned for the long climb back toward the air.

## Chapter 11. The Road Back

They went up the way they had come, and it went well, because they had earned it, and because the hall, having taken its toll on the way down, seemed content to let them leave.

Khaa watched them come back through its dome with its bored old eyes and did not stir and did not ask, because a bargain answered is a bargain closed. It only said, as they passed, “The big one carries fire now. Mind it does not start to feel like yours,” which was either a warning or a riddle or nothing at all, and they didn’t stop to find out which. They crossed Akrae’s cavern by the stones. The centre stone stood empty and cold, the binding only old scratches now, the deep pool still and black and holding nothing worse than water, and they didn’t look long at the place where Bram had gone under. Up the long passage past Okvir’s warren, the old troll glowered at them from his gold and did not rise, perhaps remembering forty blades and four wizards, perhaps only too tired to care. They let him keep his hoard and his counting and his hole, and went on, back up to the branching tunnel and along the right-hand way to its blind end, where a dead man had been waiting years for an answer.

Cadal rose up out of his bones as they came into the light, the torn mail shirt, the grey worn face, and the hope in it was almost more than any of them could bear to be the bringers of, because they carried both halves of his answer now, the part that would free him and the part that would break his heart, and there was no way to give him the one without the other.

“It’s dead,” Bram said first, because that part was clean, and a man deserves the clean part first. “Akrae. The thing that killed you. It’s dead. I went in past its reach the way you couldn’t and I finished it with a knife, and it came apart and went back into its stone, and it will never take another living thing that crosses this water. Your medallion did what you sent it to do. It guarded a living fighter. It’s the only reason I’m standing here to tell you.” He touched the cold blue thing at his chest. “You can rest, friend. The thing that killed you is gone.”

The grey face changed, the years of grief cracking, and Cadal bowed his head, and when he lifted it the hope was still there, terrible and patient, because there was the other question, the one he had been afraid to ask for years, and he made himself ask it now, with three living people standing in his tomb who had been down into the deep where he never reached.

“And Tuviol,” the ghost said. “Did you find her. Did you find what became of her.”

And Kaelene, who had not been able to cross the threshold of Tuviol’s cell, found that she could cross this one, that she could do the hard kind thing that had to be done, and she stepped forward and she told him the truth, plainly and gently, the way the truth is owed to the dead.

“We found her,” she said. “She was held in a cell below, deep in the hall, the next room to her brother’s. The sorcerer who held this place kept them both to feed his working, Cadal. He was reaching for something, the way every master of this hall has reached for something, and he burned living people to reach it, and one ordinary morning, long years ago now, they opened her door and took her out, and she did not come back. She was sacrificed. She did not grow old in that cell and she did not die in it waiting; they did not even leave her that. They took her to the fire while you lay dead in the dark two levels above her, killed before you ever reached the corridor her door opened on. And her brother was in the next room. Prince Emrys. He was kept for the same end, and before they took him too he spent his last strength scratching a message into the wall, begging whoever found it to seek out a warrior named Cadal, the owner of a sword of dread, and tell him where his Tuviol was imprisoned, so that he might come for her and save her. He wrote it not knowing you had already come. Not knowing you were already dead. Every one of you was reaching for the others, and not one of you ever knew the others were reaching back.” Her voice did not break, because she would not let it, because he deserved to hear it whole. “But there’s one thing more, and it’s hers, not mine, so I’ll give it to you the way she left it. She cut it into the wall of her cell, low down, where she knelt to do it, and it was the last thing she made in this world. She wrote: I will always love you, Cadal. Let our souls meet in paradise. And then your name. She did not die forgotten, and she did not die doubting you. She died loving you and saying so to the stone, in the dark, with her own hand. She never knew you came. But you came. It was already too late, long years too late before you ever touched the water, and it was still true. You came for her.”

The blind end of the tunnel was very quiet. Nothing moved, nothing dripped but the three of them. And the ghost of Cadal looked at the three living strangers who had come across a haunted lake and down into a drowned hall and read his whole long tragedy off the walls and carried it up to him so he would not have to wait one more year not knowing, and the grief on his grey face did not go away, because grief like that does not go away, but something settled under it, finally, the long unbearable not-knowing laid down at last, and what rose in its place, against all the sorrow, was the thing Rabindranath had dreamed on the chapel step, the thing the old holy man had seen across a lake and could not understand: a man in a torn shirt who has been waiting a very long time, and was glad.

“They took her,” Cadal said, and it was barely a voice at all now, the cold water of it going still. “They took her, and I came, and I was already too late before I set foot in the water. But I came.” He looked at Bram, at the medallion, and then at the great black sword on Bram’s back, and recognition moved over his face, and something almost like a smile. “And my blade is found. After all of it. Carried by a living man, out into the sun.” He nodded, slow. “Keep it. Keep both. They could not save me, but let them guard you, where you are going, which I think is further than you yet know.” The shape of him was thinning now, going to mist, going to the grey, the years releasing their hold one by one. “Thank you,” said the ghost of Cadal, the last of him fading into the dead air of his tomb. “Thank you for the answer. I have been so tired, and held here so long, and now there is nothing holding me. She asked that our souls meet in paradise. I could not reach her in this world, not in a hundred years of trying. But I am unbound now, and the way she went is the way I am going, and I mean to find her on the other side of it.” Something eased in the grey ruin of his face, the last of the grief going out of it. “Tell no one anything. There is no one left to tell. I will tell her myself. I am going to her now.”

And he was gone, and the blind end of the tunnel held only old bones in a torn mail shirt, at peace at last, their face toward the only way out, and the three of them stood a moment in the silence and then, because the dead were resting and the living had a long climb ahead, they went back down the worn stair into the cold flooded passage one final time and swam, hard, through the killing cold, toward the faint grey light of the island’s inner lake, and broke the surface gasping into air that smelled, after all of it, almost sweet.

The island shore was where they had left it, and so was their gear, and so, leaning against the rock exactly where Bram had set it down with such care a lifetime ago that morning, was his shield, the patched iron heater, dry and whole and waiting. He picked it up. He had set it down like a thing he meant to come back for, and he had come back for it, and he pulled the mail shirt back over his head and buckled the plate on over it, piece by piece, and settled the shield onto his arm, and stood there a moment heavier and slower and armoured and himself again, a fighter who had gone into the dark stripped down to a man and a knife and a will, and come up out of it carrying more than he went in with: the shield reclaimed, a dead champion’s fire on his back, a dead champion’s ward at his chest. Lost, and found, and found again.

The boat sat in the reeds of the inner lake where they had beached it. They put out, and Bram took the oars, slower now, his hurt shoulder stiffening, and Kaelene took the bow, and Aelthira sat in the stern and closed her eyes and reached for the heading through the fog, and the power came up cheap and bright and easy as ever, the island’s last offered gift, and she took only what she needed and put the rest back, and did not let it feel as good as it wanted to feel, and laid them a thread of true direction out through the grey toward the far shore and home.

Whatever watched the lake watched them go. Once, off the bow, the still grey water dimpled and the eye came up on its long pale stalk and turned and fixed on them, exactly as it had on the way in, and Kaelene’s hand found her knife and did not raise it, and they all held still and waited to learn whether the leaving would be allowed the way the coming had been. The eye looked at them a long moment, the boat, the three tired bleeding people, the black sword wrapped now and quiet on the big one’s back. And then it sank, smooth as oil, without a ripple, and let them pass, and did not come again. They had been invited, and they had done the thing the invitation was for, and now they were dismissed, and that, Aelthira thought, was the most unsettling courtesy she had ever been shown.

Bram rowed, and the island fell behind them into the mist, and the adrenaline drained out of all three of them at once the way it always did when the danger was finally past, leaving them shaking and hollow and very tired, and Kaelene knelt in the bottom of the boat and saw to Bram’s shoulder properly, cleaning the deep gripping wounds where the claw had bitten and binding them tight while he rowed and pretended it did not hurt, and nobody said very much, because there was not very much that needed saying.

And somewhere out in the middle of the grey water, with the island gone behind and the far shore not yet come, Kaelene sat back on her heels and looked up, and the mist was thinning overhead, and there was sky in it, pale and ordinary and grey, the plain daylit sky of the world above, and she looked at it for a long time and felt something in her chest let go that had been clenched since Jorpagna, since the crypt, since long before either.

She had gone down. She had walked the whole long way toward the exact shape of the thing that broke her, the island and the mist and the cold drowned tomb, the furniture of the nightmare that had chased her down every road, and she had gone down into it on her own two feet because she chose to. And now she was coming back up. On her own two feet, in the daylight, alive, her people around her, into the open sky. Tarek had gone down into a place like this and not come up; she had gone down into a place like this and she was coming up. She said none of it, because it did not need saying, and there was no one to say it to who did not already know. The open sky said it. The receding island said it. She had looked at the thing, square and full, the way she’d sworn at a window in Outskirt that she would. And she had lived.

The far shore came up grey and quiet out of the fog, and they had crossed, and behind them the island was already gone from sight, the thick still fog drawing shut over it the way it had hidden it from every eye for three hundred years.

## Chapter 12. Walking Away

The land came up under their boots, solid and ordinary, mud and reeds and the good rooted smell of the living forest, and behind them the Grey lay flat and silent under its fog, the thick still pall already drawn shut over the water the way it had always lain. Within an hour of their landing there was nothing out there to see at all. No island, no tower, no fireshape, only the grey lake under a grey sky that no one in Outskirt would ever fish, and now the three of them knew why, and would not say either.

They walked away from it. That was all. They shouldered their gear and turned their backs on the water and walked east into the trees, and the birds came back, the ordinary endless racket of a living wood resuming the moment the lake-silence fell behind them, and it was the most beautiful sound any of them had heard in three days. They walked the two days back the way they had come, and nothing on the road tried to happen to them, no goblins, no elves, only the green going by and the birds and the slow return of warmth to their bones, and they talked, a little, the way people talk coming down off a hard thing, in fits, about nothing, and then they did not talk, and that was good too.

Greatstead’s windows were lit when they passed it in the dusk of the second evening, and there was smoke from the chimney and the bark of farm dogs, Nipper and Greycoat, and Biorn was in the yard splitting wood by lantern-light with a new seriousness to the set of his shoulders, and when he saw them on the road he dropped the axe and shouted, and the whole warm full house emptied out into the yard to meet them. Warge, broad and weathered. Luvia with flour on her hands. Little Rosiall already running. Vagnhild behind them all with the steadier fire in her face, and Biorn in the middle of it, grinning, sixteen and alive and home.

He had started, he told them, talking fast, the way he always talked, the moment Bram clapped him on the shoulder. He had gone to the old soldier two farms over, the one who’d lost a leg in the southern wars and never spoke of it, and he had asked the man to teach him the spear, the way you hold a doorway, the way you stand a line, and the old soldier had looked at him a long moment and then said yes, and they were at it most mornings now, and Biorn meant to teach the smith’s boy next, and the miller’s twins, so that if ever anything came up the west road out of the trees there would be more than a handful of them who knew how to stand between it and the village, and Bram listened to all of it with a look on his face that Kaelene had not seen there before, a quiet uncomplicated peace, and she understood that Bram, who had walked out from under a hard father and a life that fit him wrong, was looking at the boy he might have been if his own home had ever been worth staying in, and feeling, for the first time in his road-long life, that he had given the boy the better answer, and that the boy had taken it, and that something he had broken in himself a long time ago was, in someone else, going to come out whole.

“You chose right,” Kaelene said to him, low, while the family swept them toward the house and the supper and the kept seats. “He’d have died in a ditch on some road being clever. He’s going to grow old being the wall. You did that.”

“I told him a true thing,” Bram said. “First time in a while a story of mine was just true.” He watched Biorn herd his little sister inside. “Feels strange. I think I like it.”

Before they left, Kaelene put a part of the gold on the table. Not all of it, but enough, plain coin and a few of the loose stones, slid across the boards to Vagnhild without ceremony, because Kaelene had learned that the kindest way to give a proud family money was to do it quickly and look at something else while you did. “From the island,” she said, when Warge started to refuse it. “We came back with more than we can spend on a road and you’ve a farm to keep and two who’ll want to marry off it one day. It’s no good to us in a saddlebag and it’s a roof and a dowry and a hard winter to you. Take it, and don’t make a thing of it.” Vagnhild looked at her brother, and at the coin, and understood it for what it was, not a payment and not charity but the trio handing back, in the only currency they had to spare, a little of what the warm full house had been worth the leaving alone. She closed her hand over it. “Then it goes to the training too,” she said. “Spears cost money. If Biorn’s to be the wall, the wall gets armed.” And Bram laughed, delighted, and said that was the best use of a dead wizard’s gold he’d ever heard, and that was that.

They didn’t stay long at Greatstead, only long enough for a meal and the warmth of it, and then they walked the last stretch into Outskirt in the full dark, to the Three Stags, to Bryte’s open laugh and Ulvar’s sharp glad eyes and the fire and the room that took them back the way small places take back anyone who left and returned with their skin whole and a new story behind their eyes. And Bram was already shaping the telling of it, before he’d so much as sat down. Kaelene could see the bones of the thing assembling behind his face, the mist and the drowned hall and the demon on its stone and the riddles and the black sword in the gold, and she knew that by the next inn down the next road it would have grown a fourth dwarf and a larger demon and a great deal more fire. He’d have the black blade across his knees to lay a hand on while he told it, and in the telling its runes would wake and pour out flame on command, whatever they did or didn’t do in the cold light of fact. The bear had a rival now. The bear was finished, in fact. There was a black sword out of a dead sorcerer’s hoard in the story now, and a man who killed a demon with a knife, and Bram would tell it the rest of his life, and it would be mostly true and getting less so beautifully, and that was exactly as it should be.

They carried it all out with them, the things you cannot put in a story. The Deathbringer, wrapped and quiet, its summoning asleep in the black steel. Cadal’s cold blue medallion at Bram’s chest, its work done and its dead owner finally at rest. A scar or two healing under their clothes. And the weight, which they did not speak of and would carry a while, of two small cold cells and three lives that reached for each other in the dark for all those years and never once touched.

And on the very last of it, when the fire was warm and Bram was deep in the first telling and Bryte was pouring and the whole room was leaning the right way, Aelthira sat a little apart at the small table by the side door, where Kaelene used to sit, and took out her book, the real one, with the real columns, and opened it to a clean page, and had her one private moment.

She did not write much. In the right column, where the cold true things went, she drew, very small and very exact, from memory, the figure that had been cut into the floor of the drowned cavern, the Hollow Mark, the broken double ring and the line running through and the seven cuts and the empty heart, every line of it true the way she had fixed it in her mind. And beside it, from a different memory, an older one, from a marble hall a thousand miles east where she had once found a page she was not meant to find, she drew the figure the Academy had buried, the one they had drawn small and explained away and locked behind a door she was not given the key to. She set the two side by side on the clean page, the dead sorcerer’s work and her old school’s deepest hidden secret, drawn by a hand that could never have read those books and a school that swore the thing did not work.

And she looked at the two figures, and she saw, for the first time, and only to herself, that they were the same shape. The thing her Academy had locked behind a door and sworn did not work was the thing a line of sorcerers had been cutting and recutting into the floor of a drowned hall for generations, feeding it, dying on it, and one of them had died on it only days ago and the next would come, because a door like that does not stay empty for long.

She did not say it aloud. She did not say it to Bram, lit up at the fire with the black sword across his knees, or to Kaelene, who had gone down into the dark and come back up whole and was sitting easy now in a way Aelthira had not seen in months and would not spoil for anything. She closed the book, and put it away, and let the warmth of the room come back over her, and she sat with her friends while the fire burned down, and the story grew, and the ale went round. Outside the window the forest dripped, and somewhere far to the west the fog lay over the Mirror the way it had lain for three hundred years, drawn shut again over the island, hiding it the way it had hidden it from everyone before them. The dwarves’ one parted curtain might never come again. The place where the tower stood might sink back behind the grey as if no one had ever seen it.

Bram would have a new story by the next inn, and a black sword to lay across his knees while he told it, and fire enough in the telling. Kaelene had looked at the thing that scared her, and lived, and would sleep, tonight, a little easier. And only Aelthira carried the cold off the island with her, folded small in a book in her pack, two figures on a clean page that were the same shape, a thing she now knew and could not unknow, waiting, patient, for some later day to ask what it meant.

For now, that was enough.
