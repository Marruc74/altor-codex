## Chapter One: A Flaw in the Theory

The lecture on elemental binding geometry was, in the technical sense, excellent. Aelthira Moonveil had already read the primary text twice and the secondary commentary once, which meant she spent most of the hour in her secondary column.

She kept two columns in her notes. The left was for what was said. The right was for what was not. By the second hour, the right column was considerably fuller.

Query: why does Professor Selwyn keep using 'generally' when citing the Mireth Principles? Generally means he has seen an exception. Ask after class.

She was sitting in the second row, which she had learned was the correct row. The first row made professors feel observed, which they were, but there was something about a light elf in the front seat that seemed to remind them of it more vividly. The second row was close enough to hear clearly and far enough back to be polite about it.

The student next to her was reading something under the desk.

"That's last year's binding theory exam," Aelthira said, not looking up from her notes.

Fen Marwick tucked the paper deeper into his coat. "I don't know what you're talking about."

"The coffee stain in the top left corner. I have the same one. The ink on those questions ran because someone left a window open during a rainstorm." She finished her current note. "You already know all of this. Why are you reading a past exam?"

"Because knowing it and being calm about it are two different things," Fen said.

Aelthira considered this and decided it was reasonable, even if it was not how she would have approached the problem. She and Fen had shared a study table in the Academy library for five of her six years here, which was the kind of arrangement that happened when one person kept turning up at the same table and the other person eventually stopped pretending to mind. He was warm and methodical and had an excellent sense of when she had gone too far down a rabbit hole to be productive. She valued this more than she usually said.

* * *

The discovery, when it came, was not dramatic. It rarely was.

Three evenings after the lecture, she was working through the cross-references in a treatise on animist channeling when she noticed that two of the citations said different things. Not different interpretations. Different facts. She checked the primary sources. Then she checked the sources of the primary sources. Then she sat back and looked at the ceiling for a while.

If the numbers were right, and she had checked them four times, there was a gap in the foundational model. Not a small one. Not an oversight that had simply been smoothed over in the teaching. A gap that suggested the Academy's understanding of how animist practice interacted with elemental channeling was, in a specific and rather important way, incomplete.

She spent a fourth evening accepting this.

Then she went to find Professor Carenthal.

He was the kind of man who had been at the Academy long enough that he had become part of the furniture, which she meant as a compliment. He knew every corner of the institution, and more usefully, he had never once told her she was asking the wrong questions. He listened to her lay out the discrepancy, asked two clarifying questions, and looked at her notes for a long time without speaking.

"It's an interesting reading," he said finally.

"Is it wrong?"

He paused. "These are complex texts. Interpretations can vary."

"I'm not offering an interpretation. I'm pointing at two numbers that can't both be correct."

Carenthal set down the notes, and there it was: a slight shift in posture, a careful rearrangement of attention, the practiced ease of a man redirecting a conversation he had redirected before. He asked about her upcoming examination instead. She answered his questions. She did not press.

But she noticed.

She was still thinking about it when she took the long way home, through the older part of the Academy where the corridors ran narrow and the stones were cold even in summer. She was not intending to walk past the restricted archive. She simply ended up there, because her mind was running in tight circles and her feet had taken a reasonable independent decision.

The door at the end of the corridor was supposed to be locked.

It was not locked tonight.

Aelthira stood very still and looked at it. Then she looked back down the corridor. Then she looked at the door again. It was an ordinary door. It was open, perhaps three inches, and the darkness on the other side was thick and particular in the way that only old rooms were dark.

She should go back. She was aware of this.

She lit her candle, pushed the door open with two fingers, and stepped inside.

## Chapter Two: What the Archive Knew

The archive smelled of stone and something older than stone, which was not a smell she had a word for yet. She would find one eventually. She was good at that.

It took two nights to find the relevant shelves. The first night she spent getting her bearings, moving carefully between the stacks with her candle held low, reading spines and dates and the kind of dense archival shorthand that told her nothing except that someone had cared very much about cataloguing things correctly. The second night she found the section she had not known she was looking for.

The documents were old. Not Academy-old, which was a particular kind of old involving forty-year arguments and lecture notes that had not been updated since before Aelthira was born. These were older than the Academy. Some of them were older than several things they probably should not predate.

She read carefully. She read quickly. She filled twelve pages of notes in a handwriting that got smaller and more precise as the hours passed, which was what happened when she was concentrating hard enough that she forgot to perform legibility for anyone else.

Somebody had known about the gap. Not just suspected it. Known, documented it in careful and specific terms, traced its implications through three generations of animist practice. And then somebody else had taken those documents and put them behind a locked door and had not mentioned them in any syllabus, ever, and the Academy had continued teaching the incomplete model as though the gap did not exist.

She set down her pen.

The thing the model had been quietly missing was not a theoretical problem. It was a practical one. The interaction between animist channeling and elemental binding, done in the way the Academy taught it, done in the way every student here had done it for decades, was touching something. Not intentionally. Not knowingly. But consistently, in small and accumulating ways, every time a student opened that particular channel in that particular manner, something on the other side noticed.

She picked her pen back up. There was nothing else useful to do with her hands.

* * *

The third night was when something noticed her back.

She had been reading for two hours, deep in an animist text whose notation system required its own set of margin notes to decode, when the room changed. Not changed. The word was wrong. The room became what it had always been going to be, and she had simply arrived at the moment where that was true.

There was no sound. There was no movement in the shadows at the edge of her candlelight. There was the feeling of being watched by something that did not have eyes, from a direction that did not have a name, and the absolute certainty that whatever it was had been here long before the archive was built around it, long before the Academy was built around the archive, long before anyone had thought to put a lock on the door.

It was not threatening. That was almost the strangest part. It was simply present, and aware, and patient in the way that something is patient when it has never had any reason to be otherwise.

Aelthira closed her notebook, tucked it under her arm, and walked to the door. Quickly and calmly, which is to say she walked quickly and kept her breathing even and did not run.

She stood in the corridor for a moment, listening to her own heartbeat. It was faster than usual. This seemed reasonable.

She waited until it slowed. Then she went back to her room and sat on the edge of her bed and stared at the wall for a while, and then she slept, because she was a practical person and she had two lectures in the morning.

She told Fen Marwick two days later. Not about the presence, not yet. About the documents. The gap. The deliberate omission. She laid it out the way she laid everything out, clearly and in order, and she watched his face do the thing it did when he was trying to work out whether to be alarmed.

"All right," he said, when she had finished. "Put it back."

"I'm not asking you what to do with it. I'm telling you what I found."

"And I'm telling you what to do with it." He was not being unkind. He was being honest, which was different, and the honesty was: put it back, close the door, walk away. He had spent six years watching her pull threads and he knew exactly where this one led. "Aelthira. This is the kind of thing that ends academic careers. It's the kind of thing that starts other kinds of trouble that are harder to name and worse to be in."

"I know."

"Then you know what the sensible choice is."

She did know. She had spent two days knowing it very clearly, and it had not helped.

She understood, perfectly well, that the sensible thing was to put the books back and not think about it. She had spent several days trying to do exactly that, and had only managed to think about it more, which she supposed was the answer, even if it was not a comfortable one.

Fen looked at her for a long moment. Then he refilled both their cups and did not say anything else, which was one of the things she valued most about him.

The meeting was called four days later. She was not surprised.

Carenthal's office held three people when it needed to: himself, and two senior faculty members whose names she knew from the Academy register and whose faces she knew from the kind of formal occasions where everyone stood in the right order. They did not look angry. They looked careful, which was worse.

Carenthal spoke first. He told her the archive was restricted for reasons that had been considered thoroughly, by people who understood the material better than a sixth-year student, however gifted, could be expected to. He told her the line of inquiry she had found had been formally closed many years ago. He told her the decision had not been made lightly.

One of the other faculty members said that the documents she had accessed represented a body of research that had been reviewed at the highest levels of the institution. The other said that there were things that looked like gaps from the outside that were, in fact, decisions.

None of them were lying. That was the part she kept coming back to. They believed every word of it.

"We need you to stop," Carenthal said. "Set aside your notes. Return anything you've removed. And let this rest."

She looked at him. She looked at the other two. She thought about the room going different in the dark, about the patience of something that had been waiting longer than the Academy had existed, about the neat and careful way all of this had been put behind a door and left to accumulate.

"Of course," she said. "I understand."

She meant it. She meant it in the specific way a person means something they have not quite finished deciding, which is to say she meant the words and was still working on the rest of it.

The corridor outside Carenthal's office was long and quiet and smelled of the same candle wax it always had. She stood in it for a moment with her hands at her sides.

The Academy carried on around her. Somewhere two floors above, a class was in session. Somewhere below, someone was practicing a binding sequence, the faint mineral scent of it drifting up through the stones. Everything was exactly as it had always been, and it was going to stay that way, and that was the decision everyone here had made, and they had made it so thoroughly that they had forgotten it was a decision at all.

She stood in the corridor a little longer.

Then she started walking, and she did not go back to her room.

## Chapter Three: The Problem with Hoping for the Best

She went back to the archive one more time. She had known she would. There was no point in pretending otherwise.

This time she was not exploring. She moved through the stacks with a purpose she had not had on the first two nights, going directly to the shelves she needed, pulling what she needed, reading with the focused efficiency of someone who has already done the slower work of understanding and is now only confirming. She had a new journal, purchased that afternoon from the market in the lower city, and she wrote in it carefully: the nature of the phenomenon, the conditions under which it became perceptible, the specific gaps in the Academy's defensive practice that she now understood were not gaps at all but refusals, dressed up over decades to look like settled conclusions.

The room stayed a room. The dark beyond her candle stayed ordinary dark.

She had almost expected the presence again. Some part of her had braced for it, the way you brace for a sound you heard once and cannot quite believe you will not hear again. But the archive was quiet and still, and whatever had noticed her three nights ago had either lost interest or was being patient in a way she could not measure.

She suspected the latter. Everything about what she had read suggested patience was not a thing it had to work at.

She closed the journal, tucked it into her bag with the kind of care she usually reserved for borrowed books, and left the archive for the last time. She pulled the door shut behind her. She heard the lock click, which it had not done on the night this started, and she stood in the corridor for a moment thinking about that.

Then she went to find Carenthal.

He was in his office before the first bell, which she had known he would be. Six years in this building and she had learned everyone's rhythms. That was the kind of attention that had always felt natural to her and only occasionally useful, and today it was both.

She sat down without being asked. She had thought carefully about what she wanted to say and she said it without preamble, because she owed him that much honesty and she thought he probably owed her the same.

She told him the thing the Academy had been quietly not studying was still present. That it had been present for a very long time, long enough that the oldest documents in his own archive described it in three different languages, and that the decision to stop looking at it had not made it go away. It had only made it unacknowledged, which was a different thing entirely, and a less safe one.

She told him she was not angry.

She told him she understood why the decision had been made, that she could even see the logic of it, the weight of it, the particular exhaustion of being the person who had to look at something that had no clean resolution and choose, anyway, what to do.

She told him she could not stay and teach a model she knew was incomplete, could not sit in lecture halls and watch new students open the same channels in the same ways and know what was accumulating on the other side of it, and say nothing, and hope for the best.

Carenthal was quiet for a long time. The light through his window was the particular pale gold of early morning, and it made the room look warmer than it was.

When he spoke, it was not an argument. It was not a defence. It was something that was not quite an apology and not quite an explanation, said in the voice of a man who had made a choice a long time ago and had been living inside that choice ever since, and who was, perhaps, only now looking at it clearly from the outside.

He did not tell her she was wrong. That was, she thought later, the most honest thing he could have done.

She thanked him for six years of taking her seriously. She meant it. He nodded, once, and looked out the window, and she left him to it.

Fen Marwick found her while she was packing, which she had also known he would do. He stood in the doorway of her room and looked at the open bag on the bed and the neat stacks of books beside it and did not say anything for a moment.

Then he asked if she had enough money.

"Yes."

"Where are you going?"

"South first. There's a practitioner in Jorvaine who published a paper on animist boundary theory twelve years ago and then went very quiet. I want to know why." She folded a shirt and placed it in the bag. "After that I have a list."

"Of course you have a list."

"It's a good list."

He crossed the room and sat down on the end of the bed, which was not a thing he would normally do, which told her everything about how he was feeling. She kept packing because it was easier than stopping.

"You could just," he started, and then did not finish, because they both knew how the sentence ended and they both knew what she thought of it.

"Fen."

"I know." He was quiet for a moment. "I know you can't."

She did not tell him not to worry. That would have been a lie, and she had spent the last two weeks around enough comfortable lies to last her for a while. She told him instead that she would write, and that she would be careful, and that if she found anything that seemed like it was becoming his problem too she would tell him immediately, which was the most honest version of reassurance she had to offer.

He helped her carry her bag to the south gate. He did not try to talk her out of it again. That was the thing about Fen: once he understood a thing was decided, he was simply there for it, which was its own kind of loyalty.

They said goodbye at the gate, briefly, in the way of people who are planning to see each other again and are not ready to consider the alternative.

Valtrea was beautiful in the early morning.

She had known this, in the abstract way you know things about a place you have lived in long enough that you stop seeing it. The light came off the old stone differently at dawn, golden and already warm even before the sun had fully cleared the Carenthi peaks, and the cypress trees at the edge of the upper city threw long shadows across the road that made the whole hillside look like something out of an illustrated text on ancient Berendien. She had walked these streets for six years. She knew every shortcut in the Academy quarter and most of the ones outside it.

She stopped at the south gate and looked back, once, because it would be strange not to.

The city sat on its hillside exactly as it always had, warm-stoned and certain of itself, its marketplaces still shuttered in the early quiet, its vineyards just catching the first light on the slopes below. The Academy's upper towers were visible above the roofline. Somewhere in one of those towers, Carenthal was sitting by his window. Somewhere in the student halls, Fen was probably already back at their table in the library, because he was that kind of person.

She turned back to the road.

She was not running from something. She was walking toward something. It was important to know the difference, and she did.

There were problems in the world that people had agreed, very sensibly, not to look at directly. She had always found that approach unconvincing. You did not solve a thing by hoping it would solve itself. She had tried hoping. She was not very good at it.

She shifted her pack, felt the new journal solid at the top of it, and started walking.
