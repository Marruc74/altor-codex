# The Hammer of Eshwan Theard

Eshwan Theard was one of the greatest adventurers of the fourth century, a name still spoken beside Torgam Dragonslayer and Weilan Longbow. He won wealth enough to buy any castle in the land and yet lived plainly, going on risking his neck like any common wanderer, and no trace of his gold was ever seen. That was his undoing. Rumour swelled that he had hoarded a vast treasure somewhere - some even said he was a golden dragon in human shape - and on the road home from a successful venture the wretches who called themselves his friends murdered him in his sleep, believing they had found where his riches lay. Naturally, Eshwan became a ghost.

## The Patron of Adventurers and the Poor

Near half a century passed before a band of adventurers avenged his restless soul, and in doing so uncovered the truth of the missing gold: Eshwan had taken monastic vows and given almost all of it to a cloister that helped the poor. For that he is honoured to this day as the patron of adventurers and of the poor. Even so his soul found no rest, and he resolved to do something for those who, like himself, had been betrayed by their own friends.

## The Forging

With the help of a passing merchant Eshwan called up a mighty mage, and together they made a weapon of rare power: a hammer of secret alloy, forged to formidable strength and balance by the finest dwarven smith of the age. By rituals now forgotten an ounce of Eshwan's own soul was bound into it forever, along with great workings of magic, and into the haft was set a ruby said to be a fragment of a comet. Through that ruby the good Eshwan still guides the hammer, so that its powers are spent only wisely and justly.

## The Weapon

It is a great war-hammer, slim of shaft and heavy in its eight-sided head, the grip wound in leather for a warrior's fist and the haft worked into the shape of an old helm whose eye-holes show the blood-red stone. The metal is a light, supple, matt-silver alloy that dwarves know for what it is and others find uncanny. The hammer should be treated as a thinking thing in its own right: it speaks to its bearer mind to mind, rarely and hard to deceive, and sees through lies and illusions. It steers a worthy hand toward right action, and turns its strength against a bearer who breaks a freshly sworn oath or betrays a friend or a life's purpose, punishing such treachery until the wielder mends it. Those who recognise the hammer for what it is tend to want it at any price, and that wanting is a danger of its own.
