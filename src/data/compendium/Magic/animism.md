# Animism

Animism is the eldest of the three great schools of magic, the art of the living world — of beasts and growing things, of the wild places and the green. Where the elementalist commands fire and stone and the mentalist the mind, the animist speaks to what breathes and grows, and bends it to his will. It is the oldest art men remember, older than the academies, and its mages have always kept closer to the wilderness than to the city.

## Overview

- **School:** Animism, the art of nature and animal life
- **Standing:** The oldest of the three schools
- **Domain:** Beasts and plants, the wild and the wellsprings of living things
- **Instruments:** The Dagger, mastery over the beasts; the Cup, the wellsprings of life; the Censer, the powers of the senses
- **Practised by:** Druids above all, who live close to the land and can speak with the animals
- **Beyond its reach:** The unliving — stone, metal and the dead answer to other arts

## The Animist's Art

The animist senses, finds and names the beasts about him at a distance, and knows their kind and their bearing. He calms what is wild and tames what was never broken, heals a hurt animal with a touch, and rides what no halter ever held. With the night-sight of a hunting predator he sees clearly in the dark, and the creatures of field and forest are slow to mark him as an enemy.

## Wearing the Beast

The deeper workings of the school lend the mage the powers of the animals themselves. He may grow black claws or a predator's jaws to fight with, take on the bear-soul for its strength or the leopard-soul for its swiftness, or set his own mind within a beast and steer it from afar. At the height of the art he turns himself, or another, wholly into an animal, body and all.

## The Green Art

No less than the beasts, the growing world answers the animist. He shapes and heals plants, speaks with them, and blesses a sowing to a rich harvest whatever the weather. Roused to fury, the green itself becomes a weapon: thickets of thorn that tear, grasping growth that snares an advancing foe, a whole field given sudden life to bar a road. He may even turn a man into a walking, rooted thing of leaves and bark.

## The Great Summons

The crowning works of animism call up powers greater than any single beast. The animist may summon the rådare, the guardian-godling of a kind of animal, to counsel or to aid him; he may call a terrible storm down out of a clear sky. Most dread of all is the Wild Hunt, when the horned hunter and his hell-hounds are loosed upon a chosen quarry, and run it down wherever it flees.
