# Necromancy

Necromancy is the magic of death, the art of the corpse, the curse and the grave. It is the grimmest of the workings that grew out of mentalism, the school of body and soul, and the one men shun most. The necromancer does not heal nor build; he raises the dead, calls down plague and curse, and wastes the living away, and the dead-house and the battlefield are his proper haunts.

## Overview

- **Art:** The magic of death and of the dead
- **Root:** The death-magic branch of mentalism, grown into a discipline of its own
- **Domain:** Corpses and the undead, plague, curse, and the borderland of life and death
- **The necromancer's gift:** He feels the presence of death about him, and senses the undead at a distance
- **A known codex:** *Nekron's Book*, cursed by the god of death himself
- **Disposition:** Shunned and feared, close kin to the dark art

## The Raising of the Dead

The first and best-known work of the art is the giving of unlife to a corpse, to make a zombie bound to the necromancer's will. The mightiest do not stop at one: they raise whole armies of the dead, from a score of shambling servants to many hundreds. And the necromancer who goes furthest of all works the rite of the Lord of the Undead, by which he ceases to be a living man and becomes himself a deathless thing, vampire- or mummy-like, beyond the reach of age.

## Curse, Plague and Wasting

Against the living the necromancer turns a colder craft. He bursts the blood within a body, rots a limb to ruin, withers strength and health away, and binds a death-link so that a wound meant for him falls instead upon another. He can become a walking pestilence, breathing plague upon all near him, and lay a curse upon a single person, a whole bloodline, or an entire city. By aging a stolen victim he can even renew his own failing years.

## The Necromancer

A necromancer is marked by his trade. He feels where death has been and senses the undead within reach, and he studies the "animal magnetism" of the borderland, that grey country between the living and the dead. Foremost among the books of the art is *Nekron's Book*, a codex of death-magic so steeped in power that the death-god laid his own curse upon it: whoever owns it prospers in his craft and meets a terrible end.

## The Hidden Life

Whatever the legends say of withered, skull-faced men, most necromancers look like anyone else, and take pains to. The work is a crime in nearly every land, and a necromancer of middling power lives in constant dread of being found out, so he keeps off the public square and hides his trade behind an honest seeming, trusting no tailor with the cut of his robes. He favours grey and black and red, never light colours, and the older and prouder of them wear nothing but plain black with the hood down, while the showier hang their robes with bones, sigils and small skulls. He carries a bone-staff, a walking-staff the height of a man made from the leg-bones of beasts and topped with a skull, in which he stores his spells and his strength, and a bronze knife with a bone handle for the cutting that the raising of the dead demands.

He is hunted for all of this. The witch-hunters of the stricter realms, the animists and elves who hate the disturbing of the dead, and any adventurer who catches the scent of the work will turn on him if they can. So the art is taught only in hidden places: a school buried in the Vidvind forest of old Jorpagna, an academy in Kardien that keeps some two hundred skeletons at its bidding, and a small house in Erebos given over to the breeding of perytons.

## The Price of the Art

The craft marks the man who works it, and the deeper he goes the plainer the mark. As his skill in death-magic grows, a necromancer's body begins to answer it. His eyes may redden until only a bloody white is left, though he sees as well as ever; his teeth lengthen into fangs and his nails into claws; his flesh wastes until he looks a living skeleton, or rots slowly on the bone while he still draws breath. Animals and small children grow uneasy at his nearness and flee or weep, and the larger beasts go for his throat. Some necromancers gather strange terrors as they study, a horror of growing things, of fire, of holy ground, of trees. And a few, at the last, follow the change to its end, sinking over the course of months into a zombie, their mind clear but their body dead, counted now among the very undead they once raised up.

## The Books of the Dead

The lore of the art is kept in books, and the most notorious of them are sought and feared in equal measure. Above them all stands *Nekron's Book*, cursed by the death-god so that its owner prospers and dies badly.

Others are nearly as ill-famed. *Knowledge of All and Nothing*, written in the old tongue by the mad southerner Erack Khersmir, claims to explain everything and nothing in the terms of death-magic, and only a practised necromancer can so much as read it without being driven into terror; long study of it leaves a reader wiser, no different, or a fool, by no rule he can foresee, and the true copy vanished long ago. The spellbook of Tamanrasset, the bookish goblin necromancer of the Nida mountains, survives in two copies and sets down the necromancy of his people. And the book of Farengadd of Helsing holds the rare and cruel spider-boil among its workings. Farengadd, a maker of vicious magical jokes, scattered false copies of it through the world, each an unwritten thing charged with a sigil and one of his spells to trap the curious, before he came to his own absurd end.

Not every relic of the art is a book. The twelve staves of Aarkroon look like plain wooden walking-sticks but are wrought of cold metal, and a necromancer skilled enough to wield one can raise the dead within a few paces of it and keep his risen servants from being turned or stolen by any rival. But the staff feeds on its bearer, necromancer or not, draining a measure of his vigour with every month that passes, until the day he falls and rises again as one of the dead himself.

## Kin to the Dark Art

Necromancy is the old magic of death, known here and there across Ereb wherever a mentalist follows his art to its darkest end. It should not be confused with the dark magic of Vicotnik, the grimmer discipline carried out of the east and drawing its strength from the moon - though the two are close cousins, and a land that fears the one has good reason to fear the other.
