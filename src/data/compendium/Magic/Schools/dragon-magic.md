# Dragon Magic

Dragon magic is the oldest art of the mind, the magic of dragons themselves, and the root from which three of the arts of men, mentalism, illusionism and symbolism, all grew. It is no longer a school taught in the academies, but its shadow lies over every art of consciousness that came after it.

## Overview

- **Practised by:** dragons; learned by very few others
- **Requires:** fluency in the dragon-tongue
- **Legacy:** the parent of mentalism, illusionism and symbolism
- **Mark:** worked in pure thought, with no word or gesture

## The Silent Art

Dragon magic is wrought entirely in thought, with no spoken word and no movement of the hand, so that it is almost impossible to catch in the working, and harder still to ward against. It knows no grades of power, always working at its least, and never reaches for great forces; its whole strength is in subtle and often deadly turns of the mind, the very thing the dragons call dénhlinåsh, the subtle power. It is akin to mentalism, distantly, and the few who can work mentalism can in principle learn it. From this art, in ages long past, men shaped the three arts of the mind they practise to this day.

## Thinking Like a Dragon

No human masters dragon magic from books, for it is not learning but the knack of thinking in exactly the lines a dragon thinks, and so it asks first a deep fluency in the dragon-tongue. Even then a student can carry it only so far as he truly understands the dragon mind, and no further. A dragon, of course, needs no such school at all, since it already thinks as a dragon does, and the oldest beasts have woven these workings for so long that their command of them is past any mortal reckoning.

## Dragon-Speak

Because the art is made of thought alone, a dragon can weave it while it talks on as though nothing were amiss, and the victim never sees the spell laid on him beneath the words. This is the root of the old warnings about "dragon-speak," and the reason the wary do not let talk with a dragon run long. A few quiet sentences and a mind not its own may already be turning to the dragon's purpose.

## The Subtle Workings

The dragon's quieter craft begins with a sounding of the mind, laying a victim's thoughts bare so that they can be read whatever the tongue they think in, and opening the way for the workings that follow. Once a mind lies open the dragon may slip thoughts into it that the victim takes for his own, a persuasion past detecting that asks nothing of a shared language, though it cannot drive a creature clean against its deepest nature. It can raise an illusion that exists only inside the victim's head, seen by no one else, untouched by the spells that pierce ordinary glamours, and so convincing it can kill, as when a phantom boulder seems to fall and the heart fails for terror of being crushed. Cruelest of these is the nameless dread, which dredges up the most hideous monster a victim can imagine out of his own buried fear and sets it on him; and there is a working that floods all five senses at once with more than they can bear, light and sound and pain together, leaving a mind wholly swamped and helpless.

## The Astral Body

The dragon's craft has one working unlike the rest, by which the beast sends its astral self out from its body to roam unseen. So freed it can pass through any matter and is untouched by any blow, yet it can only watch, never act, and cannot so much as speak. Its body lies senseless and defenceless the while, guarded only by the dragon's sixth sense, which warns the wandering spirit if any creature draws near with ill intent. It is by this art, more than any other, that a dragon comes to be so strangely well informed of all that passes in the world, and of where treasure lies for the taking, though it seems never to leave its cave. No non-dragon has yet managed the working, for it likely rests on that same sixth sense, which dragons alone possess.

## The Magic of the Tongue

The dragon-tongue carries a magic of its own, woven into the words themselves, and chief among its powers is the binding word that holds a speaker to the truth for a set count of sentences. Dragons, naturally, are endlessly inventive in slipping its grip, bending the shape of a sentence, saying only the bare letter of the truth, or simply not telling the whole of it. Deeper and more perilous still is the true name, the full and secret account of a creature's inmost self in the dragon-tongue: whoever knows it and can call it rightly holds that creature wholly in his power, to command or to destroy, and so the dragons guard such knowledge beyond all else.
