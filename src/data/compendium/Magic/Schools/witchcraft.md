# Witchcraft

Witchcraft is the oldest and most secretive of the woman's arts, a craft handed down from witch to pupil and never taught in the open academies. It joins two powers that other mages keep carefully apart, the green power of growth and life and the cold power of the undead, and from their union come its blessings and its curses alike. It is widely feared, for those who walk its road are marked by it in time, until the witch becomes the bent and hook-nosed crone of every fireside tale.

## Overview

- **Nature:** the joined arts of life and death, of growth and the undead
- **Aspect:** life, standing alongside animism and necromancy
- **Taught:** witch to pupil, never in academies; a witch keeps no more than two
- **Must be:** the witch's chief art, far stronger in her than any other she knows
- **Familiar:** the bjära, made in place of an animal companion
- **Mark:** the slow change of the witch into the crone of legend

## The Witch's Craft

The witch can speak with living things as the animist does and call upon the dead as the necromancer does, and she has powers all her own besides: the evil eye that withers and curses, the blight that defiles, the touch that holds a victim fast. Above all she is a brewer, and her witch's potions, worked in a well-stocked larder of herbs and stranger things, are the heart of her trade. In place of a mage's spirit-bound beast she makes a bjära, a little servant of yarn and herb quickened under the full moon, to carry her spells, her thefts and her secret words.

## The Mark of the Witch

No witch keeps the craft for long without paying for it in her own body. The years bring warts that no spell will lift, hair gone rat-grey and impossible to cut, a back that bends and shortens, a nose that hooks, a voice gone hoarse and croaking, and nails that grow without end. With them often come a shrinking from crowds, a deep mistrust of men, a sixth sense for danger, and a sudden weight of added years. So the young witch becomes the crone of the old tales, though the cunning may hide it a while behind a fair and queenly seeming.
