## The Fouled Spring

Springwater is a poor fishing village on a lonely reach of Zorakin's coast, wedged between the open sea and a dead volcano called Gullcrag. For as long as anyone remembers its people have drunk from the spring that rises at the mountain's foot, water that begins far above in the crater lake at the volcano's crown and works its way down through the old lava veins. About a month before the adventure opens the spring turned foul. People and beasts who drank from it sickened, and the village fell back on rainwater caught in barrels. The cause sits at the top of the holy peak, where a passing roc let a whale's carcass fall into the crater lake. Ten tons of rotting flesh have poisoned the water at its source, and will go on poisoning it until the body is gone.

## The Hire

Word of the trouble reaches a city hiring-hall, most likely at Pendon, where two travel-worn villagers named Bedvir and Kral stand out an evening of mockery trying to find sword-arms willing to work for a village's thin purse. The pay is around sixty copper, and the room laughs them off the podium. Those who take the offer anyway learn its real shape over a quiet table: the spring has gone bad, the village shaman means to set it right, and he needs outsiders to guard him while he works. The road back is a long day's walk over the mountains behind a small, foul-tempered pack-donkey, and it ends at sundown among the driftwood cottages of Springwater, where the smell of decay already hangs over the well.

## The Village Under the Volcano

The folk of Springwater are a lean, weathered people who live by fishing, sealing, and mussel-ropes strung in the calm water behind the headland, and by robbing the cliff-birds' nests along Gullcrag's seaward face. At their head stands the Stormbird, a slender man taller than the rest, sun-browned and gold-eyed, set apart by a linen cloak among all the sealskin and by a voice and bearing that are not quite the village's own. He sings for the seals and the autumn storms and carries the ashes of the dead up to the mountain's crown, and he alone may set foot on the holy peak. The old people say there has always been a Stormbird: when one dies another is born, wearing a new face but plainly the same man. In the village hall, over bowls of fish soup, he tells the newcomers what he means to do and why he cannot do it alone.

## Up Through the Mountain

At dawn the Stormbird leads the band down through the emptying village to the jetties, where Kral rows them round the base of Gullcrag to a sandy cleft. From there they climb the cliff to a dark crack in the mountain's skin and pass into its hollow veins, a lightless maze of narrow passages barely wide enough for a body, which only he knows how to thread. Hours of climbing and crawling bring them out onto a ledge high in a round, sheer-walled crater a hundred and fifty metres across. A grey lake fills the bottom, the torn whale floating in its middle, and the crater walls are ringed with ledges where the village lays its dead in clay urns. On one of those ledges a griffin has built her nest.

## The Offering

The Stormbird settles on a white sealskin with a small drum, warns the band to let nothing break his rhythm before dark, and falls into a trance meant to last the whole day. What happens next is theirs to hold. The griffin comes home hungry and furious to find intruders near her egg, and she is a hard thing to put down. Later, drawn by the stink of the whale, a few harpies arrive to jeer and snatch, then wheel away to fetch their sisters; at dusk they come back in their scores, lining the crater rim and raining stone-tipped spears on the shaman and his guards. Only as the band is pressed to the edge of ruin does the drumming stop. A wind climbs to storm-strength, the crater goes dark under an enormous shadow, and the roc the Stormbird has called stoops into the pit, lifts the whale clear of the lake, and is gone, leaving a single black feather on the stone. The shaman scatters a powder over the cleared water, heals the wounded with a touch, and tells them to sleep while he keeps watch.

## The Reward and the Road On

By morning the water already runs clean. The band climbs back down to Kral's boat and is rowed round to a village turned out in relief, and in the hall a long table is set with fish soup and, for the day's honour, lobster. Bedvir hands over a leather pouch of sixty-three copper, and the Stormbird gives one thing more: a wordless, high song that every gull understands as a cry for food, so that whoever sings it within a gull's hearing will be brought a fish. He asks that it be taught sparingly. The band is welcome in Springwater whenever they pass that way again. The adventure stands on its own, a short and strange errand with a couple of hard fights and a mystery in it that never gets fully explained.
