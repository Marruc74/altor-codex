## The Spider King

Four hundred years ago a magician made himself a king. Under the name Arach he bound nearly all the tribes of the Toorra desert into one host and turned it against Detwach, and the memory of his raids in every direction still frightens the region; his own bodyguard rode into battle on the backs of enormous spiders. At the height of his power he died without warning, killed, it is said, by one of his own magical experiments. His grieving guard raised a gigantic pyramid for him a short ride from the caravan city of Cerand and laid him in it among his treasures, with countless spiders and demons set to keep his rest. His empire fell apart in a bloody scramble for the throne, but the pyramid has stood untouched ever since, throwing its long shadow over the road to Faar.

## The Conjurer's Errand

In Cerand the band is sought out by an old man, Assar Besvärjaren, who wants three jade statues brought out of the pyramid and will pay well for them, gold in advance and a ring that makes a strong swimmer of whoever wears it. He gives three warnings and no explanations: a shaft runs the whole height of the tomb; several of its treasure chambers are false; and the last guardian of the hoard is, by the old story, the giant spider Lepera. Then he sends them off to the edge of the desert, where the pyramid waits under a sky of baking heat, its great stairway climbing past statues of women with the heads of insects to a polished stone disc that has not opened in four centuries.

## Into the Pyramid

The disc is a puzzle before it is a door, worked by three copper spiders and a verse in a dead tongue, and the moment the band sets to solving it a pair of giant bird-spiders falls on them out of the dark. That is the temper of the whole place. Below the entrance the pyramid is a maze of sloping passages and doors that swing shut the instant they are let go, lit only by whatever torch the party carries and stinking of old death. Where the light happens to fall, scenes from the Spider King's life still show through four centuries of soot, painted across the walls and floor of the upper chambers. Its makers had a cruel sense of humour: a bare little room that spins like a fair-ground wheel until a sniggering voice invites its bruised victims to "take a lottery ticket"; a chamber where a menacing mummy crumbles at a touch and thanks its destroyer for the mercy; a band of rowdy skeletons led, as a final joke, by the skeleton of a duck. Somewhere in the shaft squats Puutch, a spider-man bound to polish the great descending pole forever, who seizes and punishes anyone who rides it down past him.

## The False Treasures

Arach's tomb is built to punish greed. One spotless marble chamber holds an enormous pile of gold that is no gold at all but a ravenous jelly wearing an illusion; another, a chapel of purple and marble, holds the lovely ghost Ninea, who weeps that she is a victim of the dreadful Spider King and begs the band to tear down a golden-spidered tapestry on the northern wall. To do so is to spring the trap: hidden doors swing wide on fresh guardians, and only a scholar of old tongues is likely to grasp, before the killing starts, that "Ninea" is nothing but the Toorranian word for spider. Beyond and below wait worse things still: a demon woman with a crocodile's jaws crouched in a slime pit under a floor of painted cardboard, and corridors that drop their ceilings or their occupants on a whim.

## The Last Guardian

The heart of the pyramid is given to Lepera, the giant spider of the legend, the worst sight the band will ever meet, who waits in unhurried patience until the lesser servants have spent themselves before she moves. Past her lies the true crypt: a low, foul chamber at the end of a long tunnel, its floor a slab of black marble lettered in gold, **Arach konung**. Here at last are the king's real treasures, the three jade statues among them, and a hidden passage out through the pyramid's side. The hoard is gold and jewels and stranger things, and it is heavy. Heavy enough that the question of how to carry a dead king's fortune back across a desert is very nearly an adventure of its own.

## The Diggers from the Mountains

The band are not the only ones interested in Arach's gold. Deep beneath the tomb, in a great chamber of lava and the dead husk of a colossal spider, a small company of svartalfs and trolls is at work, sent by Tachmog the Cruel to take the treasure from below. Their way in is a cramped tunnel that runs miles south through the rock to Tachmog's mountain stronghold, too low and too well guarded to serve anyone trying to follow it the other way. They are led by iGunther, a learned and level-headed captain who has rightly guessed that the crypt lies just above his diggings, and they want nothing but to be left alone; they fight only if attacked, and would far rather strike a bargain. But iGunther is not a man to be robbed. Cross him too far and he will touch off a fire-and-thunder bomb that brings tons of rock down on the crypt and buries the king, his treasure and everyone reaching for it together. Whether his master then sends the trolls after a newly rich party for revenge is a thread left dangling for whatever comes next.
