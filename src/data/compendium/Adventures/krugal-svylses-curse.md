## A Joke a Hundred Years in the Making

Krugal Svylse was a real adventurer once, sung of in the hero-songs, and he hated nothing so much as the trap-stuffed underground complexes he had spent his life crawling through. So in his old age he built one of his own, not to kill but to mock: a labyrinth of absurd snares, mocking signs and harmless pratfalls, salted with clues, where he then withdrew to die among his own jokes. A hundred years on, his fortune still waits at the heart of it, and his bones with it.

The adventurers are drawn in by his descendant Mjönar Svylse, a bandit who has come by the treasure-map and wants the gold without the going. He presses the map on them in a rain-lashed tavern with a tale of his ancestor's haunting ghost, neglecting to say that the map itself is cursed: whoever carries it lives under a ceaseless downpour, clothes rotting and iron rusting, the sun returning only to one who walks far enough to leave the map behind. The truth is simpler than the ghost-story, for Mjönar means only to dodge the soaking, and to rob them of the treasure when they come back to town.

## The Road and the Monastery

The way to the treasure is a string of comic encounters, a soft-spoken necromancer glad of company, a fat halfling prophet of the god of food, a mammoth-hunter, a pair of dim bandits, a castle that turns out to be a mirage, and the clue that finally points the party on to where the great lindens grow. There stands Kalson's Monastery, a barefoot order of the fertility-goddess about its golden holy linden, and there the comedy turns briefly grave, for the place is besieged by orcs under the uruk Ushtur, who demand a treasure the monks do not have. Held off until the last hour, the siege is broken by the proud elves of Eglath Andoria, who ride in caring nothing for thanks.

## The Complex and Its Punchline

The complex itself, behind a waterfall guarded by a sour cyclops and a hydra in his bathing-lake, is Krugal's masterpiece of mockery: one-way teleports, a false wishing-well, a floor that hurls lightning at anyone who walks upright across it, a bridge that is perfectly safe, a maze undone by a simple draught of air, a book-eating piglet snoring in the library, and a mummy who ferries the dead across an underground lake for the right coin. The genuine dangers are few and far between, and the deadliest of all is the great demon who sleeps behind a curtain marked, plainly, do not draw the curtain. Past it lies Krugal's tomb, his self-enchanting arms, and a chest of gold left with his warm regards to whoever was clever enough to reach it, and the last joke is finding the way back out at all.
