## The Vengeance of Michima

Ten years ago Sugimoto's half-brother Michima raised an army to take the province for himself, and was broken on the field by Sugimoto and his ally Yamashito, dying among his men. His mother Sumie and the wizard Nitobe, who had set him on, bore his body away and buried it in secret, and went to ground in a house deep in the Boar Forest. There the matter rested, until the dead general's spirit, which had never found peace, came back. Michima's ghost has taken possession of his living brother, and through Sugimoto's hands it means to finish what the battle ended: to murder the wife and son, to strike down Yamashito at the very feast that marks the old defeat, and to turn Sugimoto's new-mustered army against the lands of the man who beat him.

## The Lord Who Changed

The adventurers come to all this as strangers, cast ashore by a storm on the sagaland of Jih-pun and taken into the service of Yamashito at Kushi. When the boy Inazo arrives in flight from his father's house, Yamashito sends them quietly across the Boar Forest to Takata, to pass as warriors come to sell their swords and to learn, without ever naming their true lord, what has befallen his friend Sugimoto. In the city they find a place turned to an armed camp under a lord no one recognises any longer, a wife kept under guard who fears her husband has gone mad, old soldiers of the dead general drifting back under warrants of free passage, and temple priests who whisper that this is no madness but a possession, and that the cure, if there is one, lies with the monks of the island of Iko.

## The Island and the Older Secret

Iko is a hard sail through wrecking rocks and sea-mist, and on it the gentle monk Hoshiro keeps the lore of casting out spirits. He will come to the mainland to do it, but the rite must be sung over the dead man's own bones, and only two living souls know where they lie. Deeper in the island's woods stands a far older ruin, a temple to a forgotten god whose carvings remember the fair-haired Ainu, the first people of the land. In its crypt, clasped to a mummified priest, waits a book that tells of a flute that opens the road into death itself, kept once by the black dragon Oolong, and a map with a small black dragon marked upon it. The tale is true, and it is the seed of what comes after.

## Laying the Dead to Rest

The bones are hidden under a meditation house on a grave-mound in the wizard Nitobe's forest compound, behind a walled garden of silent hounds and orcish guards, watched over by the ghost of Michima's own captain. To end the haunting the adventurers must learn the grave's place from Nitobe, from Sumie, or from a frightened servant, win past the dead warden, and bring Hoshiro to sing his exorcism over the bared bones while the ghost is too far off to interfere. With the rite done Sugimoto wakes himself again, remembering nothing, grateful, and the letters taken from the wizard's house stand as proof against the plotters.

## The Road On

The adventure can be played alone, but it leaves a thread hanging. The book from the temple crypt is no idle legend, and should a companion or a friend fall before the end, the way lies open to seek out the flute of the black dragon and walk down into the land of the dead to bring them back. That road is the Dragon Flute.
