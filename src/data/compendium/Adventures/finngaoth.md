Finngaoth, "the Temple of the Clear Wind," stands atop a hill as a shrine to the demigod Pigi Magilre. It is a strange and troubling place, and the church of the region has done everything in its power to keep the faithful away from it.

## The Demigod's Temple

Pigi Magilre is said to visit the land of Magilre once every three hundred and fourteen years, bestowing divine gifts and a heightened sense of smell on his followers. When he came in 314 and found his band of disciples wiped out, he raised Finngaoth himself on the hilltop. The local prelate, Albe op Conra, had the temple torn down thirteen times, and thirteen times unseen hands built it up again, until he gave up and wrote to the exark Zegedorn III in Ekeborg. The delegation sent to investigate went in, was gone for hours, and came out converted, declaring that the temple "has the right kind of smell." After that the church set guards at the foot of the stair, spread word that a dreadful demon had taken the place, and forbade the devout to go near.

## Within

The temple runs some fifty metres, a grand stair climbing to a hollow foundation and a main hall above. Inside stand two great spheres on ivory and ebony stands, one of black basalt and one of white marble, each holding a strange luminous liquid: drink from the one and a glowing halo spins about your head, drink from the other and a pair of small horns grows from your brow that nothing will remove but the other sphere's draught. A two-metre bronze statue of Pigi looms in the inner room, a three-eyed, three-legged young man with great pointed ears and horns, and beside it a bowl of peanuts that never empties however many are taken. A throne waits there too, and those who sit on it risk being converted to Pigi's creed on the spot and spirited away to the hall below, where a censer of bronze hangs on a thread of magical silk and the brown mass within, lit and breathed in, sharpens the sense of smell forever after.

## Pigi's Creed

Those the temple converts find a handful of odd tenets fixed in their minds: that what is round is good and smells good; that one should never eat fish, nor carry more gear than can be borne with ease; that every three-hundred-and-fourteenth day is supremely holy and to be marked by eating peanuts to the point of a sacred nausea; that every mockery should be returned in kind; and that the god is honoured by repeating the word "Rammas" after every sentence.
