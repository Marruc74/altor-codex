## The Valley and the Village

At the end of a long and fertile valley, four days' march from the nearest town, a band of some fifty settlers once founded a village. It prospered; within a few years it had grown to more than twenty buildings and over a hundred souls, and in its good fortune the people raised a church to honour their god, a great stone hall under a copper tower, its windows bright with saints. For the better part of a century the village flourished in its green valley, far from the troubles of the wider world. Today it is a ruin of broken walls and deep wells, and the only things that move in its streets are the dead.

## The Nameless Knight

The first shadow fell long before the end. Dark elves came down on the village to burn and plunder it, and in the worst of the fighting a knight in shining armour appeared as if from nowhere, bearing the symbol of the village's god, and drove the raiders off, but fell at the wall in the doing of it. No one ever learned his name. The grateful villagers buried him as a hero, with his silver armour and his sword and a strange horn besides, in a secret chamber dug beneath the floor of their church, and went back to their quiet lives believing the danger past.

## The Necromancer's Vengeance

It was not. A century after the knight's fall, a mighty man in the east sent a servant of death westward to scout the valley. The necromancer, going disguised as an adventurer, heard the tale of the nameless knight's grave and guessed that a great treasure lay with it in the church. He came back with twenty skeletons and set them loose upon the village. The priest met him at the altar and, with the last of his strength, called down a stroke of lightning so great that it killed the necromancer, threw down the western wall, and killed the priest himself. The village was left a ruin but for the church, emptied of every living thing. Yet the necromancer's skeletons, bound to a dead will, simply went on, and have kept their watch ever since.

## The Priest's Plea

The priest did not rest either. His spirit has lingered all this while in search of someone able to cleanse the place he died for, and at last it finds the band, tells them the whole grim history, and in return for their help promises to show them where the nameless knight lies buried beneath the church. It is no small thing the ghost asks. The dead keep good watch: four skeletons walk a fixed patrol through the ruins on a loop that never changes, circling the wells and the broken houses, and they hunt down any intruder they catch sight of and drag the bodies to the nearest well to drop them in.

## Beneath the Altar

The church itself is guarded, two black-clad skeletons flanking the altar that will not suffer the living near it. The way down is in the altar itself, which slides aside to reveal a narrow, wet stair into the catacombs: a warren of bricked passages, wandering guardians and the walled-up tombs of the village's old priests, embalmed and given to their god, eight graves in all though only seven were ever carved with a name. The deep way is trapped as cruelly as it is guarded: a corridor of three stone pillars that crush, slam and skewer the careless all at once, littered already with the bones of those who came before, and beyond it a last wall to be broken through by hand into the chambers at the heart of the dead place.

## The Knight's Grave

Past the guardians and the traps lies the nameless knight's grave, a hidden chamber where his skeleton lies stretched on a central altar, the bones of a man far taller than any of the villagers who buried him, with his arms hung about the walls: a silver shield blazoned in gold, a gem-set bastard sword, a suit of shining silver armour, and a horn. The horn is the true marvel. Carried from some other world or age, it lays its enchantment on everyone who hears it rather than a chosen few, at a cost drawn from the one who sounds it. Deeper still, behind a door of solid iron, waits the treasure the necromancer died reaching for and never reached: a great gold-inlaid chest heavy with silver and gold. Whether the band lays the dead to rest and frees the valley, or simply strips the grave and goes, is left for the telling. The priest's own belongings may hold a map, a medallion or a ring to lead them onward.
