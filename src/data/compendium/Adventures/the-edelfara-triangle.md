## The Quarrel in Edelfara

In the county of Edelfara, a forested corner of the Zorakin duchy of Pharynx, three nobles are at each other's throats. The young Count Sebestid sent a hunting party to chase dark folk through the woods of his poor but well-loved vassal, Baron Eke; the baron, fearing he would only provoke the forest folk, drove the hunters out; and the count, enraged, declared him an outlaw and laid siege to his castle of Akershus. The duke of Pharynx, wishing to seem neutral, quietly ordered a third noble, the miserly Marquis Ridderskors, to help the baron, and the marquis grudgingly scraped together five hundred gold as a peace-offering to the count. None of them guessed that the whole quarrel had been arranged.

## The Dead Courier

The adventurers come upon the affair by chance, finding a murdered man in the forest as they travel toward the town of Sortmund. He is a ducal courier, days dead, a crossbow bolt in his chest and his satchel cut open and emptied. Nearby lies a tarred packet the killers missed, sealed with the duke's own sigil, and within it a letter of safe-conduct and a secret charge to one Ererik: to find out, in confidence, who murdered the knight Sir Kettil and stole a chest of gold, and to suspect above all the count, the baron and the marquis. Ererik is already dead, and so his mission, and his safe-conduct into every castle in Edelfara, passes to the adventurers.

## The Three Suspects

Each of the three nobles is sure of one of the others, and each has a motive that holds up just well enough. The count insists the outlawed baron killed the knight to keep the gold and escape his debts, and steers every question that way, for the longer the feud runs the sooner he may lawfully take the baron's lands. The marquis blames the baron too, and mourns his five hundred gold far more than his dead knight. The baron, walled up and furious, is certain the count and marquis plotted together to send him an empty chest and pin the murder on him. Their pride keeps them from ever comparing what they know, which is exactly what the real killer is counting on.

## The Truth

None of the three is guilty. The author of it all is Lekh, the cold and clever svartalf chieftain of the Toril forest, who set out weeks ago to weaken the nobles before a great raid. He forged a letter from a guest who never came, to goad the count into the fatal hunt; he had his orcs burn a woodcutter's hut to frighten the baron; and when he learned of the marquis's gold, he laid an ambush on the road, killed Sir Kettil with a heavy crossbow no noble would carry, and took the chest. His orcs killed the duke's courier too, though they missed the dispatch nailed beneath the man's tunic. The clues that point to the forest are few and easily overlooked, the tendi-wood bolt, the barbs, the wolf-tracks, the duke-stamped coins in an orc's purse on the road, but they are there for those who think to look past the nobles to the woods behind them.

## The Reckoning

To untangle it the adventurers must survive the forest road, where Lekh's orcs lie in wait, and so come by the stolen coins and the half-eaten letters that prove the forest folk met both the knight and the courier. The trail leads to Lekh's hidden camp a mile south of Akershus, where the chest lies buried in his tent and his whole host waits to fall on the nobles once they have spent themselves. The surest end is not a charge but a word: to persuade the count's besiegers to turn from the baron and strike the dark folk instead, for Lekh's grip on his rabble is weak and they break and flee at real resistance. Fail, and the siege grinds on until the forest army bursts from the trees upon two exhausted sides at once, and Lekh, who gives no quarter, has his raid after all.
