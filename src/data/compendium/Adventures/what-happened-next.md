## After the Dark Lord

Dakoth is dead, his host scattered, his risen dead destroyed, and the heroes who broke him are made lords of the war-ruined Eastlands. It should be the happy ending of a long road, and it is not one, for there are no happy endings in such histories, only crises that come and go. The dark lord's fall has uncovered everything his terror held down: a land stripped bare with winter coming on, tens of thousands homeless and his freed slaves among them, and armies with no master and no bread. This is a tale of rule under unending crisis, and it turns on four troubles the heroes must answer one after another.

## The Demon's Last Army

The first is a demon. Daulruag, Dakoth's own, is the single power left strong enough to bind the broken orcs and sell-swords into an army again, and a battle is days away. Godvindel, the old magic-master, would have the heroes strike the demon's command-tent the night before the field, and lends them spells and draughts to do it; but the wise man is wrong about which tent is which, and the raid is a race against the camp waking. Daulruag takes no hurt from anything but magic and enchanted steel, and strikes twice for every blow, and whether he falls decides the battle that follows.

## Bread and Pirates

Winning only sharpens the hunger. On Godvindel's counsel the heroes load the enemy's captured arms and sail west to trade them for grain, and the pirate-thick crossing turns into a fight against raiders who outnumber the ship threefold. The swarming crews matter less than their captains; bring enough of those down and the rest break, and a realm that was starving buys its winter's bread.

## The Killing Cold

Then the wood-elves of the north forbid the felling of one more tree, for the war has thinned their forest to ruin, and a people who never freeze cannot see why men should die without wood. War between elf and man waits at the end of that road unless a better answer is found, and the dryad Alataria brings one: a hidden academy of elf-mages who can grow timber overnight. Finding it means a hard journey through a wilderness of starving orcs, suspicious silver elves and worse, the Wind Maiden's riddle for a guide, and at the end the discovery that the only elf who knew the way has been eaten by trolls, his damaged map all that is left to follow.

## The Castle That Was Not There

Almost at the academy, phantom riders take the heroes and drag them to a castle and a court, where they are tried in a breath and sentenced to die within the hour. Nothing of it is real. The castle is a ruin, and its lord is Djakar, a dead illusionist who buried his every failure under illusions until he forgot the world was real, died believing himself mighty, and lingers as a ghost to keep the dream and kill all who wander in. Steel does nothing, for the soldiers are not there, and yet belief kills as surely as any blade. The one way out is doubt: to say aloud that none of it can be real, or to pierce it with a spell of true sight, before the half-hour runs out. When the lie is named the mirage falls, Djakar thanks them and begs them to carry his story as a warning, and the academy's own mages, who had come to watch the dangerous ruin from afar, step out of the trees to help.

## What Comes After

Even peace is only the next crisis waiting. With no dark lord to unite them, the servants of Eledain and Sathmog may turn on one another again, and the heroes, trusted by both, may be the only ones who can keep them apart. Immigrants will come to the empty Eastlands with the spring, bringing land enough for all and resentment enough to spoil it, and other faiths besides; and the elves watch the untouched north being opened with no joy at all. None of it is solved here. It is left to those who rule, which is the whole burden of the tale.
