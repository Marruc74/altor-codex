## The Job

Far out in the Western Sea, beyond Mereld, lie the black volcanic islands of Pandarealia, rich in silver and copper and peopled by masked human colonists, dwarven miners and the native febraks. On the island of Nicostrea the dwarves of Arkatskij have opened a new silver mine, Provot Irsk, on the slopes of the dead volcano Briskera, and within a month it had turned, in their word, cursed: cave-ins, sickness in the gut, snakes in the galleries, a mine-inspector dead of a sudden seizure. Certain that a human rival is sabotaging the work and unable to catch anyone at it, the dwarves send their journeyman Rasputji to hire outsiders, and offer silver jewellery worth two thousand gold to whoever ends the trouble. The heroes sail out with him, a few days after the affair of the Merchant Prince, to a black-sand bay and an hour's muddy walk up to the mine-town.

## The Truth

The dwarves are wrong, and the wrong runs deep. The new mine has been driven into the febraks' holiest ground, the very heart of the volcano they worship as the sleeping god Nabo, and to dig on is, to them, to risk waking him and destroying the island. The febraks remember the dwarves well: when the first great mine at Arkatskij was opened they were driven off, murdered or taken as slaves, and many died underground. So they have set themselves to frighten the miners away before it is too late. Their shaman has called serpents up into the galleries and prayed the mountain's spirits to bring the roofs down, and they have poisoned the dwarves' food. What they have not done, though they easily could, is kill: every snake and cave-in and griping belly is a warning held deliberately short of slaughter.

The dwarves cannot see it. To them the forest febraks are beasts, not beings capable of organised resistance, and so they look everywhere but the truth, suspecting the perfumed human agent Don Rosario, who in fact wants only the island's timber. The signs are there for the heroes to read: the broken, enslaved febraks in the town who make a sign like a warding and grow fearful at the mine's name; the old shopkeeper Adranji's memory of the very same troubles ten years back, at another mine dug toward the mountain, which stopped the day it was abandoned; and, deep in Provot Irsk, the freshly painted red-and-white wardings that prove the "savages" have walked unseen through a guarded dwarven mine and chosen to protect rather than to kill.

## The Choice

To end it the heroes must reach the febraks, which means coming to them without a single dwarf in the party, for the febraks will not show themselves otherwise. Ten hunters and the old shaman meet them in the forest, a hundred more unseen all around, and through a halting interpreter the shaman asks the one question the whole adventure turns on: can this be settled without more blood? Three roads lie open. They can betray the febraks to the dwarves and help kill them for the reward, and lose every gift of heroism and the favour of every good god for the slaughter of pious folk guarding a holy place. They can do nothing, and let the dwarves abandon the mine in time on their own, and go unpaid. Or they can do the hard thing: win the febraks' trust, and then bring the proud, prejudiced dwarves to believe them and move the mine, in exchange for a far richer vein the febraks will reveal. That last road wins the reward and a share of the new mine besides, and buys the febraks not justice but a little more time. It is the ending the tale is built for, and the hardest to reach.
