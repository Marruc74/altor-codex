## The Feud of the Twin Mountains

Half a century ago two mighty wizards, Swarzard and Kobbler, held strongholds on the peaks of Simenov and Menov and made constant war on one another, each wanting the whole peninsula for himself. To pay for their raids they plundered the country and the villages around, and for all their spite neither ever gained the upper hand. Their sons, Flamroll and Lokfaste, were raised to carry on the quarrel, but the young men had no wish to die as their fathers were living, and met in secret to make a different bargain. One night under a waning moon they murdered both old wizards, buried them together in a great mound on the land between the mountains, and marched the joined armies north to terrorise the honest folk, leaving guards over the grave and its treasure behind them.

## Vindpina and the Old Man's Tale

The band comes to all this by way of Vindpina, a windy little village on the Laxforsen, warming themselves at the Bagpipe inn one blustery evening. An old man named Kie shuffles in, fixes on the newcomers, and for the price of a beer tells them the story of the dead wizards and the hoard said to surpass almost anything. When he has gone, the smith and the miller warn that Kie will say nearly anything for a drink, though there may be a grain of truth in it, and the village is thick with rumours besides. Some are true and some are bait: that the mound has more than one level and a well-hidden back way, that traps were set so robbers never leave alive, that reptile men make the country unsafe, and that no one who has gone south to the grave has ever come back.

## The Road South

The mound lies three days south through wild country, and the way is not empty. Travellers may cross paths with Gorin and Drego, two close-fisted dwarf brothers who will sell directions to the lost and nothing more; with a starving troll band out hunting for meat; with a trio of reptile men carrying home a slain deer; or, at dusk or dawn in the marshes, with the will-o'-the-wisps Mist and Fog, who fold the world in fog and dance a false lantern ahead until the followers walk off a cliff or down a well. The wisps are weak things that live by this trick, hauling the drowned and the fallen back to their dens to make stew, and hoarding any magic they can steal.

## Into the Burial Mound

The grave is a stone cairn topped by a slab cut with a warning: *here rest Swarzard and Kobbler, but hardly in peace.* Below it a rotten ladder and a long stair wind down into a warren too narrow to walk more than one abreast. Skeletons wait in the guard rooms by the entrance, some charging openly and some hanging back to strike from behind, among the bones of adventurers who came before. The corridor beyond is a quiet trap in itself: hidden floor-hatches drop the careless into low chambers full of venomous snakes. Further in lie a chapel whose crystal ball vanishes at a touch, its old alarm now ringing in empty rooms, and a storeroom heaped with the wizards' grave-goods, where, for the party with the wit to take it, a plain magic flask holds the spirit that will later save their lives.

## The Guardians of the Dead

The deeper rooms are given to the dead and their keepers. A ghost lies coiled in a sarcophagus, ready to burst out the instant its lid is touched. A winch and a hidden turning-handle make a puzzle of one doorway, opening a secret room rather than the portcullis a hasty hand expects. Behind a drapery in the Lion Chamber, four reptile-man brothers, Shhass, Zapp, Viss and Flll, guard the flooded passage by which they crept up from the sea, and will kill before they let it be found. At the heart of the mound, in a chamber of four pillars, Swarzard and Kobbler lie in matching coffins, and here the old hatred can be turned to use: woken one at a time the mummies fight the band, but woken together they fall upon each other instead.

## The Drowning Treasure

The last room is the cruelest. A wooden chest stands bolted to the floor, an iron chest nested inside it, and a silver plate warns in verse that whoever tries to lift the treasure will take water over his head. It is no idle threat. Lift the iron chest and a great stone block drops to seal the door while water pours in from the reptile men's channel, filling the vault floor to ceiling in ten minutes. The hoard is worth the risk if the band can live to carry it out: a silver hall mirror, a diamond goblet, jewels and gold, Swarzard's enchanted mace, and the demon-sword Dziggetai, whose bound demon serves only the master who can hold its will. Escape lies in shedding armour and swimming for the upper passage as the water climbs, or in the undine of the storeroom flask, which can bear the party out one by one and, for the last soul left, carry them through the drowned tunnel all the way to the open sea.
