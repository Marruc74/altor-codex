Minotaurs were made in the elder days by a mighty magician who failed utterly to tame his creation into the obedient servants he wanted; he was driven out, and the minotaurs spread across nearly the whole world. They live mostly in clans in harsh wilds, though lone outcasts drift to human towns as bouncers, bodyguards or hired muscle, and almost everywhere they are reckoned at the very bottom of society.

## Appearance

Near two and a half metres tall, very powerful humans with a bull's head, hooves and tail, the body covered in coarse red-brown hair. The warm pelt lets them weather cold and damp that would lay a man low, so they fare well even in the far north. They wear little but a loincloth and almost always carry an enormous double-bladed two-handed axe.

## Origins

They were the work of Gauzlin, the greatest wizard who ever lived, whose power at its height none came near, not even Molgé of Irga. It was whispered that Gauzlin was the son of a god, and the rumour woke in him a hunger to become a god himself, to do as Mother Earth had done and shape a servant race of his own. He wanted a strong people who could press their master's claim over other creatures and kingdoms, for he judged the elf-folk too soft and mild for the task. But Gauzlin was no god and lacked a god's power to create, and the work came out flawed: the race took on his own worst traits, his unruliness and his refusal to bow to anyone. Their ungovernable nature drove him to the edge of madness, until he drove them from his realm and swore never to work magic again save in the service of the weak and the oppressed.

Freed of him, the minotaurs spread fast across the world, for they bred quickly, grew with almost explosive speed, and being a people without any heritage of their own took readily to the ways of whatever folk they settled among. At first they were tolerated, but their disregard for the opinions and the property of others soon bred resentment, and they sank to the lowest rung of the social ladder, lumped together with thieves and harlots and the like. In many lands special laws were written against them.

## The Clans

In the wilds the minotaurs gather in clans under chieftains, but different clans cooperate even worse than men do, and are quick to take up arms against anyone who threatens their hunting grounds or water. Within a clan the bond is fierce, as survival demands, while every other clan and people is met with suspicion or contempt.

A chieftain holds his place by raw strength, since his subjects are an unruly lot, and his chief marks are courage and skill at arms. When he grows old and weak there is never a shortage of ambitious young bulls eyeing his seat, so a change of chieftain is usually preceded by many bloody duels. Tradition governs these fights: they are fought in the open, with the double-axe, and the victor may not kill an opponent who yields.

Their dwellings are mostly caves or abandoned houses, crammed with junk and keep-just-in-case oddments in cheerful disorder. The one thing they keep in good order is their food, which they guard with a sickly suspicion.

## Livelihood

Minotaurs live chiefly by hunting and robbery. They hunt big game, tigers and antelope, deer, bear and elk according to where they dwell, and to prove their courage the bulls scorn missile weapons on the hunt and close instead with the double axe; a successful hunter strings a necklace of teeth and claws and so raises his standing. Their main source of coin and crafted goods, though, is highway robbery, which they regard with an easy conscience, as plain trade: I let them keep their lives, the saying goes, if they let me keep their goods, and to their minds that is a fair exchange. Few live in cities, where laws and rules are strange and hard to fathom, so they are pushed into the worst quarters outside the walls and end up as toughs in criminal gangs or as bouncers in shabby taverns.

## Kin and Calf

The high birth rate of old has fallen as their lot has worsened, and now stands about level with a human's. A cow bears one or two calves at a time, with some five years between, after a very long carrying of close to two years. The calves come without their red-brown hair, the skin wrinkled and blue-grey, and grow shaggy only after a few months. They are not raised so much as left to fend for themselves once the nursing is done, a hard and natural weeding-out.

## Feasts

Minotaurs have a passion for wild feasts, and almost any excuse will do, a good hunt or a fat raid, besides the feasts that come round every year with the turning of the seasons. A feast means long, deep drinking of strong liquor, the favourite being Black Death, distilled from potatoes stolen off the nearest farms and spiced so heavily it turns almost black; the spices vary from clan to clan, but anise, hyssop, wormwood and bog-myrtle are in nearly all of them. After a few hours of drinking come the games, which are coarse and dangerous and much loved: walking a narrow plank above a great bonfire, wrestling an enraged bear, or vying to see who can swallow the most live venomous snakes without falling senseless.

## Way of War

The double-axe is the minotaur's beloved weapon, near two metres long and so heavy that only their great strength can swing it. Every minotaur learns its use, the cows among them, and they tend their axes with care, oiling and polishing them almost daily and giving them names such as Swiftslayer, Elf-bane and the Butcher. Unarmed they are no less deadly, with tough hide hard to pierce: their way is to open with a charge of the sharp horns and, if the victim still lives, to set on him with their fists, while the bite is mostly used to warn and to frighten, leaving a painful wound slow to heal.

For all their ferocity they are poor at ambush and at any careful, planned manoeuvre, for impatience gets the better of them and they attack at the first chance, each wanting to reach the spoils first. This makes them rare as hired soldiers, save in the storming of fortifications, where their wildness and lust for battle serve well.

## Magic

Because they were themselves made by powerful magic, minotaurs cannot loose magic of their own to work spells; to try would unbind the magical life-force within them and bring instant death and a ruined soul. Even magic items give them trouble. They can wield an obvious magic weapon or armour without difficulty, but as often as not fail to grasp that the thing is magical at all, and with stranger items are as likely as not to misjudge what they are even for.

## The Shaman

For all that they cannot use magic, most clans keep a shaman whose power is wholly worldly, his chief office being to serve as judge. It is the second-highest rank in the clan and much sought after, so its holder must be ready to fight for the title. The worst sentence a minotaur can be given is exile, passed only for treachery against his own clan. An outcast has his tail cut off, so that all may see what he is, and then wanders the countryside alone, a terror to honest folk.
