The Jih-mono are the people of Jih-pun, very nearly all of its ten million souls. They are short of stature beside the folk of the West, with straight black hair, flat noses and narrow eyes, and they speak a tongue, Jih-wen, that belongs to no Western family of speech.

## Out of the Sea

Long ago the Jih-mono came over the water from the mainland in boats of reed and bamboo, and drove the island's first people, the Ainu, north before them into the cold mountains. They settled the good southern country, took up the growing of rice, and in time raised the city-states, the empire and the shogunate that have shaped the island ever since.

## A People of Order

The Jih-mono live within a strict and ancient order of classes, from the court and the warrior nobility down through the farmers, craftsmen and merchants to the outcast eta, and they set great store by courtesy, rank and the keeping of one's place. They look on the pale, big-nosed strangers who began to reach their shores only lately as uncouth barbarians, and call them long-noses for it. The full account of their land and their ways is told under Jih Pun.
