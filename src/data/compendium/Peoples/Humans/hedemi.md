The Hedemi are one of the stranger human peoples of Ereb, wanderers without a homeland whose horse-drawn wagons have rolled across the mainland for as long as anyone can record. The oldest writings that name them are more than five hundred years old, and even then their wagons were already a familiar sight over much of the continent. They keep a tongue of their own, unrelated to the common speech, and a culture all their own, and their name in that tongue, hedemi, means "the unbound," for the endless road they have always travelled.

## Appearance

They run somewhat smaller than the average human, fair of skin, with hair of blond or red and eyes of blue, grey or green, marks that hint at some northern origin lost in the same fog as the rest of their beginnings.

## The Parid

The heart of Hedemi life is the parid, a word that means family but reaches wider than blood. A parid binds together those joined by blood, marriage or adoption, and commonly takes in others of the Hedemi besides, any who are poor or sick or otherwise in need of its shelter, so that fifteen to thirty souls travel together as one. Each household, a man, his wife and their unmarried children, keeps its own great wagon, drawn by four horses, with two or three sleeping-rooms and a common space; and the rule among them is that a man's home is his castle, every father ruling his own wagon as he sees fit.

The parid is governed by a council called the tnemi, made up of its men who have passed their fortieth year, or, where none are so old, of the eldest men instead, usually four to six in all. The tnemi handles the parid's dealings with the world outside, coordinates its comings and goings, settles quarrels within, and keeps and interprets the laws and customs handed down from the fathers. When two parids meet on the road their councils sit together over shared business, and it is across these meetings that marriages are arranged, matched between parids so that no household weds too close.

## Trade and Craft

The Hedemi live by trade and craft, going from town to town to ply their wares at markets and fairs. They are fine workers of metal and silver, makers of musical instruments, furriers and leather-workers, and in their trading they favour goods worth much for their weight, furs and jewels and spices, light to carry and rich in profit.

## Among Other Folk

Their dealings with settled folk are often poor, soured by suspicion on both sides. The townsfolk find them strange and secretive and a little threatening, and in most lands the Hedemi hold no full rights of citizenship, so that they meet with scorn and persecution and have learned a wary distance of their own. It is hard for an outsider to win a parid's trust, but the one who manages it has friends for life.

## War

Every Hedemi who can bear arms is trained to fight, women as well as men. Under attack they draw their wagons into a ringed wagon-fort and keep the enemy off with missile weapons. All of them carry the dirk; the men bear war-clubs and longbows, the women the trident and the throwing-star; and what armour a parid can muster depends on its wealth, studded leather being the most common. About a third of any parid will stand and fight at need.

## The Healers

The care of the hurt and the sick falls by tradition to the women, the men knowing only the rudiments of first aid. Hedemi women are greatly skilled with healing herbs, and every parid keeps one or two healing-mistresses who pass the inherited lore to the rest; the making of their remedies is a close-guarded secret no outsider is ever told, though the parid will gladly sell its salves and medicines abroad. Among the mercenaries of Ereb the Hedemi, and their women above all, are held in a superstitious awe, for many a soldier owes his life to their drugs, and it is reckoned the foulest luck to do a Hedemi harm and so risk being cut off from that healing forever. For this reason a parid can move through a war-zone in tolerable safety where others could not.

## Music

The Hedemi keep a rich tradition of music, in which the men play and the women sing. Their old instruments are the tnaros, a small harp, the hoilon, a wooden flute, and the sossi, a crumhorn.

## Religion

Their faith is ancient and has followed them through the whole of their existence. They worship a twofold god, a male half and a female half, joined and yet apart. The male is the Horse-Lord, who made the horse to carry the folk across the world and taught the men to tame beasts, to work wood and to make wheels; the female is the Flower-Woman, who made the many healing herbs and taught the women the tending of the sick and the making of medicines. At the dawn of time the pair chose the Hedemi for their own and swore to watch over them so long as the world should last. They are worshipped with song and dance and the burning of fragrant herbs, and every parid carries a wooden altar for the holy rites.

## The Messengers

In each parid the god-pair has chosen a man and a woman to be its messengers, and granted them powers beyond other folk. They are treated with the greatest respect, and to lay hands on one of them is punished hard: a Hedemi who does so is cast out of the people at once, and an outsider who does so is killed.

The man is the Horse-Master, who can speak with horses, and no horse may refuse his command. He is bound by old taboos: never to harm a horse on purpose, never to eat horseflesh in any form, and never to carry anything made of horse-leather or horse-bone. The woman is the Healing-Mistress, usually among the parid's foremost herb-lore keepers, who wields the Healing Song, mending the hurts of people and animals with song and the laying-on of hands. Her gift reaches only humans and beasts, never other thinking peoples, who lie outside the Flower-Woman's knowing, and she too is bound: she may never deliberately harm a human, a beast or any thinking creature, for her work is healing and must not be marred by violence, and she must live as a vegetarian.
