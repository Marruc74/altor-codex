Goblins live in tight-knit clans in great underground mining complexes and have reached almost the same level of craft as dwarves, at least in practical function if not in beauty, so they trade their work to the less skilled dark folk. Their cities are well planned and well built, ringed by defensive labyrinths of tunnel against thieves, and the greatest of them hold as many as ten thousand. By their own lore they were the last of the dark folk to be made, shaped by the over-god Ouahran after he had seen the flaws in all the rest, and given a keener wit and a small, lithe body fitted for the deep earth.

## Appearance

About a metre and twenty tall, with grey-green mottled skin that hides them well in the wild, slanted pale-yellow eyes, an oversized almost round head, pointed ears and patchy hair.

## Behaviour

Industrious, diligent and fiercely loyal to their clan, much like dwarves but far greedier and so desperately suspicious, posting guards everywhere out of dread that intruders mean to steal their gold.

## Abilities

Light-sensitive eyes let them see in the dark as well as a man sees by day, though they are blind in total darkness and flee from bright sun. Their craft can rise to near-dwarven quality in iron, copper and silver, and a handful build cunning systems of heated or cooled air to live in lands too cold or too hot for them.

## Religion and the Dead

Nearly every goblin is deeply devout, with daily prayers and offerings to six gods: Ouahran the war-god who made them, Timimoun the warden of craft, Siwao who weaves the night, Tarabulus goddess of the harvest, Bizerte the life-giver, and Hassi Messaoud who guards the dead. Each has its own cult and temple in every city, and before a war all the free folk gather to offer to Ouahran for victory. The worst fate a goblin can imagine is to die above ground, where the body lies open to scavengers and looters; so their dead are borne with mourning-song into deep catacombs and walled into niches with their favourite goods, with rites to Hassi Messaoud that the burial-party may return safely to the living.

## Clan and Chieftain

Goblin society is a simple thing, divided strictly only between the slaves, who are taken captives and have no rights, and the free, who are loosely the workers, the warriors, ranked among themselves by skill at arms and the number of foes slain, and the priests. Over all stands the chieftain, the hardest fighter of the clan, who holds the jealously guarded right to choose first among the spoils. Every free goblin owes a long day's labour for little more than bed and board, and works the mines and forges in their own time for what they can keep. Yet a wise observer once held that it is not the chieftain who truly rules a goblin-city but its inherited traditions, for if the goblins do not wish a thing done it will not be done, or will be done badly.

## The Hatred of Dwarves

Goblins have warred since time out of mind with the two peoples who share the underworld with them, the dwarves and the cave-elves, the one for grinding rivalry over ore and ground, the other because the elves tend the deep gently while the goblins gut it. Bitterest of all was the war of Madinat in the Tarfat mountains, where the goblins charged the dwarves with cheating them out of a worked-out silver mine that hid coal and iron beneath; the dwarves called it a drunkard's jest, the clans and the dwarf-holds came streaming to the fight, and after two and a half years the dwarves won and swept the goblins from the mountains for good. To this day a goblin will say of any hopeless venture that it will happen "when Madinat is free of dwarves." True magic is almost unknown among them and mostly feared or scorned; the great exception is Tamanrasset, a goblin necromancer who rules a city of some two thousand in the Nida mountains, and whom none dare oppose.
