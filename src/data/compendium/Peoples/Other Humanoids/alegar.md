# Alegar

The alegar are an ancient humanoid folk of Ereb's wild mountains and forests, the work of some unknown god and little changed since the day they were made. They call themselves aleg; other men call them cave-people, for they dwell in caves, though not under the earth. Squat and heavily built, with strong, sinewy muscle, great heads on low sloping brows, heavy ridges over the eyes and almost no chin, thickly haired and clad in skins, they are near kin in look to the old neanderthal men, and are at bottom ordinary folk but for two things that set them wholly apart.

## The Two Gifts

An aleg's brain is large but shaped differently from other men's, and it struggles with what is abstract and with things and events that do not yet exist. So the alegar can barely invent or imagine a new way of living, and their culture has stayed primitive and all but changeless since the world's beginning.

Against that stands their memory, which is keen past any other folk's: an aleg remembers almost all he has ever lived through, and these memories pass from parent to child. A living aleg can reach back into the memories of his forefathers, fainter the further back he goes, and all the alegar share one dim, common memory of the first fathers the gods once made. Strangers adopted into a clan, lost children of other races who take up the life and even gain a totem-spirit, never gain this inherited memory.

## Way of Life

The alegar live in small clans under a strict order of rank, each soul with its place and task: a leader, the strongest and wisest of the male hunters; a medicine-woman wise in the healing virtues of herbs; and a few tool-makers. They are peaceful hunters, living mostly on roots, berries and small game, though the hunting of the great herd-beasts carries the highest honour, and killing, within a clan or between them or against outsiders, is rare. So scattered and shy are they that isolated clans meet only once in seven years, for great feasts, and have almost no other contact.

Each aleg keeps a totem-spirit, a guardian in the shape of some animal, a lynx, a beaver, a snow-leopard, that aids those who serve it well and answers in signs that show how a thing should be done; but the watch of a totem is no certainty, and the unworthy have been known to be set upon and slain by their own totem-beasts. Few men know the alegar exist at all, and take them for dumb and beast-like; and because the folk draw away from strangers rather than fight or share their land, they have dwindled badly, a remnant of an elder age clinging to the steep and unwanted places of the world.
