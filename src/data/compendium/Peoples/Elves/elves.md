# Elves

The elf-folk, whatever their kind, forest or cave, sea or sky, share a nature unlike any mortal race. They are in practice immortal: an elf can live as long as the world allows, its body keeping its vigour and its mind its clarity, and dies only by mischance or violence, perhaps after thousands of years. They differ less from one another than men do, and are strikingly of one mind in philosophy and beauty, for their long lives make their thought change slowly.

## The Long View

Where men think in years, an elf thinks in centuries, and its aims reach further than a mortal can well grasp. One forest-folk once agreed to plant a grove of lindens as a meeting-place for their bards; they waited forty-three years for the conditions to be just right, planted some forty-five trees in a single fervent season, and the bards now wait, patiently, the hundred and fifty years it will take the grove to come into its own. With this goes the elven gift of patience, kirisantisin, the waiting for the right moment, and santisin, that moment itself, when an elf acts with terrible swiftness to win exactly the result it wants. That turn from stillness to action, without warning, has undone many a foe.

## In Harmony with Nature

An elf belongs to its people more closely than any human could, and an elf without kin is reckoned a maimed thing, robbed of half its joy in life. That belonging reaches past its own race, for an elf feels itself a part of nature, bound on a spiritual plane to every living thing. To live in harmony with that whole is kateva; to set oneself against or above nature, as men and above all dwarves do, is wonteva, and an elf finds it repellent. The few who fall out of nature's order altogether, the niwontenu, become wood-wraiths and sirens.

## The Phases of a Life

An elf passes in youth through asarim, the wandering years when it leaves its family to see the world and become itself, the most dynamic time of its life, after which its character is largely set for good; those who never grow past childhood are called wonasarim, and are cherished as children all their days. The mature elf gives itself to a panarmon, a single life-pursuit carried toward a perfection far beyond what mortals find reasonable, be it a whole school of magic or one narrow art, so that the elf becomes in part a living expression of that pursuit. Most of the elves a traveller meets among men are either youths in their asarim or panarmon-seekers whose mastery has driven them abroad.

## Birth and Rebirth

When an elf's body dies its soul is not lost but reborn into another of the elf-folk, so that it never passes to the realm of the dead as the souls of mortal races must. The clear memories of the old life do not survive the crossing; what carries over is only a deep, wordless wisdom of living. There are but a fixed number of elven souls turning in this cycle, and no child of the elf-folk can be conceived until one of them comes free, so that births are very rare and many elves never bear a child at all. The few who turn wholly from Mother Earth's way fall out of the cycle altogether and do not return as elf-folk, but as the wood-wraiths and rå that their kin shun like a plague and drive from their company, though they seldom hunt them down.

## Wardens of Tevatenu

The elves hold their calling to be the wardens of the life-force that flows from Tevatenu, Mother Earth, and they are her stewards in the world. Their ideals, reason, faith, moderation, loyalty and responsibility, run stronger in them than in any human culture, and an elf's promise is kept; their loyalty to Tevatenu stands before all other loyalties. They are not aggressive, and reject conquest as meaningless, for what, they ask, is the point of ruling over another? Yet when violence is truly needed they wage it with cold efficiency and find the human notion of chivalry in war absurd. They gather no gold, holding that a thing which wears out in a decade is worthless; the only lasting works are song and poetry, which endure as long as any mind remembers them, and for an elf that is very long indeed.

## The Faith of Mother Earth

The elf-folk worship one power only, the Earth herself in her aspect as mother of all life, in a faith with little organisation. Each community keeps a simple holy place, most often an altar in a leafy bower, where its members go to meditate and gather their strength, and at dawn the greater part of them gather to honour the Mother with choral song, with music and with poetry. Three times a year, for three days each, they hold festival at places the Mother has blessed, and the elf-folk come from near and far, even the solitary kinds: the coming of spring, when the first leaves break, under the sign of beauty; midsummer at the solstice, the time of flowering, under the sign of joy; and the autumn equinox, the falling-asleep of the year, under the sign of longing. Above all things they loathe the undead, holding the unliving a monstrous defilement of the life-force, and reckon the unmaking of them a holy task.

## The Messengers

There is no ordered priesthood, but in every tribe Mother Earth chooses for herself a Messenger, calling them through a religious vision that is almost never refused and that binds them most often for the whole of a life. A Messenger keeps the holy places, leads the worship and the festivals, and helps the tribe with counsel and with deed, and is held to a higher standard than any, for they must be a pattern of how one of the elf-folk should live. They may work no spells, for their strength comes straight from Mother Earth, drawn from the life and flow of nature itself, and so it fails them in places wholly dead, defiled by evil powers, or sealed away in metal. Known by their dress all in green, the colour of life, they bear no metal and go to war in leather only, with a wooden staff and a sling. From the Mother they have such gifts as the strength of the earth to lend their limbs, the power to melt unseen into their surroundings, the speech of every beast and growing thing, and a curse that withers an undead thing to half of what it was, and it is said a chosen few have been granted greater gifts still.

## Strength and Flaw

For all their wisdom, the elves are bound by what they are. Their minds are less supple than a man's, held fast by long experience, their culture and their loyalties, so that they struggle to leave a chosen course or to meet a changing world, and their philosophy and art stay all but static down the ages. Their tongue is satenu. What they lose in flexibility they gain in common ground, for among the elf-folk there is little strife, and by the slow merging of many ways of thought they now and then arrive at truths no quicker race could reach.
