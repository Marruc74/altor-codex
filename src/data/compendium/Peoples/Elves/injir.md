The injir call themselves the horse-brothers. They are the second-largest of the forest-elf kindreds and, after the dark elves, the most warlike of all the elf-folk. They live in the saddle, nomads who range across plains, woodland and waste, and their whole life turns on the horse, the bow and the long knife. Quick to anger but cold and controlled in a fight, they are a proud, untamed people, marked out among elves by their painted skin and their love of open country.

## Appearance

The injir wear their long hair red-orange or brown, braided, or shaved into patterns, or pulled back in a heavy horsetail behind a crest. Their skin is covered in tattoos and paint. They dress sparingly, in close leather breeches called narn and a fur jerkin, and go almost always barefoot. The tattoos are more than ornament. Injir tattoo-masters work them in long rituals, and the strongest hold a fragment of the master's own soul.

## Behaviour

The injir take offence easily and fight fiercely, yet once battle is joined they turn cold and exact. They spare only women and children. They live by their herds, roaming in bands, and they prize the horse so highly that no injir can be thrown from one, though a fall is still possible. Every fifth year the tribes gather for a week, the Enolion, to trade news, compete, hold weddings, and swear blood-oaths.

## Way of War

Injir war-bands charge at full gallop with lance and spear, shouting and singing, and they are deadly mounted archers. They are aggressive but well drilled, and they do not break easily. Their young warriors earn their place through the fire-dance: weeks alone in the wild, tested with bow, knife, spear and horse, and at the end a dance through open flames, after which a tattoo is given. The wildest of them, the tiri, fight on foot in a berserk rage.

## Abilities

An injir can never be thrown from a horse, though a fall is still possible, and the people shrug off both great heat and bitter cold. Like all elves they have keen sight and hearing, need little sleep, are immune to disease and do not visibly age.
