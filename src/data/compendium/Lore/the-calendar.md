# The Calendar

Across the lands of the Shining Way, men and women reckon the passing of their days by the Arcival calendar. It was devised in the year 212 eO by the exarch Arcival of the Shining Way, and wherever that faith is the religion of the realm, it is by his ordering of the year that births are counted, harvests timed, and holy days kept. Though the names men give the months may differ from one kingdom to the next, beneath the differing words the reckoning is one and the same.

## Overview

The Arcival year is divided into twelve months, each of thirty days, making three hundred and sixty days in all. To these is added a festival week of five days at the close of the year, set apart from the months and given over to the celebration of Odo's birth and the dawning of the new year. The calendar is kept in Kardien, Zorakin, Klomellien, Magilre, Trakorien, Jorpagna, Berendien, Felicien, Hynsolge, Erebos, and Caddo. Not all the world follows it: Ransard, Jorduashur, Barbia, and Cereval keep instead a calendar reckoned by the phases of the moon; Nidland follows the calendar of Vicotnik; and the elves and dwarves keep their own ancient reckonings, older by far than any of the works of men.

## The Holy Days of the Year

The Arcival year is strung through with sacred days, each marking a turn in the great story of Odo and the faith. As the dark of winter loosens its hold, the Spring Equinox is kept, the first of the year's great feasts. Soon after come the solemn days that mark Odo's death and his resurrection, the heart of the faith's mourning and joy. At the height of summer falls the gladdest season of all, when the people keep Odo's reunion with Etin and dance the Sun Dance at midsummer beneath the long light of the longest days.

As the year wanes toward harvest, the Great Harvest Day gives thanks for the gathered crops, and the Day of Repentance calls the faithful to reckon with their failings. The Day of Fair Ladies is kept in its season, a gentler feast. And as the year draws to its close, near the very end, the faithful celebrate Odo's birth, the holiest joy of the turning year.

## The Festival Week

When the twelve months are spent, there remain five days that belong to no month at all. These are the festival days, set apart at the close of the year. They begin with Year's End, when the old year is laid to rest, and pass through the Fall of Darkness and the Return of the Sun, by which men mark the deepest night and the slow rekindling of the light. The week ends with New Year, when the world turns over and the count of days begins afresh. Throughout this week the bells are rung for Odo's birth, and the people feast and keep vigil between the dying of one year and the dawning of the next.

## The Days of the Week

The seven days of the week bear the names of Odo and his apostles, that the faithful may remember them with every turning of the days. The week opens with Odosday, the day of rest, given over to worship and stillness. After it follow Mundesday, Tyresday, and Midweek, then Turesday, Fridsday, and Larsday, each named for an apostle of the Shining Way. So it is that no week may pass without the names of the holy ones being spoken, and the round of labour and rest is woven through with remembrance.
