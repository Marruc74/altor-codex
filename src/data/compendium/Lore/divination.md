# Divination

The reading of omens is an art older than the schools of magic, and across Ereb the seers practise it in a hundred forms. A few have a true gift, and can wring some real foreknowledge from their signs; many more have only a method and a paying customer's hope, and tell the fortune that is wanted rather than the one that is true. Each method is a craft of its own, learned and practised apart from the rest, much as the reading of the stars is.

## The Many Methods

The commonest seers read what lies close to hand: the lines of a palm or of a brow, the flight and fall of arrows cast into the air, the smoke of burning herbs, the bubbles in melted wax or the sounds in molten lead poured into water, the patterns of gemstones scattered on a flat stone, the moon caught in a mirror, a key hung free on a thread, or a single line read at random from a book of verse. Others watch the behaviour of beasts, the stamping of horses, the scurrying of mice, the movements of snakes, and the grandest read the weather and the heavenly bodies themselves.

## The Measure of Desperation

Beyond these run methods stranger and more far-fetched still: divining by cheese, by onions, by oatcakes, by salt or soot or ash, by a black hen and a white cock set to peck grain from a ring of letters, the word spelled out as an omen. The seers will tell a querent that any of it works, but the truth of such arts is plainer than their answers: the stranger and more desperate the method a person will pay for, the more desperate that person has become. For those who want their fortune told by a finer instrument there is the Shaul deck, the painted cards read by the diviners of the south.
