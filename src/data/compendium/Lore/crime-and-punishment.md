# Crime and Punishment

"The law is built on fear," runs a saying laid to a Caddo judge; "if you stop hanging criminals there is nothing left for the people to fear — so hang one now and again." Across Ereb the law is harsh, uneven, and bent as often as it is kept, and a wise traveller learns the customs of each land before he runs afoul of them, for what costs a fine in one city may cost a hand, or a head, in the next.

## The Ladder of Punishments

Punishment climbs by slow degrees. At the bottom stand the warning entered in the rolls, the fine, the stocks, the spit-walk through jeering streets, and the public flogging. Higher come the brand and the thief-tattoo, marks a man carries to his grave; then mutilation, the seizure of house and goods, outlawry that makes a man fair game for any hand, and the prison-isles. At the summit wait the deaths reserved for the worst offenders: hanging and beheading (the axe for the commoner, the sword for the noble and the priest), drowning, the garrotte, burning for witches and necromancers, breaking on the wheel, and being walled up to starve in the dark. Bribery, where the judge is corruptible, and a clever advocate can lighten almost any sentence; foreign looks, a thick accent, or an unpopular race can worsen it.

## Trial

Guilt is often less a matter of evidence than of standing, and of who has paid the judge. Where a court cannot or will not decide, older customs serve. There is the ordeal, the water-test for a suspected witch, who sinks and is innocent or floats and is burned; and there is trial by combat, the belief that the one who fights best is in the right, fought in person or by a hired weapon-master.

## The Prisons

A prison lives on what the ruler grants it and on what the prisoners themselves will pay for better treatment: a candle, decent food, a visit, a healer, freedom from the irons. The richest inmates lie almost in comfort; the poorest rot. Worst of all are the prison-isles, a death sentence in all but name, ruled by a hard commandant and reckoned a place from which no one returns. And in every cell, rich or poor, lurks the gaol-fever, which wastes prisoner and guard alike and asks no man his rank.
