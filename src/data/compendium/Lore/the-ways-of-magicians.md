# The Ways of Magicians

A true magician is never merely a man who happens to know some spells. He is a figure set apart, and he takes pains that the world should know it, for the magicians of Ereb are the scholars and engineers of their age, sitting on terrible powers, and most of them would rather be feared and left in peace to study than be pestered by curious farmers and prying children.

## Style and Presence

The greatest of them do nothing by hand that magic can do for them. A door opens before such a man on an unseen wind; a globe of cold light, never a torch, leads him through the dark; his table is laid by hands no one can see, and the pages of his book turn of their own accord. Beasts know him for what he is: cats arch their backs and hiss as a necromancer passes, and butterflies trail after an animist. Many wear their art on their very bodies. Some animists grow leaves in place of hair; an illusionist may wrap himself in a cloak that forever shifts its colours and leave no footprint behind him; a necromancer goes in black, and a faint reek of the grave lingers in a room after he has left it.

They dress in bright colours hung with symbols and charms, the old-fashioned in tall pointed hats and other startling headgear, the better to overawe plain folk. And a magician is never modest about his work: he does not say "I cast a fire upon them," but flings "Heridronemus's Pyrotechnic Embrace" at the curs who dare foul the ground he walks. The flash and thunder of it, the whistling balls of fire, are half theatre, meant to break an enemy's nerve and stiffen his own side, and only loosely bound to what the spell truly does.

## The Staff and the Tools

Chief among a magician's tools is his staff, strange to look upon and full of stored power kept in reserve against the day his own runs dry. It serves as a counter-pole for his workings, and as a plain walking-staff besides, and a well-made one may be wrought to burst into flame, to hurl lightning, or to turn into a serpent in the wrong hand. A carver or a smith can make the staff, but only the magician himself can charge it. Around it he gathers the rest of his trade: crystal balls, star-glasses and astrolabes, curios brought from far lands, and, if he is serious, a laboratory for his research, his brewing and his enchantments.

## The Book and the Library

Every magician keeps a thick spell-book, the dearest of all his possessions, and guards it with spells and sigils. The serious ones are not content with that one volume but keep whole libraries, for books are costly and therefore a measure of standing, and a magician of any ambition writes a work of his own, with a title like *An Elemental Liturgy of the Modern Art of Spellcraft*, the doctoral thesis of magic, which may be the key that wins him a place in an academy. Those academies endure at all only on a double promise: that no magic be worked on their hallowed ground, kept by powerful spells of antimagic.

## The Magical Duel

Magicians are jealous, secretive and almost always hungry for power, and they trust one another not at all, each fearing the rest will steal his secrets. When two mighty ones meet, the air can turn to a grand and dangerous duel, each probing for the other's weaknesses with working set against working. A pretender who only plays at the art is laughed to scorn by the real thing, who could turn him to a thumbnail before he finished the word.
