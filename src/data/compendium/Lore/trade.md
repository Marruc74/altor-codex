# Trade

The wealth of Altor moves upon the water. For all the roads that wind between the towns and villages of the world, land transport remains forever a local affair, fit only for carrying goods the short distance from field to market or from one neighbouring town to the next. The true commerce of the world - the long traffic in copper and salt, in cloth and wine and stranger things - travels by ship, for a single vessel may bear as much cargo as five hundred pack-animals, and bear it far faster and at far less cost.

## Overview

The great arteries of trade are the seas, and chief among them the Copper Sea, busiest and kindest of all the world's waters. A crossing of that sea takes a month or more, and it is no small undertaking, for the merchant ventures his goods and his crew against pirates, sudden storms, hidden reefs, and the raiders who haunt the lonely coasts. Yet the rewards are such that the ships put out year upon year, and the harbours of the trading nations are forever crowded with masts. The chief trade metropolises are Gringul in Erebos, Arno in Caddo, Tricilve in Trakorien, Mastika Agga in Barbia, and Sterborg in Kardien - though Sterborg, for all that it sits squarely in the path of nearly every trading ship, never won real mastery of the trade, its harbour being too shallow and too small to hold it.

## Tricilve, the Heart of Trade

Of all the cities of commerce, none rivals Tricilve in Trakorien. It stands as the central hub between rich Trakorien and the lands of the Copper Sea, the place where the threads of a hundred trade-routes are drawn together and knotted. In its markets and warehouses almost anything under the sun may be bought or sold, from the common stuffs of daily life to rarities brought from the far ends of the world. A merchant who cannot find what he seeks in Tricilve may well conclude that it does not exist, and one who has goods to sell will find no readier buyers anywhere.

## Waters That Cannot Be Sailed

Not every sea is open to the trader. The great Golwynda Sea, a vast body of fresh water lying some two hundred and fifty metres higher than the Copper Sea, is sealed off from the wider world by its own fierce currents and by the thundering falls of Raas Narram, which no ship may pass; the same bar stands between it and the Maseva Bay, where the falls called the Demon Tongue cut the water-road. Where the water will not carry goods, men must carry them overland, and so the trade of Golwynda is done by portage at the towns that have grown up beside the falls. At places such as Stabul by Raas Narram and Ircront in Nidland, cargoes are unloaded, hauled past the impassable water, and loaded once more upon ships waiting beyond, and the towns of the portage grow fat upon the labour.

## The Pirate Coasts

The merchant who sails within Caddo must reckon with the pirates of Morelvidyn, and most ships keep close to the coast on their account. The pirates, for their part, hold to a rough and unspoken bargain: they let the trade pass so long as it is not plundered too often, knowing well that a coast picked clean yields nothing, while a coast left to prosper may be bled again and again. So the ships hug the shore and pay their unseen toll in the goods now and again taken, and the traffic goes on, neither side wishing to break the fragile peace that keeps them both alive.

## The Named Trade-Routes

The long sea-roads of the world bear names of their own, spoken with respect by every captain who has braved them. There is the North Trade, which runs to the cold and distant northern lands; the Demon Trade, whose very name carries a whisper of dread; the Sulphur Trade, dealing in the goods of fire and the deep places; and the Copper Convoy, named for the metal that gives the great sea its colour and its wealth. Along these routes the fortunes of nations flow, and the captains who know them well are reckoned among the most valuable men alive.
