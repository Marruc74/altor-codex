# Metals

The working of metals has shaped the fortunes of whole peoples across the long ages of Altor, and nowhere more than in the old quarrel between metal and magic. Gold and silver have always been scarce and precious, fit for coin and ornament but too soft for honest work; copper is softer still, yet alloyed with tin it makes bronze, which took and held an edge and was long the king of all war-metals. Rarest of all at the first was iron, dearer once than gold, until the smiths learned to refine it and to harden it into steel, and with that one craft the world was changed.

## Metal and Magic

Iron and steel are a bane to magic, smothering spellcraft wherever they lie thick, and a wise householder still drives an iron knife into the doorpost to ward off evil and enchantment alike. The old magic peoples, the elves and the trolls and the dragons, cannot abide cold steel at all: it wounds them to the touch and chokes the magic in them. Not every metal is so deaf to power. Gold, silver and bronze take an enchantment well, and best of all the so-called virgin metals, smelted from ore alone and never fouled by older metal nor by the bare skin of a living thing, which are reckoned strongest of all for the binding of spells. A few rare metals, meteor-iron and galvorn among them, keep none of common iron's enmity to magic, and so are prized by elves and mages beyond their weight.

## The Legend of Steel

It is told that the dwarves first learned to make steel in the deep places of the mountains and loosed the new metal upon the world, and that for this the old magic peoples have hated them ever since. The powers of evil, the legend goes, answered the dwarves by making the orcs, a race that could bear steel without harm, to stand as a counterweight to them, and to this day the trolls set orcs to the steelwork they cannot do themselves. Ringed and threaded with steel as they are, both dwarves and orcs struggle to work any magic at all, needing long purification first. Even men carry a little iron in their blood, the tale runs, and so can never match the elves, the trolls or the dragons in sorcery, save by the grace of a god to whom they pray.

## Blood and Mithril

Stranger rumours cling to the blood of the old peoples. The trolls are said to carry copper in their veins where other creatures carry iron, as the squid and its kin do, and the pale blood of the elves is whispered to be built upon the light-metal they call mithril. What runs in a dragon's veins few have lived to tell. The dwarves found that light-metal first and named it noble-silver, but it was the elven master-smiths who learned at last to alloy it into mithril, light as nothing and strong as steel and no bar at all to magic. So closely have the elves kept the secret that not even the dwarves have ever wrested it from them, and the loss galls them to an undying hatred. Mithril is dearer than gold, and a coat of it a kingly treasure that few but the richest of elves will ever own.
