# Languages

The tongues of Altor are as many and varied as its peoples, and to trace their roots is to trace the long history of the world itself. Some are the living speech of bustling cities, others the dead languages of fallen empires kept alive only in ritual and in spellcraft, and some are so strange that no mortal mind may ever wholly grasp them. From the song-like speech of holy Caddo to the hissing tongues of the lizard-folk, every language carries within it the history and the spirit of those who speak it.

## The Jori Family

The greatest of the human language families is Jori, descended from the tongue of the fallen Empire of Jorpagna. From this single root has sprung the speech of most lands from Felicien in the south to Trakorien in the north, and over the long centuries it has branched into many forms. There is West-jori and East-jori, divided by distance and time, and the newer speech called New-jori that the common folk speak today. Above these stands Imperial jori, the high tongue of the vanished empire, now fallen silent in the mouths of ordinary men and kept only by scholars and diplomats, who use it as a language of learning and of treaties.

## The Old Tongue and the Songs of the North

Older still than Jori is Erdir, the Old Tongue, an ancient language long since dead in common use. Yet it has not wholly perished, for it endures in the words of ritual and in the working of magic, where its old power still answers to those who know its forms. Far to the north, the barbarian tongues sing their own song; for all their seeming variety, they spring from a single root called narguri, the parent speech from which the many dialects of the cold lands have descended.

## Dalkish, the Holy Speech

In Caddo the people speak Dalkish, a tongue song-like and flowing, beautiful to the ear. It is more than a daily speech, for it is the holy language of the Shining Way, the tongue in which the faith lifts its prayers and chants its rites. To learn Dalkish is to draw near to the heart of the faith, and its cadences are heard wherever the Shining Way holds sway.

## The Tongues of Dwarves and Elves

The dwarves are a people of many tongues, divided as their many holds are divided, yet over time they have raised a common dwarven speech called gryndur, by which the scattered clans may understand one another. It was first the speech of the folk of the Grynner Mountains, who, driven from their homes, spread across Ereb as travelling merchants and carried their tongue everywhere they went, until it served as the dwarves' shared speech. The elves, by contrast, speak with a single voice. Their language, Satenu, was made by Tevatenu the Earth Mother herself, and knows no dialects at all - soft and rich in vowels, it is spoken the same by every elf beneath the sky, an unbroken speech as ancient and unchanging as the elven people themselves.

## The Dragon Language

Hardest of all the world's tongues is the Dragon Language, a speech so deep and strange that it cannot truly be translated, for every word must be wholly redefined before it can be carried into another tongue. Dragons and humans, try as they might, can never fully understand one another across this gulf. Within the Dragon Language lies the deep and perilous concept of the true name - the secret word that is the very essence of a being. To know another's true name and to speak it is to gain full power over that being, and so the dragons guard such knowledge with a jealousy beyond all reckoning.

## The Lesser and Stranger Tongues

The black-folk speak many tongues, harsh and guttural, and possess no script of their own with which to set them down, though some have borrowed alphabets from those they have conquered or served. The lizard-folk and the insectoids hiss and clatter their own primitive speech, simple tongues fit to their simple needs. So the world is filled with voices, from the highest learning to the lowest hissing, each in its own measure a part of the great chorus of Altor.

## Writing

For the setting down of words, the peoples of Altor have raised many scripts. Chief among them is the Joric runic row of twenty-three runes, in which the languages of the Jori family are most often written. The barbarians of the north have their own signs, rough and angular, and the elves write in the graceful Satenu alphabet that matches the beauty of their speech. The dwarves cut their words in dwarf runes, hard-edged and enduring as the stone they love. In Felicien men write in the kimzon, and the black-folk make use of such alphabets as they have gathered. In Nidland alone the writing follows Vicotnik's own Nidlandish script, set apart like the land that uses it.
