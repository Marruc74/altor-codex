# The Catacombs of Nohstril

Beneath the south-east of Nohstril, the capital of Erebos, runs a vast and tangled underground network of passages, tomb-chambers, ceremony-halls and sewers. It was never planned as one thing. It grew out of several separate systems, dug over the centuries by groups that knew nothing of one another, until in time their tunnels broke into each other and joined. Getting down into it is easy enough; getting through it is hard and dangerous, for there are no complete maps, no light, and the passages and vaults repeat themselves until a stranger is hopelessly lost.

## The Four Systems

Oldest are the **original catacombs** in the south, dug when the city was young by persecuted minorities, both as a burial place and as a refuge to hide in. Some of their earliest passages were so poorly made that they collapsed and cut themselves off, so that short stretches of catacomb still exist that join nothing else.

Then came the system of **Ordo Nova** in the south-west, begun around 260 eO by the secret merchant society and built with far greater skill, its hidden lower halls reached by a guarded secret door.

The **duck catacombs** belong to the Anklave, the crowded, low-regarded quarter of the ducks, who had no room to bury their dead and so burned them and stored the ashes in urns underground. Hundreds of urns now fill a rough tunnel system there, and the ducks have no idea how near it runs to the great network.

Last of all came the **sewers**, cut after the catacombs when the city paved its streets, threaded around and through the older passages wherever they met. Because sewer and catacomb mostly lie at the same depth, the two seldom actually cross.

## The Dwarves' Line

The dwarves of Nohstril will not have their own mine-and-tunnel system pried into, and so it is held as law that no catacomb may be dug nearer than fifteen or twenty metres to a dwarf-passage. In return the dwarves take no interest in whatever the catacomb-diggers get up to among themselves.

## Who Walks Below

Only a few entrances are easy to find — one west of the city wall, one in the cellar of the amphitheatre — while the rest lie hidden in private houses, some known only to the householder. Down in the dark move the religious cults that first dug the place, smugglers and thieves, spies and others who would travel unseen, hidden refugees and unknown sects, and the unquiet dead of those buried there. The city watch, which cannot hope to control it, stays away.

Two figures are spoken of more than the rest. **Raugnar Rathmanner**, the Rat King, is a disillusioned old animist who long ago gave up on his own kind and went under the city, where every beast — down to the worms that eat the dead — is his friend. He keeps a laboratory cut into solid rock with no ordinary way in, passing through the stone itself with a touch of his staff, and is forever trailed by swarms of rats; cross him, and he can call worms out of the earth that bore into a man toward his heart. **Luego Ban Guiti** is one of the foremost killers of the Black Hand, a tall, gaunt, soft-moving man no one has ever seen at his work. He comes and goes through the sewer-mouth in the harbour and lives aboard a never-quite-seaworthy old skiff called the Black Cat at the south quay, and he has one strange weakness: he cannot abide cats or rats, and is seized by an irresistible need to kill any he comes near.
