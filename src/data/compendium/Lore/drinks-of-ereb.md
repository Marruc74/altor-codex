# Drinks of Ereb

The folk of Ereb brew and ferment all manner of strong drink, and a tavern stands in nearly every village, but three peoples above the rest are known for what they pour.

## The Dwarven Ales

The dwarves hold themselves the finest brewers in the world, and trace the whole art to Grumir Davaz, who, by their legend, on the fourth day of a new year found that the cliff-mushroom steeped in spring-water with other things would ferment into a heady drink. That drink, mountain-ale, was the father of a long line of brews, and Grumir was made a national hero; the recipe is a secret the dwarves guard jealously.

Dwarven ale is the common table-drink of every inn in the dwarf-realms, half-dark and pleasant. Mountain-ale, the prized original, is reckoned very fine, and non-dwarves pay double for it; small-ale, mild and watery, is brewed for the dwarves' children. Fiercest of all is gräk, a famously strong oak-aged spirit, light and burning and bitter, that the dwarves love to press on unsuspecting non-dwarves and watch them struggle to keep their feet. And there is battle-ale, the dark, warming brew served before a fight, which only dwarves may drink: for one of another race to touch it is a sacrilege that every dwarf is bound to punish.

## Other Drinks

Among the famed drinks of the rest of Ereb, San arn stands first, a sparkling fruit-drink fermented only in Pendon and its surrounds and aged thirty years and more in casks of ironlind. So much myth has gathered about it, of cures for plague and snakebite and breathlessness, that the one sure thing left is that it tastes wonderful, and a great store of it lies in the cellars of Pendon's castle.

Coarser by far is orcherdrank, the foul and barely drinkable spirit the orcs brew across their lands in a hundred near-identical sorts, easy to make and easy to find wherever orcs are. The best of it is arach, the work of the Bloody Spear orcs of the Aidne mountains, which now and then turns up in the poorer inns of Kardien and is reckoned, generously, "almost drinkable." And on Caddo, and almost nowhere else, they drink Caddovin, a sour, sharp wine of goat's milk, honey and dak-fruit that is popular above its quality for one reason: it leaves no hangover at all, and so has been the ruin of many a weak soul.
