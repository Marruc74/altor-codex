# The Brotherhood of the Red Fish

Somewhere in Ereb works a nameless organisation that seems to be everywhere and nowhere at once. It leaves almost no trace: a string of clever thefts, kidnappings where the ransom always comes to exactly what the family can afford, and a great deal of carefully laid false trail. The few who have met it and lived call it the Brotherhood of the Red Fish, after the one mark its people carry, a fish tattooed over the heart. More than that is hard to learn, for a captured brother burns himself to a small heap of ash rather than answer a single question.

It is, by its own thinking, an octopus: a single body with many arms, any of which can be cut off without the creature dying. Its goal is plain and bottomless, power and luxury on the largest scale it can reach, and its trades are espionage, theft, swindling and abduction. Perhaps three to five hundred souls belong to it.

## The Octopus

At the head sits the Council, which meets around an elliptical table in secret rooms scattered across Ereb, their locations known to very few. It is led by the Alderman, the foremost of its Listeners, and it is built from four kinds of people who also make up the body below.

The **Watchers** handle recruitment, surveillance and the brotherhood's inner security, and follow the art of illusion. The **Listeners** are the administrators and strategists, the ones who find the riches worth taking and parcel out the work at every level. The **Burners** are a small company of sorcerers, schooled in elemental magic and shamanism, whose grim speciality is the binding of fire-demons. And the **Burned** are the spearpoint, the active agents who carry out the eliminations and the hardest jobs, each with a fire-demon bound inside him.

Between the Council and the working cells run pairs called duets, one Listener and one Watcher, each watching over a handful of arms. An arm is led by a triad, a Burned, a Listener and a Watcher together, and worked by ten or twenty footmen, the bored noblemen and adventurers who actually do the stealing and spying. No one knows much beyond his own link in the chain: the footmen know only some of their fellows and the Listener of their triad, the triad does not know where its duet is or who sits in it, and the duets report to faceless councillors. Cut an arm and the body never feels it.

## The Burned

To become one of the Burned is to sell your soul. In a deep hall lit by hundreds of fires and thick with arcane smoke, the chosen one is chained while lesser sorcerers hold him still and a gate is opened onto the plane of fire; a demon-lord's roar fills the chamber, and the officiant binds a fire-demon into the runes tattooed above the man's heart, where they form the sigil of a flaming fish. One in three of those chosen burns to ash on the spot. The rest rise changed, and need never again creep about in the dark like common thieves.

A Burned man sees in any darkness, even a magical one, and can set fires with a touch of his hand. He is stronger, quicker and far steadier of will than he was, and he can work a hypnosis that plants false memories or quiet commands in another's mind, so long as the command does not cut against the victim's own nature. The demon that lives in him may keep him alive for centuries, the more willingly if it is now and then allowed to take the body and pursue its own ends. And when a Burned man is cornered with no way out, he burns: the fire that frees the demon leaves nothing of the man but ash, and no captive of the brotherhood is ever questioned twice.

## The Knight Who Solves the Crime

For all its strength the brotherhood has two soft spots, both born of its own pride. The first is its love of elegance. A theft carried off without finesse does not count, in their eyes, as anything better than common highway robbery, and an arm will happily spend two thousand on a job worth five hundred so long as the story is a fine one, which now and then runs a venture straight into loss or discovery. The second is the contempt its men hold for women, whom they treat as things to be charmed and deceived; more than one arm has been unravelled by a seduced victim who turned out to have wits and a will of her own, and the Council files such disasters away as a strange statistical curiosity rather than a lesson.

Its signature shows in the things it leaves behind. The ransom set to the exact coin a family can pay. The crime so framed that an innocent is arrested at once, often by a brother playing the hero. And above all the charming knight-errant who turns up wherever a crime is done, leads the hue and cry, and gallantly sets everything right, while the real culprits melt away. By the time anyone sees the pattern, there is usually nothing left to catch.
