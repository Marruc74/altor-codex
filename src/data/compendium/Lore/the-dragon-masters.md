# The Dragon-Masters

The word carries two meanings, and they are easily confused. The first is a people: the immortal keepers of the dragons who dwell in the high peaks above Cereval and raise the great beasts to their own ends, whom many simply call the dragonmasters and who are better named the dragon-lords. The second is a title, held by one being at a time, the appointed Dragon-Master who stands between dragonkind and the thinking peoples as a kind of cultural translator. Since the first of them, only five have ever held it.

## Overview

- **The dragon-lords:** the immortal race that keeps and raises dragons in the peaks above Cereval
- **The Dragon-Master:** a single appointed title, a liaison and chronicler between dragons and the humanoid peoples
- **Held by:** five beings so far, of several different races
- **The first:** Terrarek, a karkion, accepted by the dragons in 714 fO
- **The chronicle:** the Encyclopedia Draconicus, begun by Terrarek and added to ever since
- **The prophecy:** that there will be seven Dragon-Masters, and the seventh will end the dragon-lords' exile

## The Dragon-Lords

The dragon-lords raise dragons by telepathy, reaching the unhatched young in the egg so that the dragon takes its keeper for its father and is trained from birth. This works because a dragon is not born with its nåsh, the fierce sense of honour, power and self that makes a grown dragon sooner die than submit; that comes only with maturity, at three or four centuries, and would otherwise drive the young dragon to leave. The lords' telepathy holds their dragons in a kind of lasting childhood, willing and submissive. But not even a thousand years of it can smother a dragon's nature forever, and most so-raised dragons turn defiant somewhere between a thousand and fifteen hundred years old and are allowed to depart, kept from doing too much harm by the Moon Law. The lords' very success became their problem: nearly every dragon at the peaks is now of their own raising, leaving few wild ones to study, their movements are hemmed in, and the shared telepathy has made them all think alike, a poor thing for any people that hopes to learn something new.

## Nåsh and the Title

A dragon plays a lifelong game of nåsh, called taal-nåsha, forever weighing what honour it might win, and to kill a worthy foe is the height of it. This is why a Dragon-Master's life is always in danger, and why the role demands cunning more than strength. It was the second Dragon-Master, the elf Nillinia, who found the key: a dragon reckons a female to hold no nåsh worth taking, and so will not play the game against a woman and may submit to her wholly without shame. So sure was she of this that she held all who came after her ought to be women, and a master who is also sworn to chastity is safest of all, for then a dragon can win nothing whatever by her death. The masters still dispute what nåsh truly is: Nillinia took it for a mere display meant to draw mates, while Gadirm, holding it set above nearly all else a dragon does, reckons it an end in itself rather than a means. The title itself was made to serve two ends, to be the go-between and cultural interpreter of dragons and humanoids, and to deepen the knowledge of dragons and carry it back to the dragon-lords. The Dragon-Master reports to the lords, but the knowledge is gathered in a book of their own.

## The Encyclopedia Draconicus

Terrarek began the Encyclopedia Draconicus, and each Dragon-Master since has added to it, volume by volume. It is the fullest record of dragonkind in the world, a mixture of hard observation, gathered legend, and in places the personal devotions of its authors, and it is the nearest thing the thinking peoples have to true understanding of the dragons. The work itself is vast, costly, and written for the learned; but at the asking of adventurer friends Gadirm has set down a plainer primer drawn from it, picking out what a wanderer needs to know of dragons and their ways, strictly ordered and a touch long-winded in his manner, and warning his readers above all to set aside their settled notions and read with an open mind.

## The Line of Dragon-Masters

**Terrarek** the first, a karkion, was accepted by the dragons in 714 fO; he learned what he could from the dragon-lords, sifted the legends for their few grains of truth, and at last went among the wild dragons to study them with a mixture of fascination and distaste, writing the first volume before he passed from record around 650 fO. **Nillinia the Wise**, a wood-elf and the only known messenger of Mother Earth who was also a mage, was the most inventive of them all; she swore a vow of chastity so the dragons could win no honour by her death, and in the Third Conflux she sheltered four dragons in a sealed cave for thirteen months, the beasts giving their own flesh so she would not starve and she healing them after, a scene beloved in elven art ever since. She was killed by a young dragon in 328 fO. **Atanil Manchild**, a wandering historian, grew so obsessed with Nillinia that her own volume is half a hymn to her predecessor; she was murdered in 58 fO by an adventurer who thought killing the Dragon-Master would win him the dragons' service, and the first dragon he met freed him of both the notion and his life. **Teleria the Chaste**, a grey-elf, was the first to spread dragon-knowledge widely among the peoples; she laid the title down in 437 eO and returned to her people, and lives still.

**Gadirm the Death-Expert** holds the title now, and won it as none before him did, not by submitting to the dragon-lords' tests but by a great show of power that ended in his besting the ancient dragon Garaum, so that the dragons accepted him directly. The lords were uneasy until he sought them out in secret in 605 eO and promised to carry on the Encyclopedia. Where others disarmed the dragons' game by being worthless to kill, Gadirm warns instead that to slay him is to raise him as a death-wraith of threefold power; he has proved less a gatherer than a questioner of old certainties, and now plans an expedition to study the dragons of the far east.

## The Prophecy

When the dragons accepted the first Dragon-Master, Turcal the Black, mightiest of all known dragons, appeared and spoke a prophecy: that the world would see seven Dragon-Masters, and that the seventh would be the greatest, and that by his work the dragon-lords' exile would at last be brought to an end. Five have come and gone or remain. Two are yet to be.
