In the long dawn before bronze became common, the island of Melukha rose as one of the first great cultures of the world. Its people called their home the blooming pleasure garden, and the name was no boast - the island lay green and generous, and those who dwelt there wanted for little. Here humans and elves lived side by side in a peace so old that few could remember its beginning, sharing the same fields, the same songs and the same gods.

## Overview

- **Era:** Bronze Age high culture, deep in the fO centuries
- **Seat:** the island of Melukha, "the blooming pleasure garden"
- **Peoples:** humans and elves, dwelling together in lasting peace
- **Faith:** Mother Earth (Moder Jord) and a pantheon of peaceful gods
- **Renown:** music and architecture
- **Rule:** always a matriarchy, governed by women
- **Metal:** adopted late, and still won through trade rather than the forge

## The Blooming Garden

Melukha was an island of abundance. Its soil gave freely, its waters teemed, and its seasons were kind, so that the labour of survival never hardened into the labour of war. Where other peoples learned early to guard their fields with spears, the Melukhans learned instead to fill their days with beauty. Plenty bred patience, and patience bred a culture turned toward art rather than conquest.

## Humans and Elves Together

What set Melukha apart from every culture that came after was the union of two peoples. Humans and elves shared the island in genuine fellowship, their lives so woven together that neither ruled the other. This concord left its mark on everything the Melukhans made, for the long memory of the elves and the quick invention of humankind tempered one another into a single gentle way of living.

## Mother Earth and the Peaceful Gods

The Melukhans worshipped Mother Earth, whom they named Moder Jord, the giver of the island's bounty. Around her gathered a whole pantheon of peaceful gods, deities of growth, of harmony and of the quiet crafts, and there was little place among them for gods of slaughter or dominion. The faith mirrored the land - generous, ordered and slow to anger.

## Music and Architecture

In their long ease the Melukhans turned their gifts toward music and toward building. They raised structures of grace and proportion and filled their halls with song, and in both arts they reached heights that later ages remembered with longing. To make and to adorn was, for the Melukhans, a way of honouring the gods who had made their garden bloom.

## Rule of Women

From its first days to its last, Melukha was a matriarchy. Authority passed through women and rested in their hands, and this was held not as an innovation but as the natural order, as old as the worship of Mother Earth herself. The rule of women lent the culture its even temper and its long stability, and it endured unbroken for as long as the garden bloomed.

## Latecomers to Metal

For all their refinement, the Melukhans came late to metal. Living in such plenty, they felt little need of harder tools or sharper edges, and they never made themselves masters of the forge. What metal they used they won through trade with other peoples, exchanging the fruits of their gentle island for the work of others' fires - a culture rich in beauty yet content to leave the casting of bronze to hands less peaceful than their own.
