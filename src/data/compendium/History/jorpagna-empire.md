# Jorpagna Empire

The Empire of Jorpagna was the greatest realm Ereb has ever known - its Rome, an age of paved roads, walled cities and disciplined legions that bound a hundred peoples under a single crown. At its height it held the whole northern coast of the Copper Sea, the west of Ereb, and Efaro across the water, and even now, a thousand years after its ruin, its mark lies everywhere: in milestones along old roads, in the laws of the lands that rose from its corpse, and above all in jori, the common speech still used from coast to coast.

The jori reckoning itself begins with the empire, counting the years before and after the Victory Year in which it was founded - 1065 fO by the common count. From that year it stood for the better part of five centuries, until the Third Conflux brought it down. To the peoples of today it is both wonder and warning: a vision of order and grandeur, and a lesson in how swiftly such things are undone.

## Overview

- **Name:** The Empire of Jorpagna (Imperium Jorpagnum)
- **Founded:** the Victory Year, 1065 fO, from the union of Krun and Hynsolge
- **Fell:** around 600 fO, with the Third Conflux
- **Heartland:** the regions of Jorpagna, Tolan and Krun in central Ereb
- **Prefectures at its height:** Aidne, Mirel, Tolan, Hynsolge, Krun, and last of all Efaro
- **Capital:** Grivela, a city of nearly half a million
- **Ruled by:** emperor and senate
- **Lasting legacy:** jori, the common tongue of Ereb

## The Founding

Krun was the oldest and most cultured power of Ereb, an aristocratic republic; Hynsolge a barbarian land granted to the hyner long before in thanks for their help in Krun's old iron-war. For centuries the hynsolger took up Krun's culture while keeping their barbarian temper, until a quarrel of honour grew into open war in 1080 fO. After fifteen years of hard fighting the hynsolger won the last battle at Grivela in 1065 fO, and held the place sacred ever after, raising their new capital upon it. The young Hynsolgian king Aurelion bound Krun and Hynsolge into one realm by a formal act of state, swearing to keep Krun's constitution and the senate's lawmaking power untouched, and so the empire was born. The old Krunish colonies along the Copper Sea swore loyalty to Grivela to a man.

## The Imperial Order

Jorpagna fused the rule of two peoples: the aristocratic republic of Krun and the monarchy of Hynsolge. The emperor was first of the realm, by inheritance or by conquest, but it was the senate - drawn from the two hundred or so foremost patrician families - that made the laws. The emperor sat among them as first among equals, with the right to speak first in every debate, so that the senators could bend to his wishes without losing face. The realm was run by salaried officials appointed for fixed terms, not by hereditary lords: its provinces were governed by procurators answerable to the senate, and grouped for defence into prefectures under their prefects. Roads and shipping held it together, taxes were paid in coin rather than in kind, and a money economy reached into nearly every corner - a thing wholly unlike the feudal lands that came after, where office is inherited and dues are paid in grain.

## The Four Orders

Imperial society was divided into four. Lowest were the **slaves**, perhaps a fifth of all the people - mostly captured barbarians and the children of slaves, the property of their masters, but able in time to buy their freedom. Above them the **humiliors**, the empire's non-jori peoples and freed slaves, half the population, judged by their own traditional laws and reckoned lesser only for standing outside jori culture; the empire cared nothing for skin or mother-tongue, and an able humilior might be raised to plebeian or even, in rare cases, patrician. The **plebeians** were the common jori, a third of the people, mostly free farmers and craftsmen, entitled to jori law wherever they went and more often than not able to read and write. At the top, scarcely one in a hundred, stood the **patricians**, the nobility - but a nobility of wealth, ability and proven loyalty rather than feudal oath, great landowners and officeholders who swore fealty to no lord and served only the state.

## Culture and Learning

Krun's philosophers shaped the empire's ideals long before there was an empire. Greatest among them was Zeno, who about a century before the founding raised an academy and taught duty, self-mastery, wisdom and moderation. His thought became the empire's unofficial state philosophy, the measure every person in power wished to be seen living by, however few truly did. Zeno set little store by the old gods, speaking instead of a single divine life-force, a spark of which each soul carries and to which it returns at death like a drop falling into the sea - a teaching poised between the worship of many gods and of one. The empire tolerated every religion that preached obedience to the emperor, and in many places the emperor himself was offered sacrifice as a god.

Learning reached heights never seen before or since. In Grivela stood the Bibliotheca Magna, the finest collection of books ever gathered in Ereb, closed to all but those who came bearing a letter from the head of a known school. Poets wrote in a jori that became the model for every later age, the long epic in unrhymed hexameter most prized of all - such as Rigilion's gushingly patriotic *Aurelion's Triumph*, on the first emperor's uniting of the realm.

Three amusements were found everywhere. The theatre, inherited from Krun, where the old plays were still staged and great actors won the fame of princes. The gladiator games, brought from Hynsolge and built into arenas in every great city - grandest of all the Arena Maxima of Grivela, whose floor could be flooded for mock sea-battles. And the four-horse chariot races on their oval tracks, as Hynsolgian and as nakedly commercial as the games. The Krunish lands kept their own older sports besides - wrestling, boxing, running, the long jump and the javelin - crowned every fifth year by the Hysixian Games, contested for honour alone, in which even a patrician might run as a common competitor.

## The Legions

The imperial army was the finest Ereb has known, and it broke nearly every foe it faced. Soon after the founding the old citizen-levy gave way to a paid, professional army that could be kept under arms the year round and stationed far from home. Its core was the legions of heavy infantry - forty men to a maniple, fifteen maniples to a six-hundred-strong cohort, eight cohorts to a legion of four thousand eight hundred - recruited among jori plebeians and humiliors, each legionary in banded cuirass and Roman helm with a great shield, short spear and short sword, screened by a cohort of archers. Cavalry was an afterthought: with no stirrup known in Ereb a charging lance was impossible, so horsemen served only to scout and raid. The empire built no castles - its army was too good at breaking them, and its lords kept their power in the cities - though it walled its cities, ports and bases well.

Its enemies were few and very unlike one another. To the north lay the brave but undisciplined forest barbarians, whom the emperors spent freely to keep fighting each other rather than uniting; across the Demon Tongue, the swift hippogriff-riding nomads of Akrogal, countered by hired eagle-riders; and in the south conquered Efaro, an ancient and mystical people who refused to be made jori and seethed under occupation. Grivela's own garrison, the Praetorian Guard, could make or unmake an emperor, so the cannier emperors filled it with northern barbarians who had no ties to the great families.

## Grivela

The capital was a city without equal: nearly half a million souls, a scale Ereb had never seen and never would again. Its tenements, the insulae, rose four to six storeys and now and then came crashing down; its avenues ran between the imperial palace, the senate house, the Bibliotheca Magna, the magicians' Academia Magica, the great baths and the Arena Maxima. The crowd was kept content by the old principle of bread and games, with patricians sponsoring spectacles to buy its goodwill. To set a story in Jorpagna is, more often than not, to set it in Grivela.

## The Line of Emperors

The first was **Aurelion**, the young Hynsolgian king who founded the realm in the Victory Year and ruled it ably until his death at fifty-eight, passing the throne to his son **Tiberion**, who annexed Tolan and drew the elves of Landori into association with the empire. Under **Caion** the barbarians of Mirel invaded and were broken at Lake Trasimo, after which Caion conquered all the land from Mirel to Magilre and fixed the empire's northern bound for good. **Tamsolion I** had a council of barbarian chieftains murdered at the fort of Grafferburg, and his general Varion then led seven legions to disaster in the forests of Nargur, from which only a few hundred men returned.

The golden age closed with **Aurelion II**, ruler and philosopher both, who studied Zeno, wrote the book *Via Vitae* on the art of living, softened the lot of the lowly, and forbade necromancy throughout the empire. He died without an heir, and the realm fell into the **Warlords' War**, a generation in which the prefects tore the empire apart and did it more harm than any barbarian invasion ever had. Out of that chaos rose the archmage **Zenobia**, who set the general Markion on the road to the throne - until he went conveniently mad on the eve of his triumph, and the army hailed Zenobia herself empress. She ruled wisely for a full hundred years, the Silver Age, won women a greater standing than they had ever had, and saw her general **Belisarion** conquer Efaro, the empire's last great conquest. At her death her chosen heir was murdered within months, and the general **Domion** seized power: the Sun-Emperor, a hard despot worshipped as a demigod, who stripped the senate of its powers and so set the great families conspiring against him, even as the northern barbarians united under the warlord Tharaksos and Efaro smouldered toward revolt.

Jorpagna stood on for more than a century after Domion, until the Third Conflux fell upon it around 600 fO and the empire collapsed for good.

## Legacy

The empire fell, but it did not vanish without trace. Its deepest mark upon the world is its language. The common tongue that merchants, pilgrims and wanderers speak across Ereb today - jori - is the worn and weathered descendant of the imperial speech. When the empire collapsed and its provinces drifted apart, that single language splintered into the many dialects of the successor lands, yet beneath them all the old imperial grammar still endures. Its emblem outlived it too: a golden eagle clutching a thunderbolt, which came through the empire's fall and the Dark Age to become, ever after, the mark of great worldly power among the jori peoples.
