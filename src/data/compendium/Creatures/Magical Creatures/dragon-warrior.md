# Dragon Warrior

Dragon warriors are tall, grave fighters called up out of the ground by an old ritual, the dragon-sowing, in which a sower plants the teeth of a single dragon and raises an armed host from them. They are hard to set among the known kinds of being. They are not elementals, though some elemental mages insist they must be; they are not spirits, and they are not undead. The nearest a learned man can come is to call them a manifested life, a thing standing midway between the living and the spirit world.

## The Dragon-Sowing

The ritual that makes them is the work of a sower - a skald deep in the dragon-tongue - who carves true names into a dead dragon's teeth, sows them in a wide ring of soft ground, and over a day of song, fire, herb-smoke and his own spilled blood calls the teeth awake. The teeth rise first as figures of mist above each planted tooth, and over a few minutes harden into men. There were once two forms of the rite. The older one made the dragon-humanoids, a whole man-like race marked with the blood of dragons, and that knowledge is long lost; the form still worked was pieced back together from old legend, and only one book, the *Liber Draconicus* of Leoper Merkaptanso, is said to hold it whole, together with the three words of power that drive it. Other books carry fragments, and far more carry nonsense.

The sower binds the warriors by the true name he carves: to himself, to another person, or even to a realm, a place, or a bloodline. Carved teeth raise a host that serves whoever the name belongs to. The rite is perilous to the sower as much as to the world - done badly it can burn the whole ground to a sea of fire, wither his tongue past healing, or raise warriors who turn on the very company that called them. Done well, the sower is left mute for a time, and often falls into a dead faint or a longer coma from the strain.

## Appearance

The warriors look to be men between twenty-five and thirty-five, with clean, proud, faintly sorrowful faces. Most are men; perhaps one in seven is a woman, and the women's voices are likened to those of elves. They go in long mantles of grey or pale brown over a grey or ice-blue coat, with heavy gloves and knee-high boots of thick leather. At first they wear almost no armour, taking up a breastplate or a mail-shirt of dark red metal only as time goes on. Each carries from the first an unbreakable shield of stone, its face cut with a beast in relief - most often a running or howling wolf - that stands for the warrior's temper.

The first thing anyone marks, though, is the eyes. There is great strength and wisdom in the look of them, and it is said a dragon warrior can halt a fighting man with a glance, and read a person's fate by gazing into his eyes - though they keep what they see to themselves.

## Arms and Tokens

At two belts hang a long sword and a black field-flask the size of a wine-bottle, marked with a white rune. The flask holds dragon's blood, and a draught of it restores a warrior's strength a few times a day. It tends to stay full so long as it hangs at his side; pour the blood out for another and that share never returns. Should the flask be taken from him it slowly empties itself and crumbles away within a day, and the next day the warrior finds, "by pure chance," a near-identical flask somewhere close by. The third time the flask is lost, it is lost for good.

On the left hand each warrior wears a ring that keeps them all in silent contact, mind to mind, and frees them from any need for sleep. The leader of a host carries the bronze dagger from the ritual, in which much of the rite's power is gathered; he alone can study the hidden arts, is a fine tactician and captain, and is the first to know when the one they are sworn to needs them. He calls the host together by winding a horn of his own making, which the others hear however far off they are.

## Nature

Dragon warriors cannot lie. Their first tongue is the dragons' own, and the language binds them to truth; pressed, they answer in riddles or say nothing rather than speak falsely. They take other languages easily but always with a faint accent, and learn to read and write only with difficulty. Most have fine singing voices and a deep store of tales, and make memorable companions - until their unaging faces and their too-deep knowledge of old and obscure matters draw suspicion, and they move on. They hold themselves the equals of all folk, high or low, and have no patience for rank, which sets them at odds with proud nobles; elves and dwarves they find interesting, the rest they take or leave.

By legend a new-raised host first travels south together to seek the source of the rainbow and the mountain of spirits, where a hidden hall holds gold, gear and rest; some warriors stay there, sleeping, against the hour they are needed.

## In Battle and Death

They are said to hate fighting, and take it with an eerie calm, always waiting on the enemy's stroke. Gathered for war they line up mounted on a height, and at the leader's horn charge as one, loosing a freezing cry that unsteadies man and horse alike before they scatter through the foe and cut down all who attack. They never strike a man who is down - though one who abuses that mercy is killed without fail - and they slip away in secret as a battle ends, gone as suddenly as they came.

They are very hard to wound, feeling little pain and bleeding little; common weapons bite at only a third of their strength, holy and magical ones at half, and only a tin-tipped blade - or the blows of one warrior upon another - cuts full. The one weapon they truly dread is the one that slew the dragon they sprang from: they can smell it near, and its touch gives a pain almost past bearing. When a warrior falls his body withers within minutes to a husk of skin and bone, a sight so dreadful that onlookers may be struck with terror. Yet he is not surely dead. Unless his bones are scattered he rises again after a day or two from the place they lie; but if a bone is lost he becomes a bound ghost, unable to fight, who may possess a living body until his bones are gathered again. Whoever can gather a fallen warrior's bones is richly rewarded. Only a single blow great enough to undo him outright kills a dragon warrior for good, and then he goes with a last cry of mortal dread.
