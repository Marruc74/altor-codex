# Dragon

Of all the monsters in the world the dragon stands apart, and not for its size and fire alone. A dragon's mind runs on lines so unlike any other creature's that those who would study it are warned, before all else, to set aside what they think they know and take nothing for granted. Almost everything a dragon does, from the hoard it guards to the holiest oath it breaks, comes back in the end to a single idea its own tongue calls nåsh, for which there is no honest word in any speech of men.

## Nåsh

Nåsh is power in all its forms, and at the same time standing, pride and a kind of worth, yet unlike standing it owes nothing to what others think of the dragon. It is closer to the measure a dragon takes of itself against the whole of the world, bound up too with its sense of its own vigour. A dragon's chief aim, all its life, is to raise its nåsh by growing mightier, stronger and more cunning, and above all by showing those qualities off. The thinking runs through every part of how it lives, so that the question "why does the dragon do this?" can nearly always be answered with "it depends on nåsh."

A dragon is not wicked so much as wholly amoral. It has no notion of right or wrong, good or ill, and on the dragon-tongue a moral reproach cannot even be spoken; the word "good" does not exist in it at all. So a dragon breaks the most solemn promise without a qualm, lies whenever lying pays, shows mercy only when mercy serves it, and reaches for guile and betrayal as readily as for its claws. Pity it does not know. It honours one law only, the right of the strong, and does not so much as separate owning a thing from the right to own it.

## The Endless Contest

A dragon is forever at a game its tongue names taal-nåsha, a contest of standing it plays against every creature it meets. The root of it is taal, a struggle of any kind, of words or of force, real or in jest, in which one side's every gain is the other's loss; a game of chess is taal, while shooting at a mark, where a creature only strives against itself, is not. In taal-nåsha the dragon strives without rest to outdo whoever stands before it in cunning, knowledge, power or strength, even when no blade is drawn and no battle joined.

This is why dragons are such utter loners. Since any ally is also a rival in the game, they enter alliances grudgingly and treat them as a necessary evil at best. It is why too that a dragon, given the choice between yielding and dying, will fight to the death every time, for to submit is to surrender its nåsh, and not even death will buy that from it. And it turns insult on its head: to call a dragon a great liar is to grant that it lies convincingly, so the words land as a compliment, as do "fearsome monster," "scourge of mankind" and their like.

## The Hoard

It can seem past understanding that dragons risk their all but endless lives to gather enormous hoards they do nothing with but sleep upon. Learned folk long held that the treasure must have some use, that fine things worked on a dragon's mind and sweetened its dreams, a notion that endured only because the scholars were too bound to the humanoid way of thinking to see past it. The truth is plainer. Gold and gems and precious things raise a dragon's nåsh. They are trophies and tokens of victory, won to stand as proof of its power, and a dragon will guard its hoard to the death and never give it up.

## Hunger

For all that a dragon seems to swallow anything whole, an armoured knight and his weapons with him, it eats surprisingly little, though it grows all its life and bears itself aloft on the wing. It moves but seldom, runs cold like a serpent, and the greatest beasts spend least for their bulk. Its body works on a kind of vitriol that lets it dissolve almost any matter, and from its food it draws what it needs, above all the iron of its bones and the gas that lets it fly and breathe its fire.

Yet a dragon does not eat without choosing. By the law its tongue names präshu, the surest proof of a victory is to eat the one you have beaten, and to devour a great hero is deeply pleasing to the nåsh. The dish a dragon prizes above all others is another dragon, the mightiest meat in the world, and there are old accounts of beasts that fought to the death over territory and then ate the loser where it fell.

## The Female Phase

Normally every dragon is male. Only two to four times in its long life, somewhere between its eighth and its fortieth century, does a dragon turn for a while to a female state its tongue names khémren. The female then seeks out a male of strong nåsh and mates with him. Two years on she lays her eggs, which hatch some five to twelve years after that; she stays by the father about ten years more, then leaves to sleep a year alone. In that sleep the khémren passes and the dragon forgets, or buries the memory, for the female part of mating is bound up with submission and cannot stand beside nåsh. This is why a dragon will never own to being mother to a brood, even when somewhere within it knows the truth.

There lies a danger worth a whole tale. Wake a dragon in that year-long sleep and the forgetting may never come, so that the beast knows exactly which young are its own; and then the former female may hunt and kill its own offspring to wipe so shameful a stain from its nåsh. The same cold reckoning explains a grim old rumour. Because it raises a dragon's nåsh to slay and devour another creature's young and, above all, its mate, the tales of dragons that prey on young women are true, and it is this, not any glory won from weak victims, that draws them.

## Cunning Over Strength

A dragon would far rather kill a foe by guile and treachery than in open battle, and reckons it the finer deed. Its body already outmatches any humanoid by plain force, while the gap in wit and strength of soul is the narrower one, so it holds the subtle power higher, the wit and persuasion and inner strength its tongue calls dénhlinåsh, above the mere brawn of the "honest fight" the knights are so proud of. A victory of the mind pleases it more than one of the body. Even so, a dragon that finds itself losing such a contest will fall back on plain brute force without a moment's shame.

## The Eastern Dragon

All of this is the way of the western dragon, the kind the dragon-masters name in their books the dread dragon. Far to the east lives another sort, the lung, which seems to feed its nåsh, or whatever stands in its place, by being wise and good and exalted rather than by conquest and hoarding. Of these beasts even the dragon-masters know little, and what they have set down is scattered and uncertain, a gap that the present Dragon-Master means one day to go east and fill.
