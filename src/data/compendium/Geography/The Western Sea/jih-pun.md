# Jih Pun

![Jih Pun](/compendium/Geography/Other/Jih-Pun/Jih-Pun1.jpg "Jih Pun")
![Jih Pun male](/compendium/Geography/Other/Jih-Pun/Male1.jpg "Jih Pun male")
![Jih Pun female](/compendium/Geography/Other/Jih-Pun/Female1.jpg "Jih Pun female")

Far to the west, beyond seas that few dare cross and further still than Trakorien, lies Jih-pun, a land spoken of more in stories than on maps. It is a realm of steep mountains and mist-filled forests where cedar trees rise like silent watchers and ancient shrines cling to cliffs and valleys. Its people live in careful balance between discipline and fear, for the world they walk is not theirs alone.

Samurai serve with unwavering loyalty, bound by oath and tradition, while shinobi move unseen through the hidden spaces between power and secrecy. Yet steel alone does not rule this land. Spirits linger in rivers and forests, demons are said to sleep beneath stone and fire, and the dead are not always willing to depart.

By day, Jih-pun is a place of order and beauty, of courts, poetry and quiet ritual. By night it becomes something else, where lantern-light flickers and the unseen draws close. Few from Ereb ever reach its shores, and those who do return with tales that sound like dreams or warnings.

## The Land

Jih-pun is in the main a single island, Hokkodo, about the size of a northern kingdom and split down its length by the Fujimuri mountains, a range of three-thousand-metre peaks with a dozen live volcanoes among them. Two long bays bite into the island and all but cut it in two, leaving only the narrow isthmus of Tsun-shan to join the cold and barren north, Beikuo, to the milder south, Nankuo. Nearly three of every four of the ten million islanders live south of that neck.

The land sits on the seam where two blocks of the earth grind together, so a tremor comes at least once a week, and now and then a great quake throws down anything built of stone. For that reason the islanders build low and light, of wood and paper and lashed beam, and reckon the sea, not the road, their true highway; their first emperor called it the greatest road under the sun. The two largest cities are Nankyo, the imperial seat, and Shikyo, the centre of craft, each home to some three hundred thousand people.

The folk of the island are the Jih-mono, who came long ago over the water and drove out the first people, the fair-skinned, bearded Ainu, pushing them north into the cold mountains where that older race lingers still.

## A Strict Order

Jih-pun is a proud and ancient civilisation, and every soul knows its rung on the ladder. At the summit sit the kuge, the aristocracy of the court, and below them the buke, the warrior nobility: the daimyo who hold the land, the samurai who serve them, the jizamurai who work their own small holdings, and the masterless ronin. These are freed from all tax, stand outside the common law, and answer only to their lord or the emperor. Beneath them come the heimin, the common folk, ranked by what they make rather than what they own, so that farmers stand above artisans and artisans above merchants. Lowest of all are the eta, the unclean and the outcast, who do the work of death and filth and to whom the shadow-walking shinobi and the gambling, extorting yakuza clans both belong.

Birth fixes the rung, but a warrior's profession fixes his place within it. A samurai bears the right of kirisutegomen, to cut down on the spot any commoner who insults him, and to a samurai money itself is shameful, so that his wife keeps the household's accounts while he holds himself above the touch of coin.

## History

The islanders reckon their years from the accession of the emperor Hakamoto, who in the year nothing of that count broke the last of the warring city-states and had himself proclaimed the first emperor of a single Jih-pun. Before him the land had been a scatter of as many as two hundred city-states, each ruled by a strongman or a council and forever feuding. Hakamoto gave the realm common law and standard measures, forbade all weapons but those of his own warriors, and so raised up the samurai class; his son Takamori set down the first full book of laws and fixed the throne upon the eldest son.

The line of emperors held for centuries, through plague and famine and the slow rise of the great lords, until the office of shogun, the supreme commander of the army, swallowed the power of the throne. From 1192 the shoguns ruled in truth and the emperors only in name, and because no shogun's title passes to his son but only to the man he names, every death of a shogun has bred a fresh war. It was in 1542 of the islanders' reckoning, the year 551 by the count of Ereb, that the first Westerners reached these shores, pale and yellow-haired in their great ships, bringing tobacco, strange drugs and new sicknesses, and earning from the islanders the name of long-noses.

## The Warrior's Way

In Jih-pun it is duty and honour, not the gods, that bind a warrior, and a debt of obligation may outlast a lifetime. This is the bushido, the way of the warrior, and it asks honesty, loyalty, courage, courtesy and a hard self-discipline. Courage here is not only the absence of fear but a different reckoning of life and death, for to die in a lord's service is held an honour; yet to throw life away needlessly is a shame, and a samurai who shows pain disgraces himself, though he may freely show grief or love or joy.

The arts of war are prized above raw strength: a blade here is finer and more elegant than any forged in Ereb, and skill and timing count for more than brute force. The high nobility train in schools as the swordsmen of Ereb train in their academies, and hold the empty-handed arts in equal honour with the sword, from the open hand of karate to the throws of the soft school and the secret ninjutsu of the hidden clans.

## Honour and Seppuku

A samurai's worth, and his family's, is measured in honour, and honour lost is not easily borne. When a warrior is disgraced, falsely accused, or set an impossible task, the answer that clears his name is seppuku: in a quiet place, in a white kimono with a red sash, he declares his reason and opens his own belly with the short blade, while a second strikes off his head before the pain can show. A woman of the warrior class takes her own life instead by the knife at the throat, which they call jigai. The act can be a reproach as much as an ending, used to shame a lord into seeing a wrong, to follow a dead master into the grave, or simply to escape a vengeance that would otherwise run on for generations as a blood-feud.

## Religion

Two faiths share the land. The Way of Peace is a teaching of rebirth, in which a soul climbs or falls through many lives by the weight of its deeds and seeks at last to purify itself into the stillness of nirvana; it keeps no true gods, only the example of Bodharma, who is said to have made and then repented of the world. The Many Gods is the older worship, the religion of the eight hundred million gods, which holds that everything is ensouled and that kami, spirits, dwell in every stone and stream and mountain, to be honoured and appeased with offerings of rice and flowers. Above the countless nature-kami and the spirits of the ancestors stand thirteen greater kami, who keep the night, fruitfulness, love, justice, war, ruin, art, craft, healing and knowledge.

Both faiths agree that the world of spirits lies folded inside the world of the living, and that at certain places gaps open between them through which power streams in as the eight elements: heaven, earth, thunder, wind, water, fire, mountain and wood. The shugenja are those who have learned to gather and bend that power, and the mightiest can force the gaps open at will. In the islanders' oldest story the lonely first being, called Karaminga or Bodharma, was born into the shell of a turtle that became the round of the world, and shaped the three peoples of the earth from three porcelain figures, one fired too black, one too pale, and the third a fitting brown.

## The Spirit World

The unseen world is unusually close in Jih-pun, and many beings share the land with its people. The oni are its demons, the tatsu its dragons that run upon the air, the tengu its proud bird-folk of the high peaks, the kirin its gentle horned beasts; its ghosts are the yokai, its mountain trolls the o-bakemono, its goblins and orcs the lesser and greater bakemono. To these the islanders' own lore adds the river-dwelling kappa with their bowl of water and their love of cucumbers, the giant kumo spiders of forest and crag, the night-flying detached heads of the rokurokubi, the day-walking shutendoji who pass for living folk until dark, the serpent-tailed nymphs of mountain and spring, and the shapeshifting hengeyokai who live unseen among ordinary people. By day Jih-pun is a place of order; by night it belongs as much to these.
