# The Demon Tongue

The Demon Tongue, called Demontungan by those who dwell near it, is the strangest of the three outflows of the Golwynda Sea. Here the upper sea does not simply fall over a cliff. Instead it bores clean through the great stone bridge that links Ereb to Akrogal, driving through the rock in a single roaring channel before bursting out the far side to jet far over the Maseva Bay below. The water leaps from the stone like a vast grey tongue thrust from the mouth of the world, and it is from this sight that the outflow takes its name.

So awesome is the spectacle that the peoples of the bay do not see mere water in it. They worship the Demon Tongue as the god Okasu, and the thunder of its falling is taken for the voice of the god himself. Pilgrims and offerings come to the shores of the Maseva Bay where the tongue strikes the sea, and the moods of the water are read as the favour or anger of Okasu.

## Overview

- **Type:** Central outflow of the Golwynda Sea
- **Form:** Water tunnels through the Ereb-Akrogal stone bridge and jets over the Maseva Bay
- **Worship:** Revered as the god Okasu
- **Town:** Bahaz, on the Maseva Bay

## The Stone Bridge

The Demon Tongue would be wonder enough on its own, but it shares its place with another - the great stone bridge that joins the continents of Ereb and Akrogal across the water. The bridge carries the only land road between the two continents, and over it moves a heavy and unceasing traffic of caravans. Its western end is held and controlled by the Nidlanders, who command who may cross and what tolls they pay, making the bridge a source of both wealth and power to that people.

## Bahaz and the Maseva Bay

Below the leaping water, on the shore of the Maseva Bay, stands the town of Bahaz. It lives in the shadow and spray of the Demon Tongue, and its fortunes are bound up with both the worship of Okasu and the trade that crosses the stone bridge above. Here the caravans that have come over the bridge meet the ships of the Maseva Bay, and here the faithful gather to honour the god whose voice never falls silent.
