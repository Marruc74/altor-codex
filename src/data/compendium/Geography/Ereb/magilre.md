# Magilre

![Magilre](/compendium/Geography/Ereb/Countries/Magilre/Magilre1.jpg "Magilre")
![Magilre male](/compendium/Geography/Ereb/Countries/Magilre/Male1.jpg "Magilre male")
![Magilre female](/compendium/Geography/Ereb/Countries/Magilre/Female1.jpg "Magilre female")

Magilre is a land where wealth travels slowly and power rarely rests in one place. Across wide plains and quiet roads, caravans move under the steady pull of draft ziploditer, hauling grain, salt fish and finely worked goods between scattered towns. In the capital Munzga, trade fills the streets while nobles and merchants shape alliances behind closed doors. The land itself is divided, ruled by many barons who guard their own interests even as they bow to a distant king. Beyond the farmlands lies Efronzink, a marsh where the ground sinks into mist and something vast moves beyond sight. Magilre is rich, but never truly united, a place where patience builds fortunes and a clever tongue carries further than a sword.

## Overview

- **Population:** approx. 1.8 million (humans only)
- **Area:** approx. 225,000 km²
- **Capital:** Munzga (~36,000 inhabitants)
- **Largest city:** Moril Dilaz (~48,000), a canal-city and one of the richest in all Ereb
- **Government:** a king sharing power with self-governing nobility across 98 baronies
- **Ruler:** King Cerebuz II of the Ry'caril line, bynamed "Long-nose"
- **Religion:** the Shining Way, dominant but politically weak
- **Exports:** wheat, maize, dried and salted fish, mussels
- **Imports:** nearly everything but foodstuffs and timber
- **Unique feature:** the Low Line, a tramway network linking the whole country
- **Tongue:** Magil, a continental jori dialect

Magilre is one of the richest and most densely settled lands of Ereb, yet one of the most politically fragmented. The crown is wealthy and respected, but real power lies scattered among a proud and quarrelsome nobility who govern their own holdings much as they please.

## Geography

The heart of Magilre is famously flat farmland, plain after plain rolling toward a level horizon broken only by the tall multi-storey castles of the nobility. The fields are split by thin strips of windbreak woodland, planted to keep the loose soil from blowing away across the open ground.

Rural life turns about the villages, each ringed by farmland that radiates outward like the spokes of a wheel, most of it worked by peasants who owe labour-days to the local baron. Toward the interior the land rises through soft hills into the steep western foothills of the Klomeller Mountains, along the border with Klomellien.

West of the last hills lies Efronzink, Efron's pool, a vast swamp of mist and standing water that has defied every attempt to drain or conquer it. Its depths teem with rich and dangerous wildlife, and somewhere within it the surviving Kabrinzi Seekers keep their hidden refuge.

## Fauna

The native beasts of Magilre are the ziploditer, which come in several kinds set apart by size and temper. The swamp ziplodit is the giant of the family, so vast it collapses under its own weight at around two hundred years, and its flesh is foul to the tongue. The draft ziplodit is slow but immensely strong, a gentle leaf-eater and the ideal beast of burden, hauling the great wagon-trains along the roads. The steppe ziplodit is smaller and swift, a sharp-sensed grass-eater that raids the maize fields in herds and is loathed by farmers. Smallest of all is the Magiller snail, an arm-long, shell-less creature prized as a gourmet delicacy and now much over-harvested.

The swamps and farmland also hold two species of tiny wild "elephants," scarcely a metre tall yet famously ferocious. One walks on ordinary feet; the other is flat-footed, with toes that splay to carry it across the soft bogs of Efronzink.

## People

The Magillers themselves look much like the average folk of Ereb Altor and dress practically, the nobility excepted, whose festival garb is made as impractical as possible to show that the wearer need never stir, all metre-long sleeves of dried and perfumed seagrass, vast wheel-shaped ruff collars, hats hung with little bells, and curl-toed shoes knotted at the tip. Descended from the Joric legionaries who once civilised the land, they are no longer a warlike people, and in a tight corner they trust to a sharp tongue and deft diplomacy rather than to arms. So famed is their eloquence that the saying "to talk someone out of their wits like a Magiller" is known across all Ereb, and the country has bred many a gifted demagogue and politician.

It is whispered that this gift is more than mere practice. The loanwords the Magillers inherited from the land's first, vanished people are said to carry a true persuasive charm, so that one who masters the old dialect and speaks it to a listener who understands it can bend the mind as surely as any spell. Whether the tale is true or only the boast of a talkative race, even the well-schooled nobility are said to be swayed by it.

### The Dyler

The Dyler are the displaced barbarian folk of the mountains, short, robust and very strong, masters of woodcraft but weak in lore, and given to wild swings of rage, tears and mirth. Long ago they were driven back into the Klomeller Mountains, and their old coastal city of Jicon was taken by the incoming Jorers and rebuilt as Moril Dilaz. More than a thousand years on, the Dyler have never forgiven the theft, and still nurse the grudge of their lost city.

### The Nobility

The nobility, descended from old patrician families and retired generals, hold their duchies and baronies almost as their own. They reckon open violence against a fellow noble dishonourable, and so settle their disputes with words rather than weapons, the most convincing argument carrying the day by consensus in the Council.

## Language

The tongue of Magilre is Magil, a continental jori dialect carrying loanwords for the things peculiar to the land. It has taken in Jorduasher words for fishing and Kardiska words for matters of religion, and the older inherited loanwords are the ones credited with their persuasive charm.

## Government

Magilre is divided into 98 baronies, of which 63 lie within 7 duchies; the rest answer directly to the Crown. The richest region is the south coast, where the duchies of Galder and Bzegusta-in-the-east dominate the mussel farms.

The smallest duchy by area is Munzga, the capital itself. Through a tangle of inheritance its rightful duke is none other than the king, which leads each year to a comical ceremony in which the king solemnly swears loyalty to himself, and to the proverb "as loyal as the Duke of Munzga."

Power is shared uneasily between crown and nobles. The king's personal might is limited, but his word carries great weight, and to openly defy his "well-meaning advice" is deeply unpopular. Disputes are settled not by arms but in the Council, where whoever argues most convincingly prevails. The sharpest tension runs between the old court clique of Munzga and the new-moneyed merchants of Moril Dilaz, through whose harbour most of the realm's trade flows; the court resents the upstarts but, bound by its own code, will not move openly against them.

## Currency

Magilre's coins are struck in the shape of stylised wheat-ears and are called fogar in common speech. They come in copper, silver and gold, and are three times the size of ordinary coin, so prices quoted in fogar run accordingly high. Banking here is well developed for the world of Ereb, and the realm has even devised a primitive system of cheques, though only nobles and the richest merchants may write one.

## Religion

The Shining Way is the dominant faith of Magilre, though it holds far less political weight here than in Zorakin or Kardien. Its high priest, the kerisgass Juniz Duolik, sits in Munzga and directs all religious affairs. The Magillers have long been loyal but indifferent temple-goers. Fishers and sailors widely honour the trakoric sea-goddess Anxalis, and foreign faiths are tolerated, if grudgingly, for the sake of trade.

### The Kabrinzi Order

Older and stranger is the fallen faith of the Kabrinzi. It was founded long ago by a club of some twenty intellectuals, authors, scholars and magicians, who set out to discover the one true and unassailable religion. After half a century of research, divination and travel to other worlds, they arrived at a single god, Kabrinzi, who governs natural law and the fate of every being, and set down their teachings in the Book of the Eternal Songs.

The order's ruin began with Zumlit Zaar, an alcoholic, drug-addled alchemist recruited into the club by mistake. Convinced the doctrine must be spread by any means, he murdered the other founders, burning them alive in a magical "firework" that left a crater still visible to this day, then preached the faith himself with terrible charisma before vanishing without trace on a missionary journey. He is said to live yet, sustained by a drug for some five centuries.

After his disappearance a curia of priests founded a knightly order to spread the faith, but it degenerated into an ungodly robber band that burned fields and killed peasants in Kabrinzi's name, winning converts but ruining the order's repute. The church's true power lay in three relics that could sway a whole crowd with a single word: an amulet, a short silver staff, and a glass flask of perpetually swirling vapours, kept under heavy guard and used only with great caution.

When the rulers came to favour the Shining Way as the folk-religion, bold nobles broke into the vault, stole the three relics, and murdered the entire Kabrinzi leadership. The order collapsed, its temples destroyed or left to rot, and the relics were scattered and hidden away, since no one knew how to wield them.

A remnant survived in an inaccessible reach of Efronzink, sworn to restore the faith by recovering the lost relics. Two of the faithful at a time undergo a ceremony in which they drink the holy water Mindusvinet; should they fail to find all three relics within twenty years, the Seekers' curse strikes them dead, though before dying each may name two new Seekers, granted twenty years of their own. Today perhaps three thousand adherents remain, in three branches: the Monks, secluded in the mountains with their prayers and ritual objects; the Underground, the largest group, ordinary folk of every trade who meet in secret; and the Wandering Knights, armoured messengers and enforcers who bear no heraldry and are known only by the pure white horses they all ride.

## Moril Dilaz

Moril Dilaz is the great canal-city of Magilre and one of the richest in all Ereb. Founded ages ago by the Dyler as Jicon, it stood on the only break in the cliffs of the West Sea, but its marshy ground slowly sank until, after a century, it was unliveable, just as Joric settlers arrived with their legions and drove the Dyler out. The subsidence later stabilised, and the Jorers rebuilt the place as Moril Dilaz. Water now flows between the buildings, and folk travel by boat, by short rowboat or dolphin-drawn taxi, crossing on terraced walkways and ornate pillared bridges. The water runs so clear that on a sunny day one can glimpse the sunken old city shimmering far below.

The city falls into four districts. Old Gamla Jicon is the middle-class centre, home to craftsmen, bakers, boat-builders and officials, with decent inns and lower-end mystics. The Harbour is the working quarter of quays, customs house and warehouses, the only district with real streets, reeking of fish, beer and tar. Gunsten is the exclusive enclave by the duke's palace, where one pays a toll to enter and rich merchants, nobles and bankers keep their guards and sedan chairs. The Poor Quarters are the city's worn and lawless backside, where the great fishing fleet finds its crews.

Beneath it all lingers the legend of Rhys. Long ago, sorcerers were said to dwell under the bay, mistaken for gods, until the Dyler drove off all but the young Rhys, who took his revenge by slowly sinking their city. When the Jorers came they struck a pact: leave Rhys in peace, and he would guard the city from any great threat. To this day the common folk of Moril Dilaz look kindly on magicians for his sake.

## History

Magilre's first people left no trace, and in ancient times the barbarian Dyler rolled across the peninsula before they were stopped. The land was then colonised by patrician families out of Jorpagna, farmers from Grivela who brought civilisation and a labour force, and under their Joric estate-owners Magilre became a breadbasket, supplying grain, dried fish and mussels to much of the world.

With the fall of Jorpagna the imports of metal and other goods were cut off, and the ill-prepared land briefly reverted to stone tools before trade was slowly re-established. In time the revival of the Shining Way swept the region and ousted the older Kabrinzi faith.

The island of Bzegusta was long counted part of Magilre, until the forces of the Klavyk realm "liberated" it in a bloody conquest around the turn of the first century after Odo. The duke Ogilanz Ry'caril, who had earlier fled to the island after publicly mocking the Klavyk king, distinguished his line in the war that followed, and in 151 eO his descendant was crowned Capaz I, king of all Magilre, vowing that his house's foremost goal would be to one day reclaim Bzegusta. His line has ruled unbroken ever since, down to the present day around 610 eO, and the loss of the island still drives Magilre to send mercenaries to the wars of the trakoric isles.

## Heraldry

The arms of Magilre bear a green chevron set between two lilies, marking the unbroken continuity of the royal family, together with the arms of the Shining Way as a sign of the realm's piety.
