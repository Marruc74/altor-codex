# Sése Hesuóni

In the heart of Ereb, between Palinor and the Nargur forest, lies a wide, flat plateau of grass that the southern jori call Priglasili, the green plain, but that the people who live there name Sése Hesuóni, the whispering grass. It is dry but its soil is rich, and the grass grows so thick and deep-rooted that no ordinary plough can break it, so that grass is in effect the only thing that grows: a vast, rolling sea of it, bright green in summer and waist-high, crossed by slow streams running down out of the Nargur woods toward the south-west, and buried under snow in winter. It belongs to no kingdom and never has. Its people have bowed to no outside ruler in all their history.

## Overview

- **Names:** Sése Hesuóni, "the whispering grass" (the people's own name); Priglasili, "the green plain" (the jori name)
- **Where:** a grass plateau in central Ereb, between Palinor and the Nargur forest
- **Peoples:** the sásuit, "plain-wanderers" - humans, centaurs and sylphs living as one
- **Government:** none over the whole; each tribe is ruled by a chieftain chosen for life
- **Way of life:** nomad herders, following great cattle from waterhole to waterhole
- **Faith:** spirit-worship and shamanism
- **Trade:** composite bows and fine leatherwork, traded south to Palinor for iron

## The Plain and Its Beasts

The grass sea teems with life. Great herds of antelope, wild horse, buffalo and woolly giant rhinoceros graze the rich grass and are hunted in turn by wolves, wild dogs, cave lions and the brown, spotless northern cheetah. Burrowing prairie-dogs riddle the ground, small birds live among the stalks and fly south to the Copper Sea coasts in winter, camouflaged snakes deadly enough to kill a man with one bite hunt them, and high overhead hawks hover while vultures and white-headed eagles wheel in search of carrion.

## The Sásuit

Three thinking peoples share the plain and live as one, which is rare indeed in Ereb, where the races mostly keep apart. They call themselves together the sásuit, the plain-wanderers, and reckon all three peoples and both sexes wholly equal.

Half are the **Ténuit**, the humans, plainly northern stock: tall, blond and blue-eyed, but for the strange ridge of hair that runs down the spine, and given to long braids, the men braiding their beards as well. Two in five are the **Tsírnuit**, the centaurs, black of coat and hair yet fair-skinned and blue-eyed, who unlike most of their kind have turned away from the elf-folk to throw in their lot with the humans. The last tenth are the **Tsélai**, the sylphs: tiny winged folk, like a blond, blue-eyed human a half-metre tall with hairless bat-wings two metres across, kin to the elves in seeming but mortal as men. The centaurs herd the great beasts, the sylphs scout from the air, and the humans handle the crafts, the fighting and the dealing with other human folk.

## Tribes and Herds

The sásuit are nomads, living in tribes of thirty to two hundred, each tribe holding a herd of five to ten thousand beasts - mostly cattle close to the old wild oxen, with horses besides - that it drives across the plain from water to water. The herding is the heart of everything. Each tribe answers to its own animal totem and gives its first loyalty to the tribe rather than to the sásuit as a whole, and tribes camped near one waterhole may fall to fighting over it; among the young, stealing another tribe's cattle is a favourite sport. A tribe is led by a chieftain, man or woman, chosen for life and ruling by old custom and the counsel of its wise, for a foolish chieftain may be killed by their own people, the tribe's good standing before any one life. They speak dialects of a single tongue, hísit, which has no writing of its own but may be set down in jori runes or satenu script.

## War and Honour

The sásuit are warlike but undisciplined, and both men and women take up arms. They prize a name for toughness and seize any chance at personal glory, learning from childhood to guard tribe and herd against beast and foe with lance, spear, wooden shield, leather armour and the composite bow, and every human learns to ride superbly. They will sooner die than show a lack of courage, and hold courage, keeping one's word, and loyalty to tribe and kin the highest of virtues, with greed, lying and cowardice the most despised of faults. A human or centaur who downs a full-grown cave lion alone wears its teeth on a necklace and its hide as a jacket; a sylph who downs an eagle alone wears one of its claws. A veteran may wear several such tokens, and a great many scars.

## Craft and Trade

For all that their tools are of the stone age - they have never learned to win or work metal, taking their iron through trade with Palinor and tipping their spears and arrows with antelope horn - their handiwork is masterly. Their layered horn bows and their supple, finely embroidered leatherwork are sought far to the south, and these are the goods they trade for what the plain cannot give them. They are spirit-worshippers and shamanists, and treat magic of every kind with deep respect; a tribe will commonly keep a shaman or two, of any of the three races, who guard its traditions and legends and serve besides as healers and mixers of drugs.

## A Sleeping Power

Splintered as they are into quarrelling tribes, the sásuit weigh little in the affairs of the wider world. But they are many, and superb in the saddle, and it is whispered that if ever one chieftain rose with the gift to unite the tribes and give them discipline, the plain-wanderers might sweep south or west like a flood that nothing could stand against, in search of glory or of empire.
