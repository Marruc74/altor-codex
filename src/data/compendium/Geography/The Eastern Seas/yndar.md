# Yndar

Yndar is known across Altor as the evil island, the cradle of necromancy and the dark arts of the dead. Yet it was not always so. In ages past it was home to a peaceful human high culture, a people of learning and quiet accomplishment. That was before the coming of the margyler, whose invasion broke the old order and remade Yndar in a colder image.

Under the margyler's shadow the island grew into a cruel, proud and deeply scholarly civilization. Its thinkers laid foundations that endure across the world to this day: the first mathematics, the art of abstract writing, and the disciplined craft of war. But the same minds that mastered numbers and letters turned as readily to darker studies - the refinement of torture, and the forbidden arts of necromancy and spiritism. What had once been a gentle culture became a power that probed every secret, however terrible.

In the end Yndar destroyed itself. The catastrophe is remembered as the Yndarian Depression, a ruin born of reckless and overreaching magic. The island's cursed city of Krau-Ki vanished outright, taken up by the gods and set down again upon the distant island of Marjura. A punitive expedition sent to Slimpaku loosed the creatures called the slempukar, and these in their fury razed Kamsun and Yndar alike, leaving the proud civilization broken among its own ruins.

## Overview

- **Region:** The Eastern Seas
- **Reputation:** The evil island; cradle of necromancy
- **Fate:** Ruined in the Yndarian Depression

## History

Yndar began as a peaceful human high culture until the margyler invaded and seized it. Under their rule the island became a cruel and proud scholarly civilization, brilliant and merciless in equal measure. Its long ascent ended in the Yndarian Depression, a self-wrought catastrophe of unchecked magic that destroyed the island from within. The cursed city of Krau-Ki vanished and was carried by the gods to the island of Marjura, while a punitive expedition to Slimpaku unleashed the slempukar, who razed both Kamsun and Yndar.

## Learning and the Dark Arts

For all its cruelty, Yndar was a wellspring of knowledge. Its scholars laid the foundations of mathematics, of abstract writing, and of the craft of war, achievements that spread far beyond the island. Yet the same learning was bent toward darker ends: the methodical art of torture, and above all the forbidden disciplines of necromancy and spiritism. Yndar is remembered as the very cradle of necromancy, and it is for this above all that the wider world names it the evil island.
